Consent and Birth Philosophy Page

Philosophy · Difficulty: Intermediate · ~12 min

A recurring question in birth ethics is whether consent can matter when no one yet exists to give it. This page lays the reasoning out as a flowchart, lets you weigh the competing tensions, and gives the strongest objections a fair hearing. The goal is to understand the argument well enough to disagree carefully.

What you will learn

  • The consent argument, step by step, as a logic flow.
  • The three senses of consent: actual, hypothetical, and proxy.
  • How four competing tensions — benefit, risk, consent, responsibility — pull the question.
  • The strongest objections, and a fair reply to each.

1. The consent logic, step by step

Flowchart of the consent argument. Each step is debatable — the objection explorer below challenges them.

2. Three senses of consent

Actual consentA real person agrees in advance. Impossible before birth — there is no one yet to agree. Hypothetical consentWhat a reasonable person would agree to. Some say most endorse their own existence; others doubt a non-existent being can have preferences. Proxy consentSomeone decides on another's behalf (as parents do for a child's surgery). Can anyone validly stand in for a person who does not yet exist?

4. Objection explorer

A fair page presents the best objections. Tap each to see the objection and a reply.

"But no one consents to anything before birth"

True — which is exactly the point for some, and the dissolution of it for others. If there is no one to wrong, perhaps no wrong occurs (the non-identity problem). Reply: the argument is about whether imposing un-consented risk is justified, not about wronging a specific prior person.

"Life can still be good"

Most lives contain real goods. Reply: that a gamble often pays off does not settle whether it was permissible to take on someone else's behalf without their say.

"Parents intend love"

Loving intent is genuine and matters. Reply: good intentions can accompany decisions that still warrant ethical scrutiny; intent and justification are different questions.

"Society needs people"

Societies do depend on new generations. Reply: collective need is a separate consideration from what is owed to the individual created — and using a person as a means to a social end raises its own ethical flags.

Key takeaway

Reasonable, thoughtful people land on different sides of this — often accepting the same premises but weighing them differently. Holding the question open, without rushing to a verdict, is itself a philosophical skill.
