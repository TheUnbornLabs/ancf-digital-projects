BIAS 017

Instinct as Destiny: The Myth of the Maternal Drive

Part II · Evolutionary & Biological Framings · The Myth of the Maternal Drive

The bias: Treating a contested "maternal instinct" as proof that women must reproduce.

No universal and absolute conduct on the part of the mother exists. Maternal love is a human feeling. And like any feeling, it is uncertain, fragile, and imperfect." — Elisabeth Badinter, Mother Love: Myth and Reality (paraphrase of her central claim) --- ## Preface to this volume There is a sentence that ends arguments. A woman says she does not want children, and someone answers: You'll change your mind. It's instinct. Not a preference, not a plan, not a fact about her particular life, but a force said to be wired into her body, waiting to override whatever she currently believes about herself. The sentence does two things at once. It predicts, and it corrects. It tells her what she will feel, and it implies that what she feels now is a mistake she has not yet noticed. Few other sentences aimed at an adult carry that combination so casually. We do not tell a man who says he does not want to run a marathon that his legs will eventually override him. We reserve this move for women and reproduction, and the reservation is itself a clue that something other than pure biology is speaking. This book is about that sentence and the idea beneath it: the claim that women possess a maternal instinct, a specific, universal, biologically fixed drive not merely to care for a child once present but to want children in the first place, and to be fulfilled only by having them. The claim is doing an enormous amount of work in how our culture reasons about reproduction. It converts a contested and highly variable set of capacities into a destiny. It makes not wanting children look, rather than one legitimate life among others, like a malfunction, a woman out of contact with her own nature. And because the claim wears the prestige of biology, it is unusually resistant to the ordinary give-and-take of disagreement. You can argue with an opinion. It is harder to argue with someone who says they are simply describing your body. The bias examined here is not the belief that human beings, women included, have evolved capacities for caregiving. They do; that is not in dispute and this book affirms it plainly. The bias is the inflation of those real, conditional, evolved capacities into a uniform female drive toward reproduction, and then the use of that inflated drive as proof of what women are for. It is the move from "many mothers come to love their infants intensely," which is true, to "every woman harbors a dormant longing to bear children, and a woman without it is broken," an unwarranted leap that the evidence does not support. The distance between those two statements is the entire subject of this book. One is a description of a common experience. The other is a theory of human nature dressed up as a description, and then handed to individual women as a verdict on their lives. I want to be careful about neighbors. Book 091 concerns compulsory motherhood, the social mandate that women ought to become mothers; that is a mandate about duty and role. Book 015 concerns the naturalistic fallacy, the general move from "natural" to "good." This book sits between them and is narrower than either. Its subject is one specific empirical claim, that a maternal instinct exists in the strong sense, and the way that claim is used as a trump card. We are asking two questions and keeping them distinct. First: what is the scientific status of the maternal-instinct claim? Second: what happens when a shaky claim is treated as destiny? Keeping these apart matters, because the bias thrives precisely by letting the second question ride on an assumed answer to the first. The stance is critical but not dismissive. The strongest version of the natalist intuition here is genuinely strong, and Chapter 2 gives it a full, sympathetic hearing before any critique begins. Evolved caregiving is real. Pregnancy and lactation involve real neuroendocrine changes. Many people experience a longing for children that feels bodily and unchosen, and nothing in this book calls those experiences false. The problem is not the experience; it is the theory built on top of it, a theory that universalizes what is variable, fixes what is facultative, and moralizes what is contingent. A reader who comes to this book expecting a debunking of mother-love will be disappointed, and that disappointment is the point. The target is not love. The target is a bad theory of love that has been asked to govern lives. Two commitments hold throughout. First, evidence before ideology: where the science is genuinely uncertain, and on instinct it often is, the book says so rather than substituting a tidier story. It would be easy, and dishonest, to answer an overconfident natalist claim with an equally overconfident denial. This book tries not to. Second, non-coercion in both directions. To criticize the myth of a uniform maternal drive is not to disparage mothers, mother-love, or the women for whom the desire for children is central and precious. It is to defend the accuracy of the picture and the freedom of the woman for whom it is not. A myth that flatters some people can still injure others, and can distort the self-understanding of everyone, including those it appears to serve. The book proceeds through the series' standard arc: naming the bias, steelmanning it, tracing its origins, showing how it operates, weighing the evidence, counting the hidden costs, asking who bears the pressure, answering the objections, illustrating with cases, mapping adjacent biases, and describing what clearer reasoning looks like. If it succeeds, the reader will not conclude that maternal love is fake. The reader will conclude something more useful: that love is real, that instinct-as-destiny is a myth, and that these two facts have always been compatible. --- ## 1. Naming the bias The maternal-instinct bias is the tendency to treat a contested, largely constructed idea, that women possess a fixed, universal biological drive to bear and rear children, as an established fact, and then to use that supposed fact as evidence that women are built to reproduce and therefore will, and should, want to. Three moves are compressed into one word. Instinct first asserts existence: there is a specific drive. It then asserts universality: all women have it. It then asserts fixity: it is innate, uniform, and not much altered by circumstance or culture. Stack these, add the further step that a drive reveals a purpose, and you arrive at destiny: the woman who lacks the drive is not exercising a different preference but failing to be what she is. Each of these moves is questionable on its own; stacked and hidden inside a single familiar word, they pass without inspection. This is how a great deal of consequential reasoning about women's lives gets done, not through an argument anyone would defend if it were laid out step by step, but through a word that has already done the arguing in advance. The one-sentence thesis of this book: the evidence supports evolved, variable, conditional caregiving capacities, not a uniform maternal instinct; and treating the myth of that instinct as destiny is a bias that distorts self-knowledge and narrows freedom without a scientific warrant. It helps to separate claims that are often bundled. (a) Humans have evolved to be capable of intense parental care, which is well supported. (b) Pregnancy and lactation involve hormonal and neural changes that facilitate caregiving, also well supported. (c) There is a specific, innate desire to have children, present in essentially all women, which is poorly supported and probably false as stated. (d) That desire is so central that its absence indicates pathology, which is unsupported and, this book argues, harmful. The bias runs the four together and lets the strength of (a) and (b) launder the weakness of (c) and (d). Notice how the laundering works: because nobody serious denies (a) and (b), the person defending the instinct claim can always retreat to them when challenged, then advance again to (c) and (d) once the challenge has passed. The strong claim borrows the credibility of the weak-but-true claim and repays nothing. One distinction governs the rest: the capacity to care for a child who exists is not the same as the drive to bring a child into existence. Much of the persuasive force of the maternal-instinct claim borrows from the former, the ferocity of a mother protecting her infant, and quietly transfers it to the latter, the antecedent wish to reproduce. These are different phenomena with different evidence bases. A mother's fierce protectiveness toward a child already in her arms tells us almost nothing about whether a childless woman harbors a hidden imperative to conceive. The first is a response to a present being with a face and a cry; the second would have to be a longing for a being who does not yet exist. Conflating them is the bias's central sleight of hand, and once you see it, you see it everywhere: the image summoned to prove the instinct is always the mother and her infant, never the abstract, unfelt "drive to reproduce" that the argument actually needs. Two boundary markers. Against Book 015: our objection goes past the point that "natural" does not entail "good." Even granting that a maternal drive would be good if it existed, the prior question is whether it exists in the claimed form. This book fights partly on the empirical ground, which is what distinguishes it from a general complaint about the naturalistic fallacy. Against Book 091: compulsory motherhood can be imposed by pure social mandate with no biology invoked at all, a religious duty, a patriotic duty, a family expectation. The instinct bias is specifically the naturalized version, the mandate wearing a lab coat, claiming that the ought is really an is. It is the most modern and the most slippery form of the pressure, because it does not command; it diagnoses. It is worth pausing on how the destiny move launders values into facts. When someone says a childless woman is "unfulfilled," the word looks descriptive but is doing evaluative work: it announces a standard of fulfillment and then measures her against it as though the standard were a thermometer reading. The instinct claim performs the same trick at the level of biology. It takes a normative picture of the good female life and reissues it as a report on female physiology, so that a preference the speaker happens to hold is presented as a fact the woman happens to be violating. Once this is seen, a good deal of the claim's authority evaporates, because we are ordinarily entitled to disagree with other people's values, and the whole point of the naturalizing move was to make our disagreement look like ignorance of our own bodies. Why does the naming matter? Because a bias with a scientific costume is unusually hard to question. To decline motherhood in the face of a social rule is to disobey; to decline it in the face of an "instinct" is to be told you are lying about your own insides. The first is a disagreement. The second is a diagnosis. A woman told she is breaking a rule can at least reply that the rule is wrong; a woman told she is defying her own nature is placed in the impossible position of having to prove a negative about her own psychology to people who have already decided they know it better than she does. Naming the bias restores the possibility of disagreement. It relocates the claim from the realm of settled fact, where it silences, to the realm of contested theory, where it can be examined. And once a claim can be examined, it can also be refused, which is exactly the freedom the naturalized version was designed to foreclose. --- --- Check Yourself — Chapter 1 Quiz. 1. The strongest case for it (steelman) Now the other side, stated as strongly as I can make it, because if the critique is to be worth anything, it must survive the best version of what it opposes. A steelman that quietly weakens its subject is a cheat, and the reader is entitled to the real thing. Begin with the phylogeny. Mammalian reproduction is not casual. It requires gestation, birth, and, in our lineage, lactation and years of intensive care of a helpless infant. The human newborn is among the most dependent of any animal, unable to cling, feed itself, thermoregulate, or flee, and it remains dependent for a span of years unmatched in the animal world. Natural selection is unforgiving of parents who neglect offspring in whom they have invested so heavily; a lineage of mothers indifferent to their young would simply not persist. It would be astonishing if evolution had not equipped mammalian mothers with powerful motivational and physiological systems oriented toward offspring care. And it did. The neuroendocrinology of mammalian mothering is real and well studied: across species, the hormonal cascade of pregnancy and parturition, and the neural circuitry involving regions such as the medial preoptic area, reliably shift a female's motivation toward nurturing, protecting, and attending to young. In rodents, lesions to specific circuits abolish maternal behavior; hormonal priming can induce it even in virgin females. Oxytocin, released in labor and in nursing, is genuinely implicated in bonding and in the physiology of birth and lactation. This is not folklore. It is one of the better-mapped motivational systems in behavioral neuroscience. Now the human phenomenology, which the steelman must take seriously rather than explain away. Enormous numbers of women report a desire for children that does not feel like a considered choice. It feels given, a pull toward a particular future, sometimes intensifying with age, sometimes arriving unbidden at the sight of an infant. Women describe a physical ache, a tightening at the sight of a stranger's baby, a change that they did not decide upon and could not talk themselves out of. Many describe the arrival of their child as the awakening of a love more total than any they had known, a love that reorganized their priorities without their permission, that made the self they had been before seem smaller and stranger. To such women, "maternal instinct" is not an ideological imposition; it is the most honest available name for something they actually underwent. A theory that cannot honor that testimony is not more scientific. It is deaf. And any critique that begins by informing millions of women that their most powerful experience was a cultural script has disqualified itself before it starts. Add the cross-cultural reach. Care of infants by mothers is not a quirk of one society. In the overwhelming majority of human cultures, biological mothers are heavily involved in the care of their young children, and the mother–infant bond is treated everywhere as significant. From foraging bands to industrial states, from cultures that could not have influenced one another, the mother tending her infant recurs. A pattern that recurs across otherwise very different societies has some claim to reflect a shared human endowment rather than mere local custom. When something appears in nearly every human society ever documented, the burden shifts: the person who says it is "merely cultural" now has to explain why the same culture keeps being independently invented everywhere. And there is a moral point the steelman can press. To deny any biological grounding to maternal love can feel like a devaluation, as if the experience of mothering were "just" socialization, a story women could be talked out of. Many mothers rightly resist that. Their love is not an error to be corrected by sociology, nor a false consciousness to be dispelled by a sufficiently clever academic. If naming a bias means telling women that what they felt at their child's birth was ideology all the way down, the naming has overreached, and has reproduced in a new key the very disrespect for women's self-report that it claims to oppose. Put at its strongest, then, the position is this: reproduction is central to mammalian and human life; evolution has built real physiological and motivational systems that orient mothers toward offspring; countless women report an unchosen desire for and love of children; and the pattern is cross-culturally robust. Given all that, calling maternal love an "instinct" is not superstition. It is a reasonable shorthand for a genuine, evolved, deeply felt aspect of human nature. The burden is on the critic to show what, exactly, is wrong with it. The chapters that follow accept that burden. But the reader should carry the force of this chapter forward: the critique that follows is not a denial of evolved caregiving or of real maternal love. It is an argument about how much weight one specific word can bear, and about the difference between a shorthand and a destiny. --- --- Check Yourself — Chapter 2 Quiz. 1. Where it comes from If the strong maternal-instinct claim were simply read off the evidence, its history would be uneventful. It is not. The idea has a biography, and the biography reveals how much of the "instinct" is a cultural artifact draped over a biological core. A claim with a clean scientific pedigree looks the same in every era; this one does not, and its changes track social needs more faithfully than they track any discovery. Start with the word instinct itself. In its strong lay sense, a fixed, complex, unlearned behavior pattern released automatically, "instinct" was always a problematic concept even in animal behavior, and twentieth-century ethology steadily complicated it. Early ethologists spoke confidently of fixed action patterns; later work showed that behaviors once called instincts turned out to depend on developmental conditions, learning, hormones, and environment in ways that dissolved the neat picture. The developmental critique associated with Daniel Lehrman and others argued that even apparently innate behaviors emerge from an intricate interaction between organism and environment over the course of development, not from a pre-loaded program that merely awaits a trigger. The tidy image of a pre-programmed drive that simply switches on has not survived contact with how development actually works, even in birds and rodents. Yet the lay word kept its old confidence, and it is the lay word, not the chastened scientific one, that gets applied to women. The public still hears "instinct" and imagines a switch, long after the specialists stopped believing in switches. There is a further wrinkle in how the word migrated from the study of animals to the description of women. When ethologists spoke of instinct in geese or rats, they were describing specific, observable, stereotyped behaviors, a certain retrieval of displaced eggs, a certain crouch for nursing, that could be elicited, measured, and lesioned. The popular application to women drops all of this specificity and keeps only the aura of scientific authority. "Maternal instinct" in ordinary speech does not name a measured behavior; it names a vague, all-purpose disposition, stretched to cover everything from the wish to conceive to the tidiness of a nursery to a preference for staying home. A term earns its rigor from its precision, and this one has been inflated until it explains everything and therefore predicts nothing. That is a warning sign about a concept, not a measure of its power. Then there is the historical record of maternal behavior, which is where Elisabeth Badinter's work is pivotal. In Mother Love: Myth and Reality (French original L'Amour en plus, 1980), Badinter examined the treatment of infants in France, especially the widespread practice among various classes of sending newborns away to distant wet nurses, sometimes for years, with high infant mortality and comparatively little of the intensive maternal attachment later treated as natural and universal. She read parish records, correspondence, and medical writing, and found a culture in which maternal detachment was common and unremarked. Her argument, controversial but influential, is that the modern ideal of all-consuming maternal devotion is not a timeless constant but a historically situated construction that intensified from around the eighteenth century onward, promoted by physicians, moralists, and the state, who had reasons of their own for wanting women bound to the nursery. If maternal love were a fixed instinct expressing identically across eras, this historical variability would be hard to explain. Instincts, in the strong sense, are not supposed to go in and out of fashion. The wet-nursing history matters on its own, apart from Badinter's larger interpretation. For long stretches of European history, paid wet-nursing was routine, institutionalized, and socially accepted, not a scandal but a system, with contracts, brokers, and established rates. In some urban centers the majority of infants were sent out to nurse. A culture in which large numbers of mothers handed newborns to others to nurse, often knowing the mortality risks, is difficult to square with a universal, overriding instinct to nurture one's own infant at the breast. One need not conclude that these mothers did not love their children; one need only conclude that whatever they felt was not an irresistible biological compulsion of the kind the strong instinct claim posits. Layer on the ideological work "instinct" was recruited to do. As industrialization separated home from workplace and the ideology of separate spheres hardened in the nineteenth century, the claim that women were by nature suited to domestic nurture became useful. It naturalized a division of labor that happened to serve particular economic and social interests. To say the mother belonged in the home with the children because it was her instinct was to make a social arrangement look like a fact of biology, and to make departures from it, the working mother, the reluctant mother, the childless woman, look unnatural rather than merely inconvenient. Religious traditions supplied parallel imagery of motherhood as woman's sacred vocation, the crown of her existence. The evolutionary language that arrived later did not so much create the instinct claim as re-clothe an older conviction in the prestige of science; the conclusion was already fixed, and Darwin was recruited to certify it. It is instructive that the maternal ideal has kept changing shape even in the short span for which we have detailed records, always in step with what the surrounding economy needed of women rather than with any discovery about female biology. In wartime, when factories needed labor, mothers were assured that their children would thrive in collective care and that patriotic work was a fine expression of maternal feeling. When the soldiers returned and the jobs were wanted back, the same authorities rediscovered that a child's development depended on the constant presence of its mother at home, and that any other arrangement risked lasting harm. The biology of infants did not change between these announcements. What changed was the labor market, and the instinct narrative obediently changed with it. A drive that reliably counsels whatever the economy of the decade requires is not behaving like a fixed feature of the species; it is behaving like an ideology in search of a justification. None of this shows there is no biological basis to maternal caregiving, and it is important not to overreach in the other direction. It shows that the strong instinct claim, universal, fixed, and destiny-conferring, grew in soil richly fertilized by economic convenience, moral instruction, and a shaky pre-scientific concept of "instinct." An idea with that pedigree deserves scrutiny before it is used to tell any particular woman who she is. History does not refute the claim, but it removes the claim's air of self-evidence and shows us the hands that shaped it. --- --- Check Yourself — Chapter 3 Quiz. 1. How it operates today The bias rarely announces itself as a theory. It operates through defaults, scripts, and everyday language, most of which never mention biology explicitly while relying on it entirely. Almost no one who says "you'll change your mind" would describe themselves as advancing a contestable thesis in evolutionary psychology. They think they are stating the obvious. That is precisely how a bias does its most durable work, not as a proposition anyone defends but as a background so taken for granted that its opposite sounds like the claim requiring proof. The master script is "you'll change your mind." Said to a young woman who states she does not want children, it presupposes exactly the contested claim: that beneath her stated preference lies a truer, instinctual desire that time or the right circumstances will surface. The script cannot be refuted from inside itself. If she has children, the instinct was confirmed. If she does not, she is either still waiting for it or in denial of it. If she reaches fifty childless and content, the reply is that she has repressed the ache, or will feel it as regret at the end. A belief immune to disconfirmation is not operating as science; it is operating as dogma with a scientific accent. Notice, too, that the same people rarely tell a young man certain he wants children that he will change his mind; his self-knowledge is credited, hers is provisional. A second mechanism is the biological clock as felt imperative rather than mere fertility fact. That fertility declines with age is true and worth knowing, and no honest account should pretend otherwise. But the "clock" in cultural usage is more than a fertility timeline; it is imagined as an internal alarm, a mounting instinctual urgency that will, or should, seize a woman in her thirties, ticking louder as the years pass. The physiological fact of declining fertility and the mythologized inner drive get fused, so that a decline in one's capacity to conceive is heard as evidence of a desire to conceive. A woman may then interpret ordinary anxiety about a closing option, an anxiety anyone might feel about any narrowing door, as proof that the instinct is at last awakening on schedule. A third is the pathologizing of absence. A woman who feels no pull toward motherhood is invited to treat this as a symptom: of trauma, of a cold or damaged upbringing, of immaturity, of selfishness, of "not having met the right person," of something not yet healed. The absence is never allowed to be simply a fact about her, a legitimate variant of human wanting, in the way that not wanting to climb mountains or not liking opera is allowed to be a simple fact. Under the instinct model it must be a blockage, because the drive is assumed present in everyone; its non-appearance therefore requires explanation, and the explanation always locates the fault in her. This is the destiny move in clinical dress, and it can be internalized: a woman who has heard it enough may go looking within herself for the wound that must be there. Language does much of the work. Note the asymmetry between "maternal instinct," treated as deep biology, and "paternal instinct," invoked far more weakly or not at all. Fathers who dote are praised as exceptional and admirable; fathers who are distant are, at worst, disappointing, not unnatural. If the drive to nurture offspring were straightforwardly instinctual in the strong sense, one might expect the grammar to distribute it more evenly across the two parents; instead it clusters on women, which strongly suggests the concept is tracking a gendered social expectation as much as a biological given. Popular science supplies a steady stream of reinforcement, and here oxytocin is the star. In responsible research oxytocin is an important but complicated molecule, context-dependent in its effects, involved in stress and defensiveness as well as bonding, and by no means a simple "love hormone" that installs maternal devotion. In popular retelling it becomes exactly that: a bonding switch, a chemical proof of instinct, "the cuddle chemical," "the mommy molecule." The oversimplification does real persuasive work, letting a headline convert a nuanced and often contradictory neuroendocrine literature into confirmation of the destiny story. A reader who absorbs a decade of such headlines comes to feel that science has proven the instinct, when what science has actually produced is a far more tangled and tentative picture. Fiction and advertising keep the machinery oiled. The film that resolves a career woman's arc by having her discover, at last, that what she truly wanted was a baby; the commercial in which a mother's love is figured as instinctive and boundless the moment the newborn is placed on her chest; the ubiquitous plot in which a reluctant woman is "completed" by unexpected motherhood, are not neutral entertainments. They are a steady rehearsal of the destiny story, teaching audiences what the natural female trajectory looks like and where its happy ending lies. Because these are stories rather than arguments, they slip past the defenses one would raise against an explicit claim, and they leave behind a sense that the instinct is simply how things are, confirmed by a thousand narratives that all end the same way. Finally, defaults in institutions. Workplaces, medical settings, tax codes, and social rituals often assume that a woman's reproductive future is a when, not a whether. The colleague asks when she is planning to start a family, not whether. The form assumes future dependents. The assumption is usually unspoken, which is how a bias operates most efficiently, as the invisible background against which a childfree life registers as the odd thing, the choice that owes everyone an explanation, while the default choice owes no one anything at all. --- --- Check Yourself — Chapter 4 Quiz. 1. Honesty requires separating the well-supported claims from the overreaching ones, and admitting where the science is unsettled. The temptation, in a book like this, is to marshal only the evidence that cuts against the natalist claim. That temptation is refused here, because the argument does not need it and because a one-sided reading of the science would reproduce the very vice the book is criticizing. Well supported: mammalian and human parents have evolved neurobiological systems that support caregiving. The literature on the neuroendocrinology of mothering, hormonal shifts across pregnancy and the postpartum period, the involvement of specific brain circuits in parental motivation, the roles of oxytocin and related systems in birth, lactation, and bonding, is substantial and not in serious doubt at the level of "these systems exist and matter." Pregnancy measurably alters the maternal brain; the postpartum period involves real changes in attention and threat sensitivity; nursing engages genuine neuroendocrine machinery. This is the kernel of truth the bias exploits, and it is a real kernel. The critique gains nothing by pretending otherwise, and would lose its credibility if it tried. Also well supported: these caregiving systems are facultative and conditional, not fixed and uniform. This is the heart of Sarah Blaffer Hrdy's argument in Mother Nature (1999). Hrdy, an evolutionary anthropologist and no enemy of Darwinian thinking, indeed one of its most rigorous practitioners, marshals evidence across species and human history that maternal responses are strategic and contingent, sensitive to resources, social support, the mother's condition, and the infant's prospects. She takes seriously the widespread historical and cross-cultural evidence of maternal abandonment, differential investment between offspring, and even infanticide under duress, phenomena a sentimental account must ignore but an evolutionary one must explain. Her point is not that mothers do not love; it is that evolution did not build an indiscriminate, automatic devotion that fires identically in every female regardless of circumstance. It built a conditional decision-making capacity, commitment that can be ferocious but is calibrated, weighing what a mother can sustain against what an infant needs and what the world allows. That is the opposite of instinct-as-destiny, and, crucially, it comes from within evolutionary biology, not against it. The strongest case against the fixed-instinct picture is made by a committed Darwinian, which is why it cannot be waved away as anti-science ideology. Consider what a genuine universal drive looks like in the human record, so that we can see how the desire to reproduce fails to match the profile. The fear of falling, the startle reflex, the acquisition of language given ordinary exposure, the drive to eat when starving, these appear across every culture, resist suppression, and produce distress when thwarted, and their absence is a rare and genuinely diagnosable condition. The desire to bear children matches none of these markers. It varies enormously in intensity between individuals raised in the same household; it can be entirely absent in psychologically healthy, well-integrated adults; it can be present, then fade, then return; and its distribution shifts with education, economic security, and opportunity in ways no reflex does. A trait with that profile is not a fixed universal drive. It is a common but contingent preference, shaped by many inputs, of which evolved caregiving predispositions are one contributor among several rather than the sole author. The historical evidence points the same way. Wet-nursing as a routine institution, the wide variation in maternal practices across eras and classes, the documented intensification of the maternal ideal from the eighteenth century onward (Badinter), all describe a phenomenon with far more variability than a fixed universal drive would produce. Instincts in the strong sense do not have local histories; this "instinct" plainly does. On the specific desire to have children, as distinct from the capacity to care for one already born, the evidence for a universal innate drive is weakest of all. Childlessness by choice is a real, persistent phenomenon across societies and across history; substantial numbers of people report never having wanted children and not regretting the choice years later. That such people exist in every generation, and largely flourish, is difficult to reconcile with a truly universal, biologically mandated drive whose frustration should predictably produce misery. The variation in reported desire, by person, by culture, by era, by circumstance, looks like exactly what you would expect from a trait shaped by many interacting factors, and nothing like what you would expect from a single innate imperative present in all. A genuine universal, like the capacity for language or the fear of falling, does not have this kind of scatter. Where the evidence is genuinely uncertain, the book will not pretend otherwise, because false confidence is the disease it is trying to treat. The exact weighting of biological versus cultural contributions to any individual woman's desire for children is not something current science can cleanly resolve; the two are entangled in every real person and cannot be experimentally separated. Oxytocin's role is real but complex and easy to overstate in either direction. "Regret" studies, on childlessness and on parenthood alike, are methodologically hard, vulnerable to who agrees to answer and how the question is framed, and their findings are contested. The honest summary is therefore not "biology plays no role," a claim this book never makes. It is more disciplined: the evidence supports evolved, variable, conditional caregiving capacities, and does not support a fixed, universal maternal drive to reproduce whose absence marks a defect. The strong instinct claim outruns its warrant. That is a modest conclusion, and it is enough. --- --- Check Yourself — Chapter 5 Quiz. 1. The hidden costs Treating the maternal-instinct claim as established fact has costs that the confident framing hides, because the framing presents itself as a gift and a truth rather than as a claim with consequences. It is worth making those consequences explicit, since they fall on real people and are rarely counted. The first cost is to self-knowledge. If a woman is told that a maternal drive is universal and hers is merely dormant, she is taught to distrust her own reported preferences. Her present, examined sense that she does not want children is reframed as unreliable testimony about a self she cannot yet see, a self that the culture claims to know better than she does. This is an epistemic harm in the strict sense: it degrades a person's standing as a knower of her own mind, treats her introspection as evidence to be discounted rather than credited. And the harm is practical as well as theoretical, because it can distort decisions. A life-shaping, largely irreversible choice may be made not from one's actual values but from deference to a predicted instinct that may never arrive, or a woman may spend years waiting for a feeling she is told is coming, deferring other lives she might have built. The instinct myth does not only misdescribe; it can misdirect. A second cost falls on mothers, paradoxically, and this is the cost most often overlooked by those who think the myth simply flatters women. If maternal love is instinct, then it should be automatic, total, and immediate, arriving the moment the child does. Any mother whose experience departs from that ideal is left to conclude that something is wrong with her. The new mother who does not feel the promised flood of love in the delivery room, who finds early motherhood ambivalent or grueling or frankly regretted in its harder hours, who struggles with postpartum depression, meets a cultural script that says such feelings are unnatural as well as hard, a sign of deficiency. Shame compounds the difficulty and discourages disclosure at exactly the moment support is most needed. The instinct myth thus isolates the mothers most in need of recognition, by making their ordinary human variability feel like personal failure. A myth that claims to honor mothers ends up policing them against a standard no real human reliably meets, and punishing them for the gap. A third cost is to honest deliberation about reproduction. When a drive is assumed, the question "do I actually want this, and why?" is pre-empted before it can be asked. The instinct framing supplies the answer in advance: of course you want this, everyone does, it is your nature. Individuals and couples are nudged toward a momentous, irreversible undertaking on the strength of a supposed inner mandate rather than a clear-eyed appraisal of their own lives, means, relationships, and desires. Good decisions can and do still result, but when they do, they result despite the framing rather than because of it, and the framing has done nothing to help. A fourth cost is scientific and cultural credibility. Every time a nuanced literature, on oxytocin, on the neuroendocrinology of mothering, on evolutionary parenting, is flattened into "science proves the maternal instinct," the public understanding of that science is degraded, and the flattened version hardens into a rhetorical weapon that is then wielded against individuals. The cost is a general coarsening of how we reason about human nature, a habit of treating tentative findings as settled destiny that spreads well beyond this one topic. There is a fifth cost, subtler than the rest, borne by relationships. The instinct myth quietly instructs partners and families in how to treat a woman's stated wishes: as provisional, as symptoms, as things to be managed until the drive arrives to correct them. A partner who half-believes the myth may discount her clear "no" as a not-yet, and press, and wait, confident that nature is on his side. Parents may treat a daughter's childfree life as an open wound to be periodically reopened at family gatherings. The relationship is thereby seeded with a permanent low-grade disrespect, a standing refusal to take her at her word on the most intimate question of her life. This is corrosive precisely because it is well-meant; the people applying the pressure believe they are being kind, helping her toward a happiness she does not yet know she wants, which makes the disrespect almost impossible to name without seeming ungrateful for the concern. None of these costs is visible in the default framing, precisely because the default framing presents the instinct as a benefaction. Naming the costs does not deny the gift to those for whom it genuinely is one. It insists only that a claim used to steer lives be priced honestly, including the price paid by the lives it steers wrongly. --- --- Check Yourself — Chapter 6 Quiz. 1. Who bears the pressure The maternal-instinct bias is not distributed evenly across humanity. By its very grammar it falls on women, and specifically on women of reproductive age. The word is gendered at the root: "maternal." Whatever caregiving capacities exist are, on the evidence, broadly human and expressed by many kinds of caregivers, fathers, grandparents, adoptive parents, aunts, allomothers of every description, yet the instinct is assigned to women in particular and made constitutive of womanhood itself. The pressure is therefore inseparable from the ancient question of what a woman is for, a question rarely posed in the same tone, or with the same air of settled biology, about men. Within that broad category, the childfree woman bears a concentrated form of the pressure. Because the instinct is assumed universal, her stated preference is the anomaly that requires explanation, and she is subjected in turn to prediction ("you'll change your mind"), to diagnosis ("what happened to you? were you not loved?"), and to delay ("just wait, it'll come"). She must justify an absence that the model refuses to accept as a legitimate variant of human wanting, and she must do it repeatedly, to relatives, to doctors, to strangers, across decades, each conversation re-opening a question she considers closed. Class and circumstance modulate the pressure in ways worth naming. Where economic pressure already constrains women's options, an instinct narrative can add a further layer, framing reproduction as natural destiny rather than as a choice to be weighed soberly against real constraints of money, housing, and support. Conversely, the intensive-mothering ideal that the instinct myth underwrites is itself sharply class-inflected, demanding resources of time, attention, and money that not all mothers possess. Women are thus pressed toward motherhood as instinctual and then judged against a standard of mothering that quietly presupposes privilege, a double bind in which both the entry and the performance are rigged. Culture and religion sharpen the pressure further in communities where motherhood is treated as woman's defining sacred role and her highest calling. There the instinct claim and the moral mandate reinforce each other, biology certifying theology and theology dignifying biology, and dissent is costlier, sometimes carrying real social and familial penalties rather than mere awkwardness. Then there are the women the myth quietly wounds while claiming to celebrate them. Those who want children and cannot have them, through infertility or circumstance, find that "instinct" adds to their grief the further injury of implied incompleteness, as though they have failed at the one thing nature assigned them. And mothers whose lived experience of motherhood is more ambivalent, more exhausting, or more difficult than the automatic-devotion script allows are shamed by a standard presented as nature itself, made to feel monstrous for feelings that are, in fact, entirely ordinary. A fair accounting also notes who is largely exempt, because the exemption is itself revealing. Men face nothing comparable in force. "Paternal instinct" is invoked weakly, sporadically, and almost never deployed to override a man's stated preferences about his own future. No one tells a childless man in his thirties that his clock is ticking, or that he will surely change his mind, or that his indifference to fatherhood signals a wound. This asymmetry is not a minor curiosity; it is itself a piece of evidence, pointing to the conclusion that the concept is tracking a social expectation about women far more closely than it is tracking any fact about the human care of offspring, which men are perfectly capable of providing. The pressure also compounds across a lifetime in a way that a single confrontation obscures. It is not one conversation but a slow accretion: the aunt at the wedding, the doctor at the checkup, the colleague at the promotion, the stranger who spots a bare ring finger, each individually minor and deniable, together forming a continuous atmosphere in which a woman's reproductive status is treated as public business and her own account of her wishes as an interim report. A man is rarely subjected to this weather. The cumulative weight matters because it is what converts an external claim into an internal doubt: hear enough times that your certainty is really denial, and the certainty itself begins to feel suspect, which is precisely the outcome the instinct model predicts and, in predicting, helps to produce. --- --- Check Yourself — Chapter 7 Quiz. 1. The counter-arguments to the critique A fair book must give its opponents their best return volley, not a caricature easily knocked down. Several objections deserve serious engagement, and taking them seriously improves the thesis rather than threatening it. "You are denying real biology to score an ideological point." This is the most important objection, and the book concedes its premise while rejecting its conclusion. Evolved caregiving systems are real; nothing here denies them, and Chapters 2 and 5 affirm them at length. The critique is aimed at the strong claim, universal, fixed, reproduction-desiring, destiny-conferring, and not at the neuroendocrinology of mothering. One can fully affirm that pregnancy changes the brain, that specific circuits govern parental motivation, and that oxytocin matters, and still deny that these facts establish a uniform female drive to reproduce whose absence is pathological. Distinguishing the well-supported kernel from the unsupported inflation is not anti-biology; it is more careful biology than the inflation practices. The person who refuses that distinction, who insists that granting the kernel commits you to the whole edifice, is the one substituting ideology for evidence. "Cross-cultural universality of maternal care proves an instinct." Widespread maternal involvement is real and was granted its full weight in the steelman. But universality of a behavior under typical conditions does not establish a fixed innate drive, and still less a drive to reproduce as opposed to a capacity to care. Facultative capacities can and do produce common outcomes across societies precisely because typical conditions, an infant needing care, a mother positioned to give it, are themselves common across societies. Where the conditions are common, the response will look universal without being fixed. And the very same historical and cross-cultural record that contains the universal care also contains abundant variation, wet-nursing, differential investment, abandonment and worse under duress, which a fixed-instinct model cannot easily accommodate but a conditional-capacity model predicts and explains. The evidence, taken whole rather than selectively, favors the conditional account. "Most women do want children and love them intensely; you are marginalizing the majority." The book affirms the majority's experience as real, common, and valuable, and disputes none of it. But a common preference is simply not the same thing as a universal instinct, and the reality of a widespread desire does not license treating its absence as a defect. A great many people enjoy music; we do not diagnose the few who do not. The critique protects the minority without denying anything about the majority; it objects only to the specific move of using the majority's experience to overwrite and discredit the minority's self-report. That is not marginalizing the majority. It is declining to weaponize it. "Without an instinct, what explains the desire so many feel as unchosen?" A great deal explains it. Evolved caregiving predispositions genuinely feed into the desire and are part of the story. So do deep and early socialization, the human drive to make meaning and leave a trace, love of a particular partner and the wish to make a family with them, and the ordinary human hunger for continuity and for someone to care for. That a desire feels unchosen does not make it a fixed instinct; a vast range of culturally shaped desires, for a particular kind of home, a certain career, a religious life, feel exactly that immediate and unwilled. The book does not claim the desire is fake or shallow. It claims only that the desire's felt givenness does not settle its origin, and certainly does not establish its universality. "This will discourage women from having children." The aim is neither to encourage nor to discourage childbearing, and the book would count itself a failure if it did either. The aim is to remove a distorting pressure so that the choice, whichever way it falls, is freer and better informed. A woman who wants children loses nothing whatever by learning that her desire is hers rather than a mandate she is obeying; if anything she gains, because a chosen good is a firmer possession than a compelled one. A woman who does not want children gains the standing to be believed. No one is talked out of anything. A false certainty is simply exchanged for an honest freedom. "Regret is real; women who forgo children often regret it later, which proves the drive was there all along." Some people do come to regret childlessness, just as some parents come to regret parenthood, and neither regret should be dismissed. But regret is a poor instrument for detecting a hidden instinct. It is shaped by the very culture under examination, which teaches women to expect regret and supplies a ready script for interpreting ordinary late-life melancholy as the ache of a thwarted drive. It is also asymmetrically visible: the childfree woman's regret is narrated everywhere, while the parent's regret is taboo and therefore underreported, skewing the apparent balance. And regret about a road not taken is a universal feature of finite lives; a person can regret not having pursued a different career without this proving a career instinct. That some people regret childlessness tells us the choice was consequential and hard to reverse, which no one disputes. It does not establish that a fixed drive was present and denied. Engaged honestly, these objections refine the thesis rather than defeat it. They push the book, usefully, toward its actual and narrower claim, which is not "no biology" but "no destiny.

① In brief

Book 017 · 100 Biases of Natalism

This volume examines the maternal-instinct bias: the tendency to treat a contested, largely constructed idea, that women possess a fixed, universal biological drive to bear and rear children, as an established fact, and then to use that supposed fact as proof that women are built to reproduce and therefore will, and should, want to. The bias converts a variable set of capacities into a destiny, so that a woman who does not want children appears not to hold a legitimate preference but to be malfunctioning, out of contact with her own nature.

The book is careful about what it is and is not claiming. It fully affirms that human beings, women included, have evolved genuine capacities for caregiving; that pregnancy and lactation involve real neuroendocrine changes; and that many women experience a desire for and love of children that feels bodily and unchosen. The critique is aimed only at the inflation of these real, conditional capacities into a uniform female drive whose absence marks a defect. The distinction between the capacity to care for a child who exists and a supposed drive to bring one into existence is the book's analytic hinge; conflating the two is the bias's central move.

Read the rest of the overview

Chapter 2 gives the natalist side its strongest hearing before any critique: mammalian reproduction is high-investment, evolution plausibly built real motivational and physiological systems for offspring care, oxytocin and the neuroendocrinology of mothering are genuine, countless women report an unchosen longing, and maternal involvement is cross-culturally widespread. The book takes this seriously and then argues that it establishes evolved, variable caregiving, not a fixed universal destiny.

The evidence chapter draws on two anchors. Elisabeth Badinter's Mother Love / L'Amour en plus (1980) argues historically that intensive maternal devotion is a constructed ideal, not a timeless constant, supported by the routine historical practice of wet-nursing and by wide variation in maternal practice across eras. Sarah Blaffer Hrdy's Mother Nature (1999) argues from within evolutionary biology that maternal responses are facultative and conditional, calibrated to circumstance rather than switched on uniformly, a picture that is the opposite of instinct-as-destiny. Popular science's reduction of oxytocin to a "love switch" is examined as a mechanism that manufactures false confirmation.

The book traces how the bias operates today through unfalsifiable scripts ("you'll change your mind"), the mythologized biological clock, the pathologizing of a woman's stated lack of desire, and institutional defaults that treat reproduction as a when, not a whether. It counts the hidden costs: damage to a woman's standing as a knower of her own mind; shame imposed on mothers whose experience departs from the automatic-devotion ideal; the pre-empting of honest deliberation; and the coarsening of public understanding of science. It analyzes who bears the pressure: women of reproductive age above all, childfree women most acutely, with class, culture, and religion modulating the force, and men largely exempt.

Objections are engaged fairly: that the critique denies real biology (it does not; it targets only the strong claim), that cross-cultural universality proves an instinct, that the critique marginalizes the majority who do want children. The book's reply refines rather than abandons its thesis: not "no biology," but "no destiny."

The measured conclusion: maternal love is real, instinct-as-destiny is a myth, and these two facts have never been in tension. Naming the bias does not disparage mothers or devalue the desire for children. It returns that desire to those who have it as theirs, and returns to those who lack it the ordinary dignity of being believed.

Adjacent to compulsory motherhood (091) and the naturalistic fallacy (015), this book is narrower than both: its subject is the specific instinct claim, its scientific status and its use as destiny.

CH 3Where it comes from

If the strong maternal-instinct claim were simply read off the evidence, its history would be uneventful. It is not. The idea has a biography, and the biography reveals how much of the "instinct" is a cultural artifact draped over a biological core. A claim with a clean scientific pedigree looks the same in every era; this one does not, and its changes track social needs more faithfully than they track any discovery.

CH 4How it operates today

The bias rarely announces itself as a theory. It operates through defaults, scripts, and everyday language, most of which never mention biology explicitly while relying on it entirely. Almost no one who says "you'll change your mind" would describe themselves as advancing a contestable thesis in evolutionary psychology. They think they are stating the obvious. That is precisely how a bias does its most durable work, not as a proposition anyone defends but as a background so taken for granted that its opposite sounds like the claim requiring proof.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

What does the evidence actually show? Honesty requires separating the well-supported claims from the overreaching ones, and admitting where the science is unsettled. The temptation, in a book like this, is to marshal only the evidence that cuts against the natalist claim. That temptation is refused here, because the argument does not need it and because a one-sided reading of the science would reproduce the very vice the book is criticizing.

Treating the maternal-instinct claim as established fact has costs that the confident framing hides, because the framing presents itself as a gift and a truth rather than as a claim with consequences. CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

What does clear-eyed reasoning look like once the bias is named and set aside? Emphatically not the opposite error. The corrective for "every woman has a fixed instinct to reproduce" is not the equal and opposite overreach, "no woman has any biological disposition toward caregiving at all." That would simply replace one false certainty with another, and would insult the many women whose experience of longing and love is real. Seeing past the bias means arriving at a more accurate and, not by accident, a more permissive picture, one that has room for everyone the myth left out.

CH 12Conclusion

This book has argued a narrow and, I hope, defensible claim, and it is worth restating exactly, because the temptation to hear it as something larger and cruder is strong. Human beings, women included, have evolved genuine capacities for caregiving; pregnancy and lactation involve real neuroendocrine changes that reshape motivation and attention; and many women experience a desire for and a love of children that feels bodily, powerful, and entirely unchosen. All of that is affirmed here without reservation, and none of it is the target. What is not established, and what the evidence read honestly does not support, is the strong claim smuggled inside the single word instinct: a fixed, universal female drive to reproduce, present in essentially all women, whose absence marks a defect to be diagnosed. The caregiving capacities we can actually document are facultative, conditional, and variable, calibrated to circumstance rather than fired uniformly in every female. Between the true sentence "mothers can love fiercely" and the false one "every woman is built to want children" lies a gap, and it is a gap the bias papers over and this book has tried to reopen and hold open.

The historically constructed standard of all-consuming maternal devotion that the instinct myth underwrites and against which real mothers are judged.
Wet-nursing

Pathologizing of absence

Paternal-instinct asymmetry
