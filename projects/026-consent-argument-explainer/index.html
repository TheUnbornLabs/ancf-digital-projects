PROJECT 026

Consent Argument Explainer

Philosophy · Difficulty: Intermediate

Can consent matter when there's no one yet to give it? This explainer focuses on the consent line of reasoning, tests it against everyday analogies you can explore, and gives the leading replies — a companion to the broader consent page earlier in the collection.

1. The claim

No one consents to being born, yet birth imposes lifelong, unchosen stakes — every joy and every harm a life will contain. The consent argument says this asymmetry of imposition matters morally: we usually owe people a say before we make decisions that profoundly affect them, and birth is the one decision made entirely without that say.

2. Why consent is unusual here

Normally, consent presupposes someone who already exists to be asked. Before birth there is no such person. So the argument cannot mean "we failed to get permission" in the ordinary sense. Defenders instead appeal to ideas like hypothetical consent (would a reasonable person endorse it?) or proxy consent (deciding responsibly on another's behalf). Whether those substitutes are strong enough is the live question.

3. Test it against an analogy

We reason about consent using analogies. Pick one to see which way it tends to push. Saved on this device.

The unconscious patient — we treat someone who can't consent now
The surprise gift — giving something good without asking first
Signing a lifelong contract in someone else's name
Decisions for future generations (e.g. the climate)
Choose an analogy above.

4. The leading replies

Consent presupposes a subject

You cannot wrong, or fail to ask, someone who does not yet exist. On this view the framework simply doesn't apply before birth — and the argument never gets started.

Proxy consent is normal and fine

Parents, doctors, and societies routinely make weighty, un-consented decisions for people (including future ones). If those can be legitimate, perhaps creating a life can be too.

Hypothetical consent may suffice

If a reasonable person, looking back, would endorse having been born — and most do — that endorsement might supply the morally relevant kind of consent.

Consent may be the wrong lens entirely

Maybe the real moral work is done by care and responsibility toward the future person, not by a permission that can never literally be given.

5. A middle view

Many keep "consent" as a useful metaphor — a reminder to take the future person's interests seriously — without treating it as literal permission. On this reading, the argument is less a proof than a prompt: whatever you decide, decide as if a real person's whole life is at stake. Because it is.
