PROJECT 056

Stoicism and Antinatalism Notes

Philosophy · Difficulty: Advanced · ~11 min

Study notes putting Stoic ideas in conversation with questions about birth. Importantly, the Stoics were not antinatalists — most valued family, friendship, and civic life. These notes explore genuine points of contact and real tensions, fairly.

A simplified study aid. It does not claim Stoicism endorses antinatalism — it generally did not. Consult primary sources (Epictetus, Seneca, Marcus Aurelius) for depth.

What you will learn

  • Four core Stoic ideas, briefly.
  • Where Stoic equanimity touches questions of suffering and birth.
  • Where Stoicism actually pulls against antinatalism.

1. Core Stoic ideas

The dichotomy of control

Some things are up to us (our judgements, choices) and some are not (outcomes, others, the world). Peace comes from focusing on the former.

Virtue as the only true good

For the Stoics, a good life is one lived with wisdom, justice, courage, and temperance — not one defined by pleasure or even survival.

Amor fati — love of fate

Accepting, even embracing, what nature brings, rather than wishing reality were otherwise.

Memento mori & premeditation

Remembering mortality and rehearsing hardship in advance, to live well and meet difficulty with steadiness.

2. Points of contact

Facing suffering honestlyBoth traditions refuse to look away from life's pains — Stoicism to endure them, antinatalism to weigh whether to risk imposing them. EquanimityA Stoic calm can help one hold these hard questions without panic or despair. Reason over impulseBoth prize careful reasoning over inherited assumption.

3. Real tensions

OikeiôsisStoics taught natural affection widening to family, community, and humanity — often read as favouring participation in life, including parenthood. Acceptance vs prevention"Amor fati" leans toward accepting existence; antinatalism asks whether to create it at all. These can pull in opposite directions. Suffering's meaningStoics saw hardship as material for virtue; antinatalists weigh it as a harm to avoid imposing. A genuine disagreement.

Key takeaway

Putting two traditions in conversation isn't the same as merging them. Stoicism offers steadiness for hard questions, even as its love of fate pulls against the antinatalist's wish to prevent suffering. Holding both honestly is the point.
