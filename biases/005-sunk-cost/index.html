BIAS 005

In for a Penny: Sunk Cost and the Expanding Family

Part I · Cognitive & Psychological Biases · Sunk Cost and the Expanding Family

The bias: How past investment in a family life pressures further reproductive commitment.

The sunk cost effect is a maladaptive economic behavior that is manifested in a greater tendency to continue an endeavor once an investment in money, effort, or time has been made.
① In brief ② The book in twelve chapters ③ Key terms ④ Ten-question check ⑤ Reflection ⑥ Sources ⑦ Related biases

① In brief

This book examines how past investment in a family life pressures people toward further reproductive commitment. It applies one of the best-documented findings in decision science, the sunk cost fallacy, to reproduction, and argues that "we've already put so much into this" is a poor reason to create a new person.

A sunk cost is a cost already incurred that no future action can recover. The core teaching of decision science is that sunk costs should not influence forward-looking choices: only the future consequences of the options in front of you should count. The sunk cost fallacy (Arkes & Blumer, 1985) is the robust human tendency to violate this, to continue or escalate a course because of what has already been spent. Its engine is loss aversion (Kahneman & Tversky): stopping means realizing a loss, which we will gamble to avoid. Its social face is commitment and consistency (Cialdini); its vivid nickname is the Concorde fallacy; its scaled-up form is escalation of commitment (Staw).

Reproductive life is unusually rich in sunk costs. A wedding, a family-sized house, years of fertility treatment, a first child who "needs a sibling": each is a large, lumpy, hard-to-reverse investment that raises the felt cost of stopping and reframes continuation as loyalty to prior sacrifice rather than a fresh choice. The book's thesis: prior investment systematically pressures further reproductive commitment by making the creation of a new person feel like honoring what was spent, and this is a bias, because the past investment gives no genuine reason to create that person.

Read the rest of the overview

The book's central discipline, held from first page to last, is the person/investment distinction. Children who already exist are persons, never investments or sunk costs; the love and obligation a parent owes an existing child are legitimate and involve no fallacy. The bias lives only in reasoning about future reproduction, in the moment when love for a first child, or grief over a costly road already travelled, gets converted into a felt obligation to create another person. The book handles this distinction explicitly and humanely, and treats no existing child as a sunk cost.

Following the series template, the book states the strongest natalist case first (Chapter 2): family life is a coherent long-term project, continuity is a virtue, many "sunk" costs are really prepaid assets a further child would genuinely use (a reused cot, hard-won competence, a usable spare room), and honoring sacrifice is meaningful rather than irrational. The critique concedes all of this and narrows to a defensible core: the weight given to prior investment beyond its genuine forward value is the fallacy. The book's key diagnostic, the gift test, asks whether the pull toward another child would survive if the prior investment had cost nothing; where it collapses, it was sunk cost all along.

On evidence, the book is careful: the sunk cost effect and loss aversion are robustly established in general, but their operation specifically in childbearing is a strong inference rather than a directly measured effect, best evidenced in the fertility-treatment literature (where couples continue past the point the odds justify) and in the path-dependent structure of reproductive trajectories. It notes that the "only children are damaged" folklore, often used as a sunk cost argument for a second child, is contradicted by the research record.

Later chapters trace the bias's origins, its everyday mechanisms, its hidden costs (opportunity, autonomy, within-family and ecological), and its uneven distribution: pressing hardest on women, on those inside fertility treatment, and unevenly across class and culture, while falling lightest on those who voice it. Chapter 8 answers the serious objections honestly. The book prescribes no family size. It offers only a subtraction: remove the backward pull of sunk cost, and decide, as freely as one can, looking forward.

CH 3Where it comes from

The sunk cost fallacy is not a modern quirk of consumer culture. It draws on machinery deep in human psychology, sharpened by evolution, reinforced by economics, and dressed by culture in the specific costume of the family. Understanding its origins is not the same as excusing it, but it explains why the pull is so strong and so hard to shake.

CH 4How it operates today

The bias does not announce itself. It works through ordinary sentences, ordinary purchases, and the ordinary architecture of a family life. This chapter traces the concrete mechanisms, the actual grooves along which "we've already invested so much" runs from thought to decision.

CH 6The hidden costs

If the sunk cost frame merely persuaded people to continue a course that was, on the merits, fine, it would be a harmless quirk. The concern of this chapter is what the frame obscures, the costs it keeps off the ledger by directing attention backward, at what was spent, rather than forward, at what a further child would actually cost and yield, for everyone affected.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

Biases are not distributed evenly. The sunk cost frame in reproduction presses hardest on some people and lets others largely off, and the pattern is not random. This chapter maps who carries the weight.

What does clear-eyed, non-coerced reasoning look like once the sunk cost fallacy is named? The goal is not a new prescription about family size, this series prescribes no family size, but a set of tools for making whatever decision one makes forward, freed of the backward pull. CH 12Conclusion

The argument of this book has been narrow by design, and narrowness has been its discipline. The claim is not that people have too many children, nor too few; the series takes no position on the right number, and neither does this volume. The claim is that one specific and well-documented error of reasoning, the sunk cost fallacy, the tendency to continue or escalate a course of action because of what has already been invested in it, operates in reproductive life, where the conditions that trigger it are unusually abundant, and that when it does, it supplies a bad reason for creating a new person. "We've already invested so much" points backward, at costs that are fixed and gone; a decision to bring a person into the world requires reasons that point forward, at the costs and goods that decision will actually produce, for everyone it affects.

③ Key terms

Show all 16 terms as a list
Sunk cost
A cost already incurred that cannot be recovered by any future action; rationally irrelevant to forward-looking decisions.
Sunk cost fallacy (sunk cost effect)
The tendency to continue or escalate a course of action because of prior investment, beyond what the forward-looking case justifies (Arkes & Blumer, 1985).
Escalation of commitment
The tendency to increase investment in a chosen course of action as prior investment mounts, even when the course is failing (Staw).
Loss aversion
The finding that losses loom larger than equivalent gains (roughly twice as large); the main psychological engine of the sunk cost effect (Kahneman & Tversky).
Prospect theory
Kahneman and Tversky's account of how people evaluate outcomes as gains and losses relative to a reference point rather than in absolute terms.
Concorde fallacy
The popular name for the sunk cost fallacy, after the supersonic airliner two governments continued funding because of prior expenditure.
Commitment and consistency
Cialdini's principle that, having committed to a course (especially publicly), people feel pressure to act consistently with that commitment.
Reference point
The baseline against which outcomes are judged as gains or losses; shifting it changes whether an outcome feels like a loss to be avoided.
Realizing a loss
Converting a still-notional loss into a definite, acknowledged one by stopping; the moment loss aversion drives us to avoid, fuelling escalation.
Lumpy commitment
A large, discrete, hard-to-reverse investment (a wedding, a mortgage, a course of IVF) that raises the felt cost of not proceeding to the next stage.
Prepaid asset
A prior investment that yields genuine forward-looking value if continued (a reused cot, hard-won parenting competence); legitimately part of the forward case, unlike a true sunk cost.
The gift test
A diagnostic counterfactual: would this choice be made if the prior investment had cost nothing? If the pull collapses when the sunk cost is removed, the pull was the fallacy.
Person/investment distinction
The book's central discipline: an existing child is a person, never a sunk cost or investment; the bias lies only in reasoning about future reproduction.
Escalating staircase
The structure of a reproductive trajectory as a series of ever-larger prior commitments, each making the next step feel like continuation rather than fresh choice.
Only-child folklore
The weakly-supported belief that only children are lonely, selfish, or maladjusted; contradicted by the research record (Falbo and colleagues), yet often used as a sunk cost argument for a second child.
Mental accounting
Thaler's account of how people track costs and benefits in separate psychological "accounts"; relevant to how prior family spending is booked and felt as owed.

⑥ Sources

What this volume draws on. Where the evidence is thin or contested, the book says so rather than reaching for a number.

  • Arkes, Hal R., & Blumer, Catherine (1985). "The Psychology of Sunk Cost." Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes, 35(1), 124–140. — The foundational demonstration and naming of the sunk cost effect.
  • Kahneman, Daniel, & Tversky, Amos (1979). "Prospect Theory: An Analysis of Decision under Risk." Econometrica, 47(2), 263–291. — Loss aversion and the reference-point framing that underlie sunk cost.
  • Kahneman, Daniel (2011). Thinking, Fast and Slow. Farrar, Straus and Giroux. — Accessible synthesis of loss aversion, prospect theory, and related biases.
  • Cialdini, Robert B. Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion. (First published 1984; multiple revised editions.) — The principle of commitment and consistency.
  • Thaler, Richard H. (2015). Misbehaving: The Making of Behavioral Economics. W. W. Norton. — Sunk costs, mental accounting, and the behavioral-economics context. (Thaler's earlier academic work on mental accounting and sunk costs is also foundational.)
  • Staw, Barry M. Foundational work on escalation of commitment, beginning with "Knee-Deep in the Big Muddy: A Study of Escalating Commitment to a Chosen Course of Action" (Organizational Behavior and Human Performance, 1976). — The escalation-of-commitment literature.
Directions the volume points to (not pinned citations)
  • Falbo, Toni, and colleagues — the reviews and meta-analyses of only-child outcomes, including Falbo and Polit's meta-analytic work. The finding the book draws on is the robust one: only children are largely indistinguishable from children with siblings, with small differences that sometimes favor only children.
  • Arkes, Hal R., and Peter Ayton. "The Sunk Cost and Concorde Effects: Are Humans Less Rational Than Lower Animals?" Psychological Bulletin, 1999. — The Concorde fallacy and the contested animal evidence.
  • The literature on discontinuation of fertility treatment — which finds that much IVF dropout is driven by psychological and financial burden rather than medical futility, and that stopping is experienced as an exceptionally hard decision. The book relies on the direction of this literature and cites no share or rate.
  • Demographic work on the path-dependence of reproductive trajectories — the correlations among homeownership, marriage, and completed family size. The general patterns are well established in family demography; the book cites no specific table.
  • The reproductive-regret literature, treated more fully in Book 039. Both directions of regret, and the social suppression of parental regret, are supported here.
  • Tversky, Amos, and Daniel Kahneman. "Loss Aversion in Riskless Choice: A Reference-Dependent Model." Quarterly Journal of Economics, 1991. — Further grounding for loss aversion.
