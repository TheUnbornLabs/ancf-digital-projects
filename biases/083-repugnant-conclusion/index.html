BIAS 083

The Repugnant Pull: Total Utilitarianism and More People

Part VII · Philosophical & Ethical Fallacies · Total Utilitarianism and More People

The bias: The philosophical machinery that always demands more births.

For each possible population of at least ten billion people, all with a very high quality of life, there must be some much larger imaginable population whose existence, if other things are equal, would be better even though its members have lives that are barely worth living." — Derek Parfit, Reasons and Persons (1984) ## Preface to this volume There is a family of moral arguments that, followed carefully, seems to conclude that we are always doing too little so long as more happy-enough people could exist. Not that we should reproduce recklessly; not that any particular person owes the world a child. Rather: that the sum of value in a world is, at least in part, a function of how many worthwhile lives it contains, and that a world with more such lives is, other things equal, a better world. From this modest-sounding premise a great deal follows, and some of what follows is strange. This book is about that machinery, the total, aggregative, "sum-ranking" moral frameworks, and the standing pressure toward more births they generate. The stance of this series is critical of pronatalist bias, and this volume is no exception. But the target here is unusually abstract. Most of the biases catalogued in these hundred books are felt: the ache of the empty house, the interrogation at the family dinner, the metaphor of the ticking clock. The bias examined here is felt by almost no one directly. It lives in journal articles and effective-altruist forums, in the premises of population axiology, in the quiet assumption, often unexamined, that adding a worthwhile life is a good thing to do, full stop. And yet this abstract machinery matters, because it increasingly supplies the intellectual respectability for pronatalist conclusions that would otherwise rest on sentiment or self-interest. When a philosopher or a foundation says that the long-term future contains astronomically many potential people whose non-existence would be a loss, they are drawing on this machinery. The felt pressures of the other books borrow its authority. A grandmother's wish for grandchildren is easy to hear as pressure; an equation that says the never-born represent foregone value does not feel like pressure at all. It feels like being shown a truth one had selfishly overlooked. That is why it warrants a book. I want to be fair to it, because it is powerful. The intuition that more happiness is better than less, that suffering is bad wherever it occurs and whoever bears it, that we should not be partial to people simply because they happen to exist now rather than later: these are not crank views. They are the load-bearing commitments of one of the most humane traditions in moral philosophy. Utilitarianism gave us early and principled arguments against slavery, cruelty to animals, and the subjection of women. Its impartiality is a moral achievement, not a defect. So the aim of this book is not to sneer at aggregation but to isolate one specific consequence of it: the way total-view aggregation, when applied to the question of how many people there should be, generates what Derek Parfit famously named the Repugnant Conclusion, and the way that conclusion functions, in practice, as a pronatalist push. The book proceeds as the series template requires. Chapter 1 names the bias and distinguishes it from its neighbors: the person-affecting confusions of Book 084, the selective future-generations rhetoric of Book 089, the pronatalist-elite organizing of Book 066. Chapter 2, before any criticism, states the strongest version of the total-utilitarian case as sympathetically as I can; if I have done my work, a proponent should read it and feel understood. Chapters 3 through 7 trace where the view comes from, how it operates, what the evidence and the formal results actually show, what the framing hides, and who bears the resulting pressure. Chapter 8 turns the critique on itself and airs the best objections to this book's thesis, conceding where they land. Chapter 9 offers illustrations; Chapter 10 maps the adjacent biases; Chapter 11 asks what clear reasoning looks like once the pull is named; Chapter 12 concludes without triumph. A word on what this book does not claim. It does not claim that total utilitarianism is refuted, or that population ethics has a settled answer; it emphatically does not. It does not claim that caring about future people is a mistake; a great deal of what is best in longtermist thinking survives everything said here. It claims something narrower and, I hope, defensible: that the total view contains a structural tilt toward more people, that this tilt produces conclusions most of us find hard to accept, that the tilt is not obviously the correct reading of impartial benevolence, and that when it is imported into real decisions about reproduction it deserves to be named as a pressure rather than accepted as a discovery. Naming a pressure is not defeating an argument. But it is the first honest step, and in a domain where the argument wears the costume of pure rationality, the honest step is easy to skip. ## 1. Naming the bias The bias this book names is not a feeling but a structure, a feature of a certain way of adding up value that, once adopted, tilts every reproductive question toward "more." Call it the repugnant pull: the standing pressure, generated by total (aggregative) moral frameworks, to create additional people whenever their lives would contain more good than bad, because on those frameworks each such life adds to the total quantity of value in the world, and more total value is better. The word "standing" is doing work here. This is not a pressure encountered once, considered, and set aside. It is regenerated every single time the framework is applied to a choice, because the framework's very shape, maximize the good, where the good depends partly on how many worthwhile lives exist, reproduces the "more" as reliably as a spring returns to its rest. The one-sentence thesis of this book is this: total-utilitarian aggregation converts the impartial concern for welfare into a standing imperative to increase the number of worthwhile lives, an imperative that terminates only in conclusions almost everyone finds repugnant, and that functions in practice as a pronatalist bias wearing the costume of rigor. Let me fix the terms, because in this domain vagueness is where all the mischief hides. A moral framework is welfarist if what ultimately matters is the welfare (well-being, happiness, preference-satisfaction; the accounts differ) of individuals. It is aggregative or sum-ranking if it evaluates outcomes by summing welfare across individuals, so that the value of a world is, in the simplest version, the total welfare it contains. It takes the total view of population ethics if, when the number of individuals can vary between the outcomes being compared, it ranks those outcomes by the total sum of welfare, counting the welfare of merely-possible people who would exist in one outcome but not another. Contrast this with the average view, which ranks outcomes by average welfare per person, and with the person-affecting views, which hold, roughly, that an outcome can only be better or worse if it is better or worse for someone, so that adding a happy person, who is not thereby made better or worse off (there being no prior state of that person to compare against), is neither an improvement nor a harm to anyone. The total view has an immediate and famous implication. If each additional life above the "neutral level," the level at which a life is barely worth living, contributing a small positive quantity to the total, adds something positive, then a sufficiently large population of barely-worth-living lives can contain more total welfare than a smaller population of wonderful lives. Ten billion people flourishing can, on the total view, be beaten by some vastly larger number of people whose lives contain only a faint surplus of good over bad: "muzak and potatoes," in the familiar phrase, rather than Mozart and truffles. The arithmetic is elementary: a small positive quantity, multiplied by a large enough number, exceeds a large quantity multiplied by a smaller one. Parfit called the result the Repugnant Conclusion, and the name stuck because the conclusion is, to almost everyone, repugnant, while the reasoning that produces it is hard to fault at any single step. That combination, an intolerable destination reached by an unimpeachable road, is what makes the subject so gripping and so dangerous. I should be clear about what this book is not about, because the neighboring books cover ground that is easy to confuse with this one, and the whole value of a hundred-book series depends on each book keeping to its lane. Book 084, Person-Affecting Confusions, concerns the muddles that arise when we try to say we are benefiting or harming people who do not yet and may never exist; that book examines the person-affecting alternative to the total view and the confusions that afflict all sides of the debate. This book takes the total view as its object and asks specifically about its pronatalist tilt. Book 089, Selective Future-Generations Talk, concerns the rhetorical habit of invoking future people only when it favors more births and forgetting them otherwise; that is a critique of inconsistency and motivated reasoning, whereas this book engages a view applied consistently, and indeed the consistency is what makes it formidable. Book 066, The Pronatalist Vanguard, concerns the sociology of movements organizing for higher fertility; this book concerns the philosophy some of them cite. And Book 082, The Appeal to Consequences, concerns the "but if everyone stopped" move; the total view is not that move, because it does not rest on the disaster of extinction but on the positive value of addition. So the bias, stated exactly: it is the treating of population size as a lever on the moral good, such that adding worthwhile lives is counted as making the world better, with the result that reproduction acquires the character of a duty owed to the total. The pull is real, the reasoning is respectable, and, this is the book's claim, the conclusion is far less secure than its abstract elegance suggests. The strongest case for it (steelman) Here is the total view at its most compelling, stated without irony and, I hope, without a thumb on the scale. If I have done this chapter well, a proponent should read it and feel that their view has finally been put fairly. Begin with three commitments that are hard to reject. First, welfare matters: pleasure, flourishing, the satisfaction of what people care about, these are good; suffering, frustration, and misery are bad. This is close to bedrock; a moral view that denied it would scarcely be a moral view at all. Second, welfare matters impartially: my happiness is not more important than yours because it is mine, and a stranger's suffering is not less bad because they are a stranger. The whole moral progress of the modern era can be told as the widening of this impartial circle, from tribe to nation to species to, arguably, all sentient beings. To resist impartiality is to defend a parochialism that, spelled out, few will endorse. Third, and this is the pivotal move, the location of welfare in time or in a particular person is not, in itself, morally weighty. A pain is no less bad for occurring in the year 2300; a joy is no less good for belonging to someone not yet born. If you accept that we can wrong future people by, say, burying toxic waste that will poison them centuries hence, you have already conceded that the welfare of not-yet-existing people counts in our deliberations. Almost everyone accepts this. The totalist simply asks us to accept it consistently. Now put these together and ask: what should we want the world to contain? If welfare is good, impartially, wherever and whenever it occurs, then a world containing more welfare is, other things equal, better than one containing less. This is not a strange or greedy thought. It is what benevolence is: the wish that there be more good and less bad. And here is the pivotal observation the total view insists we not flinch from: a worthwhile life is a locus of welfare. A person whose life contains a genuine surplus of good over bad is, over their lifetime, a positive quantity of the very stuff we agreed was good. To bring such a life into existence is therefore to add good to the world. Not to benefit a pre-existing person, granted; there is no one standing in a waiting room who is made better off by being ushered into being. But it is to increase the total amount of the thing that matters. And if the total amount of what matters is what we should be promoting, then bringing worthwhile lives into existence is, quite simply, doing good. The proponent presses the point with the Mere Addition argument, Parfit's own, which is worth walking through slowly because its force is cumulative. Consider world A: ten billion people with wonderful lives. Now consider A+: the very same ten billion, exactly as well off as before, plus another group living somewhat less wonderful but still clearly good lives, in a separate region, affecting the first group not at all, not competing for their resources, not lowering their happiness, simply existing elsewhere and glad to. Is A+ worse than A? It is very hard to see how. The original ten billion are exactly as they were; the newcomers are living lives they are pleased to have. To say A+ is worse than A is to say that the mere existence of additional contented people, harming no one, makes the world worse, which sounds not like benevolence but like a grudging misanthropy. So, at most, A+ is no worse than A. Now consider B: the same total number of people as A+, but with welfare redistributed so that everyone sits at the same, high-but-lower-than-A level, and the total welfare in B is greater than in A+ (we get both perfect equality and a little more total good). B seems better than A+: it is more equal, which many count as an improvement, and it contains more total welfare besides. But if A+ is no worse than A, and B is better than A+, then B is better than A. Now repeat the whole operation starting from B. Each cycle adds a large group of people whose lives are good but a little less good than the incumbents', then levels down-and-up to a slightly lower but more populous and higher-total world. Step by unobjectionable step, you descend toward a world of enormous population and barely-positive lives, the Repugnant Conclusion. And the point that should unsettle the critic is this: at no single step were you asked to accept anything obviously false. The repugnance attaches only to the destination, never to any leg of the journey. Consider, too, what the denial of the total view seems to commit you to, for a view is only as strong as its rivals are weak, and the rivals here are in deep trouble. The average view says a world is better the higher its average welfare. But then a world containing a single, blissfully happy person could be better than a world of billions of very happy people, merely because the solitary blissful life pushes the average higher. Worse, on the average view, adding a person with a genuinely good life but one slightly below the current average makes the world worse, even though that person is glad to exist and harms no one; we are told to regret the existence of a happy person because they dragged down a statistic. The person-affecting views fare no better. They seem forced to say that it does not matter at all, morally, whether we bring into existence a life of radiant joy or a life of unrelenting torment, since in neither case is there a prior person who is benefited or harmed, both are supposed to be morally on a par, a conclusion most people find more repugnant than the Repugnant Conclusion itself. Every escape route from the total view, in other words, opens onto its own chamber of horrors. Against that backdrop, calmly accepting the Repugnant Conclusion can start to look less like a failure of nerve and more like the maturity to pay the smallest available price. There is, finally, the intuition about empty futures, which may be the most powerful arrow in the totalist's quiver. Picture a future in which humanity, having reasoned itself into voluntary non-reproduction, simply ends: the last generation ages and dies, the libraries fall silent, no one is ever born again to read them, to love, to discover, to be moved by a piece of music or to solve a problem or to watch a sunrise. Something enormous is lost, the proponent says. And notice that person-affecting views strain to explain what: since no existing person is made worse off by never being succeeded (the last generation lived out its life), a view that counts only effects on existing people must judge the permanent end of humanity as, morally, no loss at all, a matter of indifference. That is very hard to believe. The total view explains the loss at once and without contortion: the vast quantity of good those never-born lives would have contained is a real loss, an opportunity cost of extinction. To care about that loss, to feel in your bones that a permanently empty future is a tragedy and not a shrug-worthy nothing, is already to think, at least a little, like a totalist. You have already started summing. This is the strongest case, and it is strong. Impartial benevolence, the equal counting of future welfare, the embarrassments of every alternative, the badness of empty futures: these give the total view genuine force, and nothing in the rest of this book should be read as denying that force. The question the following chapters press is not whether the case is powerful. It plainly is. The question is whether it establishes what it appears to establish: a standing imperative to make more people. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 2 Quiz. 1. Where it comes from The repugnant pull did not arrive fully formed with Parfit in 1984. It is the endpoint of a long development in which the impartial arithmetic of classical utilitarianism was gradually asked to answer a question it was not originally designed to face: not "how should we treat the people there are?" but "how many people should there be?" The first question the founders answered at length; the second they mostly assumed away, treating the population as a fixed backdrop against which happiness was to be maximized. Population ethics is what happens when that backdrop is allowed to move. Classical utilitarianism, Jeremy Bentham in the late eighteenth century, John Stuart Mill in the nineteenth, and above all Henry Sidgwick in The Methods of Ethics (1874), was built around a "greatest happiness" principle. But there is a notorious ambiguity lurking in the slogan "the greatest happiness of the greatest number": do we maximize the total happiness, or the average? For a fixed population the two coincide, and so for most of the tradition's history the question never had to be faced. Sidgwick saw the problem with unusual clarity and, in a famous and remarkably candid passage, concluded that on utilitarian principles we ought to aim at the greatest total, not the greatest average, which meant that population growth was to be encouraged up to the point where the gain from additional numbers just offset the loss from any resulting fall in average happiness. This is often cited as the first explicit statement of the total view and its pronatalist implication, and it is worth pausing on: the pull was visible to the tradition's most careful nineteenth-century mind, and he did not recoil from it. He named it and endorsed it, in the plain daylight of Victorian moral philosophy, long before anyone had coined the word "repugnant" for where it leads. The twentieth century sharpened the tools. As welfare economics developed formal machinery for aggregating utility across persons (social welfare functions, interpersonal comparisons, the whole apparatus of theoretical policy evaluation) the population question could be posed with new precision, and the informal Victorian intuition could be turned into a theorem. But the decisive philosophical treatment came with Derek Parfit's Reasons and Persons (1984), Part IV of which is the founding text of modern population ethics and remains its high-water mark. Parfit did not invent the total view; he did something more unsettling than inventing it. He showed, through the Mere Addition Paradox and a battery of ingenious thought experiments, that our intuitions about these cases are jointly inconsistent, that we cannot hold on to everything we are pre-reflectively inclined to hold. He named the Repugnant Conclusion not in order to endorse it but to mark it as the thing a satisfactory theory ought to avoid, and then he demonstrated, case by case, how agonizingly hard it is to avoid without paying some other unacceptable price. He called the search for a satisfactory account "Theory X" and confessed, at the end of the most influential work of moral philosophy of his generation, that he had not found it. The honesty of that confession is part of the very texture of the subject and should chasten anyone, totalist or critic, who claims the matter is settled. The founder of the field left it open. After Parfit, the discipline professionalized and formalized. The informal paradoxes matured into a body of impossibility results, of which more in Chapter 5, associated especially with the work of Gustaf Arrhenius, whose six impossibility theorems, developed across a series of papers from the late 1990s onward, purport to show that no population axiology can satisfy all of a short list of individually plausible conditions at once. The First Impossibility Theorem, for example, holds that no axiology can jointly satisfy the Quality Condition, the Egalitarian Dominance Condition, and the Quantity Condition; later and subtler versions swap in weaker and more compelling conditions, including Weak Non-Sadism (which rules out counting the addition of tormented lives as better than the addition of many merely-good ones) and Non-Elitism. Every one of the results includes a condition that rules out the Repugnant Conclusion. The upshot of this body of work is not that the total view wins but that every view loses something; there is no free lunch anywhere on the menu. The totalist's characteristic move, in this professionalized state of the field, is to argue that what the total view gives up, our discomfort at the Repugnant Conclusion, is the least bad thing to surrender, cheaper than the horrors the rivals must swallow. The most consequential recent development, and the one that makes this book urgent rather than merely academic, is the migration of this machinery out of the seminar room and into a practical movement. Strands of effective altruism and longtermism, associated with figures such as Nick Bostrom, and given book-length popular statement in William MacAskill's What We Owe the Future (2022), take seriously the idea that the number of potential future people is astronomically large, and that if each worthwhile future life carries positive value, then the expected value of ensuring humanity's long and populous future dwarfs almost every other moral concern. Here the abstract total view acquires stakes and institutions: research centers, funding streams, cause prioritization, a public voice. It is at this junction, traced more fully in Book 066, that a claim in axiology becomes something like a program, and the repugnant pull stops being a philosopher's curiosity and starts, at least at the margins, to shape how money, attention, and moral urgency are allocated in the real world. That migration is why a book in a series about natalism must treat the total view at all: the pull has left the page. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 3 Quiz. 1. ① In brief

Some of the most humane commitments in moral philosophy (that welfare matters, that it matters impartially, that a stranger's suffering or a future person's joy is no less real for being distant) combine, when applied to the question of how many people there should be, into a surprising pressure: a standing pull toward creating ever more people. This book examines that pull, isolates its machinery, and argues that it is far less compelling than its abstract elegance suggests.

The engine is the total view of population ethics. On this view, the goodness of a world depends in part on the total amount of welfare it contains, summed across everyone who exists, including people who would exist in one possible future but not another. Since a life worth living is a locus of positive welfare, bringing such a life into existence adds to the total, and more total value is better. Follow this consistently and you reach Derek Parfit's Repugnant Conclusion (Reasons and Persons, 1984, Part IV): for any world of flourishing people, there is a better world with a vastly larger population whose lives are barely worth living, "muzak and potatoes" beating Mozart and truffles, if only the numbers are large enough. Parfit's Mere Addition Paradox shows that this conclusion follows from steps each of which seems individually undeniable, revealing our intuitions to be jointly inconsistent.

The book's stance is critical of pronatalist bias, but scrupulously fair to its target. Chapter 2 states the strongest case for the total view first and sympathetically: impartial benevolence really does seem to recommend more good wherever it can occur; the rival average and person-affecting views face their own severe embarrassments; and a permanently empty human future really does seem tragic in a way the total view explains at once. This is not a straw man, and the book concedes that the "empty future" intuition leaves a genuine residue no complete theory can ignore.

Read the rest of the overview

The critique is correspondingly narrow. It does not claim the total view is refuted, or that caring about future people is a mistake, or that reproduction is wrong. It claims that the pronatalist pull toward more is not compelled by impartial benevolence, that it is one contested option, not a discovery. The supporting moves: the impossibility theorems (associated with Gustaf Arrhenius, flagged for verification) show that every population axiology violates some plausible condition, so swallowing the Repugnant Conclusion is a choice among costs, not the deliverance of reason. The unreliability of our large-number intuitions cuts symmetrically: if it disqualifies our recoil from the conclusion, it equally disqualifies the intuition that adding lives is good. And the badness of empty futures supports continuation (not letting humanity end), which the book does not oppose, rather than maximization (making the future as populous as possible), which is where the pronatalist pull actually lives.

The book also names what the aggregate quietly prices at zero: the standpoint of the individual whose life is only barely worth living, the asymmetry between relieving existing suffering and manufacturing new happiness ("make people happy, not make happy people"), the externalized ecological and distributive costs of each birth, and the autonomy of those (overwhelmingly those who gestate and rear) on whom a "more lives" imperative would concretely fall. The impartial sum, it turns out, has a very partial incidence.

Throughout, the book stays in its lane. It is distinct from the person-affecting confusions of Book 084 (the rival view), the selective future-generations rhetoric of Book 089 (a critique of inconsistency, whereas this concerns a consistently applied framework), and the pronatalist-elite organizing of Book 066 (the sociology this book's philosophy underwrites). Its quarry is specifically the aggregative, addition-valuing structure that always demands more births.

The takeaway is a measured one. Population axiology remains unsettled; Parfit died without his "Theory X." But the person deciding whether to bring a life into being owes nothing to the sum. There is no one waiting on the ledger to be redeemed. The honest question is not whether a birth would add to the total, but whether this life, lived by this prospective person, would go well.

CH 6The hidden costs

What does the repugnant-pull framing obscure? A framing's hidden costs are the considerations it quietly prices at zero, the items that never appear on its ledger because its very structure has no column for them. The total view, applied to reproduction, prices several important things at zero, and naming them is the beginning of pricing them back in.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

An abstract axiology has no body. It floats in the space of reasons, impartial and serene. But when its pronatalist implication is translated into felt pressure and real demand, the translation is emphatically not distributed evenly across humanity. Ask who, concretely, bears the repugnant pull once it lands, and the impartial sum turns out to have a very partial incidence indeed. This chapter is where the aggregate meets the body.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

What does clear, non-coerced reasoning about reproduction look like once the repugnant pull has been named and its machinery exposed? Not the wholesale rejection of impartial benevolence, which would be a real loss, a throwing-out of one of the finest achievements of moral thought. Rather, it looks like a set of disciplines that let us keep what is genuinely good in aggregative thinking while declining the specific, contestable pressure this book has isolated. The goal is not to stop caring about welfare or about the future. It is to stop mistaking one particular tilt for the whole of reason.

CH 12Conclusion

The argument of this book has been deliberately and unfashionably narrow. Total, aggregative moral frameworks, welfarist, impartial, summing welfare across all who exist or might exist, contain a structural feature that no amount of good intention can dissolve: because each additional worthwhile life adds to the total, and because more total value is by hypothesis better, such frameworks generate a standing pressure toward more people, a pressure that terminates only in Parfit's Repugnant Conclusion, a vast population of barely-worth-living lives ranked above a smaller flourishing one. That pressure, the repugnant pull, is the bias this volume set out to name. It is not a felt ache like the empty house of Book 038, nor a social script like the interrogation at the family dinner of Book 027; it is an abstraction, felt directly by almost no one. But it increasingly supplies the intellectual respectability that cruder pronatalisms lack, lending the prestige of impartial reason to a conclusion about births, and so it has more than earned its place in this series.

Total view (totalism)
The population-ethics position that ranks outcomes by the total sum of welfare, counting the welfare of merely-possible people who would exist in one outcome but not another. The primary source of the "repugnant pull."
Average view
The position ranking outcomes by average welfare per person. Avoids the Repugnant Conclusion but implies, e.g., that a tiny blissful population can beat a large happy one, and that adding a good-but-below-average life makes the world worse.
Person-affecting view
The position that an outcome is better or worse only if it is better or worse for someone; merely adding a happy person is therefore neither good nor bad. The principal rival to totalism; examined in Book 084.
Repugnant Conclusion
Derek Parfit's result (Reasons and Persons, 1984, Part IV): for any world of people with a very high quality of life, there is a better world with a vastly larger population whose members' lives are barely worth living.
Mere Addition Paradox
Parfit's argument that a chain of individually plausible steps — adding happy people who harm no one, then equalizing-and-improving — leads inexorably to the Repugnant Conclusion, showing our intuitions to be jointly inconsistent.
Aggregation / sum-ranking
Evaluating outcomes by summing welfare across individuals. The structural feature that lets each added worthwhile life increase total value.
Welfarism
The view that what ultimately matters morally is the welfare (well-being, happiness, preference-satisfaction) of individuals.
Neutral level
The welfare level at which a life is exactly "worth living," contributing zero to the total. The anchor of total-view arithmetic; empirically very hard to locate.
Critical-level view
A totalist variant counting a life's contribution to the total only insofar as its welfare exceeds a positive threshold, blocking the descent to barely-worth-living lives. A disciplining of the pure total view (with problems of its own).
Impossibility theorems
Formal results (associated with Gustaf Arrhenius) that no population axiology can satisfy all of a short list of individually plausible conditions; every view must give up at least one. (Exact conditions to verify.)
Impartial benevolence
The commitment that welfare counts equally wherever, whenever, and in whomever it occurs. The total view's genuine and prestigious foundation.
Scope insensitivity
The documented human tendency to respond inadequately to differences among very large quantities. Invoked by totalists to discount anti-Repugnant intuitions; the book argues it cuts symmetrically against pro-total intuitions too.
Continuation vs. maximization
The distinction between valuing that a flourishing human future continue (not-ending) and valuing that it be as large as possible (maximizing). The repugnant pull lives in the second, not the first.
Creating/relieving asymmetry
The intuition, slogan "make people happy, not make happy people," that we have strong reason to relieve existing suffering but weak or no reason to manufacture new happy people.
Longtermism
The view that positively influencing the long-term future is a key moral priority. In its pure-total form it inherits the repugnant pull; its institutional life is treated in Book 066.
Theory X
Parfit's name for the satisfactory population axiology he sought but did not find; a standing reminder that the field is unsettled.
Non-identity problem
The puzzle that many acts affecting future people also determine which people exist, complicating claims that we benefit or harm "them"; a pressure point for person-affecting views (see Book 084).

⑥ Sources

What this volume draws on. Where the evidence is thin or contested, the book says so rather than reaching for a number.

  • Parfit, Derek. Reasons and Persons. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 1984. Part IV is the founding modern treatment of population ethics: the Repugnant Conclusion, the Mere Addition Paradox, the total versus average views, and the search for "Theory X."
  • Benatar, David. Better Never to Have Been: The Harm of Coming into Existence. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2006. Source for the asymmetry between the absence of suffering (good) and the absence of pleasure (not bad unless someone is deprived), which challenges the total view's symmetric treatment of adding good and bad lives. Relates to Book 079.
  • Sidgwick, Henry. The Methods of Ethics. First edition 1874 (commonly cited in the 7th edition, 1907). Contains an early explicit statement of the total view and its implication that population should be increased up to the point where added numbers offset falling average happiness.
  • MacAskill, William. What We Owe the Future. 2022. A prominent popular statement of longtermism, in which the potential scale of the future population is treated as morally weighty. Where this book characterizes longtermism, it means the position argued for here.
  • Arrhenius, Gustaf. "An Impossibility Theorem for Welfarist Axiologies." Economics and Philosophy 16, no. 2 (2000): 247–266, together with his subsequent and stronger theorems. The general upshot is what this book relies on: no axiology satisfies every condition we find independently plausible. Readers who want the formal conditions themselves should go to the papers; the argument here does not turn on which particular version is stated.
  • Ryberg, Jesper, and Torbjörn Tännsjö, eds. The Repugnant Conclusion: Essays on Population Ethics. Dordrecht: Kluwer, 2004. — The first volume devoted entirely to the problem, and still the best single place to see the range of attempted escapes.
  • Greaves, Hilary. "Population axiology." Philosophy Compass (2017). — A survey of the field; the shortest route to a map of the terrain.
  • Broome, John. Weighing Lives. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2004. — On the goodness of adding lives and the difficulty of comparing populations of different sizes.
  • Bostrom, Nick. "Astronomical Waste: The Opportunity Cost of Delayed Technological Development." Utilitas (2003). — Where the total view's stakes become astronomical, and the practical edge of the longtermist strand discussed in Chapter 4.
  • Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, "The Repugnant Conclusion," and the related entries on population ethics. A reliable scholarly overview, revised periodically.
Directions the volume points to (not pinned citations)
  • The empirical well-being literature on where a "neutral level" of welfare might sit, and on the asymmetry between judging a life worth starting and judging it worth continuing. Chapter 5 draws on this as a class of finding; nothing in the argument depends on a particular study.
