BIAS 085

The Baseline Trick: Naturalizing the Status Quo as Ethics

Part VII · Philosophical & Ethical Fallacies · Naturalizing the Status Quo as Ethics

The bias: Treating "keep reproducing" as the neutral ethical default.

"That which is customary is not thereby made right." — a maxim in the spirit of the Enlightenment moralists, paraphrased from a recurrent theme in Bentham and Mill (idea attributed, not a verbatim quotation)
① In brief ② The book in twelve chapters ③ Key terms ④ Ten-question check ⑤ Reflection ⑥ Sources ⑦ Related biases

① In brief

Book 085 · 100 Biases of Natalism

This book examines a quiet move in moral argument about reproduction: the treatment of the ongoing practice of having children, the reproductive status quo, as the neutral ethical baseline, the zero point against which every alternative must be justified. Once the status quo occupies that position, an asymmetry of burden falls into place automatically. Continuing to reproduce needs no defense; it is simply "what is done." Departing from it, or even questioning it aloud, is treated as the thing that must be argued for. The author calls this the baseline trick and argues that it is an unearned argumentative privilege, not a sound principle of reasoning.

The thesis is carefully bounded. The book is critical of pronatalist bias but does not claim that having children is wrong, nor demand that anyone stop. Its target is not the choice to reproduce but the assumption that this choice alone is exempt from justification because it is the default. The core philosophical claim, stated flatly, is that a status quo is not a normative baseline. That a practice is ongoing, widespread, and ancient is a descriptive fact; to let that fact settle where the burden of justification lies is to cross from is to ought without the bridge Hume required.

Read the rest of the overview

The book proceeds through twelve chapters following the series template. It defines the bias precisely and distinguishes it from three neighbors: existence bias (Book 002, a fact about minds), default bias (Book 001, a fact about behavior), and the burden-of-proof asymmetry (Book 086, the effect the baseline trick helps cause). Chapter 2 delivers a full-strength steelman: the Burkean case that inherited practices carry accumulated wisdom, that reproduction is not merely one practice but the load-bearing one, that under deep uncertainty a conservative burden of proof is warranted, and that Chesterton's fence counsels caution before dismantling what we do not fully understand. The author concedes this case has real force but insists that weight is not the same as exemption: a Burkean may earn a modest, defeasible, cost-counting presumption, whereas the baseline trick claims a total, unquestioned, cost-hiding one.

Later chapters trace the bias's origins (evolutionary disposition, theological inheritance, linguistic accident, and philosophical laundering), then name the concrete mechanisms by which it operates today: the default-question asymmetry ("why not have children?" is asked constantly, its mirror never); the reclassification of reproduction's costs as "just life"; the omission/commission gerrymander, in which the chosen baseline silently decides what counts as an act; the vocabulary of "starting a family" and "childless"; and the framing effects documented by Tversky and Kahneman. The evidence chapter is honest that the strongest case is philosophical and structural rather than statistical, flagging the application of framing research to reproductive intuitions as a reasonable inference, not a measured result.

The hidden-costs chapter names what the default conceals: the exposure of a created person to life's harms (citing Benatar without endorsement), the reorganization of a life, the ecological load, and the corruption of moral reasoning itself. A distributional chapter shows the pressure falls unequally: hardest on women, the economically precarious, the communally embedded, and those whose circumstances already mark them as departing from a norm. The book then answers its own strongest objections, conceding that all reasoning needs starting points and that the mirror-image error (privileging non-reproduction) is equally a trick.

The remedy is reflective equilibrium (Rawls) plus practical habits: the reversibility test, symmetry of questions, and counting the full ledger. The takeaway is deliberately modest: naming the baseline trick does not answer the reproduction question; it hands the question back, with two live options and no thumb on the scale.

② The book in twelve chapters

Each chapter opens with its own summary. Note chapter 2: every volume states the strongest case for the thing it criticises before laying a finger on it — and chapter 8 turns the critique back on itself.

CH 1Naming the bias

Let me define the bias exactly, because its power lies in that exactness: it is easy to miss because it hides inside the setup of an argument rather than in any stated premise.

CH 2The strongest case for it (steelman)The strongest case against this book

I owe the reader the best version of the view I mean to criticize, stated in full and without condescension, before I lay a finger on it. And there is a strong case for treating the reproductive status quo as a privileged baseline. It is not a foolish case. Some of the deepest thinkers about tradition and social knowledge would endorse it, and I want to give it every advantage.

CH 3Where it comes from

Where does the baseline trick come from? Why does treating reproduction as the ethical zero point feel so natural that its being an assumption goes unnoticed? The sources are layered, evolutionary, religious, linguistic, and philosophical, and it will help to separate them, because each contributes a different kind of invisibility.

CH 4How it operates today

The baseline trick is not a historical curiosity. It operates now, in ordinary moral reasoning and everyday conversation, through a set of concrete mechanisms. Naming them makes the trick visible, which is the first step to declining it.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

What can be shown, as opposed to merely asserted, about the baseline trick? The honest answer is that the strongest evidence is philosophical and structural, not statistical. This is a book about a move in reasoning, and moves in reasoning are demonstrated by analysis rather than measured by survey. But there is empirical work that bears on the claim, and I want to be careful about what it does and does not establish.

CH 6The hidden costs

Every baseline hides something. What a reproductive baseline hides is the entire cost side of a ledger, costs that, because they attach to the default, are never entered as costs at all. This chapter names what the baseline obscures. I am not here claiming these costs are decisive; I am claiming they go unpriced in the default framing, which is a different and more defensible point.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

Biases are not distributed evenly. The baseline trick presses hardest on those least positioned to resist it, and a critical account owes a distributional analysis. Who actually feels the weight of being cast as the one who must justify a deviation from the reproductive default?

CH 8The counter-arguments to the critiqueObjections to the critique

I have argued that treating reproduction as the moral baseline is an unearned privilege. Intellectual honesty requires that I now turn the guns on my own thesis and give the strongest objections a fair hearing. Several are serious, and I will not pretend they are easily disposed of.

CH 9Case studies / illustrations

Abstract analysis needs concrete illustration. Here are four cases, drawn from ordinary life and from the history of ideas, in which the baseline trick can be watched operating. I have chosen them to show the trick in different registers, conversational, institutional, philosophical, and rhetorical, rather than to prove any statistical claim.

CH 10Adjacent biases

No bias operates alone. The baseline trick interlocks with others in this series, and mapping the connections both clarifies its boundaries and shows why it is worth treating separately rather than folding into a neighbor. I will be brief on each, since each has or will have its own volume, and my aim is to locate the baseline trick in the family, not to duplicate what those books do.

Not, I stress again, to reach any particular conclusion, the whole point is that clear reasoning is compatible with concluding either way, but to reason without the unearned baseline distorting the process. ③ Key terms

Show all 17 terms as a list
Baseline trick
In moral argument about reproduction, treating the ongoing practice of having children as the neutral normative baseline against which alternatives must be justified, while exempting the practice itself from justification on the ground that it is simply what is done.
Normative baseline
The starting point in a moral argument that is treated as needing no defense, so that the burden of justification falls on any departure from it.
Moral default
The option in a practical-ethical domain that is presumed correct or acceptable absent a countervailing reason, and against which other options are measured.
Status quo
The existing, ongoing state of affairs; a descriptive fact that, this book argues, does not by itself confer normative privilege.
Is/ought gap
Hume's point that no purely descriptive premise ("what is") by itself entails a normative conclusion ("what ought to be") without an added evaluative premise.
Naturalistic fallacy
The error of inferring that something is good or right from the fact that it is natural, usual, or the way things are done.
Omission/commission asymmetry
The differing moral treatment of doing versus allowing (commission versus omission), whose application depends on which baseline an outcome is measured against.
Omission/commission gerrymander
The book's term for how the silent choice of baseline decides whether an outcome counts as something one did or merely allowed; flip the baseline and the moral bookkeeping flips with it.
Reference dependence
The finding (Tversky & Kahneman) that people evaluate outcomes as gains or losses relative to a reference point rather than in absolute terms.
Framing effect
A change in judgment produced solely by how an option is described, holding the underlying facts fixed.
Reflective equilibrium
Rawls's method of adjusting particular judgments and general principles against one another until they cohere, treating none as immune from revision; offered as the proper alternative to baseline-privileging.
Burkean presumption
The conservative view that inherited practices carry accumulated wisdom and that the burden of proof falls on the would-be reformer.
Chesterton's fence
The principle that one should not remove an existing arrangement until one understands why it was established.
Earned presumption
A defeasible, cost-counting default that has been argued for, as distinct from an assumed baseline that exempts itself from justification.
Reversibility test
The diagnostic of flipping the baseline to see whether a proposed reason survives; a reason that works in only one direction is a baseline artifact, not a reason.
Default-question asymmetry
The practice of asking "why not have children?" while never asking the mirror question "why have children?", revealing an assumed baseline.
Reclassification of costs as "just life"
The mechanism by which reproduction's real costs, because they attach to the default, are absorbed into the category of the given ("the human condition") rather than counted as consequences of a choice.

⑥ Sources

What this volume draws on. Where the evidence is thin or contested, the book says so rather than reaching for a number.

  • David Hume, A Treatise of Human Nature (1739–40) — the classic source of the is/ought gap.
  • David Benatar, Better Never to Have Been: The Harm of Coming into Existence (2006) — the leading philosophical statement of the antinatalist asymmetry; cited for the harm-to-the-created-person cost, not endorsed.
  • John Rawls, A Theory of Justice (1971) — the locus classicus for reflective equilibrium as a method.
  • Edmund Burke, Reflections on the Revolution in France (1790) — the foundational statement of the conservative/traditionalist case steelmanned in Chapter 2.
  • Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman, "The Framing of Decisions and the Psychology of Choice," Science (1981) — the classic demonstration of framing effects and reference dependence.
  • Daniel Kahneman, Thinking, Fast and Slow (2011) — accessible synthesis of reference dependence and the psychology of intuition.
  • G. E. Moore, Principia Ethica (1903) — the origin of the term "naturalistic fallacy" (in Moore's own, narrower sense).
Directions the volume points to (not pinned citations)
  • William Samuelson and Richard Zeckhauser, "Status Quo Bias in Decision Making," Journal of Risk and Uncertainty (1988) — the foundational paper on status quo bias, from which this book borrows its name and little else.
  • Eric Johnson and Daniel Goldstein, "Do Defaults Save Lives?" Science (2003) — the organ-donation default effect; the cleanest available demonstration that where you set the baseline determines what people choose.
  • Christine Overall, Why Have Children? The Ethical Debate (Cambridge, MA: MIT Press, 2012) — argues that the decision to procreate requires justification in the way the decision not to does not. The closest ally to this book's central claim, and the place to go next.
  • G. A. Cohen, "Rescuing Conservatism: A Defense of Existing Value," in Reasons and Recognition: Essays on the Philosophy of T.M. Scanlon (Oxford University Press, 2011); reprinted in Finding Oneself in the Other (Princeton University Press, 2013) — the most serious philosophical defence of a bias in favour of retaining what is of value. The steelman in Chapter 2 owes it a debt.
  • David Hume, "Of the Original Contract" — on custom and the authority of the established.
