PROJECT 082

Workplace Childfree Bias Notes

Society · Difficulty: Intermediate · ~8 min

Some workplaces quietly assume that people without children have "more time," fewer real commitments, or should always cover the holidays. This is a calm look at those patterns — and fair, professional ways to respond. None of this is about resenting colleagues who are parents.

What you will learn

  • Common subtle assumptions, named plainly.
  • Why fairness helps everyone — parents included.
  • Calm, professional phrasings you can adapt.

1. Patterns you might notice

"You have no commitments"Time outside work is everyone's own — rest, care for others, study, and health all count. Default holiday coverBeing asked to always cover holidays or late shifts because you "don't have kids." Last to leaveAn unspoken expectation to stay later than colleagues with children. Invisible workloadExtra tasks quietly routed your way on the same assumption.

The goal isn't to take from parents — it's a fair distribution that respects everyone's life outside work.

2. Fair, professional responses

Reframe calmly"I'm glad to take my fair share. Could we rotate holiday cover so it's even across the team?" Protect your time"My evenings are committed too. I can do X by Friday — does that work?" Make it about fairness"A rotation would be fairer for everyone, parents and non-parents alike."

Key takeaway

Fairness at work isn't anti-parent — it's pro-everyone. Naming an assumption calmly, and asking for an even rotation, protects your time without taking anything from colleagues who are raising children.
