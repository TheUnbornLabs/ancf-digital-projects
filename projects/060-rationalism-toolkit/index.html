Rationalism Toolkit

Learning Tools · Difficulty: Intermediate

A compact set of thinking tools you can apply to any heated discussion — including this one. Used honestly, they work on your own reasoning as much as anyone else's.

The tools

Define your terms

Most disputes shrink once both sides agree what the words mean. "Selfish," "natural," "family" — pin these down before arguing about them.

Separate facts from values

Ask: is this a disagreement about what's true, or about what matters? "Birth rates are falling" (fact) and "that's bad" (value) need different conversations.

Steelman before you reply

Restate the opposing view at its strongest. If you can't, you don't yet understand it well enough to disagree.

Check for fallacies — yours first

Watch for appeals to nature, popularity, and authority on every side, including your own. It's easiest to spot in opponents and hardest in yourself.

Consider the opposite

Ask "what would change my mind?" If nothing could, you're holding a belief, not an argument.

Hold uncertainty

"I'm not sure" is a respectable position. Honest uncertainty beats false confidence, and leaves room to learn.

Update on evidence

Changing your mind when the facts change is a strength, not a defeat. Track what you'd need to see.
