This glossary collects the key terms used throughout the book in one place. Definitions are kept short — one to three sentences — and aim at clarity rather than completeness; the chapters themselves give each idea its fuller treatment. Terms appear in alphabetical order, grouped by first letter.

A

Anchoring — In Peter Wessel Zapffe's account, one of four ways humans cope with the awareness that life lacks ultimate justification: we fix ourselves to stabilizing values or ideas — God, nation, family, a cause — that hold consciousness in place. The danger is that when an anchor fails, the underlying anxiety returns.
Antinatalism — From the Latin natalis ("of birth"). The ethical view that coming into existence carries a negative value, so that procreation requires a justification it may not be able to give. The strong version holds that creating a life always harms the one created; weaker versions hold only that the decision is morally weighty and too often made without thought.
Asymmetry (Benatar's) — David Benatar's central argument that the absence of pain is good even if no one exists to enjoy that good, whereas the absence of pleasure is not bad unless there is someone deprived of it. On this reasoning, coming into existence is always a net harm, while never existing is not a loss to anyone.
Autonomy / reproductive autonomy — A person's right to make significant choices about their own life and body, including whether to have children. Antinatalism is generally a moral claim about the ethics of the choice, not a call to override anyone's legal freedom to make it.

C

Childfree vs childless — "Childfree" describes someone who chooses not to have children; "childless" describes someone who has none, often without having chosen so. Antinatalism may inform a childfree choice but is a distinct ethical position rather than a lifestyle label.
Consent — Agreement to something that affects you. A recurring antinatalist observation is that a future person cannot consent to being born, since they do not yet exist to be asked, and the entire weight of a life is placed on them without their agreement.
Cosmic vs terrestrial meaning — A distinction associated with Thaddeus Metz and discussed by Benatar: "terrestrial" meaning is the significance a life has within human horizons (relationships, work, projects), while "cosmic" meaning would be significance from the standpoint of the universe as a whole. Pessimists argue we can have the former while lacking the latter.

D

Deprivation — Being denied a good one would otherwise have had. In Benatar's asymmetry, a person who never exists cannot be deprived of any pleasure, because there is no one for the absence to be bad for.
Dukkha — A Pali and Sanskrit term, central to Buddhism, usually translated as suffering, unease, or unsatisfactoriness. The First Noble Truth holds that ordinary conditioned existence is pervaded by dukkha — a theme that resonates with secular pessimism.

E

Efilism — A fringe position ("life" spelled backward) holding that sentient life as such is a problem and that the extinction of all sentience would be good. It goes well beyond mainstream antinatalism, which concerns human procreation and rejects coercion or harm.

H

Hedonic adaptation / treadmill — The well-documented tendency for people to return to a roughly stable level of happiness after good or bad events. Pessimists cite it to argue that life's pleasures are more fleeting than they feel, and that we systematically overestimate how well things go.

I

Isolation (Zapffe) — In Zapffe's scheme, the coping mechanism of deliberately keeping disturbing thoughts out of mind — quietly agreeing not to dwell on death, suffering, or futility. It is a kind of mental fencing-off rather than a denial of the facts.

M

Misanthropic argument — A line of antinatalist reasoning, distinct from the philanthropic one, that points to the harm human beings do — to one another, to animals, and to the world — as a reason not to create more of them. It contrasts with arguments grounded in concern for the would-be child's own welfare.

N

Negative ethics (Cabrera) — Julio Cabrera's framework, on which human life has a "structural" disvalue — built-in features such as decay, pain, and moral friction (his "moral impediment") that make a fully ethical life difficult and procreation hard to justify.
Negative utilitarianism — A version of utilitarianism that gives priority to reducing suffering over increasing pleasure. It can lend support to antinatalist conclusions, since not creating a life prevents suffering without leaving anyone worse off.
Non-identity problem — A puzzle in population ethics, sharpened by Derek Parfit: many choices determine which person comes to exist, so a child born of such a choice cannot usually claim to have been made worse off than they would otherwise have been, since otherwise they would not have existed at all.

O

Optimism bias / Pollyannaism — The tendency to remember and expect good outcomes more readily than bad ones, and to rate our own lives more positively than the facts warrant. Benatar uses it to argue that self-reports of life satisfaction are unreliable guides to how good life actually is.

P

Person-affecting view — The principle that an act can be good or bad only insofar as it is good or bad for some person. It plays a central role in debates over whether causing someone to exist can benefit or harm them.
Philanthropic argument — The antinatalist argument rooted in care for the would-be child: because any life carries a real risk of serious suffering, and the child cannot consent, refraining from creating them spares them that risk.
Procreative beneficence — Julian Savulescu's principle that prospective parents have reason to select, among possible children, the one expected to have the best life. It is a pronatalist-leaning idea often discussed as a foil to antinatalism.
Pronatalism — The set of cultural, religious, and political pressures that treat having children as expected, admirable, or a duty. Antinatalist writers argue that pronatalism makes the procreative question feel already answered when it is not.

R

Repugnant Conclusion — Parfit's term for the result that, on certain ways of adding up welfare, a vast population with lives barely worth living would count as better than a smaller, flourishing one. It is a famous stress test for theories of population ethics.

S

Samsara — In Indian religious thought, the cycle of birth, death, and rebirth driven by craving and ignorance. Liberation (release from samsara) is the goal in much Buddhist and Hindu practice, giving birth itself an ambivalent place.
Suffering-focused ethics — A family of moral views that treat the reduction of suffering as the most urgent priority, without necessarily denying the value of happiness. It overlaps with, but is broader than, antinatalism.
Sublimation (Zapffe) — The fourth of Zapffe's coping mechanisms: turning the pain of existence into something else — art, philosophy, or expression — so that awareness of life's tragedy becomes a source of creation rather than only distress. Zapffe regarded his own essay as an example.

V

VHEMT — The Voluntary Human Extinction Movement, founded by Les U. Knight, which advocates that humans voluntarily stop reproducing so the species gradually phases itself out, chiefly on environmental grounds. It is voluntary and non-coercive, and distinct from philosophical antinatalism.

W

Will (Schopenhauer's) — In Arthur Schopenhauer's philosophy, the blind, striving force underlying all reality and expressed in us as endless desire. Because desire is never finally satisfied, he held that existence is marked by frustration and suffering — a foundation for later pessimism.

Chapter in brief

  • This glossary gathers the book's key terms in one alphabetical reference.
  • Several terms — anchoring, isolation, sublimation — belong to Zapffe's account of how we cope with awareness of life's hardships.
  • Others — the asymmetry, deprivation, the non-identity problem, the Repugnant Conclusion — name the core moves in the philosophical debate.
  • Definitions are deliberately brief; the chapters give each idea its full and balanced treatment.
