BIAS 006

The Child You Already Own: Endowment Effects and Imagined Children

Part I · Cognitive & Psychological Biases · Endowment Effects and Imagined Children

The bias: Valuing a hypothetical future child as if already possessed.

The pain of losing is psychologically about twice as powerful as the pleasure of gaining." — commonly attributed to the loss-aversion findings of Kahneman and Tversky (paraphrase of prospect theory) ## Preface to this volume There is a peculiar grammar in the way many people speak about the children they have not had. They speak of "the baby we would have had." They mourn "the daughter I never got to meet." They describe a decision not to reproduce as "giving up" a child, as if a warm, particular person had been set down and walked away from. The language is not careless. It reveals something about how the mind handles imagined offspring: it files them not under things that might be acquired, but under things already possessed and now at risk of loss. Listen closely and the grammar is unmistakable. We do not say "we declined to acquire a hypothetical dependent." We say "we lost the baby," "we gave up on having kids," "the child we never had." Every one of these idioms belongs to the vocabulary of possession and its forfeiture. One gives up a possession, not an opportunity one never reached for; one loses what one had, not what one might have gotten. The mind, in choosing these words, has already made a silent classification: the imagined child sits in the ledger under assets held, not under gains available. And that classification, made below the level of deliberate thought, does an enormous amount of work in the decision that follows. This book is about that filing error. Its subject is the endowment effect, the well-documented tendency to value a thing more highly simply because we regard it as ours, applied to a child who does not yet exist and may never exist. When an imagined future child comes to feel like a possession, declining to have that child stops registering as forgoing a possible gain and starts registering as surrendering something owned. And because losses loom larger than equivalent gains, that reframing quietly inflates the felt pull toward reproduction. The distortion is not in the wanting. It is in the coding of the wanting's object as something one already holds. The stance of this series is stated plainly, and it holds here. We are critical of pronatalist bias, of the many cognitive and cultural forces that tilt the reproductive scales before a person has reasoned at all. We are not critical of parents, of children, or of the choice to have children. A well-reasoned decision to become a parent is among the most defensible choices a human being can make. The target is never the choice; it is the bias, the systematic distortion that operates upstream of the choice and can push it around without the chooser's knowledge or consent. Naming a distortion is not an argument against what lies on the other side of it. It is an argument for choosing with clearer eyes. A person who understands exactly how the endowment framing works and still chooses a child has lost nothing this book would want to protect; they have gained the thing it exists to offer. We proceed by steelmanning first. Chapter 2 gives the strongest, most sympathetic version of the natalist intuition that an imagined child is already, in some meaningful sense, yours, and grants it real force before any critique begins. Only then do we trace where the endowment framing comes from (Chapter 3), how it operates in ordinary modern life through naming, imagining, and planning (Chapter 4), and what the evidence from behavioral economics and psychology actually shows and does not show (Chapter 5). We then examine the hidden costs of the bias (Chapter 6), who bears its pressure most heavily (Chapter 7), and the serious objections to our own thesis (Chapter 8), because a book about bias that does not scrutinize itself has learned nothing from its subject. Chapters 9 through 12 turn to illustrations, adjacent biases, the practical work of seeing past the effect, and a concluding reckoning. A word on scope, because this library is built on staying in lane. This is not a book about sunk cost, the pull of past investment already made, which is the subject of Book 005. Nor is it a general treatment of loss aversion across all reproductive framings. It is specifically about ownership and endowment of the imagined child: the way mere possession, even purely mental possession, of a not-yet-existent person inflates that person's felt value and converts a forgone gain into a felt loss. Where the endowment effect leans on a broader "life feels incomplete without" narrative, that is the completeness bias treated in Book 040, and we mark the boundary rather than crossing it. Keeping these engines separate is not pedantry. Each answers to a different corrective question, and a reader who cannot tell which one is running cannot apply the right remedy. The claims here are proportionate. The endowment effect is one of the most replicated findings in behavioral economics, but its application to imagined children is an inference by analogy, not a settled experimental result, and we say so wherever it matters. What we can say with confidence is that the mechanisms are real, the linguistic traces are everywhere, and the direction of the distortion is predictable. What we cannot say is exactly how large the effect is in any given life. That honesty is the point. The aim of this volume is not to talk anyone out of a child. It is to make sure that when the felt sense of loss arrives, as it does, powerfully, for many people contemplating a childless future, the reader can ask a clarifying question: Am I losing something I have, or declining something I could acquire? The answer changes what the feeling is worth. ## 1. Naming the bias The endowment effect is the tendency to value something more highly simply because one owns it. In the classic demonstration, people given a coffee mug demand roughly twice as much to sell it as an equivalent group is willing to pay to buy the same mug (Kahneman, Knetsch & Thaler, 1990). Nothing about the mug changed. What changed was the direction of the transaction: for owners, parting with the mug is a loss, and losses weigh about twice as heavily as gains of equal size (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979). The term endowment effect was coined by Richard Thaler (1980) to name exactly this asymmetry between the value of having and the value of getting. The experimental setup is worth dwelling on, because its very austerity is the point. There is no history with the mug. The subjects did not earn it, choose it, or grow attached to it over time. They were handed it minutes before being asked to name a selling price. And yet the mere fact of holding it, of it being theirs rather than the experimenter's, roughly doubled its felt worth. This is what makes the endowment effect so strange and so instructive: it needs no biography, no investment, no love. It needs only the bare status of ownership. If a randomly assigned mug can double in value the instant it becomes "mine," we should not be surprised that a richly imagined child, dwelt upon for years, might undergo a far larger revaluation once the mind has filed it under things possessed. This book applies that finding to a strange object: a child who does not exist. The thesis, in one sentence, is that when an imagined future child comes to be experienced as already "owned," through naming, picturing, or planning, the decision not to have that child is recoded from forgoing a possible gain into losing a present possession, and the resulting loss aversion inflates the pull toward reproduction beyond what a clear reckoning of costs and benefits would warrant. Let me be precise about what makes this this bias and not a neighbor. The endowment effect here is not about anything you have already spent. If you have spent years, money, or emotional labor in pursuit of a child, and that history now tugs at you, that is the sunk-cost bias of Book 005, the pull of irrecoverable past investment. The endowment effect is different, and in a way stranger: it does not require that you have invested anything. It requires only that you have come to regard the imagined child as yours. Ownership, not history, is the operative variable. You can feel you own something you were handed a moment ago and paid nothing for. That is what the mug experiments show, and it is why the two biases, though they often travel together, must be pried apart. Nor is this the same as the completeness narrative of Book 040, which says a life without children is a life with a hole in it. That is a story about your incompleteness. The endowment effect is a story about the child's status: it is not that you lack something, but that a particular someone, pictured, named, half-real, is being relinquished. The two often travel together and reinforce each other, but they are distinct engines, and conflating them muddies both. Completeness locates the deficiency in the person; endowment locates a possession under threat. One says I am not whole; the other says someone of mine is being given up. We keep to the endowment engine: the felt possession of an imagined person. And it is broader than general loss aversion. Loss aversion is the raw asymmetry: losses hurt more than equal gains please. The endowment effect is the specific mechanism by which loss aversion gets triggered in the reproductive case. It is the step that reclassifies a nonexistent child as a possession, so that the loss-aversion machinery has something to grip. Without the endowment step, a childless future is a foregone gain, and foregone gains, however large, do not sting the way losses do. With the endowment step, the same future becomes a bereavement. Loss aversion is the engine; endowment is the ignition. The book is about the ignition, the precise moment at which a possible person is reclassified as an owned one and the whole loss-averse apparatus roars into motion. The distinction matters practically. If the pull you feel is genuine sunk cost, the clarifying question is about the past ("Should money already spent govern a forward-looking choice?"). If it is the completeness story, the question is about your own life's shape. But if it is the endowment effect, the question is about ownership status: have you, without noticing, granted yourself title to a person who has not been conceived? Naming which engine is running is the first act of seeing past it, and this book is about one engine only. To hand a person the wrong corrective, to answer an ownership distortion with an argument about completeness, is to leave the real machinery untouched. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 1 Quiz. 1. The strongest case for it (steelman) Before we criticize anything, we must state the natalist intuition in its most compelling form, because it is far stronger than a caricature of "irrational attachment to imaginary people" would suggest. Grant the full case first. A critique that has not first inhabited the position it means to examine is not a critique but a dismissal, and dismissal teaches nothing. Begin with the observation that human beings do not relate to their futures as neutral menus of options. We relate to them through commitment, through the projects, people, and possibilities we have taken up as our own. A person who has decided to become a musician does not experience giving up music as merely declining one career among many; they experience it as losing a self. This is not a bug. It is how meaning works. To have a life at all is to have staked some of it, in advance, on futures not yet realized. The imagined child, on this view, is not a fantasy mistaken for a fact. It is a commitment, an anticipated relationship that the prospective parent has already begun to inhabit, and the felt ownership is simply what commitment feels like from the inside. On this reading, to demand that a person treat their intended child as a mere "foregone gain" is to demand that they relate to their own future as a stranger would, which is not clearer thinking but a kind of self-alienation. Consider the strongest version. Many people who want children report that the wanting is not abstract. They can picture the specific weight of a child on the hip, the particular texture of a family they intend to build. Psychologists of psychological ownership (Pierce and colleagues) have documented that people form genuine feelings of possession toward targets they have invested attention in, shaped, or come to know intimately, and this can attach to intended and anticipated targets as readily as to present ones. On this account, the prospective parent who feels they already have a child is not committing an error. They are exhibiting the ordinary human capacity to bond with a future they are building. To dismiss that bond as mere framing is to dismiss the very thing that makes long-term human projects possible. The engineer who feels the bridge is "hers" before a girder is raised, the writer who feels the novel is "his" while it is still notes, are not confused; they are committed, and commitment routinely runs ahead of existence. There is an ethical version of the steelman too. Some hold that a future child one intends to bring into being has a claim on one, that the intention itself creates something like a relationship with an obligation attached. On this view, "the child we would have had" is not a phantom but a person-to-be toward whom one has already assumed a stance of care, and to abandon that stance lightly would be a moral loss rather than a psychological one. Whatever one thinks of its metaphysics, this view takes the felt sense of ownership seriously as tracking a real moral fact, and it deserves to be met on its merits rather than explained away. It is not obvious that the intuition is wrong. Many of our most defensible moral relations, to future generations, to projects we have promised to complete, bind us to entities that do not yet exist, and the intended child may belong to that honorable company rather than to the catalog of cognitive errors. And there is a pragmatic version. Suppose the endowment framing is, strictly, a bias. It may still be an adaptive one. Reproduction is arduous, expensive, and easy to defer indefinitely; a cool cost-benefit ledger, honestly kept, might counsel "not yet" forever. A mind that pre-loads the imagined child with the weight of a possession may be a mind that actually completes hard, valuable, long-horizon projects that a purely rational agent would perpetually postpone. On this reading, the endowment effect is not a distortion to be corrected but a commitment device that rescues us from our own procrastination, and many people who are glad they had children credit this felt pull with getting them over the threshold of a decision they would otherwise have deferred into regret. The bias, in other words, may be doing us a favor, binding us to a good that our unaided deliberation is too timid or too easily distracted to reach. Finally, and most simply: people who feel they already "have" the child they intend are often right in the only sense that matters. They go on to have that child, and the felt ownership was continuous with a love that turned out to be real. The bias, if it is one, frequently coincides with a good outcome. Any honest critique must reckon with the fact that the feeling under examination is, for a great many people, the leading edge of one of the best decisions of their lives. We will hold all of this in view. The chapters that follow do not deny the force of these arguments; they ask a narrower question, whether the specific mechanism of felt ownership tracks the reasons a person actually has, or substitutes for them. That is the only crack in this otherwise formidable wall, and it is the crack the rest of the book works in. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 2 Quiz. 1. Where it comes from The endowment effect toward imagined children is not a modern invention, but modernity has sharpened it. Its roots run through evolution, culture, religion, and, surprisingly, the recent history of how we plan. To understand why the feeling arrives with such force, and why it feels so much like simple truth rather than like a framing, we have to see the layered machinery that produces it. Start with the deep structure. Loss aversion is plausibly ancient. An organism that treats the loss of what it has as more urgent than the acquisition of more is an organism that survives lean seasons; in an environment where resources sit near the margin of survival, the asymmetry is protective. Losing the food you hold can mean death; failing to gain a little more rarely does. Natural selection would reliably favor minds that guard what they have more fiercely than they chase what they lack. The endowment effect is loss aversion with a status marker attached, a way of tagging certain things as mine and defending them accordingly. When this tagging system, evolved to protect food, territory, and kin, is turned on the mental image of one's own offspring, it operates as designed: offspring are the paradigm case of the thing evolution most "wants" defended. It would be strange if the mind did not tag an imagined child as an ownable, defensible good. The very intensity of the feeling is a clue to its ancient provenance; it has the non-negotiable quality of the drives selection built to keep us alive. Culture layers meaning onto this substrate. In many traditions, children are framed explicitly as possessions, inheritances, or extensions of the self: "my line," "my blood," "carrying on the name." The genealogical imagination treats descendants as property of the lineage before they are born; a person is said to "owe" the family an heir, which presupposes that the unborn heir is already, in some ledger, on the books. Where children are economic assets (labor, old-age security, a stake in the future), the anticipated child is quite literally an anticipated possession, and the endowment framing is not metaphor but accounting. Even as those material functions have faded in wealthy societies, the linguistic and emotional habits they bred persist. We inherit the grammar of possession long after the economic facts that justified it have dissolved, and the grammar continues to shape the feeling even when it no longer matches the world. Religious and metaphysical narratives deepen the sense of prior ownership. Traditions that speak of souls waiting to be born, of children as gifts entrusted to particular parents, or of a destined family, all supply a picture in which the child exists in some anteroom of being and is assigned to you before conception. To decline is then not to leave a gain untaken but to turn away one who was coming to you, a far heavier act. One need not hold any such belief for its cultural residue to shape the felt texture of the decision. The imagery of the child "meant for you," of the soul waiting its turn, saturates ordinary speech and sentiment even among the wholly secular, and it does its quiet work by presupposing that the child already, in some register of reality, belongs to you and is merely waiting to be received. Then there is the distinctly modern accelerant: the culture of planning and pre-attachment. Contemporary prospective parents are invited, often years in advance, to imagine in high resolution. Baby-name lists are curated before conception. Nurseries are envisioned; futures are Pinterest-boarded; the imagined child acquires a gender, a temperament, a first day of school. Fertility technology and its language of "your future baby" extend the horizon of ownership backward, sometimes to frozen embryos explicitly described as one's children-in-waiting. Each of these practices is a mechanism for pre-possession, for converting a possible person into an already-inhabited one. Where an earlier era might have left the future child a vague hope, the modern apparatus renders it in specifics, and specificity is the raw material of ownership. The endowment effect needs an owned object to work on; modern reproductive culture is, among other things, an efficient factory for manufacturing exactly that object well before any child exists. None of this makes the resulting feeling false or shameful. It makes it explicable, the predictable output of an ancient asymmetry, a possessive cultural vocabulary, metaphysical narratives of prior assignment, and a modern industry of vivid pre-attachment, all converging on the same imagined person. Four independent forces, evolutionary, economic, religious, and technological, push in the same direction, which is precisely why the feeling arrives with the weight of the obvious. Understanding the sources is not debunking; it is the beginning of being able to ask whether the feeling, having arrived, tracks anything one would endorse on reflection. A feeling with this many upstream manufacturers deserves, not distrust exactly, but a second look before it is allowed to decide. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 3 Quiz. 1. How it operates today The endowment effect toward an imagined child does not announce itself. It works through ordinary, benign-seeming acts that quietly move a possible person into the category of the owned. Three mechanisms do most of the labor: naming, picturing, and planning. Each is an ordinary expression of hope, and each is, at the same time, a small act of taking title. Naming. To name is to individuate, and to individuate is to create a particular someone who can be present or absent, kept or lost. A couple who have chosen "Eleanor" for the daughter they intend no longer face the abstract question of whether to add a child to their lives; they face the question of whether to have Eleanor, and the abstract "a child" and the particular "Eleanor" are not psychologically equivalent. One is a category; the other is a person with a name, and named things are ownable in a way categories are not. The name is a small act of title. Anthropologists and parents alike know that naming is never neutral: to give a name is to summon a particular being into the social world, to make a place at the table. This is why the language of loss so readily attaches to the named-but-unconceived: once there is an Eleanor, there is an Eleanor to lose. The unnamed possibility can be declined; the named person can only be relinquished. Picturing. The imagination does not sketch; it renders. When a prospective parent pictures the family, the specific scene, the child's face, the ordinary Tuesday morning, they are not entertaining a hypothesis but rehearsing a memory in advance. Psychological ownership research suggests that intimate acquaintance with a target, even an imagined one, breeds a sense of possession; the more vividly and repeatedly one pictures the child, the more one knows them, and the more one knows them, the more they are experienced as one's own. Here the IKEA effect (Norton, Mochon & Ariely) supplies a useful parallel: people value objects they have assembled with their own effort far above identical objects they merely acquired, because the labor of construction creates felt ownership. The mental labor of constructing an imagined child, the hours of picturing, wondering, planning, is effort of exactly this kind. We build the child in imagination, and having built them, we value them as builders value what they have made. Declining then feels like demolishing something one assembled by hand. And because the construction is entirely internal, invisible even to the builder, its product is mistaken for a discovery rather than recognized as a making. Planning. Every concrete preparation is a stake driven into the future that makes the imagined child more real and more owned. The spare room designated as "the nursery." The career timed around a maternity leave. The savings earmarked "for the baby." Each of these is a way of arranging present life around the anticipated person, and arrangement is a form of possession: we possess by making room. A guest for whom a room has been prepared, a chair set at the table, a name written on the door, is no longer a mere possibility but an expected presence whose non-arrival would be felt as a gap. The more life is organized around the child, the more the child is installed, and the more its non-arrival reads as an eviction of someone already housed. Planning turns intention into infrastructure, and infrastructure, once built, exerts its own pull toward being used. Language is the connective tissue running through all three. Note how naturally people say "our future kids" (a possessive), "the child we want" (a specific object of desire), "when we have children" (assumed acquisition), and, tellingly, "we decided not to have them" or "we gave up on having a baby," the verbs of relinquishment, of parting with, of giving up, which one uses for possessions, not for opportunities never taken. No one says "we declined to acquire a hypothetical person." The available idioms are ownership idioms, and idioms shape thought. A language that has ready words for losing a child but none for declining to acquire one will make the first framing feel natural and the second feel cold, and most people will reach, without deliberation, for the words their language hands them. These mechanisms are not manipulative and usually not deliberate. They are the ordinary furniture of a hopeful life. That is what makes the endowment effect hard to see: the very acts through which people express legitimate desire for a child, naming, imagining, preparing, are the same acts that manufacture the felt ownership that later distorts the decision. The bias hides inside the hope. One cannot simply be told to stop hoping, nor should one be; the point is only that the hopeful acts have a side effect, and that the side effect, once recognized, need not be mistaken for evidence about what is truly at stake. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 4 Quiz. 1. The evidence What does the empirical record actually support, and where does honest inference run out? We should be careful here, because the strength of this book's core claim varies sharply across its links. A chain is only as strong as its weakest link, and the honest way to present a chain is to grade each link separately rather than to let the strength of the first disguise the weakness of the last. The foundation is solid. The endowment effect itself is among the most robustly replicated findings in behavioral economics. The mug experiments of Kahneman, Knetsch, and Thaler (1990), showing a large gap between willingness-to-accept and willingness-to-pay for the same object once it is "owned," have been reproduced across many contexts and goods. Thaler's (1980) original identification of the effect and its grounding in the loss aversion of prospect theory (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979) are canonical. That mere ownership inflates value, and that this inflation traces to losses weighing more than equivalent gains, is about as secure as findings in this field get. It is worth adding a note of scholarly candor here: the endowment effect, like much of behavioral economics, has faced the scrutiny of the replication era, and researchers have argued about its boundary conditions, whether it requires genuine ownership or merely the expectation of exchange, how much is explained by transaction reluctance rather than valuation per se. But the core phenomenon has survived that scrutiny robustly. We are standing on tested ground when we say ownership inflates value. The second link is well supported but more qualified. Psychological ownership, the documented tendency to feel possession toward targets one has controlled, come to know, or invested oneself in (Pierce and colleagues), extends the endowment idea beyond legal ownership to felt ownership, and it shows that the feeling can attach to targets that are not straightforwardly "owned" in any formal sense. The IKEA effect (Norton, Mochon & Ariely) similarly demonstrates that effortful construction inflates valuation. These findings license the claim that ownership feelings arise from attention, knowledge, and effortful engagement rather than from legal title, which is what the application to imagined children requires. The qualification is that most of this research was conducted on ordinary objects and organizational targets, not on imagined persons, so extending it to the reproductive case is a reasonable generalization rather than a direct measurement. We are on ground that has been tested, but not on the exact terrain we mean to cross. The third link is where we must be candid: there is, to this author's knowledge, no direct experimental literature measuring an endowment effect specifically toward imagined future children. The application is an inference by analogy. It is a reasonable inference, since the mechanisms (loss aversion, felt ownership from mental effort, valuation shifts from naming and picturing) are all independently documented, and there is no obvious reason the machinery would fail to engage when the object is a mentally constructed child. But it is an inference, not a measured result, and intellectual honesty requires flagging it as such. Anyone who tells you the endowment effect toward imagined children has been quantified is overstating the evidence. We are extending a well-mapped mechanism into unmapped territory on the strength of good theory, which is a legitimate and common move in the human sciences, but it is not the same as having walked the ground. What we do have around the edges is suggestive. Demographic and psychological research on the wide, well-documented gap between desired and achieved fertility, on decision regret, and on the reframing of reproductive choices in the language of gain and loss, is all consistent with an ownership-and-loss-aversion account, but consistency is not confirmation, and each of these bodies of work admits other explanations. The affective-forecasting literature (people are systematically poor at predicting how future states will feel) bears on the reliability of the vivid imagined child, but does not by itself establish the endowment mechanism. A responsible reader should treat these adjacent findings as circumstantial support that raises the plausibility of the thesis without settling it. They tell us the account is not idle, that phenomena of roughly the predicted shape appear in the world, while stopping short of proving that endowment is the mechanism producing them. Why, then, advance the thesis at all, if the crucial link is untested? Because a well-motivated hypothesis with strong theoretical grounding and real predictive traction is a legitimate instrument of thought, provided it is labeled as such. The endowment account predicts specific things: that vividness of pre-attachment should track intensity of felt loss, that named imagined children should be relinquished with more difficulty than unnamed possibilities, that the language people reach for should be ownership language. These predictions are testable, and the account earns its keep by generating them. What it may not do is masquerade as settled science. The measured takeaway is therefore layered. It is a fact that ownership inflates value and that losses outweigh gains. It is well established that felt ownership arises from mental engagement rather than legal title. It is a plausible, mechanism-grounded, but not directly tested inference that these combine to make an imagined child feel owned and its non-arrival feel like a loss. We assert the first two as findings and the third as a hypothesis with strong theoretical support and real predictive traction, a hypothesis this book uses because it is useful and defensible, not because it is proven. Holding that distinction is itself part of seeing past the bias: the same discipline that asks whether a felt loss tracks a real one asks whether a plausible theory has been confirmed, and refuses to let confidence outrun evidence in either case. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 5 Quiz. 1. The hidden costs If the endowment framing quietly converts a foregone gain into a felt loss, what does that conversion cost, beyond simply making a hard decision harder? The costs are subtle because the framing feels like fidelity, like love, like taking the future seriously. But several go systematically unpriced, and their invisibility is precisely what makes them worth naming. The first cost is decision distortion. Loss aversion means the imagined child, once owned, is not weighed at its true value but at roughly double it, because letting it go is scored as a loss rather than a foregone gain. This is not a neutral thumb on the scale. It means the decision is made against a phantom price. A person may reason, honestly and carefully, about whether to have a child and still arrive at a conclusion warped by the fact that one option was priced as bereavement and the other as mere acquisition. The reasoning can be impeccable and the inputs still corrupted. This is the insidious feature of framing effects generally: they do not corrupt the logic, they corrupt the values the logic operates on, so that a person of perfect rationality fed distorted inputs will produce a distorted output while feeling, correctly, that they reasoned well. The second cost is the foreclosure of the actual comparison. A clear reproductive choice compares two possible futures: one with a child, one without, each with its real goods and real burdens. The endowment framing collapses this symmetry. The child-ful future is represented by a warm, named, pictured, half-owned person; the child-free future is represented by an absence, the loss of that person, a hole where Eleanor was. But that is a rigged comparison. The child-free future is not a hole; it is itself a full life with its own goods, its own relationships, its own freedoms and depths and uses of time, which the framing renders as mere subtraction. The bias does worse than weight one side more heavily. It prevents the other side from being seen as a positive thing at all. One future is populated by a vivid person; the other is depicted as that person's absence, when in fairness it should be depicted as its own populated, textured life. The asymmetry of representation does the damage before any weighing begins. The third cost is misattributed grief. Many people contemplating a childless future experience something that feels exactly like mourning, and they reasonably take that feeling as evidence: surely if it hurts like losing a child, a child is what is being lost. But the endowment account suggests the grief may be partly an artifact of the ownership framing itself. One grieves because one has been led to feel one has the child, and one has been led to feel that through naming and picturing and planning. The grief is real as a sensation; its evidential value is what is in question. To treat a manufactured sense of loss as proof that something is being lost is to let the bias validate itself, to accept the feeling it produces as evidence for the claim that produced the feeling. This is not to say the grief should be dismissed or suppressed; it is real and deserves compassion. It is to say that its intensity is a poor measure of what is actually at stake, because the intensity was partly manufactured upstream. The fourth cost is the pressure toward a decision one might not endorse on reflection. Because losses demand action to prevent them in a way that foregone gains do not, the endowment framing can push people across the reproductive threshold not by persuasion but by dread, the felt need to avert a loss. A choice made to escape the pain of a manufactured loss is not obviously a freely made choice, even when nothing external coerced it. Autonomy concerns more than the absence of outside pressure. It concerns whether one's own reasons, rather than a framing artifact, are doing the deciding. A person driven to a decision by the urgency of averting a loss that the framing itself created has been moved by something other than their reasons, even if no one else laid a hand on them. The coercion, if that is the word, is internal and invisible, which makes it harder to resist than any external demand. We must state the limit of this critique carefully, because it is easy to overreach. None of these costs shows that anyone should not have children. They show that the reasons for a given decision may be less sound than they feel, and that a felt loss is a poor instrument for measuring what is genuinely at stake. The point is diagnostic, not directive. A person who names these costs and still chooses a child has lost nothing and gained clarity. The hidden costs are costs of the bias, not of the child, and the whole aim of naming them is to let a person pay the real price of their choice rather than the inflated one the framing quotes. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 6 Quiz. 1. The endowment effect toward imagined children presses hardest on those whom culture has most thoroughly trained to pre-possess a child, and those with the least social permission to reframe the childless future as anything but loss. Who feels the effect most is not a matter of temperament or weakness but of how much pre-possession one has been handed, and pre-possession is distributed by forces no individual chooses. Gender. In most cultures, women are invited into vivid pre-attachment far earlier, more intensively, and more publicly than men. The imagined child is offered to girls as a default component of the imagined self, sometimes from the age one is handed a doll. The scripts of adult womanhood are saturated with the anticipated child in a way the scripts of adult manhood, though changing, historically were not. Where the practices that manufacture felt ownership (naming, picturing, planning, the socially rehearsed fantasy of the family) are more heavily prescribed for one gender, that gender will carry a heavier endowment. The result is asymmetric: a woman contemplating childlessness is more likely to be confronting the relinquishment of a long-inhabited, richly pictured, effectively owned child, while a man may be forgoing a more abstract possibility. The felt loss is not equal because the pre-possession was not equal. And because the reproductive burden and the social scrutiny both fall more heavily on women, the manufactured loss compounds an already asymmetric pressure. Class and economics. The modern machinery of high-resolution pre-attachment (the elaborate planning, the nursery, the earmarked savings, the fertility technology with its language of "your future baby") is unevenly available and unevenly marketed. Where reproduction is heavily planned and provisioned in advance, more scaffolding is built around the imagined child, and more scaffolding means more felt ownership. At the same time, in contexts where children remain economic assets, the anticipated child is a literal anticipated possession, and the endowment framing has material as well as psychological force. The bias thus takes different forms across the economic spectrum, but few positions are exempt from some version of it. At one end, the affluent construct the child through consumption and planning; at the other, the child is anticipated as labor, security, or lineage. The vocabulary differs; the pre-possession does not. Culture and religion. Traditions that frame children as lineage property, as souls assigned before birth, or as gifts entrusted to specific parents supply ready-made ownership narratives, and those raised within them inherit a pre-possession they did not choose. The felt sense that a particular child was "meant for you" is culturally transmitted, and where it is strong, declining reads as turning away one who was coming, a far heavier act than declining an abstract possibility. Where the surrounding community also treats childlessness as failure or deviance, the person contemplating it faces their own manufactured sense of loss and, on top of it, a chorus of others insisting that a real child is being lost, which lends the private feeling an external confirmation it has not earned. Fertility status and the technological frontier. Those undergoing fertility treatment occupy an especially intense version of this pressure, because the technology's very vocabulary ("your embryos," "your future baby") grants explicit ownership language to entities described as children-in-waiting, sometimes before any pregnancy. The endowment framing is not incidental here; it is often built into how the process is presented. This deserves care rather than judgment: people in this situation are working through genuine hope and genuine loss under language that may amplify the felt ownership beyond what the situation itself warrants. The point is not that their grief is illusory, it is not, but that the institutional framing may inflate it, and that recognizing this can be a mercy rather than a further burden, because it lets a person grieve the actual situation rather than a possession the language pressed upon them. The distributive point is not that some people are more foolish than others. No one chooses the intensity of their pre-attachment; it is a function of the culture, gender scripts, economic position, and technologies one is handed. The endowment effect presses hardest where pre-possession has been most thoroughly cultivated, which means the people who feel the imagined child's loss most acutely are often those who were most systematically trained to own it in advance. Recognizing this is a matter of compassion, not blame. The heavier the manufactured endowment, the more the person deserves, not less, a clear account of what is manufactured and what is real, offered not as a correction of their feeling but as a resource for their own use. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 7 Quiz. 1. The counter-arguments to the critique A book about bias earns nothing if it exempts its own thesis from scrutiny. Several strong objections deserve a fair hearing, and each is given its best form before any reply, because a reply to a weakened objection is worth nothing. "The imagined child isn't a bias, it's the beginning of love." The most serious objection holds that felt ownership of an intended child is not a distortion but the leading edge of genuine parental attachment, continuous with the love that will follow the child's arrival. To call it a bias, the objection runs, is to pathologize the very capacity that makes good parents. This has real force, and we should concede a great deal. The feeling is often continuous with love, and for people who go on to have and cherish the child, the pre-attachment was not error but anticipation. Our reply is narrow: the concern is not with the love but with what happens when the felt ownership is used as evidence in a decision not yet made. Love that has attached to an existing child answers to that child; felt ownership of an unconceived one answers to nothing yet, and letting it decide is letting a feeling stand in for reasons. The love is not the problem. The love functioning as an argument is. A feeling can be both genuine and epistemically unreliable, and the whole of the critique lives in that gap. "Foregone gains and losses shouldn't be treated differently, but maybe the intuition that they're different is the wise one." One could argue that loss aversion, far from being a bias to correct, encodes hard-won wisdom about the irreversibility of certain choices, and that the reproductive decision, genuinely irreversible and genuinely momentous, is exactly the kind of choice where treating the option as precious and its loss as grave is appropriate. This is fair. Reproduction is not a mug. The reply is that appropriateness must come from the actual stakes, not from a framing artifact: if the decision deserves gravity, let that gravity come from a clear reckoning of what a child's life and a parent's life involve, not from the accident of having named and pictured a particular Eleanor. The critique does not ask for less seriousness; it asks that the seriousness attach to reasons rather than to manufactured possession. Gravity earned by the real stakes is exactly right; gravity borrowed from the framing is what we mean to expose. "This is just intellectualizing away a deep human desire." A blunt objection: the whole analysis is a way of talking people out of feelings they have every right to, dressing cold reasoning as insight. We take this seriously because it is partly true, since analysis can be a defense against feeling, a way of holding at arm's length what should be lived. But the aim is not to dissolve the desire; it is to distinguish the desire from the framing of its object. One can want a child intensely and still ask whether the felt loss of this pictured child is measuring the want accurately or inflating it. Naming a framing does not abolish the want beneath it. It clarifies what the want is a want for. Indeed, a want seen clearly is more truly one's own than a want one is merely swept along by, so the analysis, far from betraying the desire, may return it to the person more fully. "There's no evidence the endowment effect even applies to imagined children." This is the strongest evidentiary objection, and Chapter 5 conceded it plainly: the specific application is an inference by analogy, not a measured result. The honest reply is not to overclaim. The component mechanisms are documented; their combination in this case is a well-motivated hypothesis with predictive traction; it is not proven. A reader who therefore treats the whole account as a useful lens rather than an established fact is reading it correctly, and we do not resist that reading. We would only add that "not proven" is not "refuted," and that a lens which brings a recurrent human experience into sharper focus, and generates testable predictions while doing so, earns its place in the toolkit even before the decisive experiment is run. "Even if it's a bias, it's a useful one, leave it alone." Finally, the pragmatic objection from Chapter 2 returns: the endowment framing may be a commitment device that gets valuable, hard, long-horizon projects completed. Perhaps debiasing here does harm, producing perpetual deferral and eventual regret. This is a genuine possibility and we do not dismiss it. Our position is only that a commitment device operating below awareness is not the same as a chosen commitment, and that a person who understands the mechanism can choose to commit, can decide, eyes open, to treat the imagined child as worth pursuing, without needing the bias to smuggle the decision past them. The goal is not to disable the pull but to make it answerable to the person, rather than the other way around. A commitment one has ratified with open eyes is firmer, not weaker, than one that depended on never being examined. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 8 Quiz. 1. Case studies / illustrations Consider four illustrations, offered not as data but as recognizable shapes the bias takes in ordinary life. They are composite portraits, not case reports, and they earn their place by being recognizable rather than by being measured. The named nursery. A couple in their early thirties have, over several years of intending to conceive, done everything hopeful people do: chosen names for a boy and a girl, kept a running list of the things they will do with each, converted the spare room in their minds into a nursery. When circumstances shift and they begin to reconsider whether to have children at all, the reconsideration does not feel like weighing two open futures. It feels like a bereavement, specifically, like losing "Sam," the son they had named. Note the structure: the pain is proportional not to the abstract value of parenthood but to the particularity they had constructed. Had they never named the children, the same decision would have been a foregone possibility; having named them, it is a loss. Nothing about the actual stakes changed between the unnamed and named states, the same biology, the same finances, the same lives. Only the endowment did. The couple did not acquire a new reason to have a child by naming him; they acquired a Sam to lose, and the loss of Sam now speaks with a voice the abstract possibility never had. The fertility clinic's vocabulary. A person undergoing IVF is surrounded by language that grants ownership before there is a child: "your embryos," "your future baby," the number of "your children" waiting frozen. When a cycle fails, the grief is genuine and deserves compassion, but observe how the institutional language has pre-installed a possession, so that a failed cycle registers not as an attempt that did not succeed but as the loss of a child already spoken of as theirs. The technology's framing does real work here, amplifying felt ownership independently of what has biologically occurred. This is the endowment effect operating not through the individual's imagination alone but through an institution's chosen words. The clinic did not intend to inflate anyone's grief; the language is warm, patient-centered, meant to honor hope. But warmth and accuracy are not the same, and language that pre-grants possession will, when the possession does not materialize, exact the price of a loss for something that was never held. The remembered "phantom" child. Some people who decide, on reflection and with no regret about the decision itself, not to have children nonetheless report a recurring sense of a specific absent person, the daughter who would now be seven, imagined at each birthday she does not have. This is the endowment effect's afterimage: a child fully constructed in imagination retains its felt ownership even after the decision, so that its non-existence is experienced as an ongoing absence rather than a mere fact. The phenomenon is worth naming because it can be mistaken for proof of a wrong choice, when it may instead be the residue of a vivid pre-possession the mind built and cannot fully un-build. The mind is better at constructing than at demolishing; having assembled a person in imagination, it retains the assembly, and the retained figure haunts the years it would have occupied. To feel this afterimage is not to have chosen wrongly. It is to have built well, in a faculty that does not easily unbuild. The reframed conversation. Contrast two ways the same person might describe the same decision. First: "We thought about whether to add a child to our lives and decided the life we have is the one we want." Second: "We gave up on the baby we would have had." The facts are identical; the framings are not. The first treats the child as a foregone possibility and the child-free life as a positive good. The second treats the child as an owned possession relinquished and the child-free life as a hole. People move between these framings without noticing, and which one is active can change whether the same choice feels like a considered preference or a loss to be mourned. The illustration is not that one framing is "correct." It is that the difference between them is doing enormous emotional work while masquerading as mere phrasing. A person who catches themselves sliding from the first into the second has caught the bias in the act, in the space of a single reworded sentence. None of these cases argues against having children, and none impugns anyone's grief, which is real whatever its source. They illustrate one thing: how readily a not-yet-existent person is installed as an owned one, and how that installation, not the underlying stakes, generates the felt loss. Seen side by side, they trace a single pattern in four settings, the private imagination, the institution, the aftermath, and the very words, and the pattern is always the same: possession precedes and produces the loss. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 9 Quiz. 1. Adjacent biases The endowment effect toward imagined children does not act alone. It interlocks with several other biases in this series, and mapping the seams both clarifies its boundaries and shows why it can feel so overdetermined. The value of the map lies in the connections it draws and equally in the borders it insists on, because a concept that swallows its neighbors explains nothing. Its nearest neighbor is sunk cost (Book 005). The two are easily confused because both make it hard to walk away, but they run on opposite fuel. Sunk cost is powered by the past, by investment already made and unrecoverable, which the mind refuses to write off. The endowment effect is powered by present felt ownership, which requires no past investment at all. You can feel you own an imagined child you have done nothing to pursue. In practice the two often stack: years of trying (sunk cost) and years of naming and picturing (endowment) reinforce each other, and a person deep in a fertility journey may be held by both at once, the labor already spent and the child already owned pulling in the same direction. But they are separable, and the clarifying questions differ, the past for one, ownership status for the other. This book deliberately isolates the endowment engine; where the pull is genuinely about past investment, Book 005 is the relevant volume. Its most frequent companion is the completeness narrative (Book 040), the story that a life without children is incomplete, missing a necessary part. Endowment and completeness are distinct but mutually reinforcing: completeness says you lack something; endowment says a particular someone is being relinquished. Together they produce a compound pressure, you are both incomplete and bereaved, that is far heavier than either alone, and their fusion is so seamless that most people experience it as a single undifferentiated ache. Keeping them separate matters because they answer to different corrections: completeness is addressed by examining the assumption that a full life requires children; endowment is addressed by examining whether you have granted title to a person who does not exist. Apply the wrong remedy and the ache persists, because the untreated half is still doing its work. The endowment effect also draws on loss aversion as its power source, the general asymmetry by which losses outweigh equivalent gains. But where a general treatment of loss aversion in reproduction would range across every gain-versus-loss framing, the endowment effect is the specific mechanism that creates the loss by first creating the possession. It is the step that gives loss aversion something to grip. Loss aversion is the standing readiness to feel losses acutely; endowment is what converts a childless future into a loss for that readiness to seize. It connects, too, to biases of anticipated regret and to the affective-forecasting errors by which people mispredict future feelings: the vividly imagined, owned child is precisely the sort of high-resolution but unreliable forecast that these biases trade on. A person who pictures, with painful clarity, the regret they will feel at never having had Eleanor is forecasting the feelings of a self that does not yet exist about a child that does not yet exist, a doubly unreliable projection that the endowment framing lends a spurious concreteness. And it shares a border with status quo bias in reverse: for those who assume children as their default trajectory, not having them is the deviation, and the endowment framing supplies the felt content of what the deviation would cost. Where children are the assumed path, childlessness is coded as departure from an owned future, and departure from what one "has" is exactly the territory loss aversion patrols. The methodological point of this chapter is restraint. It would be easy to fold all these biases into one grand theory of pronatalist pull, but that would sacrifice the precision the series is built on. A single grand theory feels powerful and explains nothing, because a cause invoked for everything predicts nothing in particular. The endowment effect is one identifiable engine, felt ownership of an imagined person, and its value as a concept depends on not letting it swallow its neighbors. We mark the connections and keep the lanes. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 10 Quiz. 1. Seeing past it What does clear-eyed reasoning about an imagined child look like, once the endowment effect is named? Not colder reasoning, and not the suppression of desire, but reasoning in which the felt sense of ownership is recognized for what it is and asked to justify itself rather than to decide by default. The goal is not to feel less but to know what the feeling is, so that it can inform the choice without silently making it. The core move is a single clarifying question, asked whenever the pull toward reproduction arrives with the flavor of loss: Am I losing something I have, or declining something I could acquire? This is not a rhetorical trick. It is a genuine diagnostic, because the two are structurally different and the mind systematically misfiles the second as the first. An imagined child is, by definition, not possessed; it does not exist. Whatever is felt as its loss is therefore, strictly, a foregone possible gain dressed in the clothing of a loss. Naming this does not make the desire disappear; it strips the desire of a distortion, so that what remains is the actual want, weighable at its actual value. The question works not by argument but by relocation: it moves the imagined child from the ledger of things held back to the ledger of things available, where it belongs, and where it can be weighed without the doubling that loss aversion imposes. For individuals, several practices follow. One is to notice the manufacturing acts as they happen, the naming, the picturing, the planning, not to stop them, but to hold them lightly, aware that they are building felt ownership as a side effect of hope. To name a child and know, even as one does it, that the name is an act of title is to keep the door open in a way that naming unawares does not. Another is to run the comparison symmetrically: to represent the child-free future not as a hole where a named child was, but as a full life with its own real goods, described in as much positive detail as the imagined child receives. The bias thrives on an asymmetry of vividness, a richly pictured child against a blank absence, and the correction is to picture both, or neither, with equal care, to give the child-free life a face, a texture, a set of ordinary Tuesday mornings of its own. A third practice is to distinguish the sensation of grief from its evidential value, to be able to say, "This feels like mourning, and the feeling is real, and it is also partly an artifact of how vividly I have pre-possessed this child, so I will not treat the intensity of the feeling as a measurement of what is truly at stake.

① In brief

Book 006 · Endowment Effects and Imagined Children

This volume examines a specific pronatalist distortion: the way a not-yet-existent future child can come to feel like a possession, so that declining to have that child registers not as forgoing a possible gain but as losing something one already owns. The mechanism is the endowment effect, the well-replicated finding that people value a thing more highly simply because they regard it as theirs (Thaler, 1980; Kahneman, Knetsch & Thaler, 1990), combined with loss aversion, the asymmetry by which losses weigh about twice as heavily as equivalent gains (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979). When these two forces are turned on an imagined child, the felt pull toward reproduction is quietly inflated beyond what a clear reckoning would warrant.

The book stays deliberately in its lane. It is not about sunk cost, the pull of past, unrecoverable investment (Book 005), which runs on the past rather than on present felt ownership. Nor is it the completeness narrative (Book 040), which says a life without children is incomplete; that story concerns your lack, whereas the endowment effect concerns a particular someone's felt relinquishment. The two often stack, but the volume keeps them separate to preserve the series' precision.

Read the rest of the overview

Following the series method, Chapter 2 steelmans the natalist intuition fully before any critique: felt ownership of an intended child is often continuous with genuine love, may work as a valuable commitment device that rescues a momentous choice from endless deferral, and frequently coincides with a decision the person is glad to have made. These are granted real force.

The critique then proceeds carefully. The bias arises from ancient loss-aversion asymmetries, possessive cultural vocabularies ("my line," "our future kids"), metaphysical narratives of souls assigned before birth, and a distinctly modern industry of vivid pre-attachment: baby-name lists, nurseries envisioned before conception, fertility technology's language of "your future baby." It operates through three ordinary acts: naming (which individuates a losable someone), picturing (which, like the IKEA effect, breeds ownership through the mental labor of construction), and planning (which installs the child by arranging life around it).

On evidence, the book is candid. The endowment effect itself is rock-solid, and psychological-ownership research shows felt ownership can arise from attention and effort rather than legal title. But the specific application to imagined children is an inference by analogy, not a directly measured result: a well-motivated hypothesis with predictive traction, not a proven law. This honesty is treated as part of the argument, not an aside.

The hidden costs are diagnostic rather than directive: the imagined child is priced at roughly double its true value; the child-free future is rendered as a mere hole rather than a full life; grief may be partly an artifact of manufactured ownership; and people may be pushed across the reproductive threshold by dread of a manufactured loss rather than by their own reasons. The pressure falls hardest on those most trained to pre-possess a child, shaped by gender scripts, economic position, cultural narratives, and fertility technology, which is a matter for compassion, not blame.

The book scrutinizes its own thesis (the imagined child as the beginning of love; loss aversion as appropriate gravity for an irreversible choice; the charge of intellectualizing away desire; the thin direct evidence; the "useful bias" objection) and answers each without overclaiming. Its constructive core is a single clarifying question to carry into any decision that arrives with the flavor of loss: Am I losing something I have, or declining something I could acquire? Asking it dissolves the misfiling without dissolving the desire, and is fully compatible with choosing to have children, eyes open. The aim is not to move anyone toward children or away from them, but to return the choice, clear-eyed, to the person whose life it is.

CH 1Naming the bias

The endowment effect is the tendency to value something more highly simply because one owns it. In the classic demonstration, people given a coffee mug demand roughly twice as much to sell it as an equivalent group is willing to pay to buy the same mug (Kahneman, Knetsch & Thaler, 1990). Nothing about the mug changed. What changed was the direction of the transaction: for owners, parting with the mug is a loss, and losses weigh about twice as heavily as gains of equal size (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979). The term endowment effect was coined by Richard Thaler (1980) to name exactly this asymmetry between the value of having and the value of getting.

CH 3Where it comes from

The endowment effect toward imagined children is not a modern invention, but modernity has sharpened it. Its roots run through evolution, culture, religion, and, surprisingly, the recent history of how we plan. To understand why the feeling arrives with such force, and why it feels so much like simple truth rather than like a framing, we have to see the layered machinery that produces it.

CH 4How it operates today

The endowment effect toward an imagined child does not announce itself. It works through ordinary, benign-seeming acts that quietly move a possible person into the category of the owned. Three mechanisms do most of the labor: naming, picturing, and planning. Each is an ordinary expression of hope, and each is, at the same time, a small act of taking title.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

What does the empirical record actually support, and where does honest inference run out? We should be careful here, because the strength of this book's core claim varies sharply across its links. A chain is only as strong as its weakest link, and the honest way to present a chain is to grade each link separately rather than to let the strength of the first disguise the weakness of the last.

CH 6The hidden costs

If the endowment framing quietly converts a foregone gain into a felt loss, what does that conversion cost, beyond simply making a hard decision harder? The costs are subtle because the framing feels like fidelity, like love, like taking the future seriously. But several go systematically unpriced, and their invisibility is precisely what makes them worth naming.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

Biases are not distributed evenly. The endowment effect toward imagined children presses hardest on those whom culture has most thoroughly trained to pre-possess a child, and those with the least social permission to reframe the childless future as anything but loss. Who feels the effect most is not a matter of temperament or weakness but of how much pre-possession one has been handed, and pre-possession is distributed by forces no individual chooses.

A book about bias earns nothing if it exempts its own thesis from scrutiny. Several strong objections deserve a fair hearing, and each is given its best form before any reply, because a reply to a weakened objection is worth nothing.

CH 10Adjacent biases

The endowment effect toward imagined children does not act alone. It interlocks with several other biases in this series, and mapping the seams both clarifies its boundaries and shows why it can feel so overdetermined. The value of the map lies in the connections it draws and equally in the borders it insists on, because a concept that swallows its neighbors explains nothing.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

What does clear-eyed reasoning about an imagined child look like, once the endowment effect is named? Not colder reasoning, and not the suppression of desire, but reasoning in which the felt sense of ownership is recognized for what it is and asked to justify itself rather than to decide by default. The goal is not to feel less but to know what the feeling is, so that it can inform the choice without silently making it.

CH 12Conclusion

The argument of this book has been narrow and, I hope, proportionate. The endowment effect, the well-established tendency to value what we regard as ours more highly than what we might acquire, can attach to a child who does not yet exist. Through the ordinary acts of hope, naming, picturing, planning, a possible person is installed as an owned one, and once installed, declining that child is recoded from forgoing a possible gain into losing a present possession. Because losses loom roughly twice as large as equivalent gains, this recoding quietly inflates the felt pull toward reproduction, and it does so below awareness, wearing the face of love and fidelity rather than of distortion. The whole of the mechanism can be stated in a sentence: possession, even purely mental possession, converts a foregone gain into a felt loss, and the felt loss then decides.

③ Key terms

Naming a mechanism is most of seeing it. Tap a card to flip it.

1 / 16
Show all 16 terms as a list
Endowment effect
The tendency to value a thing more highly simply because one owns or possesses it; owners demand more to give a thing up than non-owners will pay to acquire it (Thaler, 1980; Kahneman, Knetsch & Thaler, 1990).
Loss aversion
The asymmetry, central to prospect theory, by which losses weigh roughly twice as heavily as gains of equal magnitude (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979).
Reference point
The baseline against which outcomes are coded as gains or losses; shifting it can turn the same outcome from a gain into a loss.
Psychological ownership
A felt sense of possession toward a target, arising from control, intimate knowledge, or investment of the self, independent of legal ownership (Pierce and colleagues).
IKEA effect
The inflation of value people place on objects they have partly constructed through their own effort, relative to identical objects merely acquired (Norton, Mochon & Ariely).
Imagined child
A not-yet-existent, often specific future child constructed in the mind through naming, picturing, and planning; the object on which the endowment effect operates here.
Pre-possession
The conversion of a possible future person into an already-inhabited, felt-as-owned one, before that person exists.
Willingness-to-accept / willingness-to-pay gap
The empirical gap between what owners demand to sell a good and what buyers will pay for it; a standard measure of the endowment effect.
Foregone gain
A benefit one could have acquired but did not; psychologically distinct from, and less painful than, a loss of equal magnitude.
Felt ownership
Ownership experienced subjectively rather than held legally; the psychological state the endowment effect requires to operate.
Manufactured loss
A felt loss produced by framing an option as owned, rather than by any actual possession being taken away.
Commitment device
A mechanism (possibly the endowment framing itself) that binds one to a course of action one might otherwise defer or abandon.
Affective forecasting
Predicting one's future emotional states; often inaccurate, bearing on the reliability of the vividly imagined future child.
Sunk cost
The pull of past, unrecoverable investment on a forward-looking decision; distinct from endowment (past investment vs. present ownership); the subject of Book 005.
Completeness narrative
The belief that a life without children is incomplete; distinct from but frequently paired with the endowment effect; the subject of Book 040.
Status quo bias
The preference for the current or default state; for those who assume children as their default, not having them reads as the deviation whose cost the endowment framing supplies.
