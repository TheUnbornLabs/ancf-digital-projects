BIAS 053

Who Will Pay the Pensions?

Part V · Economic, Demographic & Policy Pronatalism

The bias: The claim that we must reproduce to fund the retirements of the old.

The idea that a society must have children to be solvent confuses an accounting arrangement with a law of nature.

① In brief

100 Biases of Natalism — Part V: Economic & Demographic Framings

Of all the arguments for having children, the pension argument sounds the most hard-headed. It appeals not to instinct or scripture but to arithmetic: someone must work when you are old, someone must staff the wards and pay the taxes out of which your pension is drawn, and if your generation does not produce enough children there will be too few workers to support you. Reproduction, in this telling, is retirement insurance for society, and the childless are cast as free-riders who draw on a system of intergenerational support to which they declined to contribute the one thing it truly needs.

This book examines the pension-provision bias: the treatment of personal or collective reproduction as the necessary remedy for pension strain, and the moralizing of that supposed remedy into a duty the childless are said to shirk. The bias is not the observation that populations are aging, nor that pension systems face real pressure; both are true, and the book grants them plainly. The bias is the unearned slide from "our pension system depends on a certain ratio of workers to retirees" to "therefore we, or you, must have more children."

Read the rest of the overview

At the heart of the argument sits a specific financial arrangement: the pay-as-you-go (PAYG) pension, in which today's workers' contributions are paid straight out to today's retirees, on the understanding that tomorrow's workers will fund them in turn. This structure, elegant when each generation is larger than the last, straining when growth stops, is what manufactures the sense that a society must keep breeding to stay solvent. The imperative is a feature of a chosen financing design, not a law of nature. Change the design and the imperative dissolves.

Following the series method, Chapter 2 gives the argument its strongest and most sympathetic hearing first. The intergenerational contract, the idea that each generation supports the one before and after it in an unwritten chain of reciprocity, is a serious and even beautiful idea, and it is stated in full before any objection. The chapters that follow grant the strain honestly: aging genuinely stresses PAYG systems, and the fairness question about who bears the costs of childrearing is real. What the book contests is the inference from strain to reproduction.

That inference fails, the book argues, because reproduction is only one item on a menu of workable responses, and the worst item on the list. Later retirement (indexed to rising longevity), higher labor-force participation, higher productivity, immigration, and a shift toward funded provision each break the supposed link between births and solvency. Reproduction, by contrast, is the slowest and costliest lever: a child does not become a net contributor for roughly two decades, during which they are themselves a dependent, and the enormous cost of raising them falls mostly on parents, disproportionately mothers. As personal financial advice, "have a child to fund your pension" barely survives contact with a calculator.

The book also traces the bias's hidden costs: the miscosted personal bargain, the instrumentalization of the child, the crowding-out of real pension reform, and the concealment of a birth's own fiscal and ecological price (the mirror-image concern of Book 065). It maps who bears the pressure: women, whose childbearing damages their own pension entitlements even as they are urged to reproduce for the system's; the poor, for whom children are the least reliable retirement plan; women of favored demographic groups, when the "pension crisis" masks a nationalist project; and the childless, branded defaulters despite a lifetime of actual contribution.

The book stays firmly in its lane. It references the graying-population alarm (Book 052) and the framing of children as labor supply (Book 059) as adjacent, but its own territory is the pension, the fund, and the promise to pay. Its stance is critical of the pronatalist use of the argument and evidence-first: neither panic nor complacency, but the insistence that a decent society makes old age secure by design, so that no one need reproduce to be cared for, and no one who does not need feel they have failed to pay.

② The book in twelve chapters

Each chapter opens with its own summary. Note chapter 2: every volume states the strongest case for the thing it criticises before laying a finger on it — and chapter 8 turns the critique back on itself.

CH 1Naming the bias

The bias this book names is the pension-provision bias: the tendency to treat reproduction, one's own or a society's, as the necessary means of funding old-age security, and to regard those who do not reproduce as having defaulted on an obligation to a system that supports them. Stated as a slogan, it is the question that gives the book its title: Who will pay the pensions?, asked not as an open policy question but as a rhetorical one, with childbearing presupposed as the only acceptable answer. The rhetorical form matters. A genuine question invites a range of answers and weighs them; a rhetorical question announces that the answer is already known and merely awaits the listener's assent. The whole persuasive work of the bias is done in that shift of register, from inquiry to foregone conclusion, and it is done so smoothly that the listener rarely notices a range of answers was ever available.

CH 2The strongest case for it (steelman)The strongest case against this book

Before any critique, the pension argument deserves its most sympathetic and complete hearing, for there is a serious idea inside it, and pretending otherwise would be both unfair and unpersuasive. Let the case be made as its ablest defender would make it.

CH 3Where it comes from

The pension argument feels timeless, but the machinery that gives it force is startlingly recent, and understanding its origins is the first step in denaturalizing it. For nearly all of human history there were no pensions at all. Old age, for those who reached it, was supported by family, by whatever property one had accumulated, by charity, or not at all. Children were, in a real and unsentimental sense, one's retirement plan, the only one available in an agrarian world with no state apparatus and no financial instruments to speak of. In such a world the link between having children and being supported in old age was not a policy but a lived reality, and the intuition that "you have children so someone will care for you when you are old" was simply an accurate description of how survival worked. It is worth dwelling on how total this link once was, because its totality is the source of the intuition's grip. There was no fund to fall back on, no state to appeal to, no market instrument to convert a working life into an old-age income. A person's surviving children, and their willingness to reciprocate the care they had received, were quite literally the difference between a supported old age and destitution. An intuition forged under those conditions does not evaporate when the conditions change; it lingers as a feeling long after it has ceased to be a fact.

CH 4How it operates today

The pension argument does its work less through explicit syllogism than through a thousand small framings, defaults, and turns of phrase that make reproduction feel like fiscal responsibility and childlessness feel like a fiscal defection. Naming these mechanisms is how one learns to see the bias operating in real time. What follows is a catalogue of the argument's working parts, the everyday devices through which a contestable inference is delivered as settled common sense. None of them is a formal fallacy one could circle in a logic textbook; each is a habit of presentation, and their power lies precisely in their ordinariness, in the fact that they pass unremarked in newspapers, dinner-table conversations, and ministerial speeches alike.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

Honesty requires stating plainly what the evidence supports and what it does not, because the pension argument rests on a real problem badly framed rather than on a fabricated one. This chapter grants the strain and then locates precisely where the inference to reproduction fails. The discipline of this chapter is to concede as much as the evidence demands and not a syllable more, because the argument's rhetorical strength comes from bundling an undeniable fact with a deniable inference, and the only way to answer it fairly is to pull the two apart and hold each up separately to the light.

CH 6The hidden costs

Because the pension argument speaks the language of prudence, it tends to hide its own costs behind a veneer of mere accounting. But the framing carries real costs, to individuals, to honest reasoning, and to the very people it claims to protect, and naming them is part of seeing past the bias. An argument that presents itself as the sober, numerate alternative to sentiment ought to be held to its own standard, and when it is, one finds that it has kept a great deal off its own books.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

Like every bias in this series, the pension argument does not fall evenly. It is aimed, felt, and enforced differently depending on who one is, and tracing its distribution reveals that the "we" who supposedly must reproduce is in practice a particular set of bodies asked to bear a particular burden. The universalizing language of the argument, its talk of "society" and "we" and "the nation," conceals a highly specific incidence, and to make that incidence visible is to change how the argument reads. What sounded like a neutral summons addressed to everyone turns out, on inspection, to be a demand that lands on some bodies and lives far more heavily than others, and rarely on the bodies and lives of those who press it most confidently.

CH 8The counter-arguments to the critiqueObjections to the critique

Intellectual honesty requires turning the critique against itself and giving the strongest objections to this book's own thesis a fair and serious hearing. Several are weighty, and none is waved away. A critique that only ever tested the argument it opposed, and never its own conclusions, would be doing exactly the one-sided work it accuses the pension argument of doing; so the objections below are put as forcefully as their proponents would put them, and answered only as far as an honest answer allows.

CH 9Case studies / illustrations

Concrete cases show the pension argument operating, and failing, in the world, and reveal how differently a society can respond to the same demographic facts. The illustrations that follow are deliberately structural rather than tied to particular named countries and dated figures, because the point they make is about mechanism, not about any one nation's statistics; a reader can supply the local instances from the news of any given year. What matters is the pattern each case exposes, the way the same demographic facts admit radically different responses depending on which lever a society reaches for and which it keeps out of view.

CH 10Adjacent biases

The pension argument does not stand alone; it interlocks with several neighboring biases in this series, and mapping the connections keeps each book in its lane while showing how the whole apparatus reinforces itself. No single bias in the series does its work in isolation; each borrows plausibility from its neighbors, and the pension argument is unusually well connected, drawing structural support from the alarm above it, the labor frame beside it, and the older duty framings beneath it. To see the connections is to understand why the argument is so hard to dislodge by direct refutation alone: knock out its fiscal claims and it leans on the moral ones; answer the moral ones and it leans on the demographic dread; and so the reader who addresses only one face of it finds the others still standing.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

What does clear-eyed, non-coerced reasoning about the pension question look like, once the bias is named? The aim of this chapter is not to replace one imperative with another, since "do not have children for your pension" is no better a commandment than its opposite, but to describe how an individual and an institution might reason honestly once the smuggled premise is removed. The goal throughout is not to arrive at a new verdict on childbearing, which is not this book's business, but to hand back to the reader a decision the pension argument had quietly annexed, so that whatever they decide, they decide it for reasons that are actually their own and actually sound.

CH 12Conclusion

This book set out to examine a bias that wears the mask of arithmetic: the treatment of reproduction as the necessary means of funding old age, and of the childless as free-riders on a system they declined to sustain. It has tried to grant everything the argument gets right and to refuse everything it smuggles. The distinction between granting and refusing has been the book's whole method, because the pension argument's persuasive power comes from bundling the two, from wrapping a smuggled demand inside a true observation so that to accept the observation feels like accepting the demand. Unbundle them, and the observation stands while the demand falls.

Pension-provision bias
The tendency to treat reproduction as the necessary means of funding old-age security and to regard the childless as free-riders on a system they draw upon; the specific bias this book names.
Pay-as-you-go (PAYG) pension
A pension arrangement in which current workers' contributions fund current retirees' benefits, rather than being saved for the contributors' own retirement. Structurally dependent on a stable or growing ratio of contributors to beneficiaries — the design that generates the "we need more workers" logic.
Funded (pre-funded) pension
A pension arrangement in which contributions are saved and invested to pay the contributor's own future benefits, reducing (though not eliminating) dependence on the size of the next generation. Carries its own risks, including market exposure.
Intergenerational contract
The unwritten reciprocal understanding that each generation supports the generation before it and the one after it — receiving care in youth and old age, providing it in productive adulthood. The most defensible form of the pension argument.
Dependency ratio (crude)
The ratio of people outside the conventional working ages to those within them, defined by fixed age thresholds. Overstates the fiscal problem by treating policy variables as demographic constants.
Economic dependency ratio
The ratio of people actually consuming public support to those actually producing and contributing. Far more malleable than the crude ratio because it depends on retirement age, participation, and productivity — none of which is fixed by fertility.
Replacement-level fertility
The total fertility rate (roughly 2.1 children per woman in low-mortality settings) at which a generation exactly replaces itself.
Total fertility rate (TFR)
The average number of children a woman would bear over her lifetime at prevailing age-specific fertility rates; below replacement across much of the world.
Pyramid/Ponzi comparison
A critique likening PAYG financing's dependence on continual population and income growth to a structure that pays existing participants from new entrants' contributions. Adopted in this book as a structural observation about financing design, explicitly not as an accusation of fraud — PAYG is a transparent, democratically adjustable public arrangement.
Free-rider framing
The rhetorical treatment of the childless as beneficiaries who declined to supply the essential input (children) to a system they draw upon; contested by the book, since the childless pay contributions and taxes their whole working lives.
Retirement-age indexation
Linking the statutory retirement age to life expectancy so that the ratio of working years to retired years stays roughly constant as longevity rises; among the most powerful levers, and one the pension argument conceals.
Labor-force participation
The share of working-age people employed or seeking work. Raising it — especially among women and older workers — adds contributors without any additional births.
Productivity growth
Increase in output per worker over time; lets each worker support more retirees, so a richer society can fund more generous transfers from the same number of workers.
Baby bonus / pronatalist incentive
A payment or tax benefit intended to raise the birth rate, often deployed in response to pension and demographic anxiety. Tends to shift the timing of births more than the total number.
Instrumentalization (of the child)
Framing a new person primarily as a means to a fiscal end — a future contributor required to sustain a fund — rather than as a person conceived for their own sake.
Externalized costs
The costs of a birth (public expenditure, resources, ecological footprint) borne by others and the planet and omitted from the "children as fiscal asset" framing; the mirror-image concern developed in Book 065.

⑥ Sources

What this volume draws on. Where the evidence is thin or contested, the book says so rather than reaching for a number.

  • Benatar, David. Better Never to Have Been: The Harm of Coming into Existence. Oxford University Press, 2006. — The philosophical anchor for the wider series' antinatalist critique; cited here for series context rather than for claims specific to pensions.
  • United Nations, Department of Economic and Social Affairs, Population Division. World Population Prospects. — The standard source class for headline demographic facts on fertility decline and population aging. Attributed at the level of the source rather than via an invented table; used only for well-established, non-specific facts (falling global fertility, sub-replacement TFR across much of the world, rising old-age shares).
  • The historical facts that state old-age social insurance was founded in Bismarckian Germany in the 1880s, and that pay-as-you-go welfare-state pensions expanded across the industrialized world through the mid-twentieth century, are well-documented matters of economic and social history and are treated as verified background.
  • The definition of replacement-level fertility as roughly 2.1 children per woman in low-mortality populations is a standard demographic constant, safe to state.
Directions the volume points to (not pinned citations)
  • Paul A. Samuelson's "consumption-loan" / overlapping-generations analysis of pay-as-you-go social security, which established how such a system depends on continued population and income growth.
  • The scholarship comparing PAYG pension financing to a pyramid or Ponzi structure. It is worth reading as a structural critique of a financing design, and never as an accusation of fraud.
  • OECD, Pensions at a Glance (recurring series), on dependency ratios, retirement-age reform, and pension design.
  • World Bank, Averting the Old Age Crisis (1994), on multi-pillar pension design and the balance between funded and PAYG provision.
  • Research on the fiscal impact of working-age immigration on pension systems and dependency ratios.
  • Research on the effectiveness of baby bonuses and pronatalist incentives on completed fertility, as against the mere timing of births.
  • The literature estimating the total cost of raising a child to adulthood, including forgone parental earnings.
  • Scholarship on the gendered distribution of childrearing costs and the effect of career interruptions on women's lifetime earnings and pension entitlements.
  • The literature on the economic ("effective") dependency ratio as against the crude age-based ratio, and on the labor-force-participation and productivity levers.
