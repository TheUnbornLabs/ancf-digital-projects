Two Intuitions and Four Premises

The Question, and How Old It Is

Some philosophical arguments do not try to install a new belief in you. They try to show you that two beliefs you already hold fit together in a way you had not noticed, and that, fitted together, they point somewhere you did not expect to go. The asymmetry of pain and pleasure works like that. It asks for no exotic premise about the badness of life. It asks you to look hard at two judgments you almost certainly make already, so reflexively that you have never stopped to examine them, and then to follow them when they are laid side by side.

The question underneath is very old. Its rigorous form is not. That it might have been better never to be born is among the oldest thoughts in the Western record, and it does not arrive from the margins. It arrives from the center. The Greeks gave Silenus, companion of Dionysus, the grim line that the best thing for a person is never to be born, and the next best to die soon; Nietzsche would later set that line at the dark root of Greek tragedy. Sophocles put the same thought into the chorus of Oedipus at Colonus, where never being born is named the first of goods. The thread runs on. It runs through Schopenhauer, for whom existence is the expression of a blind, unsatisfiable Will that can only suffer. It runs through the Norwegian Peter Wessel Zapffe, whose 1933 essay "The Last Messiah" treated human consciousness as an overgrown faculty that dooms its bearer to a dread the rest of nature is spared. It runs through Cioran's The Trouble with Being Born.

What none of them gave was an argument in the strict sense: premises set out so they can be inspected, and a conclusion claimed to follow. They gave vision and mood. Benatar's contribution, and the reason this volume opens with him and not with Silenus, is that he turned the ancient intuition into a structure. A structure can be tested. It can be attacked, defended, or abandoned on its merits. An intuition can only be resisted; an argument can be engaged. Turning the seed into an education means making that engagement possible, and Chapter One has one task: to set the structure out cleanly enough that everything which follows has something firm to grip.

That is all this chapter does. It does not defend the argument against its critics. Chapters Two and Three do that. It does not establish the consent argument or the priority of preventing suffering; those are the separate pillars of Part Two. The job here is narrower and comes first. Set the argument out so you can see exactly what is claimed, what is not, and which parts will have to bear weight when the pressure comes. Build it well and the rest of the book has a place to stand.

The First Intuition: The Child Never Born Into Suffering

Begin with a deliberately extreme case. Extremity makes the intuition visible.

Imagine a couple who know, with certainty, that any child they conceive will live a life of constant, untreatable suffering. Give the certainty a clean mechanism so nothing hangs on doubt. Say there is a condition that always expresses itself, that medicine cannot reach, that brings a short life of relentless pain and an early death. The couple understand this. They decide not to conceive. Now ask the question the whole asymmetry turns on. Was that a good decision? Not merely prudent, not merely understandable. Good. Did the choice secure something of moral worth?

Nearly everyone says yes. The couple did well to spare a child that life. Look closely at the feature it is easy to walk straight past: there is no child who was spared. No one was rescued, because no one was ever there. The being who would have suffered does not exist, never existed, and now never will. There is no person who feels relief. There is no one for whom anything goes better. And still we do not call the couple's decision morally empty for want of a beneficiary. We call it good. We judge the absence of that suffering to be good even though there is no one for whom it is good.

Hold that judgment still and look at it. It is stranger than it first seems, and its strangeness drives everything that comes after. In almost every other moral setting, a good has to be good for someone. A benefit with no beneficiary sounds like a gift addressed to no one. Yet here, in the case of the child never conceived, we return that exact verdict and feel no strain: the absence of suffering is good, and the fact that no one is around to enjoy it does not trouble us at all. This is the first of two load-bearing intuitions. Stated in the words the argument will keep using: the absence of pain is good even when there is no one to enjoy that absence.

The courts have had to wrestle with a close relative of this. The law calls it "wrongful life": a suit brought on behalf of a child born with a severe condition, claiming the child was wronged by being brought into existence at all. (This differs from "wrongful birth," which the parents bring.) Most courts have refused wrongful-life claims, and the reason is instructive. To award damages for having been caused to exist, a court must compare the plaintiff's life with their non-existence and rule the latter better, and judges balk at calling non-existence "better" for the living plaintiff in front of them. Seana Shiffrin, whose framework anchors Chapter Four, drew out the difficulty. The law's discomfort is not squeamishness. It is a real conceptual knot, the same knot Premise Three ties: the comparison of existence with non-existence. The intuition about the spared child is the very thing the courts cannot quite bring themselves to act on. That it presses on trained judges in a courtroom is some sign that it is not an artifact of the philosopher's armchair.

Honesty requires a flag this early. The case was built to make the intuition vivid, and a critic can already see the next move coming: the extreme case smuggles in the conclusion, so what about ordinary lives, which are nothing like agony? That objection is real and it has able defenders. Chapter Three takes it head-on. For now the point is small. The intuition that absent suffering is good with no one to benefit is not an antinatalist invention. The ordinary moral mind reaches it on its own, before any philosopher turns up to use it, and it is sturdy enough to unsettle a courtroom. The argument's first move asserts nothing strange. It notices something you already believe.

The Second Intuition: The Happy Child Never Conceived

Now the mirror case.

Imagine a second couple, healthy and secure, who could conceive a child near-certain to live a happy life. Loved, capable, flourishing, a life that on any reckoning holds far more good than ill. They decide not to. Perhaps they want to travel, or to give what they have to children who already exist, or simply to stay two. Ask the parallel question. Did they do something wrong? Did they break a duty, wrong a party, leave a debt unpaid?

Nearly everyone says no. Whatever you make of their reasons, they violated no obligation by not creating a happy person. There is no injured party. The child who would have been happy is owed nothing, because that child does not exist and never did. We do not lie awake grieving the happy people we declined to bring into being. We hold no memorials for the possible. We do not count, among the day's moral failures, the millions of happy persons each of us could have helped conceive and did not. The non- creation of a happy life wrongs no one. We feel this so completely that the opposite suggestion, that you are under a standing duty to conceive every happy child you could, that sleep itself is a dereliction, strikes almost everyone as not just false but slightly absurd.

Jan Narveson gave this its classic compressed form in 1967. Morality, he observed, tells us to make people happy but stays neutral about making happy people. We have strong reasons to improve the lives of those who do or will exist. We have no reason of anything like that force to bring extra happy people into being simply because they would be happy. This is not a fringe view recruited to serve antinatalism. It sits close to the default in population ethics, the thing most people believe before they have ever heard of Benatar, and it is exactly what makes the asymmetry's second premise feel obvious rather than rigged. The reasons that govern how we treat existing and future people do not seem to include a reason to maximize the number of happy people. Anyone who tried to live as though they did would have misunderstood what morality asks of them.

So here is the second load-bearing intuition, again in the words the argument will use: the absence of pleasure is not bad, unless there is someone for whom that absence is a deprivation. The happy child never conceived is deprived of nothing, because there is no one there to be deprived. No person is missing those joys the way an existing person misses joys lost or denied. There is no person at all. The absent pleasure floats free of any victim, and we are right to count it no loss.

Watch what just happened, because the whole argument lives in the relation between these two intuitions. With pain, we were happy to call its absence good with no one to benefit. With pleasure, we will not call its absence bad unless someone is there to be deprived. The same fact, nonexistence with no one present, gives opposite verdicts depending on whether the subject is pain or pleasure. Absent pain is a clean good. Absent pleasure is merely not-bad, a moral zero. That difference is the asymmetry. It is not a premise imported from outside, and it has nothing to do with any gloomy estimate of how much life hurts. It is just the name for the gap between two judgments you already hold, one of them near-orthodox in population ethics, the other strong enough to give a court pause.

The Four Premises

Now state the argument with the precision the rest of the volume needs. The asymmetry, in its canonical form, is four claims about the value of pain and pleasure across two states: present, in an existing being, and absent, where there is no being at all. Benatar's numbering is used here, because the literature uses it and a reader going to the sources should not have to translate.

Premise One. The presence of pain is bad. This is the least controversial claim in ethics. An existing being's suffering is bad, both for that being and full stop. Almost no theory of welfare denies it. Hedonists, desire theorists, and objective-list theorists all count pain as at least one kind of bad. The premise needs no argument. It needs only to be registered, and granted without reservation.

Premise Two. The presence of pleasure is good. Just as uncontroversial, and just as undefended, because it needs no defense. An existing being's joys and satisfactions, its loves and triumphs, are good. This one has to be set in bold and set early, because the commonest caricature of antinatalism depends on its being denied. The antinatalist is not committed to the idea that life's pleasures are illusory, or trivial, or secretly worthless, or always outweighed. The argument grants the full reality and the full value of every joy a life contains. Whatever it concludes, it concludes while agreeing that the goods of existence are real goods. Anyone who comes away thinking antinatalism needs a dim view of pleasure has misread it at the second line.

Premise Three. The absence of pain is good, even with no one to enjoy that absence. Here the asymmetry enters. This is the first intuition made formal, the child never born into suffering. The absence of pain is good, and it stays good even when the absence belongs to no one, because no one was created. This premise does the real and unusual work, and most of the volume's energy goes here, because this is where the argument is attacked. The next chapter is given over to a single question: how can an absence be good when there is no one for whom it is good? Two rival answers wait there, each with a price.

Premise Four. The absence of pleasure is not bad, unless someone is deprived by it. The second intuition made formal: the happy child never conceived, and Narveson's neutrality about making happy people. Absent pleasure is bad only when there is someone to suffer the want of it. With no one present, absent pleasure is not bad. It is a moral zero. This premise gets tested hard too, because the symmetry objection, the gravest threat the asymmetry faces and the spine of Chapter Three, says Premises Three and Four are not held to one standard, and that an even-handed accounting flattens the asymmetry into symmetry and the argument into silence. For now the four premises only need to be on the table, stated clearly enough that the attacks know where to aim.

The Comparison, and Where It Can Be Attacked

The premises do their work only in a comparison: a particular being existing, set against that same being never existing at all. Put the two states next to each other, with the relevant premise marked in each cell.

Read the table the way the argument reads it, one row at a time. Take the pain row. Scenario A has present pain, which is bad. Scenario B has absent pain, which is good. Compare the two and Scenario B comes out ahead on this row, since a good beats a bad. Now the pleasure row. Scenario A has present pleasure, which is good. Scenario B has absent pleasure, which is not bad. Here is the crux. Scenario A's good does not beat Scenario B's not-bad, because Premise Four denies that the absent pleasure is a harm or a loss to anyone. No one in Scenario B is missing the pleasure, so its absence costs Scenario B nothing. The pleasure row is, at worst, a wash. Scenario A holds a real good, but Scenario B suffers no real loss against it, and a real good on one side facing no real loss on the other does not, on this accounting, make Scenario A better overall. The good of Scenario A's pleasure only ever relieves an absence that, in Scenario B, was costing no one anything.

Put the rows together. On pain, never-existing wins. On pleasure, never- existing does not lose. Across both, never-existing is never worse, and on one row it is better. That is the shape of the conclusion: coming into existence is always a harm relative to never having existed. The word always is exact and earns its place. It does not mean every life is bad, or unbearable, or not worth continuing. It means that for every possible person the comparison of existing with never existing comes out, on this structure, in favor of never existing, since the four premises force that result for any life holding any pain at all, however much joy comes with it.

It pays to be precise about where the argument can actually be resisted, because critics often aim loosely, and there are three places to aim, not two. Any presentation that admits only two is overselling. The first place is the premises, mostly Three and Four, by way of the symmetry objection in Chapter Three. The second is the legitimacy of comparing existence with non-existence at all, which is what the no-subject objection and the non- identity problem call into question. The third is the one a careless account hides. Getting from the four cells to "non-existence is better overall" means aggregating across the two rows: weighing non-existence's edge on the pain row against existence's apparent edge on the pleasure row, and judging the second to be no real edge because, by Premise Four, the absent pleasure deprives no one. A serious line of criticism locates the weakness right there, at the cross-row comparison, rather than in any single premise. The charge is that the aggregation quietly runs an impersonal accounting on the pain row and a person-affecting one on the pleasure row. Whether you file that under "a premise," "the method," or "the inference itself" is partly a matter of words. What is not a matter of words is that the step to the conclusion is no mere formal deduction only a fool could reject. Grant the premises and grant the comparison and the conclusion is hard to dodge. But those are large grants, and the cross-row aggregation is a third thing that must be granted besides. So the rest of Part One is built to test all three: the premises, the comparison, and the aggregation that turns four cells into one verdict.

The Clarification Everything Depends On

Now the most important passage in the book. Most serious misreadings of the position, and a great many cheap attacks on it, come from missing this one point. The asymmetry is a comparison between two states. It is not a verdict on life from the inside.

It does not say your life is bad. It does not say your pleasures are unreal, or that your suffering outweighs your joy, or that you would be better off dead, or that you should regret being alive, or that life is not worth continuing. What it says is narrower and far stranger than any of those. Set against the alternative of never having existed, coming into existence imposed a cost. The comparison is never a living person against a dead one. It is a person against no person: existence against the blank, victimless absence before conception, an absence in which nothing is lacked because no one is there to lack it.

Hold that fixed and three of the most common objections fall away at once. They are worth naming and dispatching now, so they stop troubling you when they reappear in a critic's mouth later.

"But I'm glad I exist." Of course you are, and the argument expects it. An existing being with interests, attachments, and projects will almost always prefer to go on existing, and should. The argument grants this whole. It is not a claim about whether existing people ought to be glad; it agrees they usually will be, and finds nothing to fix in that gladness. It is a claim about whether the act of creating them imposed a cost relative to not creating them. Your gladness is a fact about you, an existing being measuring your life against losing it. It does not measure the only comparison the argument makes, existence against never-existing, which you cannot make from the inside, because the alternative has no inside.

"So you think we'd all be better off dead." No. This is the inference the argument flatly denies. Death is the deprivation of an existing being who has every interest in living, whose projects are underway and whose attachments are real, and whose destruction is therefore a grave harm. It is the opposite of the victimless, costless absence that comes before creation. Sliding from "never existing costs nothing" to "ceasing to exist costs nothing" confuses two utterly different things: the blank before a life, where there is no one, and the wound at the end of a life, where there is someone who is lost. The argument concerns only the first. The line between starting and ending lives is the permanent boundary of this whole series, set right here, and the firewall against pro-mortalism in Chapter Five is built on it.

"My life is wonderful, so the argument is false for me." The wonder of your life is Premise Two, granted in full and with admiration. It does not touch the comparison. The comparison does not ask whether your life is good; it asks whether the absent alternative was worse for you, and Premise Four says it was not, because there was no you there to be worse off. A wonderful life is a real good enjoyed by a real person who now exists. It is not the relief of a deprivation the never-created person was suffering, because the never-created person suffered nothing. The goods of your life are real, and they are yours. They are simply not rescues. The argument turns on that one word.

Keep the comparison in view and the argument is coherent and serious, and the easy dismissals lose their grip. Lose it, slide even once from existence- versus-non-existence into life-versus-death or good-life-versus-bad-life, and you are no longer arguing about the asymmetry. You are arguing about something else, usually something the asymmetry explicitly denies, and your refutation, however hard you swing it, lands on empty air. More objections die on this distinction than on any other point in the field. A reader who has truly taken it in is already most of the way to seeing why the position gets attacked so confidently for things it never claims.

A Life Worth Starting and a Life Worth Continuing

The clarification just made can be sharpened into a tool the rest of the book will use constantly, and the tool is Benatar's own. He draws a distinction between a life worth starting and a life worth continuing, and once you have it in hand a whole family of confusions resolves at once.

The two phrases answer different questions, put by different people, at different times. "Is this life worth continuing?" is the question facing an existing being, or those who must decide for one. Given that someone is already here, with interests and attachments and a future they are reaching toward, is their life good enough to go on? "Is this life worth starting?" is the question facing a prospective creator, before anyone exists at all. Should this life be begun? The questions sound alike. They are not, and the standards that answer them are not the same.

The standard for continuing is, in an important sense, low, and rightly so. An existing life clears it easily, because the alternative to continuing is death, and death is a grave harm to the one who suffers it: the severing of projects, the loss of everyone and everything the person cares about, the destruction of a being with a powerful interest in living on. We therefore demand a very strong justification before we will say a life is not worth continuing. The standard for starting is different, and the difference is not a matter of degree but of structure. No existing being's interest in living is at stake in the decision whether to start a life, because there is no existing being yet. The grave harm that makes us reluctant to end a life has no counterpart on the side of declining to begin one. So the bar for "worth starting" can sit much higher than the bar for "worth continuing" without any inconsistency, and on the asymmetry it sits so high that no life clears it without cost.

Benatar's own illustration makes the gap vivid. Most people think a life lived without a limb is well worth continuing. We would not say such a life should be ended, and we would be appalled by anyone who did. Yet most of the same people also think it better not to bring into existence a child who is certain to lack a limb, if the lack can be foreseen and avoided. The two judgments coexist without friction. We require a stronger justification for ending a life than for not starting one, and that asymmetry in what we demand is itself a piece of data, one more thing the basic asymmetry helps explain. A life can be entirely worth continuing and still not have been worth starting. The two verdicts do not have to agree, because they are answers to two different questions.

This single distinction carries more weight in this book than almost any other, and it is worth seeing in advance where it will be spent. It is what blocks the inference from "coming into existence is a harm" to "so we should all want to die." That inference runs the two questions together, reading a claim about whether lives are worth starting as though it were a claim about whether they are worth continuing. It is no such thing. The whole pro- mortalism firewall of Chapter Five is built on this distinction, and a reader who has it firmly will find that chapter's hardest work already half done. The distinction also explains the testimony of the living, which Chapter Three takes up at length. "I am glad I exist" is a verdict about a life worth continuing, delivered by someone with every reason to render it, and it leaves the question of whether the life was worth starting exactly where it was. And it sharpens the whole-life reply of Chapter Seven, where the goodness of a life, once underway, is set against the very different question of whether beginning it was the creator's to choose.

Candor requires the other half of the ledger. The distinction is contested, and by able people. David DeGrazia, while praising the rest of Benatar's work, doubts whether the worth-starting and worth-continuing thresholds really come apart as cleanly as the argument needs, and whether the gap between them can bear the structural weight Benatar places on it. Jeff McMahan presses from the opposite flank. If a life is worth living, he argues, then causing the person to exist confers on them a real, if noncomparative, benefit, and that benefit gives us some moral reason to start the life after all, which is exactly what the asymmetry denies. So the distinction is doing genuine work and is itself under fire. Hold it as the powerful, contested instrument it is, and not as a settled result, and it will serve every chapter that follows.

The Conclusion, at the Right Size

So what has the argument established, if it holds? Precision here is the whole game, because the gap between the defensible claim and the indefensible one is the gap between this argument and its caricature, and the boldest phrasings of the conclusion are also the most exposed.

It has not shown that life is not worth living, that no one should be glad to be alive, that existence is uniformly miserable, or that the joys of a life are not joys. It has shown something tighter and harder to shift. Bringing a person into existence imposes a guaranteed cost, some suffering, however much or little, on a being who, before the act, had no need of the goods offered in exchange, because before the act there was no being at all to need anything. The pleasures of a life are real. They are not rescues. They do not relieve a prior deprivation, because Premise Four says there was no prior deprivation to relieve. No one waited in a wing of nonexistence, missing out on joy, needing to be conceived in order to receive it. The goods of life answer no standing need. The harms of life fall on a being who, once created, has to live through them. That mismatch, guaranteed harm to someone in exchange for goods no one lacked, is the whole content of the conclusion.

Compressed into one sentence, it is the line the seed book set as canonical for the series: procreation imposes guaranteed, unconsented harm on a being who had no need of the goods offered in compensation. Notice how much the sentence leaves out. It does not say the harm is large, or that it swamps the goods, or that the life will be rejected by the one who lives it. It says the harm is guaranteed, true of every life. It says the harm is unconsented, necessarily, since there was no prior party to give consent. And it says the harm answers no need, by Premise Four, since the goods in compensation relieve no prior lack. Everything in the next two chapters defends that sentence against the people who think it false. Everything in Part Two argues that even if the sentence failed, two other sentences would carry the position. And the last chapter measures, premise by premise, how secure the sentence really is. But the sentence is now in place, with its parts and its careful size, and the rest of the volume can start to test it.

What This Chapter Leaves Open

No chapter in this series ends without saying what its own argument has left unsettled, and this one least of all, because it is the foundation, and a foundation that hides its cracks is the most dangerous structure there is. Three cracks are already visible, before a single critic has been answered in full. Naming them now is a promise the later chapters keep.

First, the asymmetry rests on two intuitions, offered as widely and stably held. They are widely held. The chapter has tried to earn them rather than assert them, tying the first to the courtroom's trouble with wrongful life and the second to Narveson's near-orthodox neutrality. But widely is not universally, and an intuition is not a proof. A philosopher who simply does not share the first intuition, who denies that absent pain is good when no one benefits, has made no logical mistake and cannot be shown one. Intuitions are where the argument starts, not where it ends, and an argument that starts in intuition can be resisted by anyone whose intuitions run the other way. This is a real and permanent limit, the limit of substantive moral philosophy as such. Chapter Three returns to it.

Second, and sharper: the asymmetry's force depends on Premises Three and Four being treated consistently, and the gravest objection in the field says they are not. It says Premise Three quietly enjoys a generosity, impersonal goodness, value with no beneficiary, that Premise Four is refused, since Four insists on a subject before it will count a loss. Apply one standard to both, the objection runs, and the structure collapses into symmetry. That objection has a name, the symmetry or consistency objection, and it is no quibble. It is the best reason a thoughtful person has to reject the asymmetry, and it gets a chapter of its own, answered as strongly as the answer can honestly be made, and no more strongly than that.

Third, the comparison at the heart of the argument, existence against never-existing, "better for" a being who in one of the two states does not exist, may not be a coherent comparison at all. The objection that value needs a subject, and that there is no subject on the non-existence side to be better or worse off, is older than Benatar. It runs back to Epicurus's claim that death is nothing to us, and it threatens the argument's method rather than its premises. Chapter Three takes it up as the no-subject objection, and Chapter Six, on the non-identity problem, shows it to be the bedrock beneath not only the asymmetry but every other argument in the field.

This is why the position does not rest on the asymmetry alone. If the symmetry objection wins, and careful philosophers think it does, the argument of this chapter falls, and the most famous argument in the field falls with it. The position this series defends would survive the loss, because two pillars remain that owe the asymmetry nothing: the consent argument of Chapter Four and the priority of preventing suffering of Chapter Five. The asymmetry is the foundation's most elegant column. It is not its only one. To learn, by the end of this volume, how to hold the asymmetry firmly while knowing exactly where it might give way is to learn the one thing this whole series exists to teach. The next chapter begins the lesson by trying, in earnest, to make Premise Three give way.
