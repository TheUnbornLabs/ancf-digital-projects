PROJECT 027

Risk Argument Explainer

Philosophy · Difficulty: Intermediate

Creating a life guarantees exposure to risks the person can never opt out of. The "risk imposition" argument says that imposing unavoidable risk on someone who cannot consent needs justifying. How heavy that point feels depends entirely on which ethical framework you reason with — so here you can switch lenses and watch the verdict change.

1. The idea

Every life will contain some serious risk — of illness, loss, pain, grief. A future person cannot opt out of those risks, because they aren't there to be asked. The argument holds that knowingly imposing unavoidable, unconsented risk of serious harm on another is the kind of act that calls for a strong justification, not a default yes.

3. The counterpoints

We accept imposed risk all the time

Parents and societies make momentous, un-consented decisions for future people constantly, and we judge them by care and outcomes — not by the bare impossibility of prior consent.

Risk can be reduced, and met with agency

A future person isn't a passive target of risk; they can adapt, find meaning, and reduce harm — for themselves and others.

"Some risk" proves little on its own

If any imposed risk forbade an action, almost nothing would be permissible. The argument needs the risk to be serious and the upside insufficient — which is exactly what's contested.

4. What survives

Even critics of the argument usually grant the modest core: a decision of this magnitude deserves real thought rather than autopilot. That much most prospective parents already give it — which may be the argument's most durable contribution: not a verdict, but a demand for seriousness.
