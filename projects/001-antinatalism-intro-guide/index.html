Antinatalism Intro Guide

Philosophy · Difficulty: Beginner · ~12 min

Antinatalism is the view that bringing a new person into existence deserves serious ethical thought — rather than being treated as an automatic good. This guide teaches the four core arguments, then lets you test your own understanding with interactive tools. It is respectful toward parents, children, and families, and it leaves the conclusion to you.

What you will learn

  • What antinatalism claims — and the things it does not claim.
  • The four pillars of the argument: consent, harm prevention, the asymmetry, and autonomy.
  • How the famous "asymmetry" of pleasure and suffering is meant to work — and a fair objection to it.
  • How to weigh suffering-risk when deciding for a person who cannot be asked.
  • Where your own understanding is strong, and where it could deepen.

1. The argument map

Most antinatalist reasoning rests on four connected ideas. Here they are at a glance.

Diagram: four pillars — consent (no one can agree to being born), harm prevention (every life risks some suffering), the asymmetry (missing harm is good; missing pleasure is no loss when no one is deprived), and autonomy (the choice belongs to each person).

ConsentA future person cannot agree to being born — they do not yet exist to be asked. Harm preventionEvery life carries some risk of pain, loss, or illness; non-creation avoids that risk. AsymmetryThe absence of suffering is good even if no one enjoys it; the absence of pleasure is not bad unless someone is deprived. AutonomyWhether to create a life is an ethical decision, not an automatic duty.

2. The asymmetry matrix

David Benatar's argument rests on a claimed asymmetry. Tap each cell to see how it is meant to read.

Presence of painBad
Suffering that actually exists is a harm. Few people dispute this cell.
Presence of pleasureGood
Enjoyment that actually exists is a benefit. Also widely accepted.
Absence of painGood
Here is the key move: a harm that never happens is counted as good — even if no one is there to enjoy that good.
Absence of pleasureNot bad
The mirror move: a missing pleasure is only bad if there is a real person being deprived. With no one created, no one is deprived.

The claimed asymmetry lives in the bottom two cells. Critics reply that "good for no one" is a strange kind of good — a fair objection worth weighing yourself.

Key takeaway

Antinatalism is not hatred of children or families — it is an ethical question about creating a life that cannot consent and is certain to face some risk. You can find that question worth taking seriously while still disagreeing with the conclusion, and while treating both antinatalists and parents with equal respect.
