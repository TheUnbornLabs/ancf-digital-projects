"Instead of the greatest amount of happiness for the greatest number, one should demand, more modestly, the least amount of avoidable suffering for all."

— Karl Popper

So far this book has examined antinatalism mostly through the question of consent and the asymmetry between the harms and the benefits of being born. In this chapter we turn to a wider ethical theory that often stands behind antinatalist feeling, even when it is not named: negative utilitarianism. It is one of the cleanest expressions of the idea that suffering is the thing morality should be most worried about. But it is also a view with a famous and uncomfortable problem attached to it, and learning that problem is one of the most useful things a careful reader can do.

Ordinary, or "classical," utilitarianism tells us to act so as to bring about the greatest balance of good over bad — to maximize happiness and minimize misery, weighing both on the same scale. Negative utilitarianism shifts the emphasis. It says that reducing suffering carries more moral weight than increasing happiness, and in its strongest form it says that minimizing suffering is what fundamentally matters, with the promotion of happiness being far less important or even beside the point.

Negative utilitarianism (NU) — the view that, in our ethical reasoning, the reduction of suffering should take priority over the promotion of happiness. Moderate versions give suffering extra weight; strong versions hold that lessening suffering is the only thing of ultimate moral importance. The broader modern family of related positions is called suffering-focused ethics.

Why suffering might come first

The intuition behind negative utilitarianism is not exotic. Most of us already feel that there is something especially urgent about pain that is not quite matched by pleasure. If you walk past a person in agony, the pull to help feels like a duty; if you walk past a person enjoying a sandwich, the thought "I could make their sandwich slightly nicer" carries no such force. We treat the relief of suffering as pressing in a way we do not treat the manufacture of extra delight.

Karl Popper gave this intuition a memorable form. In The Open Society and Its Enemies (1945), he argued that we should aim at the least avoidable suffering rather than the greatest happiness. His reasons were partly practical and partly moral: human misery makes a more direct and undeniable claim on us than human pleasure does, and a politics organized around relieving the worst-off is harder to abuse than one organized around engineering everyone's joy. Popper himself was not promoting a complete moral system, but his phrasing has echoed through every later discussion of the topic.

You can see, without much effort, how this connects to antinatalism. If suffering is the thing that morally counts most, and if every life that is created is guaranteed to contain some suffering, then creating a life looks like the introduction of a real and certain moral cost — one that no amount of the new person's future happiness straightforwardly cancels out. Negative utilitarianism does not have to lead here, but it clears a wide path in this direction.

Pain in front of you commands; pleasure in front of you merely invites.

The world-exploder problem

Now we come to the difficulty that anyone studying this view must confront honestly. In 1958 the philosopher R. N. Smart published a short paper raising what has become the standard objection to negative utilitarianism. His argument runs like this. Suppose suffering is the only thing that ultimately matters, and our overriding goal is to minimize it. Then the surest way to guarantee that there is no more suffering, ever, would be to bring about the painless end of all sentient life. A hypothetical figure who could push a button and extinguish everything — Smart's "benevolent world-exploder" — would, on a strict reading of strong NU, be doing the most good imaginable, because a universe with no one in it has no suffering in it at all.

Almost everyone, including almost every negative utilitarian, regards this conclusion as a reductio ad absurdum — a result so plainly monstrous that it counts as evidence the strong theory has gone wrong somewhere, rather than as a recommendation to be acted on. The world-exploder is a thought experiment built to expose a flaw, not a proposal. It is essential to understand it in exactly this spirit. No serious proponent of suffering-focused ethics walks around looking for the button; the scenario is a stress test that the theory, in its crudest form, fails.

Objection If suffering is all that matters morally, then negative utilitarianism endorses painlessly ending all life — the "world-exploder." Any view with that as its logical conclusion is not just mistaken but dangerous, and it taints the antinatalism that leans on it.
Reply The world-exploder follows only from the strongest, simplest version of NU, and essentially everyone treats it as a reason to reject that version, not a plan to carry out. Moderate negative utilitarians give suffering extra weight without saying happiness counts for nothing, so for them a world full of flourishing people is not improved by erasing it. Others build in a strong presumption against killing and against violating the wishes of existing beings, who overwhelmingly want to continue. And crucially, the antinatalist conclusion that actually follows from suffering-focused thinking is about not starting new lives — declining to add new suffering — not about ending the lives of those already here. Refraining from creating a person and exterminating people are opposite kinds of act.

Suffering-focused ethics today

The modern descendants of negative utilitarianism usually travel under the broader banner of suffering-focused ethics. Organizations such as the Center for Reducing Suffering treat the prevention of intense suffering as the central ethical priority, while explicitly distancing themselves from coercion and from the world-exploder. A related but distinct current is the "abolitionist" project associated with the philosopher David Pearce, who argues that biotechnology could one day remove suffering from sentient life without removing the life itself — a striking inversion of the exploder, aiming to abolish the pain rather than the person.

What these contemporary views share is a refusal to treat suffering as just one ingredient to be traded off freely against pleasure. What they overwhelmingly reject is the idea that this priority licenses harming or eliminating anyone. The lesson they have drawn from Smart is that a usable ethics of suffering must be hedged with respect for the living and for consent.

How NU relates to antinatalism — and how it does not

It is worth being precise here, because the two ideas are often run together. Negative utilitarianism can motivate antinatalism: if you already think suffering carries special weight, the certainty that any new life contains suffering gives you a clear reason to hesitate before creating one. But NU does not entail antinatalism, and antinatalism does not require NU. A person can reach the antinatalist conclusion through the consent argument or Benatar's asymmetry without any utilitarian commitments at all. And a negative utilitarian could, in principle, hold that some lives are so likely to be overwhelmingly good — and to reduce others' suffering — that creating them is permissible.

In plain words: Negative utilitarianism says "ease suffering first." That can push you toward antinatalism, but the two are separate ideas, and almost no one who holds the view thinks it licenses harming or ending the people already here.

The honest way to hold any suffering-focused view, then, is with the world-exploder kept firmly in mind — not as a destination but as a warning sign marking where the simple version of the theory drives off the road. The moderates' whole project is to capture the genuine moral seriousness of suffering while building in the safeguards that keep it humane.

Chapter in brief

  • Negative utilitarianism holds that reducing suffering should take priority over promoting happiness; in strong forms, that lessening suffering is what ultimately matters.
  • Karl Popper urged aiming at the least avoidable suffering rather than the greatest happiness, capturing a widely shared intuition that pain makes a more urgent moral claim than pleasure.
  • R. N. Smart's "benevolent world-exploder" (1958) shows that strong NU seems to recommend painlessly ending all life — treated almost universally as a reductio, a flaw to be avoided, not a goal.
  • Moderates escape it by weighting happiness too, by respecting the wishes of the living, and by noting that the relevant conclusion is not creating new suffering, not destroying anyone.
  • Today this lives on as suffering-focused ethics; it can motivate antinatalism but does not entail it, and its serious proponents reject coercion and harm.
