"To be born is, in itself, a problem — not a gift to be celebrated, but a condition to be examined."

— after Julio Cabrera, negative ethics (paraphrase)

The Argentine philosopher Julio Cabrera (born 1944), who spent much of his career in Brazil at the Universidade de Brasília, approaches the question of birth from an angle that is easy to miss if one is only listening for the word antinatalism. Cabrera prefers to call his project negative ethics, and he sometimes hesitates to wear the antinatalist label at all. Yet his work arrives at an antinatalist conclusion by a careful and distinctive route, and understanding that route is one of the more rewarding tasks in this book.

Where some thinkers build their case on a single asymmetry between pleasure and pain, Cabrera builds his on a description of the human condition as a whole — on what it is actually like to be a living being acting in the world. His claim is not that life contains too much suffering, as if the ledger merely came out wrong. It is that suffering and friction are woven into the very structure of being alive. He calls this the terminality of being.

The terminality of being

By "terminality" Cabrera does not mean only that we die at the end. He means that being alive is, from the first moment, a process of decaying — a condition that is always running down, always under strain, always requiring effort merely to hold itself together. To be a living body is to be hungry, to tire, to be vulnerable to pain and illness, to need constant repair, and finally to fail. This is not bad luck that happens to some lives; it is the shape of every life, the background condition against which any particular joy occurs.

Terminality of being — Cabrera's term for the structural character of human existence as something that is continuously decaying and friction-filled from the start, not merely finite at the end. Life, on this view, is not a good thing that unfortunately ends, but a condition of ongoing wear that must be actively resisted at every moment.

Cabrera asks us to notice how much of life is spent simply pushing back against this decay — eating, resting, healing, defending, distracting ourselves from discomfort. The good moments are real, he allows, but they occur inside this structure of effort and friction, not outside it. The view is sober rather than despairing: it asks us to see clearly what we are signing a new person up for.

The moral impediment

The second pillar of Cabrera's ethics is even more original, and it is genuinely a contribution to moral philosophy rather than to mood. He argues that there is, built into the human situation, a moral impediment — a structural obstacle to living blamelessly. His claim is this: one cannot live and act in the world without, at least sometimes, harming, using, or manipulating others. To pursue any goal is to compete for scarce space, to occupy resources others might have had, to influence and sometimes override the wills of those around us.

This is not an accusation aimed at bad people. It is a description that includes everyone. The kindest life still consumes, still displaces, still leans on the labor and forbearance of others. Cabrera's point is that morality sets us a standard — do not harm, do not use others merely as means — that the structure of existence makes it impossible to fully meet. We are placed, by birth, into a game we cannot play cleanly.

From these two observations — terminality and the moral impediment — Cabrera draws his conclusion about procreation. To create a new person is to place a being into a decaying, friction-filled existence and into a moral situation in which it cannot avoid harming others. We do this, as ever, without the new person's consent, and for reasons that are ultimately our own. Negative ethics concludes that this is not the obvious good our culture assumes it to be.

Abstention, not extinction

It is essential to state clearly what Cabrera does not conclude, because the structural bleakness of his picture invites misreading. Cabrera draws a firm line between abstention from procreation and any coercive project of extinction. He rejects killing. He does not advocate ending the lives of those who already exist, and nothing in negative ethics licenses harm toward living people. The only practical conclusion is a refusal — a decision not to start a new life — which is, as we have seen throughout this book, about as far from violence as an action can be.

This distinction is not a footnote for Cabrera; it follows from his own ethics. A philosophy whose central insight is the wrongness of harming and using others cannot turn around and license the gravest harm of all. The conclusion of negative ethics is quiet: to stop adding new players to a game no one can win cleanly, while caring fully for those already in it.

In plain words: Cabrera argues that life is structurally decaying and full of friction (terminality), and that we cannot live without harming others (the moral impediment). Together these make creating a new person ethically hard to justify — but he firmly rejects any harm to those already alive; his conclusion is only to refrain.
ObjectionTo call the disvalue of life "structural" overstates the case. The frictions Cabrera describes — hunger, effort, the occasional stepping on others' toes — are the ordinary textures of life that billions of people navigate perfectly well. We get hungry and we eat; we tire and we rest; we compete and we also cooperate and forgive. Calling this a permanent "impediment" or a state of "decay" dresses up the normal business of being alive as if it were a tragedy.
ReplyCabrera would not deny that people navigate these frictions; his point is about what the navigating shows. That we manage hunger does not mean hunger is nothing — it means we spend our lives managing it, which is exactly his claim. The objection treats "people cope" as if it answered "is this worth imposing on a new person?", but coping and justification are different questions. Someone can handle a burden gracefully while it remains true that the burden need not have been created. As for the moral impediment, the reply concedes its mildness in any single case and misses its scope: Cabrera's claim is not that any one act of using others is catastrophic, but that no life escapes it — that blamelessness is structurally unavailable. One may still judge that the goods of life outweigh all this; Cabrera's achievement is to make the cost visible and to insist it be counted before a new person is enrolled to pay it.

"Negative ethics" rather than "antinatalism"

Why does Cabrera sometimes resist the antinatalist label? Partly because "antinatalism" can sound like a single-issue slogan, whereas his negative ethics is a broad framework about value, harm, and the structure of existence, from which the antinatalist conclusion follows as one result among others. He is interested less in winning a debate about birth than in describing honestly what kind of situation a human life is. The antinatalist conclusion is, for him, where that honest description leads — not a banner he set out to wave.

Chapter in brief

  • Julio Cabrera (b. 1944) developed a framework he calls negative ethics, which grounds an antinatalist conclusion without starting from a single pleasure–pain asymmetry.
  • The terminality of being is his claim that life is structurally decaying and friction-filled from the start — not merely finite at the end.
  • The moral impediment is the structural fact that we cannot live and act without sometimes harming, using, or manipulating others; blameless living is unavailable.
  • Together these make creating a new person ethically hard to justify, since it enrolls a being, without consent, into this condition.
  • Cabrera sharply distinguishes abstention from procreation from any coercive extinction; he rejects killing and any harm to those already alive.
