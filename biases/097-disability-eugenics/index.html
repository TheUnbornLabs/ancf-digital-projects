BIAS 097

Which Children Are Wanted: Disability, Eugenics, and Natalism

Part VIII · Gender, Body, Identity & Autonomy · Disability, Eugenics, and Natalism

The bias: The uneasy line between "have children" and "have the right children."

The message many disabled people take from prenatal testing is not 'we want to spare children suffering' but 'we would rather people like you had not been born.' Whether or not that message is intended, it is heard." — a paraphrase of the expressivist concern articulated by disability scholars including Adrienne Asch (theme, not a verbatim quotation) --- ## Preface to this volume Pronatalism is usually heard as a single, warm imperative: have children. But listen more closely to the culture that presses people toward parenthood, and a second clause is almost always there, half-spoken, doing quiet work beneath the first. Not merely have children but have healthy children — normal children, children who will "have a good life," children who will not "suffer." The two clauses feel like one. They are not. And the gap between them is the subject of this book. This is a volume about a tension inside pronatalism that most pronatalism does not notice it contains. On one side stands the drive for more births: the celebration of fertility, the anxiety over falling birth rates, the treatment of a new child as an unqualified good. On the other side stand the selective pressures that ride alongside that drive — prenatal screening offered as routine care, the framing of certain conditions as tragedies to be prevented, the soft and pervasive assumption that a wanted child is a child of a particular kind. Pronatalism says more. But it very often means more of the approved sort. The logic that sorts the approved from the unapproved is the logic this book examines, and its uncomfortable name is eugenic. I use that word carefully, and I want to be clear at the outset about what I am and am not saying. I am not saying that a woman who undergoes prenatal testing is a eugenicist. I am not saying that individual reproductive choices — to test, to continue a pregnancy, to end one — are wrong, or that the people who make them are to be judged. This book takes no stance on any individual decision, and it defends the right of people to make those decisions free from coercion in every direction. The target here is not the chooser. It is a logic embedded in how the choices are framed, defaulted, and pushed — a logic that treats some lives as more worth bringing into the world than others, and that does so while wearing the untroubled face of health, prudence, and love. The distinction between a chooser and a frame is the hinge of the whole book, and it is worth pausing on before we go further, because almost every misreading of the argument comes from letting it slip. A choice is something a person makes; a frame is the set of defaults, words, and assumptions that reach the person before they choose, shaping what the choice looks like and which answer feels obvious. You can criticize a frame without criticizing a single person who acted inside it — indeed, the sharper the criticism of the frame, the more it exonerates the individual, because it relocates the trouble from private conscience to shared machinery. That is the move this book makes throughout: not "you chose wrongly," but "look at what was already deciding for you before you were asked.

① In brief

Pronatalism is usually heard as a single warm imperative: have children. But a second clause is almost always present beneath the first — have healthy, normal children — and the gap between the two is the subject of this book. It examines a tension inside pronatalism that most pronatalism does not notice it contains: the drive for more births coexists with selective pressures (routine prenatal screening, the framing of disability as tragedy, the conditional welcome of the "healthy baby") that carry a eugenic logic. Pronatalism says more; it very often means more of the approved kind.

The book's thesis is precise: the selective-natalism bias is the smuggling of a eugenic logic into the pronatalist good, such that some prospective lives are quietly counted as more worth bringing into being than others, while the whole arrangement presents itself as health, love, and prudent care. Crucially, the target is not any individual chooser. The book takes no stance on any personal reproductive decision, defends reproductive autonomy and access to information, and keeps a firm moral distance between private choice and state coercion. Its target is a logic — embedded in defaults, language, and institutions — not a person.

The equal worth of disabled lives is the book's premise, not its conclusion. Disability is treated throughout not as a tragedy to be prevented but as a form of human variation and human life. This premise anchors the whole argument and is never bargained away.

Read the rest of the overview

Following the series template, Chapter 1 names the bias and separates it from neighbors (058 on ethnonationalist "replacement" pronatalism; 019 on the childless body as defective; 065 on unpriced costs). Chapter 2 steelmans the selective impulse fully and in good faith: the sincere wish to prevent suffering, the responsibility of giving a child the best start one can provide, the serious philosophical position of procreative beneficence (Savulescu), and the value of information and autonomy. Chapter 3 traces the origins — ancient infant-sorting, the eugenics movement named by Galton, compulsory sterilization and Buck v. Bell (1927), the Nazi horror endpoint of Lebensborn and Aktion T4, the "new"/liberal eugenics debate, and the medical model of disability. Chapter 4 shows how the logic operates today: screening as default, the vocabulary of risk and abnormality, the tragedy script, the counseling asymmetry, cost-avoidance framing, and the phrase "as long as it's healthy."

Chapter 5 weighs the evidence honestly, including the "disability paradox" (non-disabled people systematically overestimate how bad disabled lives are) and the social model's insight that much disabling disadvantage is contingent, while conceding that some conditions involve genuine suffering — and noting that no data can establish the evaluative claim that one kind of life is worth more. Chapters 6 and 7 examine hidden costs (chiefly the expressivist harm) and who bears the pressure (disabled people first, then women, families, the poor, and communities with histories of coercion). Chapter 8 answers five strong counter-arguments, conceding the choice/coercion distinction and the seriousness of procreative beneficence while showing the core thesis survives. Chapter 9 offers case studies from the eugenics era and Aktion T4 to modern screening and the everyday "healthy baby" idiom. Chapter 10 maps adjacent biases; Chapter 11 sketches non-coercive reasoning (separate information from direction, correct the forecast, locate difficulty rightly, reform institutional defaults, refuse the ranking while keeping compassion); Chapter 12 concludes without triumphalism.

The measured takeaway: a value judgment of enormous weight — that some lives are more worth bringing into being — has been traveling undebated through clinics and idioms, wearing the face of health and love. Naming it asks no one to choose differently. It asks only that a culture eager for children stop quietly deciding, on everyone's behalf, which children are wanted.

② The book in twelve chapters

Each chapter opens with its own summary. Note chapter 2: every volume states the strongest case for the thing it criticises before laying a finger on it — and chapter 8 turns the critique back on itself.

CH 1Naming the bias

The bias examined in this book is the tendency, embedded within pronatalism, to treat the imperative to reproduce as implicitly qualified — to hear "have children" as "have healthy, normal children" — and to bring selective pressure against disability under the cover of that qualification. In one sentence: the selective-natalism bias is the smuggling of a eugenic logic into the pronatalist good, such that some lives are quietly counted as more worth bringing into being than others, while the whole arrangement presents itself as health, love, and prudent care.

CH 2The strongest case for it (steelman)The strongest case against this book

Before criticizing anything, I want to build the best and most sincere version of the selective impulse and hold it up whole. This is not a case made by villains. It is made, overwhelmingly, by people who love children and mean well, and it deserves a fair, non-strawman hearing.

CH 3Where it comes from

The eugenic logic inside natalism did not begin with prenatal testing. It has a long and specific ancestry, and naming it honestly means neither exaggerating nor softening that history.

CH 4How it operates today

The bias today rarely speaks the word "eugenics." It works through routine, defaults, and vocabulary — through practices that feel like ordinary good care. Consider the mechanisms.

CH 6The hidden costs

If the selective logic were merely a private matter of informed choice, it would need no critique. The reason it warrants one is that it carries costs — most of them borne not by the choosers but by disabled people who never chose the frame, and by the moral fabric everyone shares.

CH 12Conclusion

This book has traced a tension most of pronatalism does not know it contains. The culture that presses people toward parenthood speaks in a single warm word — more — but it very often means something narrower: more of the healthy, normal kind. Braided into the celebration of new life is a quiet apparatus of selection: routine screening, the vocabulary of risk and defect, the tragedy script, the conditional welcome of the "healthy baby." That apparatus sorts prospective lives by ability while presenting itself as care, and the name for its underlying logic, however gently it now dresses, is eugenic.

Selective-natalism bias
the tendency, inside pronatalism, to hear "have children" as "have healthy, normal children," bringing eugenic selective pressure against disability under cover of health and love. The bias lives in framing and aggregate, not in any individual choice.
Eugenics
the idea, named by Francis Galton in the nineteenth century (from Greek roots meaning "well-born"), that human heredity should be deliberately managed by encouraging reproduction of the "fit" and discouraging that of the "unfit." Note that it is pronatalist and selective at once.
Positive vs. negative eugenics
positive eugenics encourages reproduction among those deemed desirable; negative eugenics discourages or prevents it among those deemed undesirable. Historical eugenics programs combined both.
Expressivist objection
the disability-rights argument (associated with Adrienne Asch, Erik Parens, and others in Prenatal Testing and Disability Rights, 2000) that selecting against disability expresses a negative judgment about the worth of disabled lives, whether or not any individual intends that message.
Procreative beneficence
the philosophical principle (Julian Savulescu) that prospective parents have reason to select, among possible children, the one expected to have the best life, including on grounds of health. A serious position the book engages seriously and disputes; not endorsed.
Liberal / "new" eugenics
selection carried out by individuals through prenatal testing and reproductive technology rather than by state coercion. Defended as freely chosen; criticized for reproducing eugenic outcomes through the aggregate of framed choices.
Medical model of disability
the view that disability is a defect located in the individual body, to be prevented, corrected, or cured.
Social model of disability
the view that much disabling disadvantage arises from society's arrangements — inaccessibility, discrimination, absent support — rather than from the body itself.
Disability paradox
the robust empirical finding that disabled people report far higher wellbeing and life satisfaction than non-disabled people predict; non-disabled observers systematically overestimate the badness of disabled lives.
Non-directive counseling
the ideal of presenting reproductive information without steering the decision; structurally difficult to achieve when a test's architecture presumes a decision in one direction.
Aktion T4
the Nazi program that systematically murdered disabled children and adults deemed "unworthy of life." Cited as the horror endpoint of eugenic logic and a warning about frameworks — never as an accusation against present-day individuals.
Buck v. Bell (1927)
the U.S. Supreme Court decision upholding compulsory sterilization of a woman deemed "unfit," notorious for the line "three generations of imbeciles are enough"; evidence of how mainstream eugenic logic once was.
Lebensborn
the Nazi positive-eugenic program that encouraged the breeding of children deemed racially desirable.
Tragedy script
the cultural narrative that codes disability as suffering, burden, and diminished life — a narrative disabled people frequently reject as not matching their experience — which pre-loads reproductive decisions before any conversation begins.
"As long as it's healthy"
the ordinary, well-meant phrase that installs a condition on the welcome of a future child; the selective logic compressed into four warm words.
Expressivist harm
the injury done by the message that selection sends, received by disabled people who did nothing to earn it: that lives like theirs are the ones society arranges not to have.

⑥ Sources

What this volume draws on. Where the evidence is thin or contested, the book says so rather than reaching for a number.

  • Francis Galton's coinage of the term "eugenics" (late nineteenth century), from Greek roots meaning "well-born."
  • Buck v. Bell, 274 U.S. 200 (1927). United States Supreme Court decision upholding a state's power to compel sterilization of a person deemed "unfit"; the majority opinion, written by Holmes, contains the notorious line "three generations of imbeciles are enough." The decision has never been formally overruled.
  • The Nazi "euthanasia" program (Aktion T4) and the systematic murder of disabled people, and the Lebensborn program of positive eugenics. For the documentary record, see the published materials of the United States Holocaust Memorial Museum and the standard scholarly histories of the period.
  • The social model of disability as a framework distinguishing impairment from socially produced disability. A well-established concept in disability studies, associated with British disability activism and with scholars including Mike Oliver.
  • The general historical fact of twentieth-century eugenics movements and compulsory sterilization laws in numerous countries, affecting tens of thousands of people. Well documented in mainstream historical scholarship.
  • The general demographic/clinical fact that the introduction of routine prenatal screening for certain conditions has been followed, in many countries, by a substantial decline in the live-birth prevalence of those conditions. The direction of the effect is well documented, though the size of it varies considerably by country and period, and the book leans on the direction rather than on any single figure.
Directions the volume points to (not pinned citations)
  • Erik Parens and Adrienne Asch, eds., Prenatal Testing and Disability Rights (Georgetown University Press, 2000). The foundational collection articulating the expressivist objection, and still the best single place to meet the argument in its strongest form.
  • Julian Savulescu, "Procreative Beneficence: Why We Should Select the Best Children," Bioethics (2001). The principal statement of the principle engaged in Chapters 2 and 8.
  • Tom Shakespeare, Disability Rights and Wrongs (2006). A leading and deliberately unsettling treatment of the social model and the ethics of selection, by a disabled scholar who declines the easy positions on either side (later revisited in Disability Rights and Wrongs Revisited).
  • Michael Sandel, The Case Against Perfection: Ethics in the Age of Genetic Engineering (Harvard University Press, 2007). On the ethics of enhancement and selection; adjacent to the "new eugenics" debate.
  • Daniel J. Kevles, In the Name of Eugenics: Genetics and the Uses of Human Heredity (1985). The standard history of the eugenics movement.
  • Adrienne Asch's essays on prenatal testing and the disability critique, scattered across public-health journals and bioethics collections. Asch is the indispensable voice here: a disabled bioethicist who defended abortion rights and criticized selective abortion, and who refused to let either commitment soften the other.
  • See the medicalization literature — adjacent context for how the language of "health" launders value judgments (see Book 019).
