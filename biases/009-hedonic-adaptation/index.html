BIAS 009

You'll Get Used to It: Hedonic Adaptation and the Parenthood Promise

Part I · Cognitive & Psychological Biases · Hedonic Adaptation and the Parenthood Promise

The bias: Ignoring how quickly both joys and hardships of parenting normalize.

Continued pleasant experience actually raises the standard against which fresh experiences are evaluated." — Philip Brickman & Donald Campbell, describing the treadmill, 1971 (paraphrased; see references) ## Preface to this volume There is a phrase that follows expectant and new parents everywhere, offered as reassurance, as prophecy, and sometimes as gentle warning: you'll get used to it. Said of the sleepless nights, it is a comfort. Said of the diapers, the tantrums, the shrinking of an evening down to the size of a bedtime routine, it is a promise that the hard part passes. But the same three words, turned the other way, describe something the culture almost never says aloud: you'll get used to the good part too. The gaze of a newborn, the first word, the small hand in yours: these also lose their power to move you as they did the first time. Adaptation is not selective. It sands down both the peaks and the valleys of experience toward a personal baseline that is remarkably, sometimes maddeningly, stable. This book is about that fact and about a bias that flows from ignoring it. The bias is the failure to price in adaptation when imagining parenthood. When people picture life with a child, they tend to imagine a changed emotional world: a durable warmth, a permanent enlargement of joy, a self transformed for good. They are, in this, half-right and half-wrong in a specific and important way. The joy is real. The transformation is real. What is not real is the permanence of the feeling at full intensity. Human beings habituate. The psychology of well-being calls this hedonic adaptation, or the hedonic treadmill: after most major life changes, positive or negative, momentary happiness drifts back toward where it started. We run harder to stay in the same place. The natalist intuition that this book examines is therefore not a lie but an incomplete forecast. The incompleteness cuts both ways. If the imagined lasting glow is overstated, so is the feared lasting misery. The person who declines parenthood because they cannot imagine surviving the exhaustion is making the same error in the opposite direction: they, too, would adapt. A clear-eyed account has to hold both halves at once. That is why this volume is not, and cannot honestly be, an argument that parenthood "isn't worth it." It is an argument that the emotional accounting most of us do, on either side of the decision, is systematically distorted by our blindness to habituation. It helps, at the outset, to be precise about what "adaptation" does and does not mean here. It does not mean that a parent stops loving a child, or that the relationship hollows out, or that experience becomes grey. It means something narrower and stranger: that the emotional charge attached to a fixed circumstance tends to diminish as the circumstance becomes familiar, so that the same objective situation delivers, over time, a smaller and smaller jolt of feeling. The love can be undiminished while the jolt fades. Most of the confusion in this territory comes from collapsing those two things, treating the softening of the jolt as if it were the cooling of the love. It is not, and the book takes some care to keep them apart. The stance of this series is critical of pronatalist bias and evidence-first, and it steelmans the natalist case before criticizing it. Here that discipline matters especially, because hedonic adaptation contains a real escape hatch: meaning may not adapt the way mood does. The moment-to-moment pleasantness of an experience and its felt significance are different quantities, tracked by different measures, and they behave differently over time. A life can grow in meaning even as its daily emotional temperature returns to baseline. Any honest treatment of the parenthood promise has to take that possibility seriously rather than smuggle it away. We will. The book proceeds along the series template. Chapter 1 names the bias and separates it from its neighbors, affective forecasting (Book 004) and optimism bias (Book 003), which concern the machinery of prediction and directional over-optimism, whereas this book concerns return-to-baseline over time. Chapter 2 builds the strongest natalist case that adaptation does not undermine the choice to parent. Chapters 3 through 5 trace where the bias comes from, how it operates in ordinary language and expectation, and what the well-being evidence actually shows: the lottery-winner and accident-victim studies, set-point theory and its revision, the adaptation research on marriage and widowhood. Chapters 6 and 7 examine the hidden costs and the unequal distribution of the pressure. Chapter 8 turns the critique on itself and engages the best objections, including the meaning-versus-mood distinction. Chapters 9 through 11 offer illustrations, map adjacent biases, and describe what reasoning free of this particular distortion looks like. Chapter 12 closes without triumph. A last word on tone. Nothing here is a verdict on any parent or any child. Adaptation is not a flaw in parents; it is a feature of the human nervous system, as universal as the need for sleep it interacts with. To notice that we get used to things is not to diminish the things. It is only to ask that the promise be stated in full, both clauses of you'll get used to it, so that whoever is deciding can decide with open eyes. Nothing in what follows should be read as advice to have or not have children. The subject is not the choice but the forecast the choice is made on, and the aim throughout is to make that forecast truer, in both its hopeful and its fearful directions. ## 1. Naming the bias The bias this book names is the hedonic-adaptation blind spot in reproductive expectation: the systematic failure to account for the fact that, after the transition to parenthood, both the anticipated joys and the anticipated hardships will tend to normalize toward a personal emotional baseline over time. Stated as a one-sentence thesis: we imagine parenthood as a permanent shift in our emotional level, when it is far more often a temporary excursion followed by a return toward where we started, and this is true of the good and the bad alike. Take the psychology first. Hedonic adaptation is the process by which the emotional impact of a stimulus or circumstance diminishes with repeated or prolonged exposure. The hedonic treadmill is a metaphor for one consequence of that process: as our circumstances improve, our expectations and desires rise in step, so that objective gains yield little durable gain in subjective happiness. We walk forward but stay in place. A related and more contested idea is the set point: the notion that each person has a characteristic baseline level of well-being, substantially influenced by temperament and heredity, to which they tend to return after life's ups and downs. It is worth pausing on why these three ideas, though related, are not the same. Adaptation is a process, something the nervous system does. The treadmill is a metaphor for one consequence of that process under conditions of rising aspiration. The set point is a quantity, the baseline the process pulls toward. A person could accept that adaptation is real while doubting that the pull is always toward a single fixed number, and indeed the honest reading of the modern literature lands roughly there: the process is well-attested, the metaphor is useful, and the fixed-number version of the set point is the part that has needed the most revision. The bias this book targets does not depend on the strongest version of any of the three. It depends only on the modest, well-supported claim that feeling fades toward some relatively stable baseline, and that we routinely forget it will. This bias has to be separated cleanly from two neighbors in the series, because they are easy to blur. Book 004 concerns affective forecasting, the machinery by which we predict our future feelings, and its known errors, such as impact bias (overestimating the intensity and duration of future emotional reactions). Adaptation is one cause of forecasting error, but the forecasting book is about the prediction apparatus in general. This book brackets prediction and focuses on the actual temporal dynamics of feeling after the event: the return-to-baseline itself, whether or not anyone predicted it. One could, in principle, forecast one's future feelings perfectly and adaptation would still occur; conversely, one could forecast terribly about a circumstance to which no adaptation happens. The two ideas are logically independent even though, in practice, they meet constantly. Book 003 concerns optimism bias, the tendency to expect better outcomes for oneself than the evidence warrants, a directional tilt toward rosy expectations. Adaptation is not directional in that sense. It flattens both good and bad. An adaptation-blind pessimist who dreads permanent misery is making an error just as real as the adaptation-blind optimist who anticipates permanent bliss. This symmetry is the single most distinctive feature of the bias, and the reason it cannot be folded into optimism bias without losing something. Optimism bias can only explain the inflated hope; it has nothing to say about the inflated dread. Adaptation-blindness explains both, because it is a claim about the shape of the trajectory rather than its direction. So the distinctive claim of Book 009 is narrow. It is not that people predict badly in general (that is 004), nor that they lean too sunny (that is 003). It is that people treat the emotional consequences of parenthood as permanent when the robust finding across well-being research is that most such consequences decay toward baseline, often within a few years, and that this decay is roughly symmetric. The parenthood promise, and the parenthood dread, both quietly assume a level shift where the data show a spike and a settling. One clarification prevents a caricature. "Return toward baseline" is not "return exactly to baseline for everyone." The research has grown more nuanced: baselines can move, adaptation is often incomplete, some events (notably long-term unemployment and, in some studies, widowhood) show only partial recovery, and there are meaningful individual differences. The bias is not corrected by replacing "permanent change" with "no change." It is corrected by installing the right default expectation: a strong pull back toward baseline, of uncertain completeness, operating on both the joys and the burdens, with the open question, taken up later, of whether meaning obeys the same law as mood. The reader who leaves this chapter with a single sentence should leave with that one: not "nothing lasts," but "expect a spike, then a settling, on both sides, and hold the question of meaning open.

① In brief

Hedonic Adaptation and the Parenthood Promise

This volume examines a specific bias in how people imagine life with children: the failure to account for hedonic adaptation, the well-documented tendency of both positive and negative feelings to drift back toward a personal baseline over time. The phrase "you'll get used to it" is usually offered about the hardships of parenting: the sleeplessness, the tantrums, the lost freedom. But adaptation is not selective. We get used to the joys too. The imagined permanent glow of parenthood, and the feared permanent misery, both fade toward baseline. The bias is the failure to price this in on either side.

The book locates its target against two neighbors in the series. It is not about the prediction machinery that misforecasts our future feelings; that is affective forecasting (Book 004). And it is not about the directional tilt toward rosy expectations; that is optimism bias (Book 003). This book is about the actual temporal dynamics: the return-to-baseline itself, and the fact that it is roughly symmetric, flattening good and bad alike. That symmetry is why the book cannot honestly be read as an argument against having children. The same treadmill that fades the joy also rescues the parent from the dread.

Read the rest of the overview

The steelman is presented first and in full. Adaptation is the parent's friend: it means the exhausting phase genuinely passes. Baseline is a mildly positive place to return to. Children resupply novelty, first words, first steps, that partially resets habituation. And, most powerfully, meaning may not sit on the treadmill: eudaimonic significance is measured differently from moment-to-moment mood and may not adapt the same way. A life can grow in meaning even as its daily emotional temperature returns to baseline.

The evidence is presented honestly, including its limits. The founding lottery-winner and accident-victim study (Brickman, Coates & Janoff-Bulman, 1978) is described carefully and shown to be routinely overstated. Set-point theory (Lykken & Tellegen) is presented alongside its major revision by Diener, Lucas, and colleagues: baselines are stable and partly heritable, but not fixed; adaptation is often incomplete and uneven. The longitudinal life-event research is the key evidence: the happiness boost from marriage typically fades toward baseline within a few years, while widowhood and unemployment show only partial recovery, an important asymmetry. Panel research on the transition to parenthood shows a similar spike-then-settling pattern, with wide individual variation.

The hidden costs are the mispricing of the parenthood decision on inflated inputs; the disappointment tax charged to parents who experience ordinary settling and mistake it for personal failure; the re-up trap of pursuing more of a faded joy; and the way adaptation-blindness also understates the alternatives to parenthood. The pressure to deny adaptation falls hardest on those with the least buffer: disproportionately on women sold the permanent-maternal-bliss script, on parents under material stress for whom the "hard part" is less likely to be a passing phase, and on caregivers in genuinely non-remitting situations.

The book argues against itself squarely in Chapter 8, conceding that the "meaning is not mood" objection forces the thesis into its defensible form: the feeling adapts; whether the significance does is genuinely open. The correction it recommends is not a different decision but a better-priced one: forecast the ordinary plateau rather than the peak, apply the correction symmetrically to deflate dread as much as glow, separate the two currencies of feeling and meaning, and say the whole promise out loud so the disappointment tax is never charged to someone who simply, and universally, got used to it.

The measured takeaway is non-coercive: notice that we habituate, and choose the surviving, steady-state life with open eyes, not the peak that was always going to fade.

CH 2The strongest case for it (steelman)The strongest case against this book

Before criticizing the adaptation-blind view, we owe it its strongest form. And the strongest form is strong enough that a fair reader should feel its pull. This chapter is not a straw man to be knocked down in the next; it is the case the rest of the book must genuinely contend with, and much of it will survive.

CH 3Where it comes from

Why do we expect emotional permanence where the evidence shows return-to-baseline? The blind spot has several converging sources, none of them foolish. Understanding them matters, because a bias that is merely a mistake can be corrected by information, but a bias woven into the architecture of the nervous system and reinforced by culture requires a more patient kind of undoing.

CH 4How it operates today

The blind spot does not usually announce itself as a belief about emotional permanence. It operates through ordinary language, defaults, and scripts, most of which never mention feelings-over-time at all. This is what makes it hard to catch: it is not a proposition anyone defends, but a background assumption smuggled in through grammar and habit.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

What does the well-being research actually establish, and, just as important, what does it not? The stance of this series requires stating the uncertainty honestly, and the adaptation literature is a place where honesty means resisting the tidy summary as much as the tidy dismissal.

CH 6The hidden costs

If the blind spot merely made people slightly too cheerful about parenthood, it would be harmless. Its costs are more specific, and several of them fall on people who did nothing wrong except believe what they were told.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

Adaptation is a universal feature of human nervous systems. But the pressure to deny it, and the burden of the disappointment tax when it operates, is not distributed evenly. A bias that lands on everyone equally would be a matter of general education; this one lands hardest on those already carrying the most, which makes it also a matter of fairness.

CH 12Conclusion

The argument of this book can be stated in a single, chastened sentence. The joys and the hardships of parenthood both tend to normalize toward a personal baseline over time; the bias is to imagine the emotional consequences as permanent, and the correction is symmetric, deflating the imagined lasting glow and the imagined lasting misery alike.

Hedonic adaptation
The process by which the emotional impact of a circumstance diminishes with prolonged or repeated exposure, causing feelings to drift back toward a baseline. The central phenomenon of this book.
Hedonic treadmill
The metaphor for adaptation's consequence: as circumstances improve, expectations and desires rise in step, so objective gains yield little lasting gain in happiness. We run forward and stay in place.
Set point
A person's characteristic baseline level of well-being, partly heritable and relatively stable, toward which they tend to return after major life events.
Set-point theory (revised)
The updated view (Diener, Lucas, and colleagues) that baselines are stable and partly heritable but not fixed: they can shift with sustained circumstances, adaptation is often incomplete, and individuals differ in degree and even direction.
Return-to-baseline
The empirical tendency for well-being to move back toward its pre-event level after a major life change. Roughly symmetric across positive and negative events, but uneven.
Adaptation asymmetry
The finding that adaptation is uneven across events: some negatives (e.g., unemployment, widowhood) show only partial recovery, unlike many positives.
Adaptation level
The internal reference standard against which new experiences are judged. It rises with sustained good fortune, dimming ordinary pleasures — the mechanism behind the lottery-winner finding.
Affective (hedonic) well-being
Moment-to-moment positive and negative feeling. The quantity most clearly shown to adapt toward baseline.
Eudaimonic well-being
Purpose, meaning, and the sense that one's life matters. Measured separately from affect and possibly exempt from the treadmill — the hinge of the book's central open question.
Affective forecasting
Predicting one's own future emotions (Book 004). Distinct from adaptation, which is the actual temporal dynamic that forecasting often fails to anticipate.
Impact bias
Overestimating the intensity and duration of future emotional reactions; a forecasting error (Book 004) caused partly by neglecting adaptation.
Optimism bias
The directional tilt toward expecting good outcomes for oneself (Book 003). Distinct from adaptation, which flattens good and bad alike, and so also distorts the pessimist.
Peak-end pattern
The tendency to remember experiences by their most intense moments and their endings, biasing parenthood's remembered emotional profile toward un-adapted peaks.
Anticipatory savoring
Enjoyment drawn from imagining future positive events. Composed of un-adapted first-times, so it systematically omits the adapted plateau where most of life is lived.
The plateau
The steady-state emotional level reached after adaptation has done its work; where most of parenthood's hours actually fall, and the informative part of the distribution to forecast.
The disappointment tax
The unnecessary guilt or sense of failure imposed when ordinary adaptation is culturally reframed as a personal deficiency, especially in new parents.
The re-up trap
The pull to reproduce the conditions of a faded joy (e.g., another child) in order to recover an intensity that adaptation was always going to erode.
Eternal-present grammar
The linguistic habit of describing parenthood's joys in permanent, superlative terms ("children are the greatest joy") while describing its burdens in temporal terms ("this stage will pass"), installing a false asymmetry.
