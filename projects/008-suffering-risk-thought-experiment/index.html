PROJECT 008

Suffering Risk Thought Experiment

Philosophy · Difficulty: Intermediate · ~11 min

This is a reflection exercise, not a test or a forecast. It explores one of the hardest questions in birth ethics: when you decide for a future person who cannot choose, how much risk of suffering is acceptable? The simulator makes the trade-off visible instead of automatic.

This is philosophical reflection, not a prediction about any real person or pregnancy. The numbers are tools for thinking, not forecasts — and this is not medical, legal, financial, or mental health advice.

What you will learn

  • How four factors — serious suffering, ordinary hardship, strong happiness, and uncertainty — shape the picture.
  • How a risk/benefit "tension" reads when you weight serious harm more heavily.
  • The main philosophical lenses: expected value, precaution, and non-aggregation.
  • Why thoughtful people disagree here without bad faith on any side.

3. Thought-experiment cards

Four lenses. Tap each to open.

The veil of ignorance

If you did not know which life you'd be born into — the luckiest or the hardest — what level of risk would you accept before agreeing to the gamble?

Deciding for another

Taking a risk for yourself is one thing. Does choosing on behalf of someone who cannot be asked raise the bar for justification?

Certainty vs probability

Does a guaranteed small harm feel morally different from a possible large one, even if the "expected" amount of harm is the same?

Non-aggregation

Can one person's joy really "cancel out" another person's severe suffering? If not, averages and expected values may miss something that matters.

4. Frames philosophers use

Expected valueWeigh each outcome by its probability and sum. A likely good life can outweigh a small chance of a bad one. PrecautionSome harms are severe enough to avoid imposing any real risk of, regardless of upside. Non-aggregationOne person's severe suffering may not be offset by another's joy — so totals can mislead.

Key takeaway

The difficulty here does not come from bad faith on any side — it comes from genuinely competing intuitions about risk, certainty, and deciding for someone else. Whatever your sliders showed, you have done the thing the exercise asks: you made the trade-off visible instead of automatic.
