PROJECT 004

Parenthood Assumption Quiz

Society · Difficulty: Beginner–Intermediate · ~25 min · interactive toolkit

Everyday talk about parenthood is full of quiet assumptions — premises we absorb so early we never notice we are holding them. This toolkit helps you name the assumption behind a phrase, examine whether it actually holds, map which assumptions shaped you, trace where each one came from, and walk a single belief through a calm, Socratic examination. The goal is never to tell you what to choose — only to help you hold your beliefs on purpose, rather than by default.

① What an assumption is — and why it hides

Assumption — a belief we treat as obviously true without ever examining it. Assumptions aren't lies or even mistakes; many turn out to be reasonable. The trouble is only that an unexamined belief is one we hold by default rather than by choice — and the biggest decisions in life deserve to be chosen.

When someone says "you'll understand when you're older" or "a family isn't complete without children," they rarely state the premise underneath. The premise just rides along, unspoken and unquestioned. Examining it isn't about catching anyone out or proving them wrong. It's about turning a quiet "of course" into a question you're allowed to answer for yourself.

Why assumptions are so hard to see

They feel like factsA belief repeated since childhood stops feeling like a belief and starts feeling like simple reality — "just the way things are." Social proofWhen almost everyone seems to share a premise, questioning it feels not just difficult but slightly strange, even rude. Status-quo biasThe familiar, default option gets a free pass; the road not taken has to justify itself while the well-worn one never does. Description posing as prescription"Most people have children" (a description) quietly becomes "so you should too" (a prescription) — but the second doesn't follow from the first.

The one move that matters: examine, don't reject

This toolkit is not trying to talk you out of anything. Examining a belief has three honest outcomes, and all of them are wins: you keep it (now as a real choice), you adjust it, or you set it down. A belief you've looked at squarely is yours either way. A few questions do most of the work:

The examiner's questions

  • Whose voice is this? Where did I first hear it, and do I still agree with the source?
  • Is it a description or a prescription? Is it telling me what is, or smuggling in what I should do?
  • What's the evidence? If I treat it as a claim about the world, does it actually hold up?
  • What if the opposite were true? Can I imagine a good life that breaks this rule? (Almost always, yes.)
  • Who benefits from my believing it? Me, or someone else's comfort or expectations?

You'll use exactly these questions on a belief of your own in section ⑤.

Key takeaway

Noticing an assumption is not about catching anyone out — least of all yourself. It is about giving everyone a little more room. A belief you can name, trace, and examine is one you actually own, whatever you decide to do with it. Keep it, adjust it, or set it down: the point is that the choice is finally, fully yours.
