BIAS 039

No One Regrets Their Children: The Regret-Asymmetry Myth

Part III · Cultural & Social Scripts · The Regret-Asymmetry Myth

The bias: The claim that parental regret doesn't exist while childfree regret is certain.

"Regret is a life force." — Orna Donath, Regretting Motherhood (2015), paraphrasing the paradoxical stance of her interviewees, who love their children yet regret the role.
① In brief ② The book in twelve chapters ③ Key terms ④ Ten-question check ⑤ Reflection ⑥ Sources ⑦ Related biases

① In brief

Every conversation about whether to have children eventually meets the same reassurance, offered as settled fact: no one regrets their children, usually paired with its mirror, but people who don't have children regret it, and by then it's too late. Together these two claims form what this book calls the regret asymmetry: the belief that parental regret essentially does not exist while regret over not having children is close to inevitable. The two halves travel as a package, and the package has a job. It converts an irreversible, uncertain, high-stakes decision into an apparently one-sided bet, making reproduction look like the choice you cannot get wrong and childlessness the gamble against near-certain future sorrow.

The book's thesis is deliberately narrow and handled with care. It does not claim that parents secretly regret their children; by every honest measure most do not, and measured regret rates are low. It claims that the asymmetry is overstated in both halves at once. Parental regret is real, systematically silenced by taboo, and undercounted; childfree regret is also real but routinely oversold, generalised from vivid exceptions and projected onto people whose actual later testimony is far more contented than the warning implies. Correct both halves and the clean lopsided ledger dissolves into something messier and more honest: two ordinary human paths, each with its own distribution of later feeling, neither insured, neither doomed.

The treatment is sensitive throughout. Regret about a child is among the most stigmatised feelings a person can hold, because a child is a specific, present, beloved person, and regretting the role of parenthood is agonisingly easy to misread as regretting that person. Following the sociologist Orna Donath's pioneering work, the book keeps these strictly distinct: her interviewees loved their children fiercely and wished they had not become mothers. Nothing here judges any parent or any child; the target is a claim about the distribution of a feeling and the rhetorical use that claim is put to when someone is deciding.

Read the rest of the overview

Chapter 2 gives the natalist case its strongest form first and in full, because much of it is genuinely defensible: the measured parental-regret rate really is low; the low rate may reflect real love rather than mere suppression (love makes "do you regret parenthood?" partly incoherent, since it is heard as "do you wish your child gone?"); involuntary childlessness does produce documented grief; and the action/inaction regret literature (Gilovich & Medvec) suggests foregone options may sting more over the long run. The book concedes all of this. Its disagreement is that each point is narrower and more confounded than the confident slogan implies, and that even granting them fully, they license humility about the true distribution, not the clean, lopsided ledger.

The evidence chapter shows the two columns are measured under opposite pressures: parental regret under a taboo that depresses reporting, childfree regret under an expectation that inflates its salience, so the observed asymmetry is in significant part an artefact of disclosure conditions. The second half survives largely by conflating the involuntarily childless (who grieve) with the voluntarily childfree (who, on the evidence, mostly do not regret). The costs of reasoning from the rigged ledger fall on everyone: a skewed forecast at the moment of decision, an isolated minority of regretful parents told they cannot exist, a childfree majority made to live under a prophecy of regret, a corrupted communal conversation, and support misrouted away from those who need it.

The book stays firmly in its lane. Book 008 (survivorship bias) supplies the sampling mechanism that empties the parenthood column; Book 029 (the pity script) supplies the social engine that fills the childless column; Book 003 (optimism bias) sits upstream. This volume's distinctive subject is the comparative claim itself: the lopsided ledger, its evidence, and its use in decisions.

The measured takeaway is modest by design. The asymmetry does not survive as a clean lopsided ledger, but neither does it invert. What survives is two uninsured paths: mostly contentment on both, some silent difficulty on one, some real grief on the other, and no guarantee either way. Naming the asymmetry tells no one what to choose; it returns to them the honest uncertainty within which choosing is genuinely their own.

② The book in twelve chapters

Each chapter opens with its own summary. Note chapter 2: every volume states the strongest case for the thing it criticises before laying a finger on it — and chapter 8 turns the critique back on itself.

CH 1Naming the bias

The regret asymmetry is a belief about the distribution of two feelings across two life paths. Stated fully, it holds that (a) parental regret is vanishingly rare, perhaps nonexistent, so that a person who has children is effectively insured against later wishing otherwise; and (b) regret over not having children is common, deep, and close to inevitable, arriving late when it can no longer be remedied. The two halves are not usually asserted separately. They travel together as a package, and the package has a function: it converts an irreversible, high-stakes, uncertain decision into an apparently one-sided bet. If one path cannot be regretted and the other almost certainly will be, there is nothing to deliberate. You simply take the insured option.

CH 3Where it comes from

The regret asymmetry did not appear from nowhere, and understanding its origins helps explain why it feels less like a contestable claim and more like common sense. Several deep currents converge to produce it, and none of them is primarily about the actual distribution of later feeling.

CH 4How it operates today

Origins explain where the asymmetry came from; this chapter describes the concrete machinery through which it acts on an actual person deciding today. The machinery is ordinary, largely invisible, and remarkably effective, and it works through language, defaults, timing, and the selective circulation of stories.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

What does the actual evidence show about the two halves of the asymmetry? Less than the slogan claims, and with far more confounding, but not nothing, and honesty requires holding all of that at once. This chapter states what the data do and do not support, and where the uncertainty is irreducible.

CH 6The hidden costs

If the asymmetry were merely inaccurate it would be a curiosity. It matters because reasoning from it imposes real costs, and those costs fall even if parental regret is genuinely rare, because the harm lies in the lopsidedness of the ledger, not in any particular rate. Several costs deserve naming.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

The asymmetry does not press on everyone equally. Both of its halves are heavier for some people than others, and naming the distribution is a test of the thesis, because a genuinely structural bias will fall along predictable lines rather than at random. A bias that fell randomly would be noise; a bias that falls systematically on the already-burdened is a structure, and the pattern of who bears it tells us something about what the bias is for. If the asymmetry served truth, it would press equally on everyone who reasons badly; that it presses hardest on those with the least room to resist it is a sign that it serves an interest instead.

CH 8The counter-arguments to the critiqueObjections to the critique

A book that steelmans its opponents must steelman the objections to itself. Several serious challenges to this critique deserve honest engagement, and answering them is what keeps the thesis bounded rather than a licence to dismiss any inconvenient testimony.

CH 9Case studies / illustrations

Several illustrations show the asymmetry at work. They are meant to make the mechanism visible, not to establish a rate. A ledger you can watch being drawn is one you can watch for in your own reasoning.

CH 10Adjacent biases

The regret asymmetry does not stand alone; it interlocks with several other biases in this series, and mapping the joints clarifies what belongs to this book and what belongs to its neighbours. I map the connections without duplicating their content.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

What does clear-eyed reasoning about regret look like once the asymmetry is named? Not a counter-slogan: "actually, parents regret children and the childless don't" would be as false and as lopsided as the original. Seeing past the asymmetry means refusing both clean columns and holding, instead, a more honest and more uncertain picture. Several practices follow, for individuals and for institutions.

CH 12Conclusion

I have argued that the regret asymmetry, the paired belief that parental regret essentially does not exist while childfree regret is near-certain, is overstated in both of its halves at once, and that its persuasive power comes from the lopsided ledger the two overstatements jointly produce. Let me restate carefully what was and was not claimed, because on a subject this sensitive the boundaries are the argument.

Show all 15 terms as a list
Regret asymmetry
The paired belief that parental regret essentially does not exist while regret over not having children is near-certain; the subject of this book.
Lopsided ledger
The metaphor for the asymmetry's structure: parenthood in one column marked regret-proof, childlessness in the other marked regret-guaranteed. The lopsidedness, not any single rate, does the persuasive work.
Role/person conflation
The confusion, exploited by the asymmetry's first half, between regretting the role of parenthood and wishing a specific, loved child had not existed. The two are distinct, and most parents refuse only the second.
Role-level regret
Regret about having taken on the parental role, held simultaneously with love for one's actual child; the feeling documented by Orna Donath.
Disclosure conditions
The social pressures under which a feeling is reported or narrated. Parental regret is reported under a taboo (which depresses counts); childless regret is narrated under an expectation of regret (which inflates its salience).
Social-desirability bias
The tendency to give the socially approved answer even in anonymous settings, because the respondent is still an audience to their own reply.
Wording sensitivity
The substantial movement of measured regret rates with question phrasing; a signature of a construct not being measured cleanly.
Voluntary vs. involuntary childlessness
The distinction the asymmetry's second half erases. Documented grief concentrates among those who wanted children and could not have them, not among those who freely chose otherwise.
Action/inaction regret
The finding (Gilovich & Medvec) that short-run regret favours actions while long-run regret favours inactions. Invoked for the asymmetry's second half but not cleanly applicable to the love-transformed choice of childlessness.
Selective circulation
The propagation of stories that confirm the ledger (the joyful parent, the "too-late" childless person) and the non-propagation of those that do not (the ambivalent parent, the contented childfree elder).
Dismissal machinery
The standing rules ("she doesn't mean it"; "deep down they'll regret it") that discount any testimony contradicting the ledger, rendering it near-unfalsifiable in casual discourse.
Prophecy of regret
The projected future sorrow installed over the childfree by the asymmetry's second half, colonising present contentment with predicted future collapse.
Existence proof
Qualitative testimony (e.g. Donath's) that demonstrates a feeling is real without claiming a rate; sufficient to falsify the strong "no one" form of the first half.
Reversibility asymmetry
The genuine fact that childlessness becomes irreversible once fertile years pass, which the bias quietly promotes into an asymmetry of regret.
Survivorship filter
(See Book 008.) The sampling mechanism by which regretful parents drop out of the visible testimony pool; the engine beneath the asymmetry's first half, referenced here but not this book's subject.

⑥ Sources

What this volume draws on. Where the evidence is thin or contested, the book says so rather than reaching for a number.

  • Donath, Orna. Regretting Motherhood: A Study. North Atlantic Books, 2017 (originally published in German/Hebrew contexts, 2015). — Foundational qualitative documentation of role-level parental regret: mothers who love their children yet regret becoming mothers. Central to Chapters 1, 5, and 9.
  • Kahneman, Daniel. Thinking, Fast and Slow. Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2011. — On affective forecasting, adaptation (the hedonic treadmill), and the limits of self-prediction; background for the forecasting arguments in Chapters 5 and 11.
  • Benatar, David. Better Never to Have Been: The Harm of Coming into Existence. Oxford University Press, 2006. — Cited only as adjacent context for asymmetry arguments in the philosophy of reproduction; this book takes no antinatalist position and does not rely on Benatar's conclusions.
  • Kuran, Timur. Private Truths, Public Lies: The Social Consequences of Preference Falsification. Harvard University Press, 1995. — On the gap between privately held and publicly expressed preferences; relevant to the "disclosure conditions" argument in Chapter 5.
Directions the volume points to (not pinned citations)
  • Gilovich, Thomas, and Victoria H. Medvec. "The Experience of Regret: What, When, and Why." Psychological Review, 1995. — The action-versus-inaction regret distinction: the short-run regret of things done, the long-run regret of things left undone.
  • Survey research on parental regret in Germany and elsewhere, much of it prompted by the #regrettingmotherhood discourse. Press coverage has circulated headline shares — "roughly one in five" and others — but the number is acutely phrasing-dependent, and no fixed rate should be read out of it.
  • Longitudinal and cross-sectional studies of later-life regret among the voluntarily childfree, as distinct from the involuntarily childless. The distinction is the one most often collapsed in popular argument, and the one that matters most.
  • Clinical-psychology literature on grief and distress among the involuntarily childless and among infertility patients. This book draws on it only to establish that involuntary childlessness produces real grief; it says nothing about the voluntarily childfree.
  • Popular accounts of end-of-life reflection and "top regrets," which often list family and relationships. These lists are widely repeated and methodologically thin; they belong in a book like this one as anecdote, not evidence.
  • The #regrettingmotherhood discourse (post-2015), which documents both the reality of role-level parental regret and the ferocity of the taboo against voicing it. Chapter 9 refers to it descriptively; no individual post or poster is cited, and none should be.
