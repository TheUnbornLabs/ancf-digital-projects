The Asymmetry They Skip: Benatar and the Harm of Coming to Be

Part VII · Philosophical & Ethical Fallacies · Benatar and the Harm of Coming to Be

The bias: The overlooked argument that non-existence harms no one.

"Coming into existence is always a serious harm." — David Benatar, Better Never to Have Been (2006)
① In brief ② The book in twelve chapters ③ Key terms ④ Ten-question check ⑤ Reflection ⑥ Sources ⑦ Related biases

① In brief

Benatar and the Harm of Coming to Be

This volume opens Part VII of the series (Philosophical & Ethical Fallacies) and serves as its keystone: several later books reference it. Its subject is not the claim "life is bad" (the series takes no such position) but a procedural bias about a specific argument.

The bias named. The book targets what it calls the asymmetry-skip: the near-universal tendency of pronatalist reasoning to bypass, misstate, or prematurely dismiss the axiological asymmetry between pain and pleasure, and thereby to treat the ethics of coming into existence as if the strongest anti-procreative argument did not exist. The thesis is deliberately modest: pronatalism has not refuted the asymmetry; it has mostly skipped it, and a bias that survives only by not being confronted deserves confronting. The book explicitly does not claim the asymmetry is true.

Read the rest of the overview

The argument, stated exactly. Following David Benatar's Better Never to Have Been (2006), the asymmetry rests on four claims about a possible person: (1) the presence of pain is bad; (2) the presence of pleasure is good; (3) the absence of pain is good even if no one exists to enjoy that good; (4) the absence of pleasure is not bad unless there is someone for whom it is a deprivation. Claims 1–2 are symmetric; the whole argument turns on the asymmetry between 3 and 4. Comparing existence (pain present = bad; pleasure present = good) with never-being (pain absent = good; pleasure absent = merely not-bad), coming into existence carries a real harm with no compensating benefit. The book stresses what the argument is not: not a claim that existing lives are not worth continuing, not the non-consent argument (book 080), not the gift-of-life fallacy (book 081), and not the hedonic-balance claim that lives contain more pain than pleasure.

Structure. Twelve chapters follow the series template. Chapter 1 names the bias and states the argument exactly. Chapter 2 gives the strongest pro-natal / anti-asymmetry steelman first: near-universal reflective endorsement of one's existence, the person-affecting denial of premise 3, an alternative explanation of Benatar's supporting intuitions, and the "lives worth living" reply. Chapters 3–4 trace the skip's origins (selection, religion, economics, optimism bias) and its five everyday mechanisms (default-and-burden shift, worth-of-life conflation, glad-to-exist testimony as a stopper, hedonic-balance substitution, linguistic pre-loading). Chapter 5 handles evidence carefully: the core axiological claim is insulated from empirical test, but the Pollyanna/optimism-bias premise is empirically supported, undercutting the confident intuition against the argument without proving the argument. Chapters 6–7 cover the hidden costs of deciding-without-arguing and the uneven distribution of the skip's enforcement. Chapter 8 engages the serious objections, person-affecting, incommensurability, worth-continuing vs. worth-starting, fairly, reaching a split verdict: these may defeat the asymmetry, but they leave it a live, defended position, not a refuted one, and none of them is what does the work in everyday pronatalism. Chapter 9 offers four illustrations (advice column, philosophy seminar, pronatalist policy, private reckoning). Chapter 10 maps adjacent biases (080, 081, 084). Chapter 11 describes non-coerced, decision-neutral clarity. Chapter 12 concludes without triumphalism.

Stance and care. Critical of pronatalist bias, evidence-first, non-coercive. The book removes the bias without dictating the choice: one may meet the asymmetry, reject it on principled grounds, and have children with a better-grounded conscience, or find it weighty and choose otherwise. Because the material touches existence, harm, and the wish never to have been, the book flags its sensitive character and insists throughout on the distinction between the asymmetry (creation vs. never-being) and the separate question of whether existing lives are worth continuing.

② The book in twelve chapters

Each chapter opens with its own summary. Note chapter 2: every volume states the strongest case for the thing it criticises before laying a finger on it — and chapter 8 turns the critique back on itself.

CH 1Naming the bias

The bias this book names is the asymmetry-skip: the tendency of pronatalist reasoning to bypass, misstate, or prematurely dismiss the axiological asymmetry between pain and pleasure, and thereby to treat the ethics of coming into existence as if the strongest anti-procreative argument did not exist. The one-sentence thesis of this book is: pronatalism has not refuted the asymmetry argument; it has mostly skipped it, and a bias that survives only by not being confronted is a bias worth confronting.

CH 2The strongest case for it (steelman)The strongest case against this book

Fairness requires that before this book criticizes the skipping of the asymmetry, it state the strongest case for skipping it: the strongest reason to think the asymmetry is not a live threat that pronatalism must answer, and that ordinary confidence in the goodness of coming to be is warranted. I will put that case as powerfully as I can, and I will not pretend it is weak. If it is right, then the pronatalist who sets Benatar aside is not skipping anything; she is exercising sound judgment about an unsound argument, and this book's thesis fails at the first chapter of its own making.

CH 3Where it comes from

If the asymmetry argument is at least serious, why has pronatalist thought so rarely engaged it? The origins of the skip lie deeper than any individual failure of attention. They are structural, historical, and, fittingly for this series, cognitive. It helps to separate the layers, because each contributes something distinct and each is, on its own terms, entirely understandable. The skip is not a scandal of stupidity; it is the joint product of forces that were never aimed at the truth of the matter at all.

CH 4How it operates today

The asymmetry-skip is not a historical curiosity; it operates now, in ordinary reasoning about children, through a handful of concrete mechanisms. Naming them lets us catch the skip in the act. Each mechanism is a specific route by which the argument is prevented from surfacing as a claim that could be examined, and once you have the list, you begin to notice it everywhere: in conversations, in columns, in your own deliberations.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

What can evidence contribute to a dispute that is, fundamentally, about the structure of value rather than about facts? Less than partisans on either side wish, and more than the purely a priori character of the asymmetry might suggest. We must be careful about what the data can and cannot do, because the misuse of evidence is itself one of the skip's favorite disguises: nothing looks more like engagement, and does less of it, than a table of happiness statistics wheeled in to settle a question the statistics cannot reach.

CH 6The hidden costs

What does the asymmetry-skip cost, given that skipping an argument is not obviously expensive? The costs are of a peculiar, second-order kind: they are the costs of forming and enforcing a momentous decision without having met its strongest counter-argument. They are not the costs of being wrong, one can skip an argument and still land on the right answer, but the costs of not knowing whether one is right, and of building lives, policies, and a culture on a premise that was never allowed to be examined.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

Biases are not distributed evenly, and neither is the asymmetry-skip. Its burden and its enforcement fall differently across lines of gender, class, culture, and ability; mapping that distribution is part of taking the bias seriously as a social fact rather than a purely logical one. An argument, considered in the abstract, presses on no one; but a taboo against considering it presses hardest on those least able to bear the social cost of transgression, and the asymmetry-skip is enforced as much by taboo as by reason.

CH 8The counter-arguments to the critiqueObjections to the critique

This book's thesis is that pronatalism skips the asymmetry rather than refuting it. The honest objection is: perhaps it has been refuted, and the "skip" is really a considered rejection that the book is refusing to credit. This chapter presents the serious counter-arguments to the asymmetry, the ones that, if sound, mean pronatalism is entitled to set it aside, and engages them fairly. I want these to be strong, because if they are strong then the book's own thesis is qualified, and a thesis that cannot state its own best objection is guilty of exactly the skip it complains of.

CH 9Case studies / illustrations

Four illustrations show the asymmetry-skip operating, and show what non-skipping would look like. They are composites, not reports of particular individuals, chosen because each isolates a different setting in which the skip does its work: the intimate, the academic, the institutional, and the private. Reading them side by side makes visible what a single case obscures, that the same maneuver recurs across wildly different registers, always feeling appropriate to its setting, always leaving the argument unmet.

CH 10Adjacent biases

The asymmetry-skip does not stand alone. As the keystone of Part VII, this argument is referenced by several neighboring books, and it is worth mapping the boundaries so that each argument keeps its own work and none is made to do another's. The mapping matters for more than tidiness: many of the "refutations" of the asymmetry are really confusions of it with a neighbor, so that keeping the boundaries sharp is itself a defense against the skip.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

What does clear-eyed, non-coerced reasoning about the asymmetry look like, once the skip is named? Seeing past a bias does not mean adopting its opposite. The aim here is emphatically not to convert readers to antinatalism; the series is non-coercive, and the asymmetry remains contested. The aim is to reason about procreation with the strongest argument actually in view. What would that take, for individuals and for institutions?

CH 12Conclusion

This book argued a single, deliberately modest thesis: that pronatalism has not refuted the axiological asymmetry, but has mostly skipped it, through non-encounter, misstatement, or premature dismissal, and that a bias which survives only by not being confronted deserves to be confronted. I want to close by being exact about what has and has not been shown, because the temptation to overclaim is, here, its own version of the skip.

Show all 16 terms as a list
Axiological asymmetry
Benatar's core claim that the absence of pain is good even with no one to enjoy that good, while the absence of pleasure is not bad unless someone is thereby deprived; the asymmetry between premises 3 and 4 on which the whole argument rests.
Coming into existence
The event evaluated by comparing the resulting existing person with the alternative in which no such person is ever brought into being; the object of the asymmetry argument.
Presence of pain (premise 1)
The claim that pain, when present, is bad. The uncontroversial member of the pain pair.
Presence of pleasure (premise 2)
The claim that pleasure, when present, is good. The uncontroversial member of the pleasure pair.
Absence of pain (premise 3)
The claim that the absence of pain is good even if there is no one for whom it is good; the load-bearing and most contested premise.
Absence of pleasure (premise 4)
The claim that the absence of pleasure is not bad unless there is somebody for whom this absence is a deprivation.
Person-affecting view
The position that value must be value for someone; the basis of the strongest objection, which charges that premise 3 illicitly posits a subjectless good that premise 4 denies for pleasure.
Impersonal-comparative goodness
Benatar's reply that the goodness of absent pain is a comparison against the alternative in which pain is present, not a benefit conferred on a subject.
Worth continuing vs. worth starting
Benatar's distinction: many lives are worth continuing though, he argues by the asymmetry, none is worth starting.
Pollyanna principle / optimism bias
The tendency to over-rate one's well-being and prospects; Benatar's ground for treating glad-to-exist self-reports as unreliable evidence about the value of coming to be.
Hedonic-balance claim
The distinct empirical claim that lives contain more pain than pleasure; not required by the asymmetry and frequently confused with it.
Incommensurability
The view that existence and non-existence cannot be ranked in value at all; if true it defeats both the asymmetry and the cheerful pronatalist axiom.
The asymmetry-skip
This book's target: the tendency to bypass, misstate, or prematurely dismiss the asymmetry instead of refuting it.
Non-encounter / misstatement / premature dismissal
The three forms the skip takes: never meeting the argument; restating it as a straw man; or waving it away with a reply (e.g. "people are glad to exist") that answers a different question.
Default-and-burden shift
The mechanism by which having children is the presupposed default and only not having them requires justification, so the goodness of creating a person is never stated as a challengeable premise.
Antinatalism
The view that procreation is generally wrong or a harm; the asymmetry is one argument for it, but the two are not identical, and one can hold either without the other.

⑥ Sources

What this volume draws on. Where the evidence is thin or contested, the book says so rather than reaching for a number.

  • Benatar, David. Better Never to Have Been: The Harm of Coming into Existence. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2006. — The primary source. Origin of the axiological asymmetry (the four premises), the supporting asymmetries (procreational duties, retrospective judgments, distant vs. absent suffering, bringing suffering vs. failing to bring happiness), and the Pollyanna/optimism argument against rosy self-report.
  • Benatar, David, and David Wasserman. Debating Procreation: Is It Wrong to Reproduce? Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2015. — Benatar's anti-natal case and Wasserman's pro-natal reply in one volume; the natural steelman/counter pairing for Chapters 2 and 8.
  • Parfit, Derek. Reasons and Persons. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 1984. — Foundational work in population ethics and person-affecting reasoning; frames the person-affecting and incommensurability objections in Chapter 8.
  • Shiffrin, Seana Valentine. "Wrongful Life, Procreative Responsibility, and the Significance of Harm." Legal Theory 5 (1999). — An influential asymmetry-adjacent argument about the ethics of imposing existence on someone who cannot be asked.
  • Harman, Elizabeth. Critical study of Benatar's Better Never to Have Been, Noûs 43 (2009). — The best-known critical response, and the one Chapter 8 answers most directly.
  • Bradley, Ben. Well-Being and Death. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2009. — On the harm of death and person-affecting value; bears on the status of premise 3.
  • Broome, John. Weighing Lives. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2004. — Population ethics; the background for the objection that premise 3 requires a subject to be spared just as premise 4 requires a subject to be deprived.
Directions the volume points to (not pinned citations)
  • Belshaw, Christopher. "A New Argument for Anti-Natalism," South African Journal of Philosophy, and his later "Anti-Natalism and the Asymmetry" in the Journal of Controversial Ideas. — An antinatalist case built on foundations other than Benatar's, and a useful check on whether the asymmetry is load-bearing.
  • The psychology behind the Pollyanna premise — the research programs on optimism bias, the better-than-average effect, hedonic adaptation, affective forecasting, and the peak–end rule, which together support the claim that self-assessments of well-being read high. Cited here as a class of finding; the argument does not turn on any single effect size.
  • Note. The headline claims used in the text without a precise citation — that most people report being glad to exist, and that humans systematically over-rate their well-being — are treated as well-established classes of finding and attributed to the relevant research programs rather than to any single table. Nothing in the argument depends on a precise number, which is as it should be.
