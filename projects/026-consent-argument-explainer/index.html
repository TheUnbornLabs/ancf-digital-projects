PROJECT 026

Consent Argument Explainer

Philosophy · Difficulty: Intermediate · ~18 min · interactive

The consent argument asks a sharp question: how can it be permissible to impose the risks of a whole life on someone who could not possibly agree first? This explainer builds the idea up gently — an everyday analogy, a sorter that reveals exactly what makes birth different, the premises you can test, and the leading replies. It argues for no verdict.

① An everyday analogy② Is this valid consent?③ Test the premises④ The leading replies⑤ Quick check⑥ Reflection

① An everyday analogy

The core intuition — for almost anything significant we do to a person, we ask them first. Where we cannot, we proceed only with great caution and for their clear benefit. Birth is the lone act done to a person before there is a person to ask.

The gold-coins case (after Seana Shiffrin)

A rich benefactor flies over a town dropping cubes of gold to enrich everyone. One cube lands on a man, breaking his arm — but leaves him $5 million richer. Was he wronged, even a little?

Most of us feel yes, somewhat — because a serious burden was imposed on him, without his consent, for a benefit he never asked for. Shiffrin argues birth has this shape: existence is a profound benefit that also unavoidably imposes serious harms (pain, loss, mortality), done to someone who could not agree.

② Is this valid consent?

For each case, decide: does it count as the person's own free, informed, prior agreement? Then reveal the answer. By the end, birth's place in the pattern becomes clear.

③ Test the premises

Mark each premise accept or reject; the conclusion updates. No toggle is "correct".

Key takeaway

The consent argument doesn't claim birth is like an ordinary harm; it claims birth is the one case where consent is not merely missing but impossible. Whether that impossibility is decisive, or simply means consent is the wrong tool here, is exactly where thoughtful people divide.
