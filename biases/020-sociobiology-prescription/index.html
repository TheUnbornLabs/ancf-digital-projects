From Is to Ought: Sociobiology Turned Into Prescription

Part II · Evolutionary & Biological Framings · Sociobiology Turned Into Prescription

The bias: Using adaptationist stories to justify social pressure to reproduce.

The naturalistic fallacy is the mistake of inferring what ought to be from what is." — a standard textbook formulation, tracing to G. E. Moore (1903) and, for the is/ought gap, to David Hume (1739). ## Preface to this volume There is a particular move that recurs whenever falling birth rates make the news, or whenever a columnist wants to explain why a childless friend is quietly unhappy. The move begins with a genuine piece of science: human beings, like all sexually reproducing organisms, are the descendants of ancestors who reproduced. Natural selection has shaped our brains, hormones, and desires in ways that, on average and in the environments where our lineage evolved, tended to produce offspring. This is true, well-evidenced, and interesting. Then, in a single unmarked step, the move slides from that description to a prescription: therefore people should have children, therefore a society that fails to promote family formation is fighting biology, therefore the childless life is a rebellion against nature that will exact its revenge in regret. The science was real. The conclusion does not follow from it. The subject of this book is that gap between the evolutionary is and the reproductive ought. The reason this move is worth a whole volume, rather than a footnote, is that it is not a rare or clumsy error. It is a smooth, almost invisible transition that appears in respectable places: in the reflections of thoughtful writers, in the speeches of serious politicians, in the private worry of people making the largest decision of their lives. It slips past because each half of it is attractive on its own. The descriptive half draws its force from the genuine authority of evolutionary biology, a mature and successful science. The prescriptive half draws its force from a real human wish for guidance about how to live. When the two are welded together, the join is hidden by the very plausibility of the parts, and the reader arrives at a conclusion about how they ought to live without ever having been shown an argument for it. Naming the join is the first act of clear thinking, and it is the act this book is organized around. This is a narrow topic, and its borders are worth marking, because the series it belongs to treats several neighboring biases separately. Book 015 examines the bare naturalistic fallacy: the inference from "natural" to "good" in its most general form. Book 018 examines the selfish-gene metaphor and the sense of duty it can smuggle in, the idea that we owe something to our genes. Book 017 covers the appeal to instinct. This volume is about something more specific and, in the modern era, more socially consequential: the use of sociobiology and evolutionary psychology as social prescription. Not the vague sense that nature knows best, but the dressed-up, scientific-sounding argument that because researchers have reconstructed why the disposition to reproduce evolved, we now know how individuals should order their lives and how governments should order their populations. The bias is the conversion of an explanatory science into a normative program. The stance of this book is critical of that conversion, but it is emphatically not critical of evolutionary science. This distinction matters enough to state at the outset, and to repeat, because it is the distinction most often lost in these debates. Evolutionary biology is one of the great intellectual achievements of the modern era, and evolutionary approaches to human behavior have produced real insight into everything from kinship to cooperation to the architecture of emotion. To say that no ought follows from an evolved disposition is not to deny that the disposition exists or that studying it is worthwhile. It is to insist on a logical point that the founders of the field themselves often affirmed: explaining the origin of a trait tells you nothing, by itself, about whether you should act on it. E. O. Wilson, whose 1975 Sociobiology: The New Synthesis gave the field its name and its notoriety, was careful in places to separate description from endorsement, and was fiercely attacked, sometimes unfairly, for a final chapter that many read as sliding across that line. Part of the work of this book is to be fairer to Wilson than his angriest critics were, while still holding firm on the logic that his sloppiest popularizers ignore. The book proceeds in twelve chapters. Chapter 1 names the bias precisely and distinguishes it from its neighbors. Chapter 2 gives the strongest version of the natalist argument its full due: evolutionary explanation of our reproductive drives is legitimate, informative, and not to be waved away. Chapter 3 traces where the prescriptive misuse comes from. Chapter 4 shows how it operates in ordinary language and policy today. Chapter 5 weighs the evidence. Chapter 6 counts the hidden costs. Chapter 7 asks who bears the resulting pressure. Chapter 8 gives the objections to this book's own thesis a fair hearing. Chapter 9 offers concrete cases. Chapter 10 maps the adjacent biases. Chapter 11 describes what clear reasoning looks like once the bias is named. Chapter 12 concludes without triumph. Throughout, the target is never a person who has children, or wants them, or loves them. The target is a specific inferential error, one that is all the more persuasive for wearing a lab coat. ## 1. Naming the bias The bias named in this book is the sociobiological-prescriptive slide: treating an evolutionary explanation of why humans are disposed to reproduce as if it were, or entailed, a prescription that humans should reproduce, and that societies should be arranged to make them do so. Stated as a single sentence, the thesis of the book is this: even if every adaptationist story about our reproductive drives were completely true, none of them would tell us what any person or any society ought to do, and the practice of pretending otherwise is a bias worth naming, because it borrows the authority of science to close questions that science leaves open. The structure deserves careful attention, because the whole argument turns on it. There are two claims here that must be kept apart. The first is descriptive: natural selection shaped human beings to have, among other things, dispositions that in ancestral environments raised the odds of reproduction, such as sexual desire, pair-bonding tendencies, and parental attachment. The second is normative: therefore a given person should have children, or a given government should subsidize births, or a childless life is a mistake. The first claim is a matter for biology and can be true or false depending on the evidence. The second is a matter for ethics and politics and cannot be settled by biology at all. The slide is the unmarked passage from the first to the second, as though the truth of the description guaranteed the truth of the prescription. It is worth dwelling on why the passage is unmarked: a valid argument from the descriptive claim to the normative one would require a bridging premise, an explicit "and whatever we evolved to do, we ought to do," and the moment that premise is written down it looks obviously false. The slide survives precisely by never writing it down. This is a version of what philosophers call the is/ought gap, first sharply articulated by David Hume in 1739: from statements about what is the case, no statement about what ought to be done follows without some further premise that itself contains an "ought." Hume's own formulation, in Book III of the Treatise, is a small masterpiece of noticing: he observes that authors proceed for a while with the ordinary copulas is and is not, and then, imperceptibly, every proposition is connected instead with ought or ought not, a change so subtle that it passes without remark yet is, he says, "of the last consequence." The bias of this book is that same imperceptible substitution, performed with the vocabulary of Darwin rather than of eighteenth-century moral philosophy. The is/ought gap is closely related to what later writers, following the biologist Julian Huxley and others, called the naturalistic fallacy in its ethical sense, though the philosophical genealogy is tangled and G. E. Moore's original 1903 use of that phrase meant something narrower and more technical, concerning the definition of "good." There is also a mirror-image error, sometimes called the moralistic fallacy: inferring that because something ought not to be so, it therefore is not so, for instance insisting that because coercion is wrong, no natural disposition toward it can exist. Both fallacies haunt debates about human nature, and both appear in natalist argument; this book takes care to commit neither. What makes the sociobiological version distinctive, and worth its own volume, is the authority it borrows. The bare appeal to nature (Book 015) can be brushed off as folk wisdom; when someone says "it's only natural," a listener feels licensed to shrug. But when the appeal comes wrapped in the vocabulary of inclusive fitness, evolved modules, and reproductive strategies, it feels like a scientific finding rather than a value judgment, and the shrug becomes harder to justify. The reader is invited to believe that objecting to the prescription means objecting to Darwin, that to doubt the ought is to doubt the is. It does not, and it is not. One can accept the entire descriptive apparatus of evolutionary psychology, every module and every selection pressure, and still hold that no one is under any obligation, biological or otherwise, to reproduce. That is the terrain this book defends, and it is a defensible terrain precisely because it never has to fight the science. Two further clarifications set the borders. First, this book does not claim that having children is unnatural, unwise, or unfulfilling; it claims only that its naturalness settles nothing about whether any particular person should. The naturalness of a thing is simply the wrong kind of consideration to answer a question about what one ought to do; it is a fact about causal history, not about value. Second, this book does not claim that evolutionary psychology is pseudoscience; it claims that even good evolutionary psychology cannot do the normative work that natalist rhetoric asks of it. A science can be excellent and still be silent on questions outside its domain, just as a flawless map of a coastline tells you nothing about where you ought to sail. The bias is not in the science. It is in the illegitimate promotion of science into commandment. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 1 Quiz. 1. The strongest case for it (steelman) Fairness requires that the natalist argument be stated at its strongest before it is examined, and the evolutionary version is genuinely strong. It deserves to be met at its best, not caricatured. A critique that only ever engages the crudest form of its target wins cheaply and teaches nothing; this chapter therefore builds the natalist case with real conviction, and the reader who finds it persuasive here is having the intended experience. Begin with what is simply true. Every human being alive is the endpoint of an unbroken chain of ancestors who successfully reproduced. Not one of our forebears died childless. This is not a slogan; it is a fact about descent, and it means that whatever heritable dispositions made reproduction more likely have been relentlessly favored for hundreds of thousands of generations. Sexual desire, the pleasures of intimacy, the fierce attachment parents feel for infants, the pang many people feel around babies, the widespread and cross-cultural valuation of family: these are not arbitrary. They are, at least in significant part, the psychological residue of selection for traits that raised reproductive success. To deny this outright is not sophistication; it is a refusal to look at the evidence. A person who insists that our reproductive psychology is a pure social construction, unshaped by our evolutionary past, has picked the losing side of a settled scientific question, and has also, not incidentally, handed the natalist an easy victory by making the disagreement appear to be about biology when it is not. The steelman goes further, and here it becomes persuasive. Because these dispositions are deep, the natalist argues, they are not easily overridden without cost. A person who suppresses a strong evolved drive, for food, for sleep, for social connection, often pays a price in well-being. Why should the drive toward family be different? If our minds are built, in part, around reproduction and child-rearing as central life projects, then a culture or an individual choice that sets these aside may be sailing against a deep current. The natalist can point to the ache of some who postponed children and later grieved it, to the reported meaning that many parents find in raising children, to the cross-cultural near-universality of family life, to the way that the arrival of a child reorganizes a person's sense of what matters. These are not nothing. If evolution built us to find deep meaning in raising the next generation, then advice to pursue that meaning is not arbitrary moralizing; it is advice aligned with the grain of human nature, and ignoring the grain has consequences. The carpenter who works against the grain of the wood is not committing a logical error, but he is inviting splinters, and the natalist is claiming something like this about a life lived athwart its own deepest dispositions. At the collective level the steelman is equally serious. A society is not exempt from arithmetic. Populations that do not reproduce at replacement shrink and age; the young support the old, fund the pensions, staff the hospitals, and sustain the institutions on which everyone, including the childless, depends. If evolved reproductive dispositions are weakening under modern conditions, such as contraception, urbanization, the high cost of children, and competing sources of status and meaning, then a society may face a real collective-action problem: each individual choice may be reasonable while the aggregate is a slow demographic contraction that no one chose and everyone must live in. The natalist who invokes our evolved family-orientation is, on this reading, not fetishizing nature but pointing to a resource: a deep human capacity for parental devotion that policy might sensibly support rather than neglect. To promote family formation, on this view, is to work with human nature toward a sustainable future rather than against it, in the way that good public health works with, rather than against, the body's own systems. There is a subtler philosophical version worth stating, because it is the hardest to answer. Perhaps, the natalist says, the sharp is/ought divide is itself overdrawn. Our values do not float free of our nature; they grew out of it. What we find good, such as love, connection, and the flourishing of those we care for, is not independent of the creatures we evolved to be. A being with a wholly different evolutionary history would find wholly different things good; our conception of the good life is not a view from nowhere but the view of one particular kind of animal. If well-being just is, in part, the satisfaction of deep evolved capacities, then an evolutionary account of what those capacities are is not irrelevant to ethics; it is a partial map of the human good. On this view the appeal to evolved reproductive nature is not a fallacy at all but a reasonable inference from what makes this kind of animal flourish to what this kind of animal has reason to pursue. This is the neo-Aristotelian move, updated with Darwin, and it is genuinely formidable, because it does not try to leap the is/ought gap so much as to argue that, for creatures like us, the gap is narrower than Hume supposed. This is the case at its strongest: not "nature commands," but "our nature is the source of our values, evolution reveals that nature, and a central part of it is oriented toward children, so of course reproduction has a claim on us." The rest of the book takes this seriously; it does not pretend the steelman collapses at a touch. But it will argue that even at full strength the argument establishes far less than natalist rhetoric claims, and that the gap it papers over is real. The neo-Aristotelian may narrow the gap; the "we're wired for it" columnist leaps clean across a canyon and calls it a step. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 2 Quiz. 1. Where it comes from The prescriptive misuse of evolutionary reasoning has a specific history, and knowing it helps explain why the bias feels so authoritative. A pattern of reasoning that has been practiced for two and a half millennia and rebuilt with each new scientific vocabulary does not feel like a fallacy; it feels like common sense, because it has had time to sink into the grammar of how we talk about nature and value. The deep root is older than Darwin: the long tradition, running through Aristotle and the Stoics and into natural-law theology, of reading purpose into nature and then deriving duties from it. Aristotle's biology was frankly teleological; every organ and appetite had its telos, its end, and to understand a thing was to grasp what it was for. The Stoics folded this into an ethics of living "according to nature." Medieval natural-law theology, above all in Aquinas, systematized the move: if the natural end of sexual union is procreation, the natural-law argument runs, then acting against that end is acting against nature and therefore against the good. Darwin did not create this pattern of reasoning; he inherited a culture already primed to move from "the function of X is Y" to "one ought to pursue Y." What evolution added was a new and powerful vocabulary of function, such as adaptation, fitness, and selection, that could be poured into the old normative mold. The irony is sharp: Darwin's own achievement was to explain apparent purpose without a purposer, to show that the eye is for seeing only in a deflationary, historical sense. But the deflationary sense is easily re-inflated by anyone who wants a duty, and the old mold was waiting. The nineteenth-century abuse of that vocabulary is notorious. "Social Darwinism," associated with Herbert Spencer and others (though Darwin himself was more careful, and the label was applied largely in retrospect), took the description of selection and turned it into a justification for social arrangements: the poor deserved poverty, the strong deserved to prevail, and interfering with this natural sorting was folly that would only weaken the species. It was Spencer, not Darwin, who coined "survival of the fittest," and it was the moralizing of "fittest" into "best" that did the damage. Later, eugenics carried the same logic into reproduction directly, prescribing who should and should not breed in the name of the species' evolutionary health, and in the twentieth century this prescription was enforced by law and worse in many countries. This history matters here because it is the original sin of turning evolutionary is into social ought, and its shadow falls across every later attempt, including well-meaning ones, to derive reproductive prescriptions from biology. One cannot understand why the critics of 1975 reacted so fiercely without remembering that they were reacting to a pattern with a body count. The modern chapter opens in 1975 with E. O. Wilson's Sociobiology: The New Synthesis. Most of the book was an authoritative synthesis of the biology of social behavior in animals, from insects to primates, and it was widely admired; twenty-six of its twenty-seven chapters were uncontroversial among biologists. The controversy fell almost entirely on the final chapter, which extended the framework to human beings and speculated about the biological roots of human social behavior, from aggression to religion to the division of labor. Critics, organized as the Sociobiology Study Group and including the Harvard biologists Richard Lewontin and Stephen Jay Gould, read that chapter as licensing the derivation of social norms from supposed genetic facts, and as reviving, in scientific dress, the old pattern of naturalizing the status quo. They said so in a widely circulated open letter, and the dispute grew personal and occasionally ugly. Wilson protested that he had described, not prescribed, and that his critics had read a program into speculation. The dispute was often bitter and sometimes unjust to Wilson personally. But it crystallized the exact issue of this book: the ease with which an evolutionary account of behavior gets heard as an endorsement of it, so that even a scientist trying only to describe finds a prescription attributed to him by half his readers. Two intellectual developments then supplied the machinery the bias runs on today. The first was adaptationism, the tendency, criticized by Gould and Lewontin in their 1979 paper "The Spandrels of San Marco and the Panglossian Paradigm," to assume that every trait is a finely tuned adaptation shaped for a purpose, when some traits are byproducts, accidents, or constraints. Their architectural metaphor was exact: the spandrels of a domed cathedral, the tapering triangular spaces between the arches, are necessary byproducts of mounting a dome on rounded arches, yet a naive observer, seeing them beautifully decorated, might conclude they were designed as canvases. When every feature of human life is read as an adaptation "for" reproduction, the raw material for prescriptive stories multiplies without limit. The second was the rise of evolutionary psychology in the late 1980s and 1990s, associated with Leda Cosmides, John Tooby, and David Buss, which recast the mind as a set of evolved information-processing mechanisms. Rigorous in intent, and often rigorous in practice, evolutionary psychology nonetheless generated a popular literature rich in reproductive narratives, about mate choice, parental investment, and the sexes, that lent themselves readily to prescriptive retelling in advice columns and policy essays. The critics' phrase for the weaker of these narratives, borrowed from Kipling's tales of how the leopard got its spots, was just-so stories: plausible-sounding tales of adaptive origin that were hard to test and easy to believe. The bias of this book lives where an untested or unfalsifiable just-so story about why we reproduce is presented as settled science and then, without a further word, as a reason we should. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 3 Quiz. 1. How it operates today The slide from is to ought rarely announces itself. It works through ordinary phrases and unstated assumptions, and its most effective versions never state the fallacious inference outright; they let the reader complete it. This is what makes it a bias rather than a mere bad argument: a bad argument can be laid out and refuted, but the slide never fully surfaces, so the person who commits it can always say, truthfully, that they never claimed the offending premise. It lives in the gap between what is said and what is heard. The commonest vehicle is the phrase "we're wired for." We're wired for family. We're wired to want children. Humans are hardwired to reproduce. Each sentence is offered as an explanation and received as a recommendation. The word "wired" does double duty: as description it is a loose gesture at heritable disposition, a metaphor borrowed from electronics that implies fixed circuitry; as rhetoric it implies that the wiring is both authoritative and unwise to defy. The listener is left with the impression that resisting the wiring counts as malfunctioning rather than merely unusual, a broken circuit rather than a legitimate choice. The metaphor smuggles in a whole picture: brains as devices with correct operating states, and deviation as fault. No premise linking "wired for" to "should" is ever supplied, because supplying it would expose the gap, and also because the metaphor has already done the work that an explicit premise would have to defend. It is worth noticing, too, that "wired" overstates the fixity even as description: the human dispositions in question are flexible, context-sensitive, and routinely redirected, nothing like the soldered permanence the word suggests. A second vehicle is the language of the biological clock and of "natural" timing. Here an evolved fact, the decline in fertility with age, is folded together with a normative claim about when and whether one should reproduce, so that the biology of ovarian reserve becomes an argument about how to live one's twenties and thirties. The clock is real; the ought it is made to carry is added on. The phrase itself is instructive, because a clock does more than measure time, it summons, it demands a response; to say a woman's clock is "ticking" is to imply she is late for something, and the something is never specified as a value she has chosen but presented as an appointment biology made for her. Two people with identical fertility curves and different life plans hear the same clock saying different things, which is the surest sign that the clock is not, by itself, saying anything normative at all. A third operates in policy discourse, where evolutionary framings dignify pronatalist programs. Falling birth rates are described as a species going against its nature, or as evolved family instincts being thwarted by modern life, and the implied remedy is to realign society with biology. The framing does real work: it converts what is a contestable political choice, how much to subsidize childbearing and at whose expense, into the apparently neutral task of removing obstacles to what people "naturally" want. This launders a value-laden program as the mere facilitation of nature. The rhetorical advantage is considerable, because facilitation sounds modest and permission-granting where prescription sounds coercive; a government that says it is merely helping people do what they already, naturally, wish to do appears to be expanding freedom rather than steering it. The steering is hidden inside the word "naturally.

① In brief

Book 020 · 100 Biases of Natalism

This volume examines a specific and unusually persuasive natalist bias: the sociobiological-prescriptive slide, in which an evolutionary explanation of why humans are disposed to reproduce is treated as a prescription that they should reproduce, and that societies should be arranged to make them. The book's one-sentence thesis is that even if every adaptationist story about our reproductive drives were completely true, none of them would tell us what any person or society ought to do; pretending otherwise borrows the authority of science to close questions science leaves open.

The bias is a member of a family but has its own lane. It differs from the bare naturalistic fallacy (Book 015, the plain natural-to-good inference) because it invokes the reconstructed evolutionary reasons for a disposition and so wears a scientific gloss that the folk appeal to nature lacks. It differs from the selfish-gene metaphor (Book 018), whose error runs through a metaphor of duty to one's genes rather than through the is/ought inference proper. And it differs from the appeal to instinct (Book 017) by targeting not the felt urge but the explanatory story about the urge's origin.

Read the rest of the overview

The book is critical of the prescriptive misuse of evolutionary science, not of evolutionary science itself. It concedes the whole descriptive picture: human beings descend from an unbroken line of successful reproducers, and selection has shaped desire, attachment, and the machinery of reproduction. Chapter 2 gives the natalist steelman its full strength, including the well-being argument (thwarting a deep drive costs us), the collective-arithmetic argument (societies that don't reproduce shrink and age), and the subtlest version, that our evolved nature is itself the source of our values, so an evolutionary map of that nature is partly a map of the human good.

The critique then holds a single firm line. Between the fact of our evolved constitution and any conclusion about what we ought to do lies a gap, Hume's is/ought gap, that no biological evidence can close. The book shows the slide operating today through phrases like "we're wired for family," the biological clock, pronatalist policy rhetoric that frames subsidy as "realignment with nature," and the selective use of an evolved-drive story to explain the unhappiness of the childless but never of parents. It weighs the evidence honestly: the descriptive core is solid, but claims that thwarting reproduction reliably produces suffering are not well supported, popular adaptive stories are often untestable "just-so stories," and the ultimate/proximate distinction is routinely blurred.

The hidden costs are to honest reasoning (a value question disguised as a fact), individual autonomy (doubt recoded as malfunction), self-knowledge, political scrutiny of who pays for pronatalist policy, and to the credibility of evolutionary science itself. The pressure falls hardest on women, on those in precarious circumstances, and on the involuntarily childless and the childfree. The book fairly answers the strongest objections, including that the is/ought gap is overstated and that critics of sociobiology are ideologically motivated, conceding what is fair while holding that evolved dispositions drastically underdetermine what we should do.

Seeing past the bias means separating the descriptive and normative claims whenever they are fused, granting the biology freely while calmly refusing the inference, noticing the story's selective deployment, and, for institutions, making any pronatalist case on its real merits as an honestly costed value choice rather than laundering it through biology. The measured conclusion: the evolved is describes where we came from; it does not command where we must go. Keeping the two apart is not a rejection of human nature but the beginning of thinking clearly about it.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

What does the evidence actually establish, and where does it run out? Being scrupulous here is the whole point, because the bias trades on overstating what the science shows. The discipline of this chapter is to concede everything that is genuinely established, generously and without hedging, and then to mark, just as clearly, the precise points at which the prescriptive argument asks the evidence for more than it can give.

CH 6The hidden costs

Turning a descriptive science into a prescription is not a harmless philosophical slip, the kind of thing that matters only to logicians. It has costs, real ones borne by real people and by public life, and because they are hidden by the very framing that produces them, they are worth pricing explicitly. A bias that announced its costs would be self-correcting; this one conceals them behind the reassuring surface of science, which is why they must be excavated.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

Like most natalist biases, the sociobiological-prescriptive slide does not press equally on everyone. The evolved-drive story is applied selectively, and tracing the selectivity reveals whose autonomy it most constrains, and how a supposedly universal claim about human nature comes to fall, in practice, along familiar lines of sex, class, and circumstance.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

What does clear, non-coerced reasoning about reproduction look like once this particular bias is named and its workings understood? The aim is not to reach an anti-natalist conclusion, since this book takes no view on whether anyone should have children, and would be committing its own mirror-image error if it did. The aim is to restore the question to its rightful owners: the people whose lives and societies are at stake, reasoning in the open, in the language of values and evidence honestly weighed rather than pre-empted by a story about what they must want.

CH 12Conclusion

The argument of this book has been narrow and, within its lane, firm, and it is worth restating its exact shape so that neither its modesty nor its firmness is missed. Human beings are the products of natural selection, and our minds bear the marks of an ancestry of successful reproducers; not one of our forebears died childless, and the psychology that made that so is in us still. This is true, and evolutionary science that investigates it is legitimate and valuable; nothing in these twelve chapters disputes that, and much of the book has been concerned to insist on it against the temptation to win the argument cheaply by denying the biology. But between the fact of our evolved constitution and any conclusion about what a person or a society ought to do lies a gap that no quantity of biological evidence, however excellent, can close, because the gap is logical and the evidence is empirical, and empirical evidence cannot span a logical gap. The sociobiological-prescriptive slide is the practice of stepping across that gap without acknowledging it, of letting an explanation of why we evolved to reproduce pose as an instruction that we should. Named plainly, the move loses much of its power, because its power depended entirely on not being seen; a hidden premise, once written down, must defend itself, and the premise this slide relies on cannot.

Show all 17 terms as a list
Sociobiological-prescriptive slide
the bias named in this book: treating an evolutionary explanation of why humans are disposed to reproduce as if it were, or entailed, a prescription that they should reproduce and that societies should promote it.
Is/ought gap
David Hume's observation (1739) that no statement about what ought to be done follows from statements about what is the case without a further premise that itself contains an "ought."
Naturalistic fallacy
in its common ethical sense, the mistake of inferring what is good from what is natural or what is; the phrase originates with G. E. Moore (1903) in a narrower, more technical sense.
Moralistic fallacy
the mirror-image error: inferring that because something ought not to be so, it therefore is not so; refusing to accept a natural fact because one dislikes its apparent implications.
Sociobiology
the systematic study of the biological, especially evolutionary, basis of social behavior; the field named and popularized by E. O. Wilson's 1975 synthesis.
Evolutionary psychology
the study of the mind as a set of information-processing mechanisms shaped by natural selection; associated with Leda Cosmides, John Tooby, and David Buss.
Adaptationism
the tendency to assume, by default, that a trait is an adaptation shaped by selection for a specific function; criticized as frequently unwarranted by Gould and Lewontin.
Spandrel
a trait that is a byproduct of other features rather than a direct target of selection; from Gould and Lewontin's 1979 architectural analogy to the spaces in San Marco.
Just-so story
a pejorative term for an untested or untestable evolutionary narrative constructed after the fact to explain why a trait exists.
Ultimate causation
the evolutionary reason a trait or disposition exists (its selective history).
Proximate causation
the immediate mechanism that produces a behavior (hormones, desires, cues); prescriptive talk routinely conflates ultimate and proximate causes, following a distinction due to Ernst Mayr.
Inclusive fitness
the evolutionary measure of reproductive success that includes effects on genetic relatives; part of the technical vocabulary that lends the slide a scientific gloss.
Social Darwinism
the historical misuse of evolutionary description to justify existing social arrangements; the original template for turning evolutionary "is" into social "ought."
Eugenics
the historical program of prescribing who should and should not reproduce in the name of a population's biological quality; the darkest precedent for reproductive prescription drawn from biology.
Pronatalism
the constellation of attitudes, norms, and policies that favor human reproduction; the destination toward which the prescriptive slide is typically directed.
"Wired for"
a popular phrase that performs descriptive and normative work simultaneously, loosely gesturing at heritable disposition while implying that defying it is a malfunction.
Appeal to evolved consensus
the rhetorical move of treating a disposition, because presented as ancient and universal, as authoritative, so that dissent is reframed as error rather than as a different value.
