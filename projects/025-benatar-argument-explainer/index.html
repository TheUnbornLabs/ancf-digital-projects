Benatar Argument Explainer

Philosophy · Difficulty: Advanced · ~13 min

David Benatar's asymmetry argument (2006) is the most discussed case for antinatalism. This page lays it out fairly — the famous four-cell matrix, the premises you can test for yourself, and the strongest objections — so you can decide where you stand. Holding the question open is a valid place to land.

What you will learn

  • The four cells of Benatar's pleasure/pain asymmetry.
  • How those cells combine into the conclusion that coming into existence is a harm.
  • Which premise you personally accept, doubt, or reject.
  • The strongest objections — especially to "a good for no one".

1. The asymmetry matrix

Tap each cell to expand. The argument turns on the bottom two.

Presence of painBad
Suffering that exists is a harm — uncontroversial.
Presence of pleasureGood
Enjoyment that exists is a benefit — uncontroversial.
Absence of painGood
A harm that never happens is counted as good — even with no one to enjoy that good. This is the key claim.
Absence of pleasureNot bad
A missing pleasure is only bad if a real person is deprived. With no one created, no one is deprived.

Putting it together: for a possible person, non-existence has one clear plus (no pain — counted as good) and no minus (the absent pleasure is "not bad"). So, Benatar argues, never existing is always at least as good as existing — and usually better.

3. The strongest objections

"A good for no one isn't really good"

The main objection targets P3: if there is no one to benefit, calling the absent pain "good" looks like a category error. Benatar replies that we routinely judge the absence of suffering as good when comparing scenarios, even impersonally.

Most lives are worth living

People overwhelmingly report being glad to exist. Benatar replies that self-reports are unreliable (we adapt, and discount past pain), so they don't settle the impersonal comparison.

The asymmetry can be explained another way

Critics argue our intuitions about not creating suffering vs not creating happiness can be explained without Benatar's strong claim — e.g., by duties to avoid harm being weightier than duties to create benefit.

It proves too much

Some say the argument implies conclusions most reject (about the badness of all existence). Benatar embraces much of this; opponents treat it as a reductio.

Key takeaway

Benatar's argument is rigorous and unsettling, and its key move — that an absent harm is good for no one in particular — is exactly where thoughtful people part ways. Understanding precisely where you get off the train is more valuable than rushing to agree or dismiss.
