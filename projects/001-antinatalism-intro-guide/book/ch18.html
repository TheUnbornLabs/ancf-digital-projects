Part III · The ThinkersCHAPTER 18

Other Modern Voices

Akerma, de Giraud, Sarah Perry, and the wider contemporary field.

"The case against birth has been made in many languages, by many hands, who never met."

— editorial note

The thinkers gathered in this book did not form a school. They wrote in different countries and languages, came to their conclusions by different roads, and would disagree with one another on a great deal. Antinatalism is less a movement than a scattering of independent voices who happened to converge on a similar question. This chapter is a brief survey of several modern figures who deserve mention but whose work does not fill a chapter of its own — together with a glance back at the nineteenth-century pessimists who anticipated them.

Théophile de Giraud and the naming of the idea

Although the underlying question is ancient, the word "antinatalism" is recent. The Belgian writer and activist Théophile de Giraud is generally credited with bringing the term into circulation in 2006, with a manifesto-style book arguing against procreation. De Giraud's contribution was less a new technical argument than an act of naming and provocation: he gave a diffuse set of intuitions a banner and a public profile, and helped a scattered conversation recognize itself as one. The reader should hold this lightly — ideas of this kind have many partial origins — but the 2006 coining is a useful landmark.

Karim Akerma: philosopher and historian of the idea

The German philosopher Karim Akerma has done two distinct services. As a philosopher he has defended antinatalist conclusions in his own right; as a historian of the idea he has traced its long, half-buried lineage, showing that the case against birth is not a twenty-first-century novelty but a recurring thought with deep roots. Akerma's historical work is especially valuable for resisting the impression that antinatalism sprang fully formed from a single recent book. He helps situate the present conversation within a much longer story.

Naming vs. originating — A recurring feature of this field. The term "antinatalism" is recent (2006), but the ideas it gathers are old. Coining a name can make a scattered conversation visible without being the source of its content.

Sarah Perry: Every Cradle Is a Grave

The American writer Sarah Perry published Every Cradle Is a Grave in 2014, a book that approaches the subject from outside the usual academic channels. Perry weaves together reflections on suffering, on the ethics of creating new people, and — handled with notable seriousness — on the difficult subject of how a society treats those who find life unbearable. Her book is valued for its directness and for bringing a humane, essayistic sensibility to questions that are too often left to dense monographs. As with every voice in this book, her concern is with whether to begin a life, never a counsel of despair to those already living one.

Matti Häyry: the analytic philosopher

The Finnish philosopher Matti Häyry represents antinatalism's presence within rigorous analytic philosophy and bioethics. Häyry has engaged the arguments with the tools of his discipline — careful distinctions, attention to logical form, sensitivity to where an argument genuinely succeeds and where it overreaches. In a notably personal essay, "Confessions of an Antinatalist Philosopher" (2024), he reflected on holding such a view from inside the academy. His work matters because it shows the position can be stated and examined with full analytic seriousness, rather than only as provocation or mood.

A scattered field is not a weak one; convergence across distance is itself a kind of evidence.

David Pearce: a suffering-focused but distinct project

The British philosopher David Pearce is often mentioned alongside antinatalists, but he belongs here with an important qualification. Pearce's project, set out in The Hedonistic Imperative, is abolitionist rather than antinatalist: he argues that suffering itself could and should be abolished through biotechnology, re-engineering life so that sentient beings are no longer capable of misery. This is a transhumanist vision aimed at a future without suffering, not a case against bringing new beings into existence. Pearce shares the antinatalist's deep concern with suffering as the central moral fact, but draws a very different practical conclusion: not fewer beings, but beings freed from pain. Including him here marks both the kinship and the divergence — suffering-focused ethics can lead toward abolition as readily as toward abstention.

In plain words: Modern antinatalism is a loose constellation: de Giraud named it (2006), Akerma defends and historicizes it, Perry wrote a humane popular book, Häyry brings analytic rigor, and Pearce shares the focus on suffering but aims to abolish it through technology rather than to prevent births.

Nineteenth-century forerunners

None of this is unprecedented. The pessimist philosophers of the nineteenth century, working in the shadow of Schopenhauer, anticipated much of the modern discussion. Eduard von Hartmann built an elaborate system around the idea that existence is, on balance, not worth its cost, imagining a kind of collective redemption from the will. Philipp Mainländer, more radical and more haunting, developed a metaphysics in which existence itself tends toward its own dissolution — a "will to death" running beneath the surface of life. These figures are not always easy to read, and their systems are products of their time, but they show that the modern voices are heirs to a tradition rather than inventors of one.

ObjectionLook honestly at the list and what stands out is its weakness. The field is small, heterodox, and loosely connected — a horror novelist, a few activists, a couple of bioethicists, some obscure nineteenth-century systematizers, and a transhumanist who isn't even an antinatalist. These people barely agree with one another. A position held by such a scattered handful, with no settled core, hardly commands serious attention.
ReplySmallness and disagreement are real features of the field, but they cut less than the objection supposes. First, the size of a view's following is not a measure of its truth; many ideas now taken for granted were once held by a scattered few. Second, internal disagreement is a sign of a living inquiry, not a dying one — these thinkers differ because they are arguing, not reciting a creed, and a position robust enough to survive disagreement among its own defenders is stronger, not weaker, for it. Third, the "loose connection" looks different once one notices that figures separated by language, century, and method keep arriving near the same question independently. Convergence across distance is itself a kind of evidence: it suggests the question is being found rather than invented. A heterodox field is exactly what one would expect of an idea that is genuinely difficult and genuinely recurring.

Reading the constellation

The right way to read this chapter is not as a roll call of authorities but as a map of a conversation. No single figure here carries the whole case, and several would resist being grouped at all. What they share is a refusal to treat the creation of a new person as a settled, unexamined good — and a willingness to think hard about a question most cultures decline to ask. The reader who has followed the earlier chapters can now place these voices in relation to the central arguments, taking what is illuminating and setting aside what is not.

Chapter in brief

  • Théophile de Giraud is credited with coining "antinatalism" in his 2006 manifesto — naming, rather than originating, the idea.
  • Karim Akerma defends antinatalism and has written its history, showing the idea's long lineage.
  • Sarah Perry's Every Cradle Is a Grave (2014) brings a humane, essayistic voice; Matti Häyry brings full analytic rigor.
  • David Pearce shares the focus on suffering but is an abolitionist transhumanist, not strictly an antinatalist — a kindred but distinct project.
  • Nineteenth-century pessimists von Hartmann and Mainländer are forerunners; the field's smallness and disagreement reflect a living, recurring inquiry rather than a weakness.
