PROJECT 097

Disabled People and Reproductive Autonomy

Inclusion · Difficulty: Intermediate · ~9 min

Reproductive autonomy belongs to everyone — including disabled people, fully and without exception. That means freedom from coercion in any direction: never pressured to have children, and never pressured not to. Support, never control.

This page centres disabled people's own voices and choices. It opposes coercion in every form, and assumes no one's wishes based on disability. Decisions belong to each person, with whatever support they choose.

What you will learn

  • Why autonomy must be unconditional.
  • How coercion can run in both directions.
  • The difference between support and control.

1. Autonomy, unconditional

Everyone's rightThe freedom to decide whether to have children isn't lessened by disability. Coercion in any directionHistory shows both forced sterilisation and being wrongly denied parenthood — both are wrong. No assumptionsNo one should presume a disabled person does, or doesn't, want children. Accessible informationReal choice needs information and care that are genuinely accessible.

2. Support, not control

Support meansAccessible info, respect for the person's own decision, and help they ask for. Control meansDeciding for someone, pressuring them, or overriding their wishes — never acceptable. Listen firstThe person's own voice leads; everyone else supports.

Key takeaway

Autonomy is unconditional. Disabled people have the full right to decide about children — free from coercion in either direction. The line that matters is support versus control: information and respect, never deciding for someone.
