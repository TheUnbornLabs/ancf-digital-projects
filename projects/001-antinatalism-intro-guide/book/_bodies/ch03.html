"Not to be born is, beyond all reckoning, best; but when a man has seen the light of day, this is next best by far, that with all speed he should go back from where he came."

— the chorus in Sophocles, Oedipus at Colonus (echoing the older "wisdom of Silenus")

Antinatalism is a young word fastened to a very old mood. The term itself dates only to the early twenty-first century, but the suspicion behind it — that coming into existence might not be the unmixed gift we assume — surfaces again and again across the long history of human thought. This chapter walks that history in order. The aim is to be fair on two fronts at once: to show how genuinely recurrent the mood has been, and to resist the temptation to pretend that every gloomy line ever written was secretly an argument against having children. Resonance is not the same as identity, and we will hold that distinction firmly all the way through.

Ancient pessimist motifs

The oldest thread is Greek. A famous fragment of folk wisdom, known as the "wisdom of Silenus," runs roughly thus: the best thing for a human being is never to have been born, and the second best is to die soon. The motif appears in the poetry of Theognis of Megara and is voiced, with great power, by the chorus in Sophocles' late tragedy Oedipus at Colonus. These are not philosophical treatises arguing that procreation is wrong. They are expressions of a tragic sensibility — a sense that life is heavy with toil and grief, and that nonexistence might have spared us the weight.

The wisdom of Silenus — an ancient Greek saying that the greatest good is never to be born, the second is to die quickly. It is a poetic verdict on the burden of living, not a worked-out ethics of reproduction.

It matters that these voices stop where they do. They lament existence; they do not draw the further conclusion that we therefore have a duty not to create new people. That step — from "it would have been better not to be" to "we ought not to bring others into being" — is exactly the step the modern argument makes, and the ancients largely did not.

Indian thought and Buddhism

Move east and the theme reappears in a different key. Several Indian traditions treat ordinary existence as shot through with unsatisfactoriness. In Buddhism this is named directly: dukkha, the pervasive suffering or unease of conditioned life; samsara, the wheel of repeated birth and rebirth; tanha, the craving that keeps the wheel turning; and nirvana, the release that ends it. A tradition that sees rebirth itself as the problem, and liberation from the cycle of births as the goal, can sound startlingly close to antinatalism.

A note of caution

It is tempting to file Buddhism as "antinatalism with incense." But the resemblance is on the surface. Buddhism is a soteriology — a path to release for the being caught in the cycle — not an ethical argument that human parents wrong their children by conceiving them.

The goal in Buddhist thought is the end of rebirth through awakening, not a campaign against childbearing in this life; many Buddhist cultures are warmly pronatalist. The shared insight — that existence carries built-in suffering — is real and worth honoring. But the conclusions drawn from it run in a different direction. We can let Buddhism illuminate the mood without conscripting it into a position it never held.

Gnostic and Manichaean world-rejection

In the early centuries of the common era, several currents of thought treated the material world itself as a kind of mistake. Gnostic sects often held that the cosmos was the botched work of a lesser, flawed maker, and that the spark of the divine was trapped in matter and longed to escape it. Manichaeism framed reality as a war between light and dark in which embodied life entangled the light in darkness. For thinkers of this cast, generating more bodily life could look like prolonging a cosmic imprisonment rather than celebrating a gift.

Here too the resonance is suggestive and the identity false. These were religious and cosmological systems with their own elaborate metaphysics, not secular arguments about consent and harm. Still, they show that world-rejection — the refusal to grant that existence is self-evidently good — has deep and varied roots.

The long quiet centuries

For a long stretch afterward, the pessimist note went quiet in the mainstream of Western thought, or at least subordinate. Cultures organized around continuity — religious commands to be fruitful, agrarian economies that needed hands, dynasties that needed heirs — left little room for the question to be asked aloud. The mood never vanished, surfacing in stray verses and private despair, but it had no settled vocabulary and no defenders willing to make it a thesis. That silence is part of the history too: it reminds us how strong the presumption in favor of birth has always been.

Schopenhauer and nineteenth-century pessimism

The modern story really begins with Arthur Schopenhauer (1788–1860). In The World as Will and Representation he argued that the deepest reality is a blind, striving "Will" that drives all things, and that this striving makes suffering the baseline of existence: desire is painful, satisfaction is fleeting, and boredom waits on the far side of every fulfilled want. Schopenhauer did not found an antinatalist movement, but he gave pessimism a rigorous philosophical form and treated procreation with open ambivalence — as the Will perpetuating its own restless hunger through us.

Philosophical pessimism — the view that suffering is structurally central to existence, not an accident to be engineered away. It is a frequent companion of antinatalism but is not the same thing; one can be a pessimist about life and still favor birth, or an optimist and still question it.

A darker figure followed. Philipp Mainländer (1841–1876), in The Philosophy of Redemption, pushed pessimism to an extreme, speaking of a cosmic "will to die" as the hidden engine of the universe. Mainländer died by suicide, and his life is sometimes used to caricature the whole tradition. We state the fact plainly and without sensationalism, and we hold to the principle that runs through this book: a philosophy of birth concerns future people, and is never an argument against any living person's life or a reason for despair.

The mood is ancient; the word is new; the two should not be confused.

Zapffe and Cioran

In the twentieth century the strand grew sharper and more secular. The Norwegian philosopher Peter Wessel Zapffe, in his 1933 essay "The Last Messiah," argued that human consciousness over-developed itself, leaving us aware of horrors we cannot resolve, and that we cope by repressing this awareness. He drew an explicitly anti-procreative conclusion. Decades later the Romanian-French writer E. M. Cioran gave the mood its most quotable literary voice in The Trouble with Being Born (1973) — aphorisms circling the strange misfortune of having been summoned into being. Zapffe reasons; Cioran broods. Neither used the term that would soon arrive, but both stand close to the modern position.

The word arrives: 2006

The position acquired its name and its sharpest modern arguments in the same period. In 2006 the philosopher David Benatar published Better Never to Have Been: The Harm of Coming into Existence, offering a careful, analytic case — including his well-known "asymmetry" argument — that coming into existence is always a harm. Around the same time the French author Théophile de Giraud published a manifesto popularizing "antinatalisme" as a label for the position. Only here does the scattered mood of millennia consolidate into a named, defended thesis with a recognizable vocabulary of consent, harm, and risk.

In plain words: People felt the pull of this idea for thousands of years, but it only became a clearly stated, arguable position — with a name — in the mid-2000s.

The internet era

What turned a niche academic and literary position into a recognizable public stance was the internet. Forums, blogs, and later social media let scattered individuals find each other, share Benatar's arguments, and debate them in the open. The label spread, sometimes carefully and sometimes loosely, until "antinatalist" became a word ordinary people used to describe themselves. That diffusion is a mixed blessing: it broadened the conversation while also detaching the term from the disciplined arguments that gave it content.

Objection

Isn't this whole chapter a trick? Stitch together a few gloomy quotes from Sophocles to Cioran and you can manufacture a grand "tradition" behind almost any idea. The cherry-picking creates an illusion of continuity that was never really there.

Reply

It would be a trick if the chapter claimed a single, continuous lineage handing down one doctrine — and we have been careful not to. The honest claim is narrower: the underlying intuition, that existence may not be self-evidently good, recurs independently across cultures and eras. That is evidence the intuition is natural and persistent, not that there was a hidden creed. The modern position is genuinely new in 2006; the precursors are precursors, not members. Naming the difference is precisely how we avoid the trick.

Chapter in brief

  • The mood behind antinatalism is ancient — the wisdom of Silenus, Theognis, and Sophocles among the Greeks — but these were tragic laments, not arguments against procreation.
  • Buddhism's dukkha and samsara, and Gnostic and Manichaean world-rejection, resonate strongly yet remain distinct systems with their own goals; resonance is not identity.
  • Schopenhauer gave pessimism rigorous form in the nineteenth century; Mainländer pushed it to an extreme; Zapffe (1933) and Cioran (1973) sharpened it in the twentieth.
  • The word "antinatalism" and its strongest modern arguments arrived together around 2006, with Benatar and de Giraud.
  • The internet then spread the term as a named, self-applied position — broadening the debate but loosening its ties to the careful arguments beneath it.
