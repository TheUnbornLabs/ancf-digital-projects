BIAS 076

Plan B by Default: Framing Adoption and Childfree Life

Part VI · Media, Language & Rhetoric · Framing Adoption and Childfree Life

The bias: Alternatives to biological parenthood cast as consolation prizes.

We adopted after years of trying. People still ask, kindly, whether we ever got to have 'our own.'" — a composite of remarks reported in adoption memoirs and interview studies; illustrative, not a verbatim quotation. ## Preface to this volume There is a quiet grammar in how people talk about becoming a parent, and it orders the world before anyone notices. First comes the wish for a child who shares one's genes, carried in one's own or a partner's body. Then, if that does not happen, comes medical intervention: the clinics, the injections, the calendars of hope. And only after that, when the treatments end, does a further set of options tend to surface: adoption, fostering, step-parenthood, or a life without children at all. The order is so familiar that it looks like nature. It is not. It is a hierarchy, and this book is about the hierarchy. The bias examined here is not the desire for a genetic child, which is real, deep, and legitimate. Many people long to be pregnant, to see a face that echoes their own, to carry forward a lineage they love. That longing deserves respect, not correction. The bias is something narrower and more corrosive: the ranking that treats biological parenthood as the genuine article and every other path (adoption, fostering, blending a family through a partner's children, or choosing not to parent) as a consolation prize, a fallback, a Plan B. It is the difference between saying "I wanted a genetic child" and saying "adoption is what you do when the real thing fails." The first is a preference. The second is a bias with victims. Those victims are specific. Adoptive parents describe a lifetime of being asked whether they ever had children "of their own." Adopted people grow up inside a story in which they were the second choice, the thing you settle for. People who foster hear their commitment framed as a stopgap. Step-parents are told they are not the "real" parent even after two decades of school runs and midnight fevers. And people who remain childfree, whether by circumstance or conviction, are read as having run out of options rather than having chosen well. In each case the harm is the same: a life-defining path is subordinated in advance, its value discounted before it is even lived. It is worth stressing at the outset that none of these people is asking for pity, and this book does not offer any. What they are denied is not sympathy but standing: the plain recognition that the family they built, or the life they chose, is a first thing and not a leftover. This volume proceeds in the series' standard order, and it takes one commitment seriously. In Chapter 2 it steelmans the other side first and in full: it lays out, sympathetically, why the pull toward a genetic child and a pregnancy runs so deep, why it is not mere vanity, and why a fair critique must begin by conceding its force. Only after that does the book turn critical. It traces where the biological-first hierarchy comes from (Chapter 3), how it operates through everyday language and medical sequencing (Chapter 4), what the evidence does and does not show about outcomes and satisfaction across family types (Chapter 5), the costs the hierarchy hides (Chapter 6), and who bears its pressure most heavily (Chapter 7). It then turns its own thesis over, presenting the strongest objections to the critique and answering them honestly (Chapter 8), before offering illustrations (Chapter 9), mapping adjacent biases (Chapter 10), and asking what clearer reasoning would look like (Chapter 11). A word on tone. This book is not an argument for adoption over biology, nor a brief against IVF, nor a celebration of childfree life as superior. It is an argument against ranking these paths in advance. The claim is modest and, I think, hard to refute: that a person deciding how to build a family, or whether to, is served by seeing the options as genuinely open, each with its own goods and griefs, rather than arranged on a ladder with "your own baby" at the top and everything else descending below it. To name the ladder is not to knock anyone off it. It is to notice that it was built, that it could have been built differently, and that some people are standing on its lower rungs only because someone else decided that is where they belonged. Readers who came to this series suspicious of it will find no scolding here, and readers who came hoping for a manifesto against biological parenthood will be disappointed. The argument is narrower and, for that reason, sturdier: it survives even if you grant the other side everything it asks for. ## 1. Naming the bias The bias this book names is the biological-first hierarchy: the tacit ranking of paths to family in which genetic, gestational parenthood occupies first place as the real, default, and ideal form, while adoption, fostering, step-parenthood, and the childfree life are positioned as fallbacks, options one turns to when the primary plan fails. Call it, for short, Plan-B framing: the habit of treating every non-biological path not as a first-class choice with its own reasons but as a residual, a consolation, what is left over. The thesis of the book is a single sentence: the problem is not the desire for a biological child but the hierarchy that files every alternative under "fallback," and that hierarchy distorts real decisions, wounds real families, and hides costs that the default framing never has to pay. It helps to say clearly what Plan-B framing is and is not, because the series contains neighboring biases that could be confused with it. This book is not about the claim that biological family is "more real," that a genetic child is family in a way an adopted child is not. That is the real-family bias, treated in Book 071, and it concerns the boundary of kinship itself. Nor is this book about the idea that having children completes a person or a couple, that the childless life is missing something essential; that is the completeness narrative of Book 040. Plan-B framing is distinct from both. It grants, for the sake of argument, that adopted children are real family and that childfree lives can be complete. Its work happens one level up, in the ordering: even when all these paths are conceded to be legitimate, we still arrange them, and biology sits at the top by default. The mark of Plan-B framing is sequence and salience rather than exclusion. It does not deny that adoption is family; it assumes adoption comes second. It does not say the childfree are not real adults; it reads their state as a failure to launch rather than a destination. You can hear the hierarchy most clearly in the language of trying. A couple is "trying for a baby." When they stop, they have stopped "trying," as though adoption or a childfree life were not itself a positive undertaking but the cessation of the real project. To "try" is to pursue the genetic child; everything else is what you do when trying ends. The verb encodes the ladder. Three features distinguish this bias. First, it is ordinal, not categorical: it ranks rather than excludes, which makes it harder to see and easier to defend ("Of course adopted children are just as loved; we simply tried for our own first"). Second, it is revealed in investment: the enormous resources, medical and emotional, that couples pour into achieving a genetic child before considering adoption disclose the true ranking more honestly than any stated attitude. Third, it is asymmetric in its burden of proof: the biological path needs no justification, while the alternatives are asked to explain themselves. No one asks a pregnant woman why she did not adopt. Adoptive parents are routinely asked why they did not "have their own.

① In brief

Book 076 · 100 Biases of Natalism

This volume examines a quiet but powerful bias in how people think about building a family: the biological-first hierarchy, or Plan-B framing. This is the tacit ranking in which genetic, gestational parenthood is treated as the real, default, and ideal form of family, while adoption, fostering, step-parenthood, and the childfree life are positioned beneath it, as fallbacks, consolation prizes, or what is left when the primary plan fails.

The book is careful about its target. It does not criticize the desire for a genetic child or a pregnancy. That desire is embodied, ancient, meaningful, and legitimate, and Chapter 2 steelmans it fully and sympathetically before any critique begins: pregnancy is sought for its own sake; genetic continuity connects people to lineage and mortality in ways that are not reducible to vanity; and pursuing a genetic child first can be prudent given how genuinely difficult, costly, and uncertain adoption is. The bias the book targets is narrower: the hierarchy that moves from "I want this for myself" to "this is the top of the ladder and everything else is Plan B."

Read the rest of the overview

Plan-B framing is distinguished from two neighbors in the series. It is not the real-family bias (Book 071), which concerns whether non-biological relatives count as family at all; the hierarchy can grant that adopted children are fully real family and still rank the adoptive path as secondary. And it is not the completeness narrative (Book 040), which pushes people toward parenthood at all; Plan-B framing operates among those already deciding how to build a family, ranking which kind counts as the genuine reward. The distinctive object here is the ordering itself.

The book traces the hierarchy's origins in evolution (handled with care, noting the human capacity for alloparenting), kinship and property law, religious and cultural narratives of lineage, and, most tellingly, the modern assisted-reproduction industry, whose structure installs the sequence in which the clinic is Plan A and adoption is raised only "if treatment is unsuccessful." It shows how the bias operates today through the language of "trying," through medical sequencing that positions adoption as the failure branch, through social scripts (the questions about children "of your own," the condolence offered to the childfree), and through institutional defaults like unequal parental leave.

On the evidence, the book finds the ranking poorly supported: adopted children generally fare well, with difficulties tracking pre-adoption adversity rather than adoption itself; adoptive and biological parents report comparable satisfaction; and childfree adults are not, as a class, unfulfilled. The clearest evidence is the revealed priority, the vast investment in achieving a genetic child before a child who already needs a family is considered, which discloses the hierarchy without justifying it.

The hidden costs fall on adopted people (raised as someone's second choice), on adoptive and step-parents (whose deliberate commitment is discounted as a fallback), on people whose decisions are distorted by the pressure to exhaust treatment first, and on childfree people (whose lives are read as unfinished). These costs land hardest along lines of gender, class, sexuality, and culture.

Chapter 8 turns the thesis over, engaging the strongest objections (that this is preference not prejudice, that sequencing is prudence, that pregnancy and genes are real goods, that the critique risks its own coercion) and concedes what is true in each while showing that none rescues the ranking. The closing chapters offer illustrations, map adjacent biases, and describe non-coercive ways forward for individuals (decoupling private desire from public ranking, weighing options together and early, linguistic care) and institutions (neutral early counseling, equal leave, better-resourced adoption).

The measured takeaway: there is nothing wrong with wanting a genetic child, or pursuing it first. There is something wrong with a culture that arranges family paths into a ladder and hands people a rung before they have spoken. Naming the ladder does not knock anyone off it. It notices that it was built, could have been built otherwise, and leaves each person free to build, or not build, a family with open eyes.

② The book in twelve chapters

Each chapter opens with its own summary. Note chapter 2: every volume states the strongest case for the thing it criticises before laying a finger on it — and chapter 8 turns the critique back on itself.

CH 1Naming the bias

CH 2The strongest case for it (steelman)The strongest case against this book

Before criticizing the biological-first hierarchy, honesty requires putting the strongest version of the other side, sympathetically and in full. The pull toward a genetic child and a pregnancy is not shallow, and a critique that treats it as vanity will deserve to fail.

CH 3Where it comes from

Hierarchies feel natural to the people inside them, which is exactly why their origins are worth tracing. The biological-first ranking did not fall from the sky. It was assembled from evolutionary pressures, kinship law, religious doctrine, property arrangements, and, most recently, a medical industry organized around delivering a genetic child. Naming these sources does not debunk the hierarchy; some of its roots point at genuine goods. But it does dislodge the sense that things could not be otherwise. A ranking that turns out to have a history is a ranking that could have had a different one, and that alone is worth the excavation.

CH 4How it operates today

A bias survives by hiding in defaults, scripts, and sequences that feel like common sense. Plan-B framing operates less through explicit statements ("biological children are better"), few would say that, than through the ordinary machinery of language, medicine, and social ritual, which quietly installs the ranking without anyone having to defend it. This is what makes it durable: a belief that no one holds out loud cannot be argued down, because there is no one to argue with; it lives in the grammar and the paperwork rather than in anyone's stated creed.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

What does the evidence actually show about the different paths to family, and about the assumptions the hierarchy rests on? The honest answer is that the data undercut the ranking more than they support it, though with real uncertainty that must be stated plainly. The reader should be warned in advance that this chapter delivers no knockout study, because none exists; what it offers is a consistent direction across imperfect evidence, and a direction is enough to unseat a ranking that has always claimed to be simple common sense.

CH 6The hidden costs

Because the biological-first hierarchy frames itself as the natural default, its costs go unpriced. The genetic path is treated as free of trade-offs, and the alternatives are asked to justify themselves; but the ranking itself imposes real burdens, and they fall on people who never agreed to it. A default that never has to defend itself never has to count its costs either, which is precisely how a structure this heavy manages to feel weightless to the people who benefit from it.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

Biases are never distributed evenly. The biological-first hierarchy presses hardest on particular groups, and mapping them shows that Plan-B framing is not a free-floating attitude but a weight that falls along familiar lines of gender, class, sexuality, and culture. Where a burden lands tells you a great deal about its nature, and the pattern here is grimly familiar: the weight tracks existing lines of vulnerability, so that the hierarchy ranks families and, in the same motion, compounds inequalities that were already there.

CH 8The counter-arguments to the critiqueObjections to the critique

A fair book turns its thesis over and looks for the strongest objections. Several deserve serious engagement, and none is frivolous. The test of the critique is not whether it can dismiss these objections but whether it can survive conceding what is true in each of them, and the reader should judge the book by how much it gives away here rather than by how loudly it defends itself.

CH 9Case studies / illustrations

Four illustrations show the hierarchy at work, drawn from recognizable patterns rather than invented specifics; where a case reflects documented phenomena, it is described as a type, not attributed to named individuals. Their purpose is not to prove anything, since a type cannot prove, but to make visible in the concrete what the earlier chapters argued in the abstract, and to show that the hierarchy is not a theorist's construct but a texture people actually live inside.

CH 10Adjacent biases

Plan-B framing does not stand alone; it interlocks with other biases in the series, and mapping the joints keeps this book in its lane while showing how the pieces reinforce one another. The point is to locate the distinct work this bias does, not to re-argue the neighbors. Precision here is not pedantry: a bias that is not distinguished from its neighbors cannot be unlearned separately from them, and the whole value of naming this one lies in showing that it can survive the defeat of the others.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

What does clear-eyed, non-coerced reasoning look like once the biological-first hierarchy is named? Not a new ranking with the old order inverted, and not a prescription that anyone adopt or remain childfree. Seeing past the bias means something more modest and more demanding: holding the paths to family as genuinely open, each with its own goods and griefs, so that a choice among them is a choice and not the descent of a pre-built ladder. The modesty and the difficulty are related, because it is far harder to hold options genuinely open than to invert a ranking; inversion just swaps the top rung, while openness asks a person to notice the ladder every time they reach for it and decline to climb by reflex.

CH 12Conclusion

This book argued a narrow, defensible claim: the problem is not the desire for a biological child but the hierarchy that files every alternative (adoption, fostering, step-parenthood, and the childfree life) under "fallback," and that hierarchy distorts decisions, wounds families, and hides costs the default framing never has to pay.

Biological-first hierarchy
The tacit ranking of paths to family in which genetic, gestational parenthood is the real and ideal form and all alternatives are positioned beneath it. The central object of this book.
Plan-B framing
The habit of treating adoption, fostering, step-parenthood, and the childfree life as fallbacks or consolation prizes rather than as first-class choices with their own reasons.
Bionormativity
The assumption that genetic parenthood is the norm and the ideal against which other family forms are measured. (Term and authorship to verify.)
Genetic continuity
The valued connection of a child sharing one's genes, resemblance, and lineage; a real good for many, distinct from — and not to be confused with — the hierarchy built around it.
Gestational parenthood
Parenthood via pregnancy and birth; the embodied experience many people seek for its own sake, not merely as a means to acquiring a child.
Revealed priority
The ranking disclosed by where resources actually go (for example, the vast investment in achieving a genetic child before adoption is considered), as opposed to stated attitudes.
The language of trying
The idiom in which pursuing a genetic child is "trying," so that adoption or a childfree life register as "giving up" rather than as distinct, active undertakings.
Medical sequencing
The standard infertility pathway that escalates through increasingly intensive treatment and raises adoption only at the end, structurally coding it as the failure branch.
Alloparenting
Care of children by adults other than their genetic parents; a deep and widespread human practice that undercuts "invest only in genetic kin" accounts of human nature.
Feasibility ordering
Choosing the more available path first for practical reasons; a partial and legitimate explanation for the common sequence that does not, by itself, justify the symbolic ranking.
Ordinal (not categorical) bias
A bias that ranks options rather than excluding them, which makes it harder to notice and easier to defend ("of course adopted children are just as loved").
Narrative dispossession
Being denied authorship of one's own life story — for example, a chosen childfree life read as failure — by a pre-installed cultural ranking.
Consolation framing
Receiving a positive family outcome (adoption, a blended family) as though it were compensation for a prior failure rather than a good in its own right.
Assisted reproductive technology (ART)
Medical means (IVF, ICSI, donor gametes, surrogacy) of achieving a genetic or gestational child; a genuine good for many that also institutionalizes the biology-first sequence.
Step-parenthood
Parenting a partner's child; in the hierarchy's grammar frequently marked with an asterisk ("step-") that codes it as the lesser edition despite equal labor and love.
Fostering
Caring for children who are not one's genetic offspring, often temporarily; a profound commitment routinely framed by the hierarchy as a stopgap.
Childfree life
A life without children, whether by choice or circumstance; in Plan-B framing read as the residue left when other plans fail rather than as a destination.
Non-coercive framing
Reasoning that removes pre-installed defaults so all paths are genuinely open, without pushing anyone toward or away from any of them; the stance of this book.

⑥ Sources

What this volume draws on. Where the evidence is thin or contested, the book says so rather than reaching for a number.

  • David Benatar, Better Never to Have Been: The Harm of Coming into Existence (Oxford University Press, 2006). A well-known work of antinatalist philosophy; cited here as adjacent to the childfree discussion, with the note that its argument is about the ethics of coming into existence rather than about the hierarchy of family forms.
  • Sally Haslanger and Charlotte Witt, eds., Adoption Matters: Philosophical and Feminist Essays (Cornell University Press). A collection of philosophical and feminist essays on adoption, kinship, identity, and genetic essentialism.
  • The English and Romanian Adoptees (ERA) study, the long-running UK follow-up of children adopted out of Romanian institutions in the early 1990s, led by Michael Rutter and Edmund Sonuga-Barke. Referenced for the general shape of its findings: large recovery after adoption, alongside deprivation-specific difficulties that persist for those who spent longest in institutions.
  • United Nations, World Population Prospects (successive revisions). The standard source for global fertility and demographic trends; referenced as a class of source rather than for any specific figure.
  • The broad, well-established demographic fact of falling global fertility and sub-replacement total fertility rates in many countries. Attributed to the class of source (UN WPP and national statistical offices) rather than to any single table.
Directions the volume points to (not pinned citations)
  • The bioethics and philosophy-of-family literature on "bionormativity" and on the comparative moral standing of genetic versus adoptive parenthood.
  • The social-science literature on parenthood and subjective well-being, including its cross-national variation and the recurring finding that parents are not, on average, measurably happier than non-parents.
  • Research on the well-being, life satisfaction, and reported regret of childfree adults compared with parents.
  • Studies of adoptive-parent satisfaction and attachment alongside the comparable literature on biological parents.

  • The anthropological and cross-cultural literature on alloparenting, kin fostering, and communal child-rearing as widespread human practices.
  • The historical and legal literature on adoption institutions, including Roman adoption, and on inheritance, legitimacy, and bloodline in kinship law.
