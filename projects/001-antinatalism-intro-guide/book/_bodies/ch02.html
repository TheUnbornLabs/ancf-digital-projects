"Before you exist, there is no one for the decision to be about. After you exist, the decision is already made — and it was made by someone else."

— a way of stating the procreative asymmetry

Think for a moment about the ordinary moral life. We are forever making decisions that touch other people — hiring and firing, lending and borrowing, teaching, healing, marrying, voting. In almost every one of these, the person affected is somewhere nearby, able in principle to be asked, to agree, to refuse, to complain afterward if we got it wrong. The whole machinery of ethics — consent, fairness, apology, compensation — assumes a person on the receiving end who already exists.

Birth is the one decision that does not fit this picture. When we choose to create a new person, the being who will bear the entire consequence of that choice — a whole life, with all its joys and all its wounds — is not yet there to be consulted. They cannot agree, because there is no "they." They cannot refuse for the same reason. And once the decision takes effect, it has already produced the very person it was about. This chapter is about why that structure makes procreation a genuinely special case, and about what does and does not follow from its strangeness.

The structural asymmetry

Let us name the oddity precisely. In a normal decision for someone, two parties exist: the one deciding and the one affected. In the decision to procreate, only one party exists at the moment of choosing. The affected party is summoned into being by the decision itself. This is not a temporary gap that better planning could close. It is built into the situation. You cannot ask a future child whether they wish to be born, not because we lack the technology, but because there is, as yet, no child to ask.

Procreative asymmetry (structural) — the feature, unique to creating a person, that the one most affected by the decision cannot be a party to it, because the decision is what brings them into existence. It matters because every other tool we use for governing decisions about others assumes the affected party already exists.

Compare birth to the hardest cases we already handle. We constantly make weighty decisions for people who cannot consent: an unconscious patient in an emergency room, a newborn who needs surgery, a relative with advanced dementia. In none of these can we ask the person in the moment. So we have built careful institutions around them — medical proxies, guardians, advance directives — and we bind those institutions to a single governing idea.

Best-interest standard — the principle that when we decide for someone who cannot consent, we must act for their benefit, as they would plausibly want, not for our own convenience. It is the law's and medicine's answer to the problem of the silent patient.

Here is the crucial difference. The unconscious patient and the newborn already exist. They already have interests — a stake in living, in being free of pain, in being treated with dignity. When we decide for them, we are stewards of interests that are already there, waiting. The best-interest standard works because there is a self whose interests we can try to serve.

Procreation is not like this, and the gap is not a detail. Before conception there is no interest-bearer at all — no one who wants to be born, no one being deprived of birth, no one waiting in some antechamber of possible people. The decision to create a person does not serve a pre-existing interest; it manufactures the very thing — a center of needs and stakes — that all our other decisions presuppose. You cannot act in the best interest of a person who does not yet exist, because "their interest" has no subject until the act is done.

Every other decision serves an interest that is already there. Birth creates the interest-bearer itself.

Declining to create harms no one

This brings us to the asymmetry's most distinctive consequence, and one worth holding carefully in mind for the chapters ahead. If you fail to help a person who already exists — you withhold medicine, you ignore someone drowning — there is a victim. A real person is worse off than they would have been. Their interest in living, in being helped, goes unmet.

But if you decline to create a person, who is the victim? There is no one who fails to be born. There is no disappointed candidate, no one deprived, no one who would have wanted the life and now grieves its absence. The absence of a possible person is not bad for that person, because there is no person for it to be bad for. This is the heart of what later thinkers, especially David Benatar (whose four-claim asymmetry we will examine in a coming chapter), tried to formalize. Refraining from creating a life is, in this respect, unlike almost any other "failure to act" we can name: it leaves behind no one with a grievance.

In plain words: If you don't have a child, there is no sad someone wishing they had been born. The "missing person" is genuinely missing — there is no one there to be deprived.

Why "we can't ask" is not the end of the argument

It would be too quick to conclude from any of this that procreation is therefore wrong. The asymmetry tells us that birth is structurally unusual; it does not, by itself, tell us what to do about that. We should resist two opposite errors here.

The first error is to think that because we cannot ask, anything goes — that consent is simply irrelevant to birth and so drops out of the moral picture entirely. But we do not generally reason this way. When someone cannot consent, we usually become more cautious, not less. The impossibility of asking a patient does not free a surgeon to do as they please; it raises the bar. So the antinatalist suggests that the impossibility of asking a future person should, if anything, make us more careful about imposing existence — not give us a free pass.

The second error is to think that because we cannot ask, procreation must be wrong. That does not follow either. The point of this chapter is narrower and more honest: birth is a special case, structurally unlike our other decisions, and that specialness is a reason to think hard rather than a finished verdict. What we make of it is the work of the rest of the book.

Objection

The asymmetry cuts both ways. If, before someone exists, there is no one for the decision to be about, then there is no one who is wronged by being born and no one who is wronged by being created either. A child who is brought into a good life is not a victim of anything. So the "no victim either way" point cannot single out non-procreation as the safe choice — it dissolves the moral question rather than answering it. The special case, far from grounding a duty not to procreate, shows that neither choice wrongs a non-existent party. Why, then, treat refraining as the cautious default?

Reply

The objection is fair, and the antinatalist should grant its first half: a child born into a good life is not, in any simple sense, a victim. But the two choices are not symmetrical in their risks. To decline to create is to guarantee that no one is exposed to harm — there is no one to be hurt, and so no possibility of a bad outcome for anyone. To create is to expose a real, future person to the full range of life's possible suffering, including outcomes that can be terrible and that the person never agreed to face. The asymmetry does not claim that birth always produces a victim; it claims that only birth produces someone who can be a victim. When one option risks a serious harm to a person who cannot consent, and the other option risks nothing to anyone, caution does not treat them as a coin-flip. That is not yet a proof that procreation is wrong — but it is why the "special case" leans the way it does, rather than balancing out.

Holding the question open

None of this asks you to feel that existing lives are mistakes, or that the people around you would have been better off unborn. As the first chapter stressed, antinatalism concerns future births only; it says nothing against anyone already here, whose interest in their own life is real and weighty and worth defending. What the structural asymmetry offers is humbler: a clear-eyed picture of just how unusual the act of creating a person is. We make this one decision blind to the only person who will fully pay for it, and we cannot, even in principle, lift that blindfold. A thoughtful person can disagree about what follows. A thoughtful person should not pretend the situation is ordinary.

Chapter in brief

  • Birth is the only decision we make for a person before that person exists — so the one most affected cannot be a party to it.
  • We routinely decide for people who can't consent (patients, infants), but those people already exist and already have interests; a best-interest standard serves those interests.
  • Procreation is different because it creates the interest-bearer rather than serving an interest already present.
  • Declining to create harms no one: there is no deprived "missing person" — unlike failing to help someone who already exists.
  • "We can't ask" does not automatically mean "so it's fine"; with people who can't consent, impossibility of asking usually raises caution rather than removing it.
  • The asymmetry is a reason to think hard, not a finished proof — but because only birth can produce a victim, the cautious lean is real.
