PROJECT 060

Rationalism Toolkit

Learning Tools · Difficulty: Intermediate · ~9 min

Seven practical tools for thinking clearly about emotive topics — including this one. Use them on others' arguments and, harder still, on your own. Tick the ones you'll bring to your next discussion.

What you will learn

  • Seven thinking tools you can apply immediately.
  • How to turn down the heat and turn up the clarity.
  • How to audit your own reasoning, not just others'.

1. The tools

Steelman

State the opposing view at its strongest before replying.

Ask for evidence

"What would show this is true — or false?"

Separate is from ought

A fact about the world doesn't, by itself, settle what we should do.

Spot the fallacy

Name the move (ad hominem, false dilemma) without naming-calling the person.

Check your motives

Am I reasoning, or rationalising what I already want to believe?

Hold uncertainty

"I might be wrong" is a strength, not a weakness.

Plus the seventh, which underlies them all: be kind — clear thinking and contempt rarely travel together.

Key takeaway

Clear thinking is a practice, not a personality. Steelman, ask for evidence, keep facts and values apart, hold your uncertainty — and stay kind. Turned on your own views, these tools are how beliefs earn their keep.
