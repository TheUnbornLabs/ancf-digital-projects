BIAS 089

For the Children Not Yet Born: Selective Future-Generations Talk

Part VII · Philosophical & Ethical Fallacies · Selective Future-Generations Talk

The bias: Invoking future people only when it supports having more of them.

"We do not inherit the earth from our ancestors; we borrow it from our children." — attributed variously (proverb, provenance uncertain)
① In brief ② The book in twelve chapters ③ Key terms ④ Ten-question check ⑤ Reflection ⑥ Sources ⑦ Related biases

① In brief

Book 089 · 100 Biases of Natalism

The phrase think of future generations is often used to close reproductive arguments in one direction only: have more children, or approve of those who do, because the future needs to be peopled. This book names and dissects a specific failure of consistency in that appeal: not the belief that we owe something to those who come after us, which the book defends, but the selectivity of invoking obligations to future people only when they favor more births, while quietly setting the same obligations aside when they would counsel caution, fewer births, or attention to the quality of the world and lives we hand on.

Concern for future people has two faces. Continuation-concern looks at whether future people exist and counsels sustaining the human project. Welfare-concern (the qualitative face) looks at how future people will fare and counsels attention to their prospects and inherited world. A genuinely future-regarding ethic holds both in view. The bias, whose central metaphor is the one-way valve, keeps one face turned toward us and the other turned away. The one-sentence thesis: concern for future generations is a genuine moral reason, but it is misused when invoked selectively to favor more births while ignored where it would counsel restraint; consistency requires that the same concern be allowed to cut both ways.

Read the rest of the overview

The book is critical of pronatalist bias but is neither anti-natalist nor coercive. It prescribes no reproductive outcome. It also carefully distinguishes itself from neighboring biases: Book 078 (Children Are the Future) targets the affective slogan; Book 083 targets the repugnant conclusion internal to total utilitarianism; Book 065 targets reproduction-as-legacy. This book's lane is narrower: the inconsistent use of an otherwise legitimate moral idea, a structural problem that is theory-neutral.

The book steelmans the sincere pronatalist case at full strength (the human relay, the intrinsic worth of continued persons, Rawls's just savings principle, the asymmetry that most people affirm their own existence, collective stakes), then shows that this establishes only that future-concern can give reason to reproduce, not that it points only one way. It traces the selectivity's origins (evolutionary drive toward more but not better-off; the Abrahamic "be fruitful and multiply"; pension and demographic institutions rewarding rising populations; the rhetorical stickiness of hope over caution; the manipulable abstraction of a faceless beneficiary). It catalogues the operating mechanisms: conflation of continuation with quantity, directional gating of the same premise, the selective non-identity dodge, temporal cherry-picking, and burden-shift by abstraction.

On evidence, the book insists honesty about what is empirical and what is normative: falling fertility and sub-replacement TFR are real (feeding the quantitative face), and IPCC-documented inherited climate burdens are real (feeding the qualitative face), so a consistent reading requires holding both. The hidden costs of selectivity include losing the specific child as a subject, corroding the reasoner's integrity, misallocating moral energy away from stewardship, hiding distributive injustice, and foreclosing deliberation. The pressure falls hardest on women, the poor, and specific children with foreseeably hard prospects, precisely those whose interests sit on the suppressed side.

Seeing past the bias means applying future-concern honestly: run the consistency test (let the premise count on both sides), distinguish quantity from quality explicitly, put the specific future person at the center, separate the stewardship duty from the reproduction question, and proportion to uncertainty evenhandedly. This yields no formula and tells no one whether to reproduce. It replaces a script with genuine, two-sided deliberation. The book's parting demand: if we owe the not-yet-born anything, we owe them consistency, the willingness to let their interests count even when they count against us.

② The book in twelve chapters

Each chapter opens with its own summary. Note chapter 2: every volume states the strongest case for the thing it criticises before laying a finger on it — and chapter 8 turns the critique back on itself.

CH 1Naming the bias

The bias named in this book is selective future-generations talk: the practice of treating our obligations to future or not-yet-existing people as a reason that operates in one direction only, toward more reproduction, while suppressing or ignoring the same obligations when they would operate in the other direction.

CH 2The strongest case for it (steelman)The strongest case against this book

Before criticizing the selective use, I must present, as sympathetically and completely as I can, the sincere position that concern for future generations gives real and weighty reason to reproduce. This is not a straw target. Many thoughtful people hold it, and the best version of it deserves to be met at full strength. If the reader finishes this chapter unpersuaded that the pronatalist has something serious to say, I will have failed at the one task that gives the rest of the book its integrity.

CH 3Where it comes from

Why is future-generations talk so easily bent in the pronatalist direction? The selectivity has deep roots, evolutionary, religious, economic, and rhetorical, and naming them helps us see the bias as something that had to be installed, not something self-evidently true. A tilt that arrives from four directions at once will feel like the natural lay of the land rather than a slope someone graded, and part of the work of this chapter is to make the grading visible.

CH 4How it operates today

The bias does its work through concrete mechanisms in ordinary discourse and decision-making. These are worth cataloguing, because the selectivity is usually invisible to those exercising it. Naming the moves makes them recognizable, and a move that has been named is much harder to make unawares.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

What can be said with evidence, as opposed to intuition, about the two faces of future-generations concern? The honest answer is that some of the relevant claims are empirical and testable, others are irreducibly normative, and the bias thrives partly by blurring which is which. A great deal of the persuasive force of selective future-talk comes from letting an empirical fact ("populations are shrinking") do the work of a normative conclusion ("we ought to have more children"), a transition that no quantity of data can license by itself.

CH 6The hidden costs

The selective use of future-generations talk carries costs that its one-directional framing conceals. Because the appeal is allowed to push only toward more births, the considerations it suppresses simply drop out of view, and their disappearance is itself a cost. What is not weighed is not thereby weightless; it is merely unweighed, and the failure to see it does not make it go away, it only ensures we meet it unprepared.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

Selective future-generations talk does not fall on everyone equally. Its pressure is distributed along familiar lines, and tracing that distribution reveals whose interests the selectivity most reliably erases. An abstraction that is applied evenhandedly might at least distribute its errors at random; a bias, by contrast, has a direction, and the direction here is not neutral.

CH 8The counter-arguments to the critiqueObjections to the critique

Intellectual honesty requires that I now turn the critical apparatus on this book's own thesis and present the strongest objections to it. A critique of selectivity that would not itself survive scrutiny from the other side would be guilty of exactly the vice it names.

CH 9Case studies / illustrations

Abstract argument gains traction from concrete instances. Here are several illustrations of the bias in action, offered not as data but as recognizable patterns. Each is a composite of a familiar type rather than a report of a specific event, and the point of each is the same: to make the one-way valve audible in ordinary speech, so that the reader can hear it operating in conversations they will actually have.

CH 10Adjacent biases

This bias sits within a web of related pressures catalogued elsewhere in this series. Mapping the connections keeps our lane clear and prevents duplication. It also guards against a specific danger: because these biases so often travel together, it is easy to think that refuting one has refuted the others, when in fact each needs its own answer.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

What does clear-eyed, non-coerced reasoning about future generations look like once the selectivity is named? The remedy is not to reject future-concern; it is to apply it honestly, which turns out to be more demanding and more interesting than the slogan. The moves below are not a technique for reaching any predetermined answer; they are a discipline for making sure the question is genuinely open before it is answered.

CH 12Conclusion

I have argued for a narrow and, I hope, durable claim. Concern for future generations is a genuine moral reason; the steelman in Chapter 2 was offered at full strength and not retracted. But that reason is misused when it is invoked selectively: admitted as decisive when it favors more births, and ignored or deflected when the very same concern, honestly applied, would counsel caution about the welfare of the child or the burdens laid on descendants. The bias is not pronatalism and not future-concern. It is the one-way valve that lets the future speak only when it agrees with us.

Show all 17 terms as a list
Selective future-generations talk
The bias of invoking obligations to future or not-yet-existing people only when they favor more births, while ignoring the same obligations where they would counsel caution.
One-way valve
Metaphor for the directional gating by which a single premise is admitted when it supports a favored conclusion and blocked when it would undermine it.
Quantity–quality substitution
The conflation of "more people" with "good," displacing the welfare of actual and possible lives with their sheer number.
Continuation-concern
The face of future-regard that looks at whether future people exist and counsels sustaining the human project.
Welfare-concern (qualitative face)
The face of future-regard that looks at how future people will fare and counsels attention to their prospects and the inherited world.
Non-identity problem
Parfit's observation that a particular future person owes their existence to the very choices we might call harmful, complicating claims that we make them worse off than they would otherwise have been.
The asymmetry
The view that there is a strong reason not to create a miserable life but no comparable duty to create a happy one.
Just savings principle
Rawls's principle that each generation should preserve and improve the conditions of justice for the next.
Intergenerational justice
The field concerned with what present people owe to future people.
Directional gating
The mechanism by which the same premise is welcomed on one side of an argument and dismissed on the other.
Temporal cherry-picking
Selecting a flattering time horizon after the conclusion is chosen, to make the appeal land where desired.
Burden-shift by abstraction
The under-weighting of qualitative future-concern because its beneficiaries are faceless while the celebrated birth is vivid and concrete.
Stewardship duty
The obligation to leave future people a better world (climate, institutions, resources, knowledge), distinct from any duty to increase their number.
Consistency test
The remedy: asking whether one is willing to let "think of future generations" count on the caution side, not only the pro-birth side.
Steelman
The strongest, most sympathetic statement of an opposing view (here, that concern for future generations gives real and weighty reason to reproduce), presented in full before it is criticized.
Sub-replacement fertility
A total fertility rate below the level (roughly 2.1 children per woman in low-mortality settings) needed to sustain a population's size across generations.
Self-undermining charge
The objection that refusing to reproduce out of concern for the future defeats itself by protecting future people through ensuring there are none; the book replies that this trades on the conflation it exposes.

⑥ Sources

What this volume draws on. Where the evidence is thin or contested, the book says so rather than reaching for a number.

  • Derek Parfit, Reasons and Persons (1984) — the non-identity problem and the foundations of population ethics.
  • David Benatar, Better Never to Have Been: The Harm of Coming into Existence (2006) — the strongest statement of the asymmetry between the interests of the existent and the non-existent.
  • John Rawls, A Theory of Justice (1971) — the just savings principle and intergenerational justice.
  • Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), Assessment Reports — the physical-science basis for burdens imposed on future people.
  • United Nations, World Population Prospects (successive revisions) — the demographic backdrop of falling fertility and sub-replacement total fertility rate.
