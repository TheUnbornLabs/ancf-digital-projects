① In brief

Contraception and Childlessness as Failing

This volume examines a specific and sharp-edged bias within the wider natalist field: the framing of contraception, deliberate childlessness, and limited family size as sin, selfishness, or spiritual failure. It is distinct from the broad sacralization of childbearing treated in Book 043 (the sacred-duty bias) and from the secular selfish stereotype of Book 028; its target is the specifically religious and moral condemnation of those who prevent or forgo birth, and the burden of guilt that condemnation imposes.

The book's central move is a distinction it returns to on every page. It is one thing to hold, as a considered position, that a sexual act ought to remain open to the transmission of life, a claim about acts. It is another thing to look at the couple who use contraception, or the woman who does not bear, and pronounce them failures before God and neighbor, a verdict about persons. The first is a contested but respectable ethical claim; the second is the bias. The whole argument lives in the gap between them.

Read the rest of the overview

Because the stance of the series is to steelman the religious side first and in full, Chapter 2 presents the Catholic natural-law argument against contraception at its strongest and most sincere: a coherent metaphysics of human nature; the thesis of Humanae Vitae (Paul VI, 1968) that the unitive and procreative meanings of the sexual act are intrinsically inseparable; a genuine prophetic warning about the instrumentalization of persons; and a luminous theology of life as gift. The book insists this is not prejudice to be mocked but a serious position held by intelligent, devout people, and that respecting it is compatible with, and indeed helps, naming the bias that rides on its back.

The origins chapter traces the framing through scripture, Augustine, and the natural-law systematization of Aquinas. It handles the story of Onan (Genesis 38) with care, flagging the genuine exegetical debate: for centuries the passage anchored the condemnation of contraception (and gave English the word "onanism"), yet much modern scholarship locates Onan's sin in his refusal of levirate duty and his exploitation of Tamar, not in the physical act as such. This is presented as an open, unsettled question rather than a settled refutation.

The evidence chapter marks its own limits honestly. Its firmest datum is the deep internal diversity of the traditions: the Catholic magisterium condemns artificial contraception, but most Protestant traditions (after the twentieth century), many Jewish authorities, and much Islamic jurisprudence permit it without a sense of sin. That diversity is itself evidence that the "sin" coding is one contested position, not a univocal command of "religion." The chapter also notes the well-documented gap between official teaching and actual contraceptive behavior among many believers, and treats the literature on religious guilt and well-being as suggestive but thin, flagging specifics to verify.

Later chapters count the hidden costs: disproportionate guilt, the eclipse of the tradition's own subtle doctrines of conscience and culpability, the weight on the vulnerable, and the self-subversion of a "gift" enforced by fear, and ask who bears the pressure: disproportionately women, the most devout, the most constrained, and those least able to meet the demand. The counter-arguments chapter gives the objections to the book's own thesis a fair hearing, including the searching claim that the very idea of guilt-as-cost imports a secular, therapeutic worldview.

The book's constructive close disentangles the ethic from the verdict. Seeing past the bias, for the individual, means recognizing that the ethics of an act is not a sentence on one's soul, and using the tradition's own tools (conscience, the objective/subjective distinction, mercy, gradualism) to resist the leap. For pastors and institutions, it means teaching a demanding ethic without auditing persons.

Throughout, one rule governs: critique the bias, never the believer. The subject is the framing and the guilt it manufactures, never the faith, and never the faithful.

② The book in twelve chapters

Each chapter opens with its own summary. Note chapter 2: every volume states the strongest case for the thing it criticises before laying a finger on it — and chapter 8 turns the critique back on itself.

CH 1Naming the bias

The bias this book names is the framing of preventing or forgoing birth as a moral or spiritual failing: the coding of contraception, deliberate childlessness, or the deliberate limiting of family size as sinful, selfish, or a shortfall before God. Call it, for short, the sin-of-not-bearing bias. Its signature is a moral verdict where a permission might have stood: the person who prevents or forgoes a birth is read not as exercising legitimate discernment but as having failed a duty.

CH 2The strongest case for it (steelman)The strongest case against this book

Honesty requires that the religious case be built at its strongest and most sincere before a single objection is raised, and the natural-law argument against contraception is stronger than its secular critics usually allow. I will present it as its ablest defenders present it, in their own spirit, and I will not caricature it.

CH 3Where it comes from

The framing of prevention and non-reproduction as sin did not spring up whole. It is the sediment of several long histories, scriptural, patristic, philosophical, and institutional, and seeing the layers explains why the verdict feels so weighty even to those who, questioned closely, would not endorse its logic. A conviction that carries the accumulated authority of scripture, of the Fathers, of a rigorous philosophy, and of centuries of institutional practice is not easily examined; it arrives feeling less like an argument than like the shape of reality itself. Naming its layers is a way of loosening that grip enough to look at it.

CH 4How it operates today

A bias lives not in doctrine alone but in the ordinary texture of speech, expectation, and feeling. The sin-of-not-bearing framing operates today through mechanisms that are mostly quiet, often well-meaning, and for that reason hard to name and resist. None of these mechanisms requires anyone to be cruel or even to consciously judge; they work through the ordinary furniture of a shared life, which is exactly what makes them durable.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

What can be said with reasonable confidence, and what cannot, about this framing and its effects? The evidence is of several kinds, and honesty requires marking its limits as clearly as its content. I will not inflate a suggestive pattern into a proven finding, nor manufacture a statistic to lend the argument a false precision; where the ground is firm I will stand on it, and where it is soft I will say so.

CH 6The hidden costs

The default framing, prevention and non-reproduction as failing, presents itself as morally serious, even generous. What does it obscure? The costs it hides fall on the interior life, on the practice of conscience, on the vulnerable, and on the tradition's own best self. These costs are hidden precisely because the framing wears the face of a high ideal; it is hard to see the wound when the knife is called generosity.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

Biases are not distributed evenly, and this one has a distinct incidence. It falls hardest along the fault lines of gender, of devotion, of vulnerability, and of gatekept authority. To see who bears the pressure is not to change the argument but to sharpen it, because a framing that falls most heavily on those least able to resist it, and least responsible for it, has a moral character that abstract statements of the doctrine conceal.

CH 8The counter-arguments to the critiqueObjections to the critique

Fairness demands that the strongest objections to this book's own thesis be stated plainly and engaged honestly, not waved away. Several are serious, and a critique that could not survive its best objections would not be worth making.

CH 9Case studies / illustrations

Four illustrations, drawn from history and from ordinary life, show the framing at work and show, too, how the tradition itself has contained resources against the bias. None is offered as a knock-down proof; each is offered as a lens, a way of seeing the general argument take a concrete shape.

CH 10Adjacent biases

This bias interlocks with several others in the series, and mapping the joints clarifies what is proper to it and prevents this book from duplicating its neighbors. The value of the map is more than tidiness: each bias, seen clearly against its neighbors, is easier to name and resist in the wild, where they rarely appear one at a time.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

What does clear-eyed, non-coerced reasoning about contraception and childlessness look like once this bias is named? Not the abolition of the religious ethic, that is neither the aim nor within this book's authority, but the disentangling of the ethic from the verdict, for individuals and for institutions. The goal is not to talk anyone out of a sincerely held conviction about the marital act, but to interrupt the reflex that turns that conviction into a sentence upon persons.

CH 12Conclusion

This book has argued a narrow and, I hope, durable claim. The framing of contraception, deliberate childlessness, and limited family size as sin, selfishness, or spiritual failure is a bias, an illegitimate extension of a contested ethic about the meaning of sexual acts into a blanket condemnation of persons, and the guilt it imposes is a real harm that the underlying ethic, properly understood, does not require.

Sin-of-not-bearing bias
The framing of contraception, deliberate childlessness, or limited family size as sin, selfishness, or spiritual failure; the specific target of this book. Its signature is a moral verdict on persons where a permission might have stood.
Natural law
The ethical tradition, running from Aristotle through Aquinas, holding that moral norms are read from the inbuilt ends or purposes of human nature and its faculties, rather than from arbitrary rules.
Unitive and procreative meanings
The two meanings of the sexual act — bodily union of persons, and openness to the transmission of life — which Humanae Vitae holds to be intrinsically inseparable, such that deliberately severing them contradicts the act's inner truth.
Humanae Vitae
The 1968 encyclical of Pope Paul VI reaffirming Catholic teaching against artificial contraception on natural-law grounds; the primary modern statement of the "inseparability" thesis.
Onan / onanism
The figure of Genesis 38 whose "spilling of seed on the ground" was long read as a divine condemnation of contraception, giving English the word "onanism." Much modern exegesis instead locates his sin in the refusal of levirate duty (to verify).
Levirate duty
The custom obliging a man to father a child with his deceased brother's widow to continue the brother's line and name; the duty Onan refused in Genesis 38.
Openness to life
The disposition of welcome toward possible children that the traditional ethic prizes as a form of trust and self-gift; the bias codes its deliberate limitation as a failing.
Objective wrong vs. subjective culpability
The moral-theological distinction between an act's inherent character and a person's actual moral responsibility for it, given conscience, knowledge, and circumstance. The bias tends to collapse the two.
Conscience
The faculty of practical moral judgment. In many traditions a well-formed conscience is the proximate norm of action; the bias tends to override it with a flat verdict.
Scrupulosity
Chronic, excessive religious guilt over perceived sin, often over matters that are permitted or trivial; a documented pattern that the "sin" framing can foster.
Contra naturam
"Against nature"; the scholastic charge that deliberately frustrating a natural faculty's end (such as generation) is disordered — the philosophical spine behind the traditional condemnation.
Steelman
The practice of stating an opposing position in its strongest and most sincere form before critiquing it; applied to the natural-law case in Chapter 2.
Internal diversity of traditions
The fact that serious, devout religious traditions differ deeply on contraception (Catholic condemnation; Protestant, Jewish, and Islamic permissions), evidence that the "sin" coding is one contested position rather than a univocal command of faith.
Gradualism
The pastoral principle of accompanying persons in a gradual growth toward an ideal rather than pronouncing a flat verdict; a resource within the traditions that the bias tends to flatten.
Theology of the gift
The vision of life as something received and passed on rather than manufactured, and of the family as a school of self-donation; the most beautiful form of the traditional ethic, which the bias risks corrupting when it enforces the gift by fear.
Instrumentalization
The treating of persons (a partner, a possible child) as means or products rather than ends; the harm Humanae Vitae warns follows from severing sex from procreation, and the strongest strand of the steelman.

⑥ Sources

What this volume draws on. Where the evidence is thin or contested, the book says so rather than reaching for a number.

  • Paul VI, Humanae Vitae (encyclical letter on the regulation of birth), 1968. The primary modern statement of the Catholic natural-law argument and of the thesis that the unitive and procreative meanings of the marital act are intrinsically inseparable.
  • The Book of Genesis, chapter 38 — the narrative of Judah, Tamar, and Onan, including Onan's "spilling of seed on the ground." Any standard scholarly translation (e.g., the NRSV or JPS Tanakh).
  • Augustine of Hippo, On the Good of Marriage (De bono coniugali) and related writings on marriage, procreation, and concupiscence. Widely available in translation; source of the influential emphasis on the procreative purpose of sex and the goods of marriage (offspring, fidelity, sacrament).
  • Thomas Aquinas, Summa Theologiae — treatments of natural law (esp. the Prima Secundae) and of the generative faculty and its ends. Standard editions; the philosophical spine of the contra naturam argument.
Directions the volume points to (not pinned citations)
  • John T. Noonan, Contraception: A History of Its Treatment by the Catholic Theologians and Canonists (Harvard University Press) — the major scholarly history of the doctrine, and the single most useful book behind Chapter 6.
  • John Paul II, Man and Woman He Created Them: A Theology of the Body (the collected Wednesday audiences) — the developed personalist articulation of the Humanae Vitae ethic, and the strongest modern statement of the position Chapter 2 steelmans.
  • The Anglican Communion's 1930 Lambeth Conference and its resolution cautiously permitting contraception within marriage — commonly cited as the first major Protestant permission, and the hinge of the twentieth-century shift.
  • Scholarship on the exegesis of Genesis 38 and the "sin of Onan" — including the debate over whether the text condemns withdrawal as such or the refusal of levirate duty and the exploitation of Tamar. This book takes no side; readers should know that the traditional reading is one reading among several, which is precisely its point.
  • Scholarship in the psychology of religion on religious guilt, scrupulosity, and well-being. The general pattern — that guilt- and shame-based religious framings correlate with poorer mental-health outcomes where other framings do not — is well attested. Work specifically on contraceptive or reproductive guilt is thinner, and the book's claims here are correspondingly modest.
  • Comparative treatments of contraception in Jewish law (halakhah), including the debate over permitted circumstances and methods.
  • Comparative treatments of contraception in Islamic jurisprudence (fiqh), including the classical permission of ʿazl (coitus interruptus) and its extension by many jurists.
  • UN and national demographic sources on sub-replacement fertility, including within religious populations, and on the distance between official teaching and actual contraceptive behavior among Catholic populations. Cited here at the level of the class of source; this book states no precise figure it cannot stand behind.
