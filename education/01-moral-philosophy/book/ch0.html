Groundwork: A Short Map of Moral Philosophy

Groundwork

A Short Map of Moral Philosophy

T his book argues a hard claim in moral philosophy, and it names the philosophers it argues with as it goes. A reader who already knows the field can skip this chapter without loss. A reader who does not should not skip it, because the chapters that follow lean on a small set of tools and distinctions that they assume rather than teach. This chapter teaches them. It is a short map, not a survey, and it has one purpose: to put in your hands, before the argument starts, the few instruments you will need to follow it and to judge it. Nothing here is antinatalist. It is the common equipment of the discipline, the same whether you end up agreeing with this book or against it.

What Moral Philosophy Is For

Most questions are about how things are. Moral philosophy asks a different kind of question: not how things are, but how they ought to be, and what we ought to do. It is the study of right and wrong, good and bad, and the reasons that stand behind them.

It helps to separate two things the field is asking about, because the rest of the book moves between them and the move is easy to miss. The first is value: what is good and what is bad, what is worth having and worth avoiding. The second is obligation: what is right and what is wrong, what we are required to do and forbidden to do. These come apart. A thing can be good without anyone being obliged to bring it about, and an act can be wrong even when it produces something good. Pleasure is good, but you are not required to manufacture as much of it as you can. Lying to spare a feeling might produce a good result and still be wrong. Keep the two apart and a great deal of confusion dissolves. Much of this book is about value, the goodness and badness of pain, pleasure, existence, and non-existence. But one of its three central arguments, the one from consent, is about obligation, about what we may and may not do to another without their agreement. When the book seems to switch registers, this is the switch.

One more boundary, quickly. Moral philosophy is not the reporting of what people happen to believe is right, which is the work of sociology or anthropology. That a practice is widespread, traditional, or expected tells you how people behave, not whether they behave well. The whole of this series turns on that distinction. The later volumes describe the forces that make procreation feel obligatory; this volume asks the separate question of whether it actually is. Confusing the two, treating "everyone does it" as if it settled "it is right," is among the oldest mistakes in the subject, and naming it now will save trouble later.

Are There Moral Truths at All?

Everything so far has assumed that moral claims can be better or worse supported, even true or false, that "this is wrong" is the sort of thing one can argue about and get right. A thoughtful newcomer may balk at the assumption. Are there really moral truths, or is "cruelty is wrong" just a way of voicing a feeling, with no more fact behind it than a boo or a cheer? This is the territory of metaethics, the part of the field that asks not which acts are right but what we are doing when we call an act right at all. The book does not need to settle these questions, but a reader should know they exist and know where the argument stands toward them, because the most common dismissal of any moral argument, that it is "just an opinion," is really a half- formed metaethical claim.

The first division is between cognitivists and non-cognitivists. Cognitivists hold that moral claims are genuine assertions, capable of being true or false, like claims about the world. Non-cognitivists hold that moral claims do not state facts but express attitudes, approval, disapproval, endorsement, so that "cruelty is wrong" is closer in function to "boo, cruelty" than to "grass is green." Within the cognitivist camp the division goes deeper. Realists hold that there are moral facts that hold independently of what anyone thinks, and that these facts make moral claims true. Constructivists, a kind of anti-realist, hold that moral truths are real enough but depend on us, on what suitably idealized agents would agree to, or on our own deepest and most coherent commitments. Error theorists hold the bleakest view: that moral claims do aim at facts, exactly as the realist says, but that there are no such facts, so that every positive moral claim is, strictly, false.

Here is why the book can take no side in this and still proceed, and why that is not an evasion. The argument of this volume is conditional in form. It does not announce a moral fact from nowhere. It shows that if you hold certain widely shared starting judgments, a particular conclusion follows from them. A conditional of that shape holds whatever one's metaethics. The realist will read the argument as tracing the contours of mind-independent moral facts. The constructivist will read it as drawing out what our own considered commitments, once made consistent, actually require of us. Even the non-cognitivist can read it as showing that a person who starts with the relevant attitudes is committed, on pain of inconsistency, to the concluding attitude, since consistency governs attitudes as much as beliefs. Three readers with three theories of what morality fundamentally is can work through the same argument and each find it intelligible in their own terms. What the argument cannot do is move someone who has no relevant moral attitudes whatever, who feels and grants nothing about suffering or duty or worth. But no moral argument can move such a person, and the inability is not special to this book.

The one position that would dissolve the whole enterprise is the global error theory, on which all moral claims alike are false. If that view is right, then this book, and every rival to it, and the natalist's confident assumption that having children is fine, are all equally groundless, and the disagreement is too deep to be joined inside a work of first-order ethics. The book sets it aside, not because it is refuted here, but because it is a quarrel about whether to do moral philosophy at all, and this is a work that does it. With that one exception noted, a reader of any metaethical persuasion can engage what follows on equal terms.

This is also the place to retire the lazy version of "it's just your opinion." An opinion offered with no reasons, immune to argument, indifferent to consistency, is one thing. An argument whose premises are named, whose inferences are laid open, and whose weak points are marked for inspection is another, and everything in this book is meant to be the second kind. Whether or not there are moral facts in the realist's strong sense, there is a real difference between a claim you can interrogate and one you can only assert, and the work of the chapters ahead is to make every claim the interrogable kind.

Three Ways to Judge an Action

When philosophers ask whether an act is right, they tend to answer in one of three broad styles. You do not need to choose among them to read this book, but you need to recognize them, because the book's three pillars draw on different ones, and seeing which is which keeps the argument clear.

The first style is consequentialism. It judges an act by its results. An act is right, on this view, if it brings about the best outcome, the most good and the least bad, counting everyone affected. Utilitarianism is its most famous form: maximize well-being, minimize suffering. The appeal is obvious, since results plainly matter. The difficulty is equally familiar, since pure attention to results can seem to permit terrible means for good ends. Much of the asymmetry argument is broadly consequentialist in spirit. It weighs the goods and bads of existence against those of non-existence and asks which outcome is better.

The second style is deontology, from the Greek for duty. It judges an act by whether it conforms to the right rules or respects the right constraints, regardless, within limits, of results. Some things may not be done to a person even to produce a good outcome: they may not be used merely as a means, coerced, or have serious harms imposed on them without their consent. The appeal is that it protects the individual against being sacrificed to the sum. The difficulty is saying where the constraints come from and what to do when they collide. The consent argument of this book is deontological. It does not ask whether creating a person produces a good outcome; it asks whether imposing the condition of existence on someone who cannot agree is a thing one is permitted to do.

The third style is virtue ethics. It shifts the question from the act to the agent: not "what should I do?" but "what would a good person, a person of compassion, honesty, and practical wisdom, do here, and what kind of person does this choice make me?" It is the oldest of the three, and though this book is not built on it, its spirit runs through the series' insistence that a position be held without cruelty or self-deception. How one holds a hard view, the series suggests, is itself a matter of character.

These three are not always rivals; often they converge on the same verdict by different routes. Naming them matters less for taking sides than for locating an argument. When this book reasons about better and worse outcomes, it is working in the first style. When it reasons about what may be imposed without consent, it is working in the second. A reader who can feel that shift will never mistake one argument for the other, which is the most common way these debates go wrong.

What Makes a Life Go Well

The asymmetry is stated in terms of pain and pleasure, and its very first premise mentions, in passing, three theories about what makes a life go well. They deserve more than a passing mention, because the whole argument is an argument about welfare, about what is good and bad for a person, and you cannot weigh the goods and bads of existence without some view of what they are.

There are three main answers in the literature. The first is hedonism, which holds that well-being consists in pleasure and the absence of pain, understood broadly to include all enjoyable and unpleasant experiences. A life goes well to the extent that it feels good from the inside. The second is the desire-satisfaction theory, which holds that well-being consists in getting what you want, in having your preferences fulfilled, whether or not the fulfilment is felt as pleasure. On this view a life goes well when the things the person actually cares about come to pass. The third is the objective-list theory, which holds that certain things are good for a person whether or not they are wanted or enjoyed, things like knowledge, friendship, achievement, autonomy, and that a life goes well to the extent that it contains them.

Each captures something. Hedonism honors the obvious fact that suffering is bad and joy is good. The desire theory honors the thought that a life should go the way its liver wants it to. The objective-list theory honors the sense that some lives are impoverished even when contented, and some goods matter even when unfelt. Each also has its strain. Hedonism struggles with the person who would rather know a hard truth than enjoy a comfortable illusion. The desire theory struggles with desires that are confused or self- destructive. The objective-list theory struggles to say who draws up the list and on what authority.

This book does not need to settle which theory is correct, and it is careful not to. Its claims are meant to hold on any of the three, because every plausible theory of welfare agrees on the two points the argument actually uses: that suffering is bad for the one who suffers, and that the goods of a life, whatever exactly they are, are real goods. When the argument speaks of "pleasure," read it widely, as shorthand for whatever makes a life good, on whatever theory you favor. The asymmetry does not depend on hedonism, and a reader who assumes it does has misread it before it begins.

Good, and Good For Whom

Now the single most important distinction in the book, and the one a newcomer is most likely to stumble over, because ordinary language hides it. It is the distinction between something being good for a particular person and something being good full stop.

When we say a holiday was good, we usually mean good for the people who took it. The goodness is anchored in a subject; it is good for someone. Philosophers call this personal value, or value "good-for." But we also speak, and seem to mean something, when we say that a world with less suffering in it is better than one with more, or that it is good that a wildfire burned out before it reached a town, even in a version where everyone would have escaped. Here we do not seem to be saying it is good for any particular person. We seem to be saying it is good period. Philosophers call this impersonal value, or value "good-simpliciter."

Whether there really is such a thing as impersonal value, goodness with no one for whom it is good, is one of the genuine disputes in the field, and this book does not pretend it is settled. A whole tradition, called the person-affecting view, denies it, insisting that nothing can be good or bad unless it is good or bad for someone. The slogan of that tradition is that what is bad must be bad for somebody. The opposing camp holds that some value is impersonal, that an outcome can be better without being better for any particular person. You will meet both views by name many times, because the asymmetry's third premise, that the absence of pain is good even when there is no one to enjoy that absence, seems to require impersonal value, and the person-affecting tradition is its standing opponent. For now, simply fix the distinction in mind. "Good for her" and "good, full stop" are different claims, and a great deal turns on which one an argument is making.

A near relative of this distinction is the difference between value and reasons. That an outcome would be good is one thing; that you have a reason, still more a duty, to bring it about is another. We will see that some of our clearest moral judgments about creating people are best stated as claims about reasons, that we have strong reason to prevent suffering and little or no reason to manufacture happiness, rather than as claims about value. When an argument shifts from "this would be better" to "this is what you have reason to do," it has moved from value to reasons, and the move is worth noticing.

How an Argument Works, and How to Break One

This book is built out of arguments, and it will repeatedly ask you to judge whether one succeeds. That is a skill, and it has a shape worth making explicit.

An argument is a set of claims, the premises, offered in support of a further claim, the conclusion. A good argument has two separate virtues, and keeping them apart is the whole art of reading philosophy well. The first virtue is validity: the conclusion really does follow from the premises, so that anyone who accepts the premises is committed to the conclusion. The second is soundness: the premises are not merely connected to the conclusion, they are actually true. An argument can be valid and still worthless if a premise is false. It can have all true premises and still fail if they do not actually support the conclusion. Validity is about the connection; soundness adds that the starting points are true.

This gives you exactly three ways to resist any argument, and only three. You can deny a premise, showing that one of the starting points is false or unsupported. You can deny the validity, showing that even granting the premises, the conclusion does not follow. Or, in arguments that move from several considerations to a single verdict, you can challenge the way those considerations are combined, the step where they are weighed against each other and added up. When you find yourself unconvinced by an argument, the useful question is always: which of these am I doing? Not "I disagree," but "I deny this specific premise," or "I grant the premises but deny they get you there." A disagreement that cannot name its target is just a mood. The chapters that follow will model this constantly, stating an argument, then locating precisely where its critics get off, and you will get more from them if you read with the three exits in view.

One more habit worth naming, because this book practices it deliberately. After making its case, each chapter turns and states the strongest objection to itself, and marks honestly what that objection leaves unresolved. This is not weakness or hedging. It is how a position earns trust. An argument you have only ever seen defended is one you cannot really assess. An argument you have seen attacked at full strength, and judged to survive, you can hold with reason rather than faith. Expect every chapter to end by showing you its own soft spots. That is the design, not a lapse.

How to Read This Book

A last orientation, so the structure ahead is not a surprise.

The book makes its case in three independent arguments, called the three pillars, on the theory that a position resting on one argument falls when that argument falls, while a position resting on three can lose one and still stand. The first pillar is the asymmetry itself, the claim that the absence of pain and the absence of pleasure are not morally on a par, developed and defended in Part One. The second is the argument from consent, that existence is a serious condition imposed on someone who cannot agree to it, developed in Part Two. The third is the priority of preventing suffering over creating happiness, also in Part Two. Part Three then enters the hardest disputed territory, where all three pillars finally rest on a common and unsolved problem about how moral claims attach to people who do not yet exist. Part Four gathers every weakness the book has admitted along the way into a single honest inventory.

Each chapter follows the same rhythm: it states a claim, builds the case for it, raises the strongest objection against it, and ends by marking what remains unresolved. You can read straight through, or, if you want the spine of the position without the full apparatus, read the opening and closing sections of each chapter and the final chapter entire. However you read it, read it as the series means it: not as a doctrine to be swallowed or spat out, but as a position and its best opposition, set side by side, for you to weigh. The groundwork is laid. The argument begins.
