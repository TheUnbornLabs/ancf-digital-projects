BIAS 044

Sanctity Extended: From Protecting Life to Producing It

Part IV · Religious, Spiritual & Moral Framings · From Protecting Life to Producing It

The bias: Sliding from "life is sacred" to "you must create new life."

Thou shalt not kill" is a fence around a life that already stands. Whether one must also raise new lives to stand behind the fence is a different question, and the fence, by itself, does not answer it. — Paraphrase of a distinction drawn throughout the ethics of the sanctity of life (see Ch. 1; wording not a verbatim quotation). --- ## Preface to this volume There is a principle, ancient and widely held, that human life is sacred, that it possesses a worth so deep it may not be weighed on ordinary scales, traded away, or extinguished at convenience. In its religious forms this principle holds that persons bear the image of God; in its secular forms it holds that persons possess a dignity beyond price. Across both, its practical shape is remarkably consistent: it commands us to protect the life that is, to refrain from killing, to shield the vulnerable, to treat every existing human being as an end and never merely as a means. This is among the noblest commitments a person or a civilization can hold, and this book approaches it with respect rather than suspicion. The subject of this volume is not the sanctity principle itself. It is a particular move made from that principle, a move so quiet that those who make it rarely notice they have made it. The move is this: from "existing life is sacred and must be protected," one slides to "you must therefore produce new life." A commitment about how to treat the living becomes, without visible argument, a commitment to bring more of the living into being. A fence around existing persons is silently reforged into an engine for manufacturing new ones. The claim of this book is narrow and, I hope, exact: this slide does not follow. A principle that protects existing life carries no automatic implication that one must create additional life; treating the one as though it entailed the other is a category slide, and naming that slide requires taking no position whatever on the questions the sanctity principle was designed to answer. I want to be as clear as language allows about what this book is not. It is not an argument against the sanctity of life; I regard that principle as serious and, in its proper scope, admirable. It is not an argument about abortion, euthanasia, or the moral status of the unborn. It takes no stance on any of these, and a reader looking for one will be disappointed, by design. It is not an attack on people of faith or on the conviction that human beings are precious. The book concerns one thing only: the logical extension by which a protection ethic is stretched into a production obligation, and the reasons that extension fails as a matter of plain reasoning. One can affirm the sanctity of every existing life with the whole heart and still notice that this affirmation, on its own, says nothing about whether one is obliged to create a new life next year. The gap between protecting and producing is the entire territory of this book, and I mean to stay inside its fences. Why does this matter, if it is "merely" a point of logic? Because the slide does real work in real lives. When the sacredness of life is felt to imply a duty to have children, people who cannot or choose not to have them are made to feel they have failed not merely a preference but a sanctity, that in declining to produce life they have somehow offended against the value of life itself. That is a heavy burden to place on anyone, and if it rests on a logical error, then lifting it wrongs no one and relieves many. Naming the slide is an act of care toward believers and non-believers alike: toward believers, because a faith rests more securely on valid inferences than invalid ones; toward everyone, because no one should be shamed by an argument that does not actually reach its conclusion. The book proceeds in twelve chapters. The first names the bias and distinguishes it from its neighbors: the direct sacred duty to a divine command (Book 043) and the framing of childlessness as sin (Book 045). The second builds the strongest case a thoughtful person could make that the sanctity of life really does recommend producing it, and that case deserves a serious hearing, which Chapter 2 gives it before any criticism begins. From there the book turns to the origins of the slide (Chapter 3), its operation in ordinary life (Chapter 4), the philosophical evidence that protection and production come apart (Chapter 5), the hidden costs of conflating them (Chapter 6), and who bears the resulting pressure (Chapter 7). Chapter 8 answers the objections to the book's own thesis. Chapters 9 and 10 give illustrations and map adjacent biases. Chapter 11 asks what clear-eyed reasoning looks like once the slide is named, and Chapter 12 concludes without triumphalism. A final word on spirit. It is possible to make a logical point in a way that belittles those who reasoned differently, and I have tried hard not to do that. The people who feel the pull from sanctity to procreation are not confused or foolish; they are responding to a genuine and beautiful sense that life is good, and the mistake, where there is one, is a mistake anyone might make. To love the value of life so much that one wants there to be more of it is an understandable impulse. This book asks only that we notice where the impulse outruns the argument, and it leaves everything else, as it should, to the reader's own conscience. --- ## 1. Naming the bias Begin with two verbs that this book will keep firmly apart: to protect and to produce. To protect a life is to act toward something that already exists, to shield it, sustain it, refrain from destroying it. To produce a life is to bring into existence something that does not yet exist. These are different kinds of act with different objects. Protection has a patient: there is a someone, already here, toward whom the duty runs. Production has no patient in advance: before the act, there is no one whom the act benefits or wrongs, because there is as yet no one at all. Much of what follows turns on keeping this difference in view, because the bias this book names is the collapse of the difference, the treatment of "protect existing life" and "produce new life" as though they were two expressions of a single commitment. The sanctity-of-life principle, in its standard formulations across ethics and theology, is a principle about protection. Its canonical expression is a prohibition: thou shalt not kill. Its characteristic applications are defensive, against murder, against the exploitation of the vulnerable, against treating a person as disposable. When theologians speak of life as sacred because it bears the image of God, or when secular philosophers speak of an inviolable dignity, the work the principle does is to erect a boundary around persons who exist, declaring them not-to-be-violated. The principle's scope, in short, is the treatment of existing life. Whether one is additionally obliged to cause life is a different question, on which the prohibition against killing is silent. A rule that says "do not destroy what is here" does not thereby say "make more.

① In brief

From Protecting Life to Producing It

100 Biases of Natalism

This volume examines a single, quiet inference: the slide from "existing life is sacred and must be protected" to "you must therefore produce new life." A commitment about how to treat the living gets silently reforged into an obligation to bring more of the living into being. The book's claim is narrow and exact: this slide does not follow. A principle protecting existing life carries no automatic implication that one must create additional life, and naming that gap requires taking no position whatever on the sanctity principle itself or on any contested question to which it is usually applied.

Read the rest of the overview

The stance is one of respect. The sanctity of life, in its religious form that persons bear the image of God, in its secular form that persons possess a dignity beyond price, is treated as a serious and admirable commitment, not a target. The book is scrupulously fair to pro-life and sanctity-of-life ethics and takes no stance on abortion, euthanasia, or the moral status of the unborn. Its entire territory is the logical extension from protecting-life to producing-life and why, as plain reasoning, it fails.

The core distinction is between two verbs. To protect acts toward something that already exists; it has a patient, a someone already here toward whom the duty runs. To produce brings into being what does not yet exist; before the act there is no one whom it benefits or wrongs. The sanctity principle, in its standard formulations, is a protection ethic; its canonical form is a prohibition, thou shalt not kill, and its scope is the treatment of existing persons. Whether one is additionally obliged to cause life is a different question, on which the prohibition is silent.

Following the series template, Chapter 2 first builds the strongest natalist case at full strength: that reverence for a supreme good flows naturally into wanting more of it; that revering life while declining to transmit it wills its extinction; that gratitude for the gift of one's own life should flower into giving it onward; that if value is action-guiding, the supreme value of life should generate the strongest reason to create it; and that procreation is a participation in the divine creative act. The book concedes this case establishes that production can be a fitting expression of reverence, but argues it never shows production is entailed by it.

The positive case draws on population ethics: Jan Narveson's distinction between "making people happy" and "making happy people" (the person-affecting intuition that an act is better or worse only if better or worse for someone); the asymmetry (Book 079, via Benatar) that there is a duty not to create suffering but no symmetric duty to create happiness; and non-identity considerations. Together these show one can affirm the sanctity of existing life to the fullest while coherently denying any duty to produce new life. The book honestly flags that these commitments are contested, and that Savulescu's procreative-beneficence view runs the other way.

The slide is distinguished carefully from its neighbors: Book 043 (sacred duty, a direct divine command to procreate) and Book 045 (sin of childlessness, the consequence attached to not procreating). This book addresses only the inference from the value of life, not any command and not any verdict of sin.

The hidden costs are real: unearned guilt laid on the infertile, single, celibate, and childfree; a vulnerability introduced into the sanctity principle by burdening it with an invalid step; muddled moral reasoning; and amplified inequity falling hardest on women, the involuntarily childless, the devout, and the poor. The book closes by sketching clear-eyed reasoning past the slide, for individuals and institutions, that lifts an unwarranted burden while leaving the reverence, and every contested question, exactly where it found them. The reverence is real; the obligation is imported; telling them apart is an act of care.

② The book in twelve chapters

Each chapter opens with its own summary. Note chapter 2: every volume states the strongest case for the thing it criticises before laying a finger on it — and chapter 8 turns the critique back on itself.

CH 1Naming the bias

Begin with two verbs that this book will keep firmly apart: to protect and to produce. To protect a life is to act toward something that already exists, to shield it, sustain it, refrain from destroying it. To produce a life is to bring into existence something that does not yet exist. These are different kinds of act with different objects. Protection has a patient: there is a someone, already here, toward whom the duty runs. Production has no patient in advance: before the act, there is no one whom the act benefits or wrongs, because there is as yet no one at all. Much of what follows turns on keeping this difference in view, because the bias this book names is the collapse of the difference, the treatment of "protect existing life" and "produce new life" as though they were two expressions of a single commitment.

CH 2The strongest case for it (steelman)The strongest case against this book

Before criticizing the slide from protection to production, I want to build the strongest case that it is not a slide at all, that a serious, reflective person who reveres the sanctity of life could hold, with reasons, that this reverence really does recommend and perhaps require producing new life. This case deserves to be stated at full strength and in its own voice, because if it were weak the book would be trivial, and because the people who hold it are owed the dignity of their best argument rather than a caricature.

CH 3Where it comes from

The slide from protecting life to producing it did not arise from nowhere, and understanding its sources makes it easier to see as a contingent inference rather than an eternal truth. Several currents, religious, historical, linguistic, and psychological, converge to make the extension feel natural, even inevitable. None of these origins shows the inference to be wrong; origins never do that work. But seeing where a compelling idea comes from loosens its grip enough that we can examine it, which is all this chapter attempts.

CH 4How it operates today

The slide from protecting life to producing it is rarely stated as a formal argument. It works, instead, through a repertoire of everyday phrases, framings, and expectations that carry the inference without ever exposing it to scrutiny. This chapter catalogs the concrete mechanisms by which the extension operates in ordinary life, because a bias that never announces itself can only be resisted once its disguises are named.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

This is a book about an inference, so its "evidence" is largely the evidence of ethical and logical analysis rather than of data, though demographic and empirical facts enter at the edges. What we need to establish is that the move from "protect existing life" to "produce new life" is genuinely invalid: that one can hold the first with full conviction and consistently deny the second. The resources for showing this come from population ethics, from the person-affecting family of intuitions, and from the asymmetry between creating suffering and creating happiness. This chapter assembles them, stating honestly what they do and do not establish.

CH 6The hidden costs

Conflating the protection of life with the production of life is not a harmless imprecision. When the two are fused, real costs follow, costs borne by individuals, by the sanctity principle itself, and by the quality of moral reasoning in the communities that hold it. This chapter names those costs. None of them is an argument that the sanctity principle is false; each is an argument that the slide damages things worth protecting, including the principle.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

Biases are never distributed evenly, and the sanctity slide presses hardest on those already most subject to reproductive expectation. This chapter asks who actually carries the weight when reverence for life is fused with the obligation to produce it, because the answer reveals that the abstract inference has very concrete, and very uneven, human consequences.

CH 8The counter-arguments to the critiqueObjections to the critique

Intellectual honesty requires giving the objections to this book's own thesis their full strength before answering them. The thesis is that the sanctity principle does not entail a duty to produce life. Several serious objections press against it, and a reader who has followed the steelman of Chapter 2 will feel their force. I state each as strongly as I can, then respond, conceding what should be conceded.

CH 9Case studies / illustrations

Abstract argument is easier to grasp through concrete instances. This chapter offers four illustrations, composed to be representative rather than to report identifiable individuals, of the sanctity slide at work, and of what changes when it is named. The point of each is the same: to show that the reverence for existing life and the obligation to produce new life come apart in practice, and that separating them relieves a burden without diminishing the reverence.

CH 10Adjacent biases

No bias in this series operates in isolation, and the sanctity slide interlocks with several others. Mapping these connections clarifies what is distinctive about the slide and prevents this book from wandering into territory its neighbors cover better. The aim is to show the joints, not to duplicate the anatomy found in other volumes.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

What does clear-eyed reasoning about the sanctity of life look like once the slide from protection to production has been named? This chapter sketches it, for individuals wrestling with the question in their own lives, and for institutions that teach and transmit the sanctity principle. The aim is not to tell anyone what to conclude but to describe the shape of thinking that neither commits the slide nor abandons the reverence.

CH 12Conclusion

This book has made a single, narrow claim and tried to make it carefully: that the principle holding existing life to be sacred and worthy of protection does not, by itself, entail any obligation to produce new life. The move from protect to produce is a category slide, a stretching of a protection ethic into a production obligation, and it does not follow. Naming this slide has required no position on the sanctity principle itself, which the book has treated throughout with respect, and no position on any of the contested questions to which that principle is usually applied. The book takes no stance on abortion, on euthanasia, or on the moral status of the unborn, and a reader who came looking for one has, by design, not found it. The territory has been the inference and only the inference.

Sanctity of life
The principle that human life possesses a worth beyond price and may not be violated; in standard formulations, a principle about protecting existing persons and refraining from killing.
Protection ethic
A moral commitment concerning how existing life is to be treated: shielding, sustaining, and not destroying persons who already exist.
Production obligation
A putative duty to bring new life into existence; the alleged conclusion the sanctity slide illicitly derives from the protection ethic.
Sanctity extension (the slide)
The tendency to move from "existing life is sacred and must be protected" to "you must therefore produce new life"; the central bias examined in this book.
Category slide
An invalid inference that treats two different kinds of thing (here, protecting versus producing) as though they were expressions of a single commitment.
Making people happy vs. making happy people
Jan Narveson's distinction: a duty to benefit existing persons, but no parallel duty to create additional persons merely to add happiness.
Person-affecting intuition
The view that an act can be better or worse only if it is better or worse for someone; before a life exists, there is no one for whom its creation is better.
The asymmetry
The intuition (see Book 079) that there is a duty not to create a life of suffering, but no symmetric duty to create a happy life.
Non-identity consideration
The observation that who exists depends on when and whether one procreates, so a "duty to produce life" is owed to no particular person in advance.
Procreative beneficence
Julian Savulescu's view (cited as a contrast) that prospective parents have reason to select the child expected to have the best life; concerns which child, not whether to have one.
Maximizing reflex
The habit of treating any value as a quantity to be increased, which pushes "life is good" toward "so make more of it" without argument.
Openness to life
Institutional language recasting a decision about production as an attitude toward life itself, so that non-production is made to sound like hostility to life.
Continuity argument
The claim that revering life while declining to transmit it wills the extinction of what one holds sacred; answered by distinguishing existing persons from life-in-general.
Symmetry argument
The natalist claim that because value is action-guiding, the supreme value of life should generate a reason to create it, just as the badness of suffering generates a reason not to.
Sacred duty (Book 043)
The distinct claim that procreation is directly commanded by God or a sacred order; contrasted with, not identical to, the sanctity slide.
Sin of childlessness (Book 045)
The distinct framing of a childless life as a moral or spiritual failing; downstream of, and deliberately not entered by, this book.
Image of God (imago Dei)
The theological grounding, in several traditions, for the sanctity of existing persons; a reason to protect life that does not by itself specify a duty to produce it.
