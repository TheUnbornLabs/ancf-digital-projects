The Non-Identity Problem

Can you harm someone by creating them, if the alternative is that they never exist?

"It is not true that we can compare a person's existence with their non-existence and find the existence worse, for there is no one in non-existence to bear the comparison."

— a common paraphrase of Derek Parfit's worry

Much of the antinatalist case rests on a single, intuitive-sounding claim: that being born harms the person who is born. If creating a life is bad for the one created, then we have a powerful, person-centered reason to refrain. But there is a famous problem, set out most carefully by the philosopher Derek Parfit in Reasons and Persons (1984), that makes this claim far harder to state than it first appears. Parfit called it the non-identity problem, and it is one of the deepest puzzles in all of ethics. This chapter explains it in plain terms, shows why it presses so hard on the antinatalist, and then sets out — honestly, and without pretending the matter is settled — how antinatalists try to answer.

An everyday version of the puzzle

Imagine a woman who is told by her doctor that if she conceives this month, while she has a temporary, treatable condition, her child will be born with a mild but lifelong disability. If she waits two months and takes a short course of treatment, she can conceive a perfectly healthy child instead. Most of us feel she should wait. But here is the twist: if she waits, she will conceive at a different time, from a different egg and a different sperm. The child born in two months would be a different person — a different individual entirely — from the child she would have had this month.

So consider the child she actually has if she does not wait. That child has a disability. Could that child later complain, "You harmed me by conceiving me now"? It seems not — because the only alternative for that particular child was never to exist at all. The mother could not have given this child a healthier life; she could only have had a different child. And unless the disabled child's life is so bad as to be not worth living, that child has not been made worse off than they would otherwise have been. There is no "otherwise" for them. Their existence was the price of their existing.

Non-identity problem — Derek Parfit's observation (1984) that many choices which seem to "harm" a future person also determine which person comes to exist. If the alternative for that very individual is never existing at all, it is puzzling to say the act made them worse off, because there is no better state of theirs with which to compare it.

Why this threatens the antinatalist

The puzzle generalizes far beyond disability. Any child you create could only ever have existed by being created — by you, at that time, from those cells. There was no waiting version of that exact person enjoying a painless non-existence, who was then dragged into a life of suffering. For each actual child, the only options were this life or no them at all. So when the antinatalist says "you harmed the one you created," the critic can reply: harmed them compared to what?

This rests on a widely held idea called the person-affecting view.

Person-affecting principle — the view that an act is bad only if it is bad for someone — that there must be a person who is made worse off, or who has a complaint, for a real harm to have occurred. "No one wronged, no wrong done."

If the person-affecting principle is correct, the antinatalist's central claim looks confused. Take the strong antinatalist line that every birth harms the one born. The critic asks: which person is worse off, and worse off than what? Not worse off than their own non-existence — for in non-existence there is no them to be better off. There is no subject sitting in the void, deprived, with a grievance. The harm seems to float free of any person who bears it.

The objection

Birth cannot harm the one born, because harm is comparative. To be harmed is to be made worse off than you otherwise would have been. But the only alternative to your existence is your non-existence — and there is no "you" in non-existence to be better off. So there is no one for whom being born is worse, and on any person-affecting view of ethics, no harm has been done. The antinatalist's headline claim — "coming into existence is always a harm to the person created" — may not even be coherent. Whatever is troubling about a hard life, it cannot be that the person was made worse off by being given it, for the alternative was not a better life but no life of theirs at all.

This is a serious objection, and it should be felt as one. It does not deny that lives contain suffering. It grants all of that. It simply denies that "suffering exists in a life" adds up to "the person was harmed by being given the life," once we notice there was no prior, better-off version of them to be set back.

Harmed compared to what? For each of us, the alternative was not a better life but no life at all.

How antinatalists try to answer

Antinatalists have two main routes, and it is fair to say neither is uncontested.

The first route is impersonal or non-comparative. Some antinatalists, drawing on David Benatar, argue that we should not insist harm always be comparative in the person-affecting sense. We can sometimes judge an outcome bad without finding a particular person who is worse off than they would have been. We readily say a world with less suffering in it is, on that count, better — even if no specific resident has been spared. On this view, coming into existence introduces a guaranteed quantity of suffering that would otherwise not have occurred at all, and that is a real downside, full stop, whether or not we can name a deprived subject in the void.

The second route is comparative but careful. Benatar himself argues we can compare existence with non-existence — not by imagining a person who enjoys non-existence, but by noting that non-existence contains no pain, and a life does. We can say it is better for a person never to suffer than to suffer, and read "never having existed" as the state in which they never suffer. The comparison is between two ways things could go, not between two states a single subject occupies. We will examine the weak point in this move in the next chapter.

The reply (contested)

The antinatalist grants that the non-identity problem dissolves the simplest version of "birth harms you." But they argue the person-affecting principle is too strict. We make impersonal judgments about future suffering all the time — we think it matters whether the future contains more agony or less, even when the sufferers' identities depend on our choices. If that kind of judgment is legitimate, then creating a guaranteed bundle of suffering can be bad without our having to point to a pre-existing victim. Antinatalists also note that the problem cuts both ways: if non-identity blocks the claim that we harm a child by creating them into hardship, it equally blocks the cheerful pro-natal claim that we benefit a child by creating them into happiness — for there was no prior them to benefit either. The asymmetry, they argue, must be settled on other grounds.

Be clear about the honest state of play. Many philosophers think the non-identity problem shows that person-affecting ethics, taken strictly, cannot ground the strong antinatalist claim — and that the antinatalist must therefore lean on an impersonal principle that is itself disputed. Others think the impersonal move is exactly right and that strict person-affecting ethics gives implausible results elsewhere too. What you should not conclude is that the puzzle is a mere trick. It is a genuine fault line, and where you stand on it will shape how much of the antinatalist case you can accept.

In plain words: The hardest objection here is not "lives aren't bad" but "you can't be harmed by being born, because there was never a better-off you to harm." Antinatalists answer by appealing to a broader, impersonal sense of bad — a move that has real force, and real critics.

Chapter in brief

  • The non-identity problem (Parfit, 1984) notes that the choices which "harm" a future person often determine which person exists at all.
  • On a person-affecting view, an act is bad only if it is bad for someone — and there is no pre-existing person in non-existence to be made worse off.
  • This threatens the strong claim that being born harms the one born, since there is no better state of theirs to compare a hard life with.
  • Antinatalists reply by appealing to impersonal or carefully non-comparative judgments about suffering — a move with genuine force but genuine critics.
  • The puzzle is real and unresolved; it should make us cautious about the simplest version of the harm claim.
