The Fence-Sitter’s Toolkit

Practical ways to explore the question when you genuinely don’t know yet.

"I can live with doubt and uncertainty and not knowing. I think it is much more interesting to live not knowing than to have answers that might be wrong."

— Richard Feynman

Somewhere between the people who have always known they wanted children and the people who have always known they did not, there is a large and frequently overlooked middle: those who genuinely do not know. In childfree communities they are affectionately called fence-sitters, and if that describes you, the first thing to hear is that you are not broken, late, or indecisive in some shameful way. You are facing one of the largest decisions a human being can make, one that is irreversible in one direction and only sometimes reversible in the other, and treating it with uncertainty is not weakness — it is proportion.

Fence-sitter — someone who has not yet decided whether to have children. Far from being a rare or troubled state, it is an extremely common and entirely legitimate stage. Many decided people, in both directions, were fence-sitters first.

This chapter is a toolkit rather than a lecture. None of these exercises will hand you an answer; they are designed to bring your own answer closer to the surface where you can see it. Use the ones that resonate and ignore the rest.

Why ambivalence is normal — and even useful

We tend to treat certainty as the mark of a healthy mind and doubt as a malfunction. For ordinary purchases and weekend plans, fine. But for a decision of this size, certainty arrived too quickly is often just an inherited script (see Chapter 5) wearing the costume of conviction. Ambivalence, by contrast, is your mind insisting on taking the question seriously. It is uncomfortable, and the discomfort has a purpose: it keeps you looking until you have looked enough.

In plain words: Sitting on the fence is not failing to decide. It is deciding carefully. The goal is to climb down on the side that is genuinely yours — not to climb down fast.

Tools for the undecided

What follows are concrete practices that fence-sitters across many years and many books have found clarifying. Think of them as instruments, each measuring a slightly different thing.

1. Keep an honest journal

Vague feelings stay vague until they are forced into sentences. Write, without an audience, about your hopes and dreads on this question — not what you think you should feel, but what actually surfaces when no one is watching. Patterns emerge over weeks that no single moment of reflection reveals. Notice which entries bring relief and which bring dread; the body often knows before the mind admits it.

2. Run the regret-minimization experiment

Project yourself far forward — to seventy or eighty — and look back. Imagine the version of you who chose children, and the version who did not. Which "future self" do you flinch from, and which do you envy? This thought experiment, popularized in business decision-making, works because it quiets today's anxieties and asks which path your oldest self could most peacefully live with. It does not predict the future; it surfaces your deepest priorities.

3. Talk to happy people on both sides

Seek out contented parents and contented childfree people — not the cautionary tales, but those who chose well and are glad. Ask them what is hard, what is wonderful, and what they wish they had known. Hearing two genuinely happy lives, lived in opposite directions, breaks the illusion that one path is happiness and the other is regret. It also shows you which life, in its honest detail, makes you lean forward.

4. Separate the fear-based "no" from the genuine one

As Chapter 5 explored, dread and preference can feel identical. A practical version: list your reasons, then mark each as toward something you want or away from something you fear. A no built mostly of fears may dissolve when the fears are examined; a no built of genuine preference will not.

5. Ask whether you want a baby or want to parent

A great deal of broody longing is for a baby — a soft, brief, enormously appealing season. Parenting, however, is the eighteen-to-eighty-year project of raising and worrying over a whole person. Picture the teenager, the adult, the lifelong responsibility, not just the infant. If the long version still draws you, that is meaningful. If only the baby does, that is meaningful too.

6. Notice whether you want the life or the approval

Imagine choosing children and receiving zero congratulation — no proud parents, no social nod, nothing but the daily reality. Does the appeal survive? Now imagine choosing childfree and facing only disapproval. Does the appeal survive that? Stripping out the social reward isolates what you actually want from what you have been trained to want.

Worth knowing

  • Many people remain genuine fence-sitters for years, and some find clarity only when an external change — a relationship, a birthday, a health event — finally tips a long-balanced scale.
  • There is no deadline of conscience, only the softer deadline of biology — covered carefully, and without panic, in the next chapter.
  • Indecision is itself a kind of data: persistent inability to choose parenthood, when the choice is genuinely open, sometimes reflects a quiet preference that has not yet been given permission to speak.
  • You are allowed to change your provisional answer as many times as you need. Nothing is decided until something is done.
You do not have to choose today. You only have to keep listening honestly until the answer is unmistakably yours.

A short checklist to revisit

Return to these from time to time. The aim is not a tally but a feeling — noticing whether your answers are steadying over time or still genuinely split.

  • When I imagine each path at eighty, which future self is more at peace?
  • Am I drawn to the baby, or to parenting a person for the rest of my life?
  • How much of my hesitation is fear I could address, and how much is preference I should respect?
  • If approval and disapproval were removed entirely, which life would I quietly pick?
  • Over the past months, has my leaning steadied in one direction, or stayed evenly split?
  • What single piece of information or experience would help me most — and how might I go get it?

There is no prize for deciding quickly and no penalty for deciding slowly, within the limits the next chapter describes. A fence-sitter who eventually steps down with clear eyes has done better than someone who jumped early to escape the discomfort of not knowing. Stay on the fence as long as you honestly need to. Just keep the conversation with yourself open, and keep it kind.

Chapter in brief

  • Being a fence-sitter — undecided about parenthood — is common and legitimate, not a flaw.
  • Ambivalence about a huge, partly irreversible decision is proportionate; it keeps you looking until you have looked enough.
  • Practical tools include journaling, the regret-minimization thought experiment, talking with happy people on both sides, separating fear-based from genuine reasons, asking whether you want a baby or want to parent, and stripping out social approval to find your real preference.
  • Watch whether your leaning steadies over time; persistent indecision can itself be quiet data.
  • There is no deadline of conscience — only biology's softer one, handled calmly in the next chapter.
