Part V · Objections & RepliesCHAPTER 27

The Optimist's Reply

The case that life is, for most, a gift worth giving.

"Most people are glad to be alive, and not because they have miscalculated."

— a fair summary of the optimist's starting point

So far the critic has chipped at the antinatalist's foundations — the harm claim, the asymmetry. This chapter takes the offensive. It assembles the strongest positive case against antinatalism: the view that creating a life is not a harm to be excused but, often, a gift worth giving. Call it the optimist's reply. It does not require denying that life contains suffering. It requires only denying that suffering is decisive — that the bads of a life automatically outweigh, or poison, the goods. And on that narrower point, the optimist has a great deal to say.

What most lives actually report

Begin with the plainest fact, the one the antinatalist must explain away rather than the other way around: most people, asked directly, are glad to exist. Across cultures and circumstances, self-reported life satisfaction tends to land well above the midpoint. Most people, given the honest choice, would not undo their own existence, and most do not regard their lives as a mistake they were tricked into. The optimist takes this seriously as evidence. If a life is bad for the one living it, the one living it is usually in the best position to notice — and usually does not.

Benatar has a famous counter: people are unreliable judges, their cheerfulness inflated by adaptation, optimism bias, and a reluctance to face the worst. The optimist grants there is something to this — but presses the obvious rejoinder: adaptation cuts both ways. If our minds distort upward, perhaps the antinatalist's gloom is the distortion and the contented majority the better guide. There is no neutral perch from which to declare that everyone who enjoys their life is fooled while the philosopher who doubts it sees clearly. To assume so is to assume the conclusion.

The optimist's reply — the family of pro-natal arguments holding that the goods of a typical human life (love, meaning, achievement, relationship, joy) are real and weighty, that suffering does not automatically outweigh them, and that to refuse to create a life is to foreclose all of those goods for the sake of avoiding harms most people judge worth bearing.

The reality of the goods

The heart of the optimist's case is that the goods of a life are not consolation prizes or illusions. Love is real. So is the satisfaction of work done well, the depth of long friendship, the wonder of understanding something, the warmth of being known. The antinatalist ledger tends to treat pleasures as mere relief from prior lacks — hunger fed, boredom broken — and so as never quite getting ahead. The optimist denies this accounting. Much of what is best in a life is not the patching of a deficit but a positive achievement: a piece of music, a reconciliation, a child's trust, a morning that is simply good. To call all of this a thin coating over an essentially negative substance is, the optimist says, to mis-describe the very thing under discussion.

And here is the move that gives the reply its sting: non-existence forecloses every one of these goods. The antinatalist focuses on the suffering a never-born person is spared. But that same person is spared nothing they could enjoy, because they enjoy nothing. To prevent a life is to prevent not only its pains but its loves, its discoveries, its laughter — and the optimist insists those count. A policy of universal refusal is not neutral; it is the permanent closing of a door behind which most of what we cherish lives.

The objection (the optimist's case)

Most people are glad to exist, and they are not obviously deceived. The goods of a life — love, meaning, achievement, relationship — are genuine and substantial, not mere relief from prior want. Suffering is real but not decisive: we routinely and rationally accept real costs for goods we judge worth the price, and a life is the grandest such bargain. Antinatalism asks us to treat the worst possible reading of every life as the true one and to weigh avoided pain at full value while pricing the forgone joys at zero. That is not caution; it is a thumb on the scale. To refuse to create a person who would, in all likelihood, be glad you did is not obviously a kindness to them — it is the prevention of someone who would have wanted to be. The burden of proof sits with the one who would close the door.

The critics, and what each move represents

The optimist's reply is not a single argument but a coalition, and several serious philosophers occupy its different wings. It is worth naming them, because doing so shows that resistance to antinatalism is not mere flinching but a body of careful work.

David Wasserman presses the value of existence and challenges the claim that creating a life wrongs the one created; he is a leading voice arguing that procreation can be permissible and even good. Elizabeth Harman attacks the asymmetry directly (we met her in the last chapter), defending the view that the goods of a life can make creating it benefit the one created. Christopher Belshaw takes a subtler path: he is sympathetic to some antinatalist conclusions yet resists the strong, universal version, arguing the badness of death and the value of lives are more nuanced than the blanket claim allows. Thaddeus Metz, a leading theorist of life's meaning, argues that meaning is a real and weighty good that the antinatalist ledger tends to undercount. Saul Smilansky raises sharp paradoxes — including the worry that consistent antinatalism would have unsettling implications we are unwilling to accept. And Rivka Weinberg, in The Risk of a Lifetime (2016), gives perhaps the most disciplined middle position: procreation is neither automatically fine nor blanket-wrong, but permissible under conditions — when prospective parents take on the right responsibilities toward the risk they impose.

Notice the spread. These are not people denying that life is hard. Several grant the antinatalist a great deal. What they share is a refusal to let the reality of suffering settle the question by itself — and a sense that the goods, the gladness, and the conditions of responsible choice all belong in the accounting.

To prevent a life is to prevent not only its pains but its loves, its discoveries, its laughter.

The antinatalist reply — without pretending it is a knockout

The antinatalist has answers, and they are not feeble — but they do not flatten the optimist either. To the gladness data: that most people endorse their lives shows the lives are bearable and loved, but not that it was permissible to start them without consent under guaranteed risk; a gift no one could refuse is still imposed. To the reality of the goods: the antinatalist need not deny the goods are real, only that their existence justifies creating a being who must also be exposed to the worst the world can do — and the worst can be very bad, and falls on the unlucky who never agreed to the gamble. To the foreclosed joys: a never-existent person is not deprived of love or discovery, because there is no one there to be deprived; whereas an actual person can be ground down by misfortune. The asymmetry, the antinatalist insists, still does some work even after the optimist's case is granted its full weight.

The antinatalist reply

Gladness shows lives are loved, not that imposing them was permissible — consent cannot be obtained from someone who does not yet exist, and the risk is real and one-directional. The goods are granted; the question is whether their availability licenses gambling another person's whole existence on the world treating them gently, when it sometimes does not. And the forgone joys harm no one, because no one is forgoing them, while the forgone sufferings would have fallen on a real person. None of this proves the optimist wrong. It locates exactly where the disagreement lives: in how much weight consent and worst-case risk should carry against the genuine, undeniable goods of a life. Honest people weigh them differently — and that, not a decisive refutation, is the truthful end of the matter.

In plain words: The strongest case against antinatalism is not "life isn't hard." It is "most people are glad they exist, the goods are real, and refusing to create a life closes off all of them." The antinatalist answers with consent and worst-case risk — a real answer, not a knockout. Thoughtful people land on both sides.

Chapter in brief

  • The optimist's reply grants that life contains suffering but denies that suffering is decisive.
  • Its evidence: most people are glad to exist and are not obviously deceived — and adaptation cuts both ways.
  • Its core claim: love, meaning, and achievement are real goods, not illusions, and non-existence forecloses every one of them.
  • Serious critics span a spectrum — Wasserman, Harman, Belshaw, Metz, Smilansky, and Weinberg's conditional permissibility — each a distinct, careful move.
  • The antinatalist answers with consent and worst-case risk; the disagreement is genuine and not settled by either side.
