"The best time to plan was years ago. The second-best time is this afternoon."

— a common saying among estate planners

There is a quiet assumption built into how most societies handle death and serious illness: that somewhere there is an adult child who will step in. They will be the next-of-kin a hospital phones, the person who knows where the will is, the one who makes the hard call when a parent can no longer speak for themselves. For people without children, that default does not exist — and the absence is exactly why planning matters more, not less. This is not a morbid chapter. It is, in a real sense, one of the most loving things this book can offer: the practical machinery for making sure your wishes are known, your affairs are tidy, and the people you trust have the authority to help you when you most need it.

Everything below is offered at a general level. The names of documents, the formalities that make them valid, and the powers they grant vary enormously from one country, state, or province to another. Treat this as a map of the territory and a vocabulary to bring to a professional — not as legal advice. The single most useful action this chapter can prompt is a conversation with a qualified estate-planning attorney or notary in your own jurisdiction.

In plain words: Without adult children, no one is automatically appointed to speak for you or sort out your affairs. Planning documents let you choose who does — instead of leaving it to a court or a default rule that may not reflect your life at all.

Why the stakes are higher without adult children

When someone dies or becomes incapacitated without clear instructions, the law falls back on default rules. Those rules were largely written with a traditional family in mind — spouse, then children, then parents, then siblings, and outward through blood relatives. If you have no children, the people the law reaches for next may be a sibling you are not close to, an elderly parent, a distant cousin, or — if no relative can be found — a public official or the state. The friends, partners, and chosen family who actually share your life often have no automatic standing at all, however devoted they are. Planning is how you close that gap.

There are really two jobs to plan for, and people often think only of the first:

  • What happens after you die — handled mainly by a will (and sometimes a trust).
  • What happens if you are alive but cannot decide for yourself — handled by powers of attorney and advance directives. This second category is the one most often neglected, and arguably the one where having no adult child to step in bites hardest.

The will, and the person who carries it out

Will — a legal document setting out who receives your property after you die, and who is responsible for carrying out those wishes. Without a valid will, your estate is distributed according to your jurisdiction's intestacy rules, which follow blood relationships and may bypass the people and causes you care about most.

A will lets you name your beneficiaries — and here the childfree life opens up a genuinely wide field. Beneficiaries can be a partner, friends, nieces and nephews, godchildren, and just as readily the causes that have given your life meaning: charities, a university, an animal shelter, a community organization, a research fund. Many people without children find deep satisfaction in directing what they have built toward work that will outlast them (Chapter 19 explores this kind of legacy more fully).

Executor (sometimes called a personal representative) — the person you appoint to administer your estate: gathering assets, paying debts, and distributing what remains according to your will. It is a real job with real paperwork, so choose someone organized, trustworthy, and willing — and ask them first.

Choosing an executor matters more when there is no obvious adult child to assume the role. A capable friend, a trusted relative, or a professional (a solicitor, a bank's trust department, a licensed fiduciary) can all serve. It is wise to name a backup in case your first choice cannot act. Where assets are substantial or family circumstances are complicated, a trust may also be worth discussing with an attorney — but whether it suits you is exactly the kind of question that depends on local law and your own situation.

A plan is simply you, on a good day, taking care of yourself on a bad one.

Planning for incapacity, not just death

Illness or injury can leave a person alive but unable to make or communicate decisions — temporarily after surgery, or longer-term with a stroke or dementia. For a parent, an adult child often quietly fills this gap. For everyone else, the authority has to be granted in advance, in writing, while you are well. Three documents do most of this work, though their names differ by place.

Healthcare proxy / medical power of attorney — names a trusted person to make medical decisions on your behalf if you cannot. This is the agent the doctors will turn to. Without one, the choice may default to a relative you would not have picked, or stall while the question is sorted out.
Durable / financial power of attorney — authorizes someone to manage your money, property, and day-to-day affairs (paying bills, managing accounts) if you become unable to. "Durable" generally signals that it remains in force even after you lose capacity — which is the whole point — but the term and its rules vary, so confirm locally.
Advance directive / living will — records your own wishes about medical treatment near the end of life: what interventions you would or would not want. It speaks for you when you cannot, and it spares the people you love from guessing.
Common worry

"All my trusted people are around my own age. Won't they be gone or frail exactly when I need them?"

In reality

This is a genuinely good reason to think across generations. Childfree people often appoint agents who are noticeably younger — a niece or nephew, a younger friend or colleague, a goddaughter — precisely so the person is likely to be available and capable when the time comes. Naming alternates, and revisiting your choices every few years, builds in resilience. Professionals (fiduciaries, trust companies) can also serve where no individual fits.

Choosing — and keeping — your trusted people

The documents are only as good as the people you name in them. Look for trustworthiness and good judgment over mere closeness; willingness (always ask before naming someone); and, often, relative youth and stability so your agent is likely to be there and able. Spreading roles across more than one person can help — the friend who is wonderful in a hospital room may not be the one you want managing your investments.

Finally, plans go stale. Lives change: people move, relationships shift, named agents age or pass away, laws are amended, assets grow or shrink. A plan made and forgotten can be worse than none, if it appoints someone who is no longer the right choice. Build in a review — many people revisit everything every three to five years and after any major life event.

Worth knowing

  • Documents only work if people can find them. Tell your agents where the originals are kept, and consider giving them copies.
  • Beneficiary designations on retirement accounts and life insurance often pass outside the will and can override it — review them directly.
  • Digital life matters too: think about who can access your accounts, photos, and devices, and how.
  • If you have pets, you can plan for their care; ask a professional how that is best arranged where you live.

A starting checklist to take to a professional

Make or update a will, naming beneficiaries (people and/or causes).
Name an executor and a backup — and ask them first.
Set up a healthcare proxy / medical power of attorney.
Set up a durable / financial power of attorney.
Record an advance directive / living will.
Consider naming at least one younger trusted agent or alternate.
Check beneficiary designations on insurance and retirement accounts.
Tell your agents where the documents live.
Schedule a review every few years and after major life events.
Consult a qualified attorney or notary in your jurisdiction to make it all valid.

Chapter in brief

  • Without adult children, no one is automatically appointed to speak for you — which makes planning more important, not less.
  • Plan for two things: distribution after death (a will, an executor, beneficiaries — including causes and charities) and incapacity while living (healthcare proxy, financial power of attorney, advance directive).
  • Choose agents for trustworthiness and willingness; appointing someone younger helps ensure they are available when needed.
  • Keep documents findable and review them every few years and after major changes.
  • Rules vary widely by country and state — use this as a map, and consult a qualified attorney to make your plan valid.
