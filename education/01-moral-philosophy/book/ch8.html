Chapter Eight · Part Four · 3,874 words

Where Our Arguments Are Vulnerable

A foundation you have never stress-tested is not a foundation you can build nine books on. This chapter is the stress test, gathered into one place and set down without the comfort of an immediate rebuttal. Every chapter before this one ended by naming what it left open. This chapter is nothing but what is left open: the position's vulnerabilities assembled, named, and weighed, with no closing reassurance that they all come out fine. They do not all come out fine. That is the point. A reader who reaches the end of this chapter unsettled has been served correctly, because a position whose weaknesses leave its holder unsettled is a position its holder actually understands.

There is a discipline at work here that the rest of the series calls calibrated retreat: the willingness to say where an argument's force ends and to stop there, rather than defend every inch as though all ground were equally holdable. Calibrated retreat is not weakness. It is what credibility is made of, because only someone secure in a position's real strength can afford to be candid about its real limits. The natalist literature that pretends procreation faces no serious moral question is not strong. It is brittle, and it shatters on the first determined objector. This series aims at the opposite failure. Be so clear about the vulnerabilities that no critic can surprise the reader with one. So what follows is not an apology. It is an inventory.

The argument begins in intuitions that can be refused

The asymmetry rests on two foundational intuitions. The priority-of- suffering pillar rests on a third, the asymmetry of reasons. All three are widely shared, and this volume has argued they are well-supported by the further judgments they explain. None is a theorem. A philosopher who simply does not share them, who feels no pull toward the asymmetry of procreational duties, who would accept a duty to create happy people, who holds suffering and happiness in strict symmetric balance, has made no logical error and can be shown none. Arguments that begin in intuition can be resisted by anyone whose intuitions differ, and the resister is not thereby irrational. This is the most general vulnerability of the position, and it cannot be removed, because it is the vulnerability of substantive moral philosophy as such. The claim is not that the intuitions are inescapable. It is that their denial is costly, that the symmetric, no-asymmetry view has to swallow conclusions most people find harder to accept than the antinatalist's. That is a real argument. It is not a proof, and a reader who weighs the costs differently has not failed to follow it.

The symmetry objection may simply be right

The deepest internal challenge to the asymmetry, that Premises Three and Four are not held to one consistent standard, gets, in this volume's judgment, an answer that is good but not decisive. The answer is that the values are read off the four sub-asymmetries rather than assigned arbitrarily. It is met by a serious rival, the wide person-affecting principle, which explains the same four asymmetries without impersonal value, and the contest between them is unresolved in the literature and unresolved here. A reader who concludes that the symmetry objection prevails has backed a respectable side of a live dispute. The position's defense against this is not to insist the objection fails. It is to have built itself so the objection's success is survivable, which is the whole reason for the three-pillar structure. This vulnerability is real, it is central, and it is contained rather than cured.

The no-subject problem is a permanent tax

The claim that non-existence can be "better for" a person who never exists, or that absent pain is "good" with no one to benefit, faces the standing objection that value and betterness require a subject. The volume's response was to offer two readings of the key premise, impersonal and comparative, each of which answers one form of the objection and inherits another, so that no reading escapes it entirely. This is not a vulnerability that gets resolved. It is a tax paid every time the argument is run. What it comes to is that the antinatalist must accept some unintuitive commitment about subjectless value or non-existent-subject comparison, and defend it, with no option that is cost-free.

Non-identity has no agreed solution

The bedrock beneath all three pillars, how harm, benefit, good, and bad attach to people whose existence depends on the act being judged, is unsolved, in the exact sense that no response commands general assent four decades after Parfit posed the problem. The antinatalist has three coherent routes through it, which is more than a position in trouble usually has. Each route requires a contested metaphysical commitment, and on each the natalist can refuse to follow. The fair statement is the one Chapter Six reached. The non-identity problem does not refute antinatalism, but it denies antinatalism the clean person-affecting victory it would like, and it puts the position on contested ground, as it puts every alternative to it on contested ground, which is the only thing that keeps this vulnerability from being fatal.

Whole-life goodness pushes the strong claim off the field

The unconditional claim, that every coming-into-existence is, full stop, a harm, faces in the whole-life evaluation challenge an objection with real and sincere force, and the most honest of the available replies gives ground to it, conceding that a created life may be a good whole for the one who lives it and retreating to the claim that creating is an unjustified imposition. This is the clearest case in the volume of altitude traded for solidity. The reader should know exactly what was traded. The position that survives the whole-life challenge intact is not the grand one but the modest one, and anyone who wants to defend the grand one has to answer the challenge better than this volume has managed.

The testimony of the living

Nearly everyone who has lived and been asked has affirmed their own existence. The volume argued that this testimony answers a different question, the value of continuing a life rather than of starting it, and so does not refute the asymmetry, and that argument is, in this author's judgment, largely correct. But a position that has to explain why the considered self- assessment of almost all human beings does not bear on its central claim is carrying a real evidential weight, and the lighter and less condescending the explanation, the better. The danger here is not chiefly logical. It is moral. The explanations that reach for mass self-deception corrode the very compassion the position is founded on, and the discipline of declining those explanations guards the antinatalist's character as much as their logic.

The consent pillar's borrowed horizon

The consent argument was offered as independent of the asymmetry, and in its starting point it is. But its key reply to the "proves too much" objection rests on a generative-versus-modifying distinction that frays at the margins, and its deepest premise, that existence is not a benefit conferred on a prior person, leans back against the same non-identity ground the other pillars rest on. The pillars are independent in their premises and convergent in their horizon. This is not a flaw to be hidden. It is the structure of the field, in which a small number of contested metaphysical questions sit under every argument on every side. But a reader told the pillars were fully independent would have been misled, so the qualification is entered here.

What Survives

An inventory of vulnerabilities is only half an honest accounting. The other half is what stands after the inventory, and something substantial does stand. It is not the grandest version of the thesis. It is a version that has been, all through this volume, quietly separating itself from its own overreach.

What survives is roughly this. Procreation is a morally serious act, not a morally neutral default, because it imposes on a being who cannot consent, and who had no prior need of the imposition, a condition of guaranteed suffering and uncertain overall value, in circumstances that fit none of our ordinary templates for permissible unconsented harm, for the sake of goods that generate no duty to provide them and answer no deprivation that preceded the act. Every clause in that sentence has survived a chapter of attack. None of it requires the unconditional claim that every life is bad, or that no one should be glad to exist, or that the living would be better off dead. The claims that draw the most fire draw it because they are not the claims the position needs. What the position needs is the seriousness of the act and the absence of a justification of the right kind for it, and that core has held.

Whether the core, having held, delivers the full conclusion, that procreation is not merely grave but impermissible, is the question on which honest people who accept everything above still divide, and this volume will not pretend the gap closes itself. The step from "imposes serious unconsented harm answering no prior need, unjustified by the templates that justify other impositions" to "therefore impermissible" requires the judgment that this particular imposition is one that may not be made. A reader who has followed every argument and still judges that the goods of a life can license its creation has not failed to understand the position. They have weighed the same considerations and come down elsewhere, which substantive moral disagreement permits. What such a reader can no longer do, having read this volume, is treat procreation as the obvious, weightless, unexamined default it is nearly universally assumed to be. That assumption does not survive. Its destruction is the firmest result the foundation delivers, and it is enough to require everything the next nine volumes examine: the apparatus that enforces the default, the drives that make it felt, the institutions that profit from it, and the disciplines of carrying a difficult truth without being broken or hardened by it.

On Holding This Foundation

A foundation is meant to be stood on, not stared at, and a reader who has worked through eight chapters of argument and counter-argument may wonder what it is, in the end, to hold a position this hedged. The answer is the spirit the whole series is built to teach, and it is worth stating before the volume closes.

To hold this foundation rightly is to hold a strong argument while knowing exactly where it might give way. To be neither the zealot who mistakes a contested position for a proof, nor the cynic who mistakes the existence of objections for refutation. It is to be able to state the case against your own view at full strength, as this chapter has, and to find that your conviction survives the statement, chastened and clarified rather than destroyed. The natalist default rests on no comparable self-examination. It has never been asked to defend itself, because it has never been seen as a choice that requires defense. This volume's deepest claim is not any single premise. It is the reframing the eight chapters together accomplish: that procreation is a choice, that choices answer to reasons, and that the reasons here run harder against the act than almost anyone supposes, even if they do not, in the end, run all the way to the certainty the boldest formulations claim.

That is the foundation. It is strong where it is strong and candid where it is weak, and it has been built so that the discovery of a weakness moves the reader's weight onto a pillar that holds rather than collapsing the structure. The nine volumes that follow will lean on it constantly. Political theory will borrow the consent argument. Trauma psychology will borrow the priority of suffering. Rhetoric will borrow the discipline of calibrated retreat this chapter has just modeled. Emotional regulation will borrow the spirit of holding gravity without bitterness. They can lean on it because it was tested here, in the open, against its own best critics. A foundation stress-tested in full view is the only kind worth the weight of nine books, and this one has now borne the test.

Every serious position eventually faces a choice: remain an intuition, or become an education. The foundation is laid. The education continues.

Glossary of Key Terms

A reader's reference to the terms the argument relies on, defined as the book uses them. Where a term belongs to a particular philosopher, the relevant chapter develops it more fully.

Antinatalism. The view that coming into existence is a serious harm and that procreation is therefore morally wrong, or at the least gravely in need of a justification it usually does not receive. This volume defends it as a strong but contested position, not a proof.

Asymmetry, the. The claim, set out in Part One, that the absence of pain and the absence of pleasure are not morally on a par: absent pain is good even when no one enjoys it, while absent pleasure is merely not bad. Stated formally as the four premises. Also called the axiological or basic asymmetry, to distinguish it from the asymmetry of reasons.

Asymmetry of reasons. The separate claim, central to Chapter Five, that we have a strong and binding reason to prevent suffering but no comparable reason to create happiness. Held far beyond antinatalism and independent of the axiological asymmetry.

Calibrated retreat. The book's practice of defending an argument exactly as far as its force reaches, and openly marking where it gives way. Treated throughout as a source of credibility rather than a weakness.

Consent argument. The second pillar, developed in Chapter Four: existence is a serious, life-shaping condition imposed on a being who cannot consent, in circumstances that fit none of our templates for permissible unconsented harm. Descended from Shiffrin's work.

Consequentialism. The family of moral theories that judge an act by its outcomes. The axiological asymmetry reasons in broadly consequentialist terms.

Counterfactual comparative account of harm. The view, given canonical form by Feinberg, that to be harmed is to be made worse off than one would otherwise have been. It makes Premise Three hard, since the never-created person has no counterfactual welfare level to fall below.

Deontology. The family of moral theories that judge an act by whether it respects the right constraints, regardless of outcome. The consent argument is deontological.

Good-for versus good simpliciter. The distinction between something being good for a particular subject (personal value) and good full stop (impersonal value). A great deal of the asymmetry turns on which of the two an argument is using.

Hedonism. The theory of welfare on which well-being consists in pleasure and the absence of pain. One of three theories the book deliberately leaves open.

Hypothetical consent. Presumed agreement, used to license unconsented impositions such as emergency surgery on the unconscious. The book argues it cannot license procreation, which has no prior person whose will could be reconstructed.

Impersonal value. Value that is not value for any particular subject: goodness or badness "full stop." Premise Three, on one reading, requires it; the person-affecting tradition denies any such value exists.

Metaethics. The branch of moral philosophy that asks not which acts are right but what we are doing when we call an act right at all: whether moral claims can be true, and what would make them so. The book's argument is conditional in form and holds on any metaethics except the global error theory.

Moral realism and its rivals. Realism holds there are mind-independent moral facts; constructivism, that moral truths depend on our idealized or considered commitments; non-cognitivism, that moral claims express attitudes rather than state facts; error theory, that moral claims aim at facts but none exist, so all are false. The volume takes no side, save for setting the global error theory aside.

Narrative value. Velleman's term for the irreducible good that belongs to a life as a whole and is not reducible to the sum of its moments. The basis of the whole-life evaluation challenge.

Negative utilitarianism. The view that morality's aim is to minimize suffering, with happiness carrying little or no independent weight. The book refuses it, because it implies the world-destruction conclusion; the priority of suffering is the weaker, safer claim it keeps instead.

Non-comparative account of harm. The view, associated with Shiffrin, that certain states are harms in themselves, with no baseline required. It eases the harm side of the argument but makes the asymmetry between Premises Three and Four harder to sustain.

Non-identity problem. Parfit's problem: because a person's existence and identity depend on the very act that creates them, they cannot straightforwardly be said to have been made worse off by it, which strains person-affecting morality. The bedrock beneath all three pillars.

No-subject objection. The objection, Epicurean in lineage, that non-existence cannot be better or worse for anyone, since there is no subject for whom it holds. A standing tax on the comparative reading of Premise Three.

Objective-list theory. The theory of welfare on which certain goods (knowledge, friendship, achievement) are good for a person whether or not they are wanted or enjoyed.

Person-affecting view. The view that nothing can be good or bad unless it is good or bad for someone. It denies impersonal value and is the asymmetry's standing opponent.

Premises, the four. The asymmetry's canonical statement, in Benatar's numbering. One: the presence of pain is bad. Two: the presence of pleasure is good. Three: the absence of pain is good, even with no one to enjoy it. Four: the absence of pleasure is not bad, unless someone is deprived by it.

Priority of preventing suffering. The third pillar, developed in Chapter Five: relieving suffering takes moral priority over creating happiness. Defended in non- utilitarian form by Mayerfeld, among others.

Pro-mortalism. The view that it would be better for existing people to die. The book walls antinatalism off from it: antinatalism concerns the starting of lives, not their ending.

Pure benefit. Shiffrin's term for a good whose absence would not be a harm or a deprivation. The goods of a life are pure benefits with respect to the never-created person, which is why imposing them without consent falls in the disfavored category.

Repugnant Conclusion, the. Parfit's result that, on certain impersonal principles, a vast population of barely-worth-living lives can come out better than a small flourishing one. The standing trouble for impersonal escapes from the non- identity problem.

Symmetry objection, the. The deepest objection to the asymmetry: that Premises Three and Four are not held to one consistent standard, so an even- handed accounting collapses the asymmetry into symmetry. The book's clearest marked vulnerability.

Theory X. Parfit's name for the undiscovered principle that would handle the ethics of creating people without paradox. He never found it, and neither has anyone since.

Validity and soundness. An argument is valid if its conclusion follows from its premises, and sound if it is valid and its premises are true. With the aggregation across the table's rows, these mark the places any argument can be resisted.

Weak and strong asymmetry. McMahan's distinction. The strong asymmetry says the world is better for the happy life's absence and that not creating is positively preferred; the weak says only that there is no reason to create. The weak version survives Broome's formal attack; the strong does not.

Whole-life evaluation challenge. The objection, developed in Chapter Seven, that a life is a meaningful whole rather than a sum of moments, so the goods of a good whole can redeem the suffering within it. The most resilient objection to the conclusion.

Worth starting versus worth continuing. Benatar's distinction between whether a life should be begun and whether an existing life should go on. The standards differ, so a life can be well worth continuing yet not have been worth starting. It grounds the pro-mortalism firewall.

Wrongful life. A legal claim brought on behalf of a child that it was wronged by being brought into existence. Mostly refused by courts, for reasons that track the difficulty of Premise Three.
