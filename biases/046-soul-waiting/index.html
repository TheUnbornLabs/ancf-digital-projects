The Soul Waiting to Be Born

Part IV · Religious, Spiritual & Moral Framings

The bias: The intuition that unconceived souls are owed a chance at existence.

We are the arrows, and the bow is the past.

① In brief

100 Biases of Natalism · Book 046

This volume examines the waiting-soul intuition: the tender and widespread sense that there exist particular unconceived persons, souls, spirits, or determinate possible children, who are waiting to be born and are, in some morally weighty way, owed existence. On this intuition, to remain childless, or to have fewer children than one might, is to deny a specific waiting someone its chance at life, and thereby to deprive, disappoint, or wrong it. The book's thesis is that this someone does not exist: not yet, and, for any particular non-conceived person, not ever. You cannot deprive, disappoint, or wrong a person who does not and may never exist. The waiting soul is a vivid image without a subject.

The stance is critical of the bias and respectful of the faith and love in which it is often housed. The book does not adjudicate whether souls exist or whether any tradition's doctrine of pre-existence is true; it repeatedly and explicitly exempts believers who affirm genuine pre-existent souls on independent theological grounds, since for them there is an actual subject and the critique does not apply. Its target is the far more common move in which the feeling of a waiting someone is allowed to do moral work, generating guilt, pressing a decision, without any account of who, exactly, is waiting.

The central conceptual tool is the distinction between a type (a general kind, "a child we might have") and a token (a particular individual with a specific identity). The intuition trades on an unnoticed slide from the true, unremarkable claim that a couple could have a child (an open type) to the false, consequential claim that a particular someone already occupies that category, waiting to be admitted into existence (a determinate token). The general possibility is real; the particular waiting person is not. The vividness of an actual, loved child is projected backward, "she was the one waiting all along," but before conception there was no she, only an open type fillable by any of astronomically many non-identical possible people, no one of which had any prior claim to being the one.

Two philosophical results carry the argument. Derek Parfit's non-identity problem (Reasons and Persons, 1984) shows that which particular person is conceived depends on the exact circumstances, so there is no determinate individual who "would have been" conceived and was not, hence no particular him or her whom non-conception denied. The person-affecting principle shows that an act can be bad only if it is bad for someone, and a merely possible person who never exists has no interests to set back, no welfare to diminish, no standpoint from which anything can go worse. "It would have been good for her to exist" has no truth-value where there is no her.

The book traces the intuition's roots (religious doctrines of pre-existence, the mind's agency-detecting machinery, and a grammar that forces us to name possible children with the definite reference reserved for existing persons), and its costs: a manufactured guilt with no floor and no possible resolution, the distortion of reproductive decisions by a phantom obligee, and the hijacking of the childless person's own legitimate grief, relocated from their unlived life onto a "waiting child" who never existed. It shows the pressure falling hardest on women and on those experiencing infertility, for whom the intuition converts a medical fact into a moral drama with a victim.

The book carefully distinguishes its lane from neighbors: the divine-gift bias (042, gratitude toward a Giver), the consent-impossibility argument (080, its mirror image), and the legacy biases (049, which concern the parent's own continuation and do have a real subject). Its liberating conclusion is not cold but kind: no one is waiting; you have failed no one. Setting the phantom aside returns grief to its rightful owner and lets the real decision, into what conditions one might bring an actual person, come clearly into view.

② The book in twelve chapters

Each chapter opens with its own summary. Note chapter 2: every volume states the strongest case for the thing it criticises before laying a finger on it — and chapter 8 turns the critique back on itself.

CH 1Naming the bias

The bias named in this book is the waiting-soul intuition: the tendency to feel or believe that there exist particular unconceived persons, commonly imagined as souls or spirits, but sometimes simply as determinate possible children, who are waiting to be born, such that the decision not to conceive denies a specific waiting someone its chance at existence and thereby deprives, disappoints, or wrongs it.

CH 2The strongest case for it (steelman)The strongest case against this book

Before I lay a finger on the intuition, it deserves the fullest and most sympathetic statement I can give it, not a straw effigy but the real thing, argued as its most thoughtful holders would argue it. I will grant a great deal here, because a great deal is grantable, and because an intuition this widely and sincerely held across so many cultures is unlikely to be simply confused. If I am going to argue that it rests on a metaphysical mistake, I owe it first the dignity of its full force.

CH 3Where it comes from

An intuition this deep and this widespread does not come from nowhere, and understanding its sources helps us see why it grips even people who, on reflection, do not believe in waiting souls at all. The waiting-soul intuition has at least three tributaries, religious, evolutionary-psychological, and linguistic, and they reinforce one another until the image feels less like a belief one holds than like a fact one perceives. It is worth stressing at the outset that tracing an intuition's causal origins is not, by itself, a refutation of it; to think otherwise would be to commit the genetic fallacy, dismissing a belief because of where it came from rather than whether it is true. The point of this chapter is more modest and more useful. Understanding the sources of the intuition explains its grip, why it feels compulsory even to those whose considered views reject it, and that explanation is exactly what a person needs in order to loosen the grip enough to examine the belief on its merits.

CH 4How it operates today

Stripped of doctrine, the waiting-soul intuition operates today largely below the level of articulate belief, in the textures of ordinary speech and private feeling. Most people who are moved by it would not, if asked directly, affirm that a particular unconceived person is literally waiting somewhere. But they speak and feel as though one were, and the speaking and feeling do the work. This chapter traces the concrete mechanisms, the scripts, phrases, and emotional patterns, through which the intuition presses on real decisions. The important thing to notice, running through all of them, is that the intuition rarely operates as an explicit proposition one affirms and could therefore examine; it operates as a mood, a framing, a default shape that experience takes on before reflection has a chance to intervene. That is what makes it both powerful and hard to catch.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

The word "evidence" sits oddly over a chapter concerned largely with metaphysics, and I want to be honest about that. There is no experiment that detects or fails to detect waiting souls; the question of whether pre-existent persons exist is not one that data can settle, and I do not pretend otherwise. What philosophy can do is examine the structure of the intuition's central claim, that a particular someone is deprived, disappointed, or wronged by not being conceived, and ask whether that claim is even coherent on any account of persons that does not simply presuppose the disputed doctrine. Here the relevant body of thought is the philosophy of possible people, and its findings, while not "evidence" in the empirical sense, are as close to decisive as reasoning gets. The move I am making is one of conceptual clarification rather than discovery: I am not reporting a fact about the world that further investigation might overturn, but exhibiting a confusion in the intuition's own terms, showing that its central claim, taken literally, has no subject to be about.

CH 6The hidden costs

If the waiting-soul intuition were merely a harmless poetry of longing, there would be little reason to write against it. But it carries costs, and the costs fall precisely because the intuition is felt as truth. This chapter names what the intuition obscures and what it charges. I want to be careful not to overstate: for some people, in some moods, the image is a source of comfort and nothing worse, and I will grant that fully in Chapter 8. But comfort in one place is bought, often, at the price of a burden in another, and the burdens are distributed unequally and land hardest on those least able to bear them. It is those hidden charges, easy to miss precisely because the intuition presents itself as tender, that this chapter is concerned to expose.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

Like every natalist bias, the waiting-soul intuition does not press equally on everyone. Its weight is distributed along familiar lines, and naming that distribution matters, because a pressure felt as universal and metaphysical is in fact channeled by very particular social facts. To say that a metaphysical error has a demography is not to trivialize it; it is to observe that abstractions do their damage through concrete lives, and that the same abstract confusion, the personification of the possible, is routed by culture toward some shoulders far more than others. Seeing whom it lands on is part of seeing what it is.

CH 8The counter-arguments to the critiqueObjections to the critique

A book that steelmanned its opponent in Chapter 2 owes an equally honest hearing to the objections against its own thesis. Several are serious, and I will not pretend they are easily dispatched. I take them in what I judge to be descending order of force, beginning with the one I cannot answer and ending with the ones I think I can.

CH 9Case studies / illustrations

Three illustrations, drawn from recognizable patterns rather than from any named individual, show the intuition at work and show what changes when it is named. I offer them not as proofs, a story proves nothing, but as mirrors, in which a reader who has felt the intuition's pull may recognize its shape and, in the recognition, gain a little distance from it. In each case the aim is the same: to locate precisely where the personification enters, and to show what the situation looks like once it is gently withdrawn.

CH 10Adjacent biases

The waiting-soul intuition sits in a dense neighborhood of related natalist biases, and part of seeing it clearly is seeing where it ends and its neighbors begin. I mark the borders here without duplicating those volumes. The value of the exercise is not taxonomic tidiness for its own sake; it is that biases which are felt together can be argued against separately, and a person who has untangled the waiting-soul feeling from its neighbors is far better placed to examine each on its own terms rather than defending or surrendering the whole bundle at once.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

What does clear-eyed, non-coerced reasoning about reproduction look like once the waiting-soul intuition has been named and set down? Not colder, and not emptier, but clearer, and, for many, lighter. This chapter sketches what remains when the phantom is gone, for individuals first and then for the culture around them. I want to insist at the outset that "seeing past" the intuition is not a single act of insight after which one is permanently free; it is more like a practice, since the grammar and the imagery that generate the phantom are woven into the culture and reassert themselves, and the work is less a demolition than an ongoing, gentle correction of a habit of mind.

CH 12Conclusion

This book has argued a single, narrow claim, and tried to argue it with the respect its subject deserves. The claim is that the waiting-soul intuition, the sense that a particular unconceived person is waiting to be born, owed existence, and wronged by its denial, supposes a subject where there is none. The general possibility of a child is real; the particular waiting child is not. And because the moral weight of the intuition attaches entirely to the particular waiting child, that weight rests on nothing. You cannot deprive, disappoint, or wrong someone who does not and may never exist. The waiting soul is a vivid image without a subject, and the guilt it generates is a debt to no one.

③ Key terms

Show all 15 terms as a list
Waiting-soul intuition
The sense or belief that a particular unconceived person is waiting to be born and is owed, deprived, disappointed, or wronged by not being conceived. The bias this book names.
Token
A particular individual: this person, with a specific identity, genome, and history, distinguishable from every other.
Type
A general kind or category — "a child we might have," "a future son" — which any of many possible tokens could fill.
Type/token slide
The unnoticed move from the real general possibility of a child (type) to a supposed particular someone occupying that possibility in advance (token). The core error of the intuition.
Possible person / merely possible person
An individual who could exist under some circumstances but does not actually exist. For those never conceived, no determinate such individual is picked out in advance.
Non-identity problem
Derek Parfit's observation (Reasons and Persons, 1984) that which particular person is conceived depends on the exact circumstances of conception, so there is no determinate individual who "would have been" conceived and was not.
Person-affecting principle
The view, roughly, that an act can be bad only if it is bad for someone — that harms and wrongs require an actual subject whose interests are set back.
Impersonal value
Value that is good simpliciter rather than good for any particular person. Invoked by "total" views in population ethics; distinct from, and in fact abandons, the waiting-soul claim.
Pre-existence (of souls)
The theological doctrine that persons or souls exist prior to their embodiment (e.g., strands influenced by Platonism; Latter-day Saint pre-mortal spirits). A serious conviction this book does not adjudicate and explicitly exempts from its critique.
Retrospective sealing
The backward projection by which the vivid reality of an actual, loved child is felt to have "been waiting" — populating the empty pre-conception space with a subject that was never there.
Personification of the possible
Treating an open possibility as an already-existing individual, typically through naming, imagining, and the grammar of definite reference.
Phantom obligee
An imagined party to whom something is owed but who does not exist; here, the "waiting child" whose supposed claim distorts a reproductive decision.
Manufactured guilt
Guilt with no possible resolution, arising from a felt debt to a creditor (the waiting soul) who cannot exist to be paid or to grant forgiveness.
Agency-detection
The cognitive disposition to over-attribute agency, perceiving a someone where there is at most a something; a psychological source of the waiting-soul image.
Backward projection
The illusion of hindsight by which the particular child one actually has is felt to have been the one waiting to arrive, when before conception there was no particular someone at all.

⑥ Sources

What this volume draws on. Where the evidence is thin or contested, the book says so rather than reaching for a number.

  • Derek Parfit, Reasons and Persons (Oxford: Oxford University Press, 1984). The canonical treatment of the non-identity problem, the metaphysics of possible people, and the difficulties facing person-affecting claims about future individuals. Central to Chapters 5, 8, and 12.
  • David Benatar, Better Never to Have Been: The Harm of Coming into Existence (Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2006). Cited as adjacent context for the mirror-image asymmetry and consent debates referenced in Chapters 8 and 10; not central to this book's positive argument.
  • The repugnant conclusion and impersonal ("total") views of value, referenced in Chapter 8, are treated at length in Parfit (1984), which remains the place to meet them.
Directions the volume points to (not pinned citations)
  • Jonathan Glover, Causing Death and Saving Lives (Penguin, 1977) and his related work on the ethics of creating people — for the broader population-ethics context.
  • Standard reference-work entries and survey articles on the person-affecting principle and its puzzles within population ethics.
  • Primary Latter-day Saint doctrinal sources on pre-mortal existence and pre-mortal spirits. The belief should be met as its adherents state it, in the tradition's own authoritative texts, and not in any critic's paraphrase — this one included.
  • Sources on Platonic and Neoplatonic doctrines of the pre-existence of the soul and their reception, referenced in Chapters 2–3.
  • Scholarship on agency-detection and the cognitive science of religion, including work on the "hyperactive agency-detection device," referenced in Chapters 3–4.
  • Anthropological and ethnographic literature on folk beliefs about unborn children, ancestor return, and "spirit-children" across cultures, referenced in Chapters 2–3.
  • Literature on the experience of longing for a child and on the language people use for imagined future children, referenced in Chapters 2 and 4.
  • Literature on the grief associated with infertility and involuntary childlessness, referenced in Chapters 6–7. This book treats that grief qualitatively and cautiously, and readers should be suspicious of anyone — on any side of this argument — who quantifies it briskly.
