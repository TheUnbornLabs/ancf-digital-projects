Part III · The ThinkersCHAPTER 11

Arthur Schopenhauer

The will, its endless wanting, and the nineteenth-century root of modern pessimism.

"Human life must be some kind of mistake."

— Arthur Schopenhauer, "On the Suffering of the World"

No thinker did more to give philosophical pessimism a serious, systematic shape than Arthur Schopenhauer. He was not himself an antinatalist in the modern sense — the word did not exist in his lifetime, and he never built an argument that procreation is wrong. Yet almost every later figure in this book reaches back to him. He is the great precursor: the philosopher who argued, in careful detail, that existence is shot through with suffering, and that this is not a flaw to be patched but a feature of what it means to be a living, wanting thing.

Schopenhauer was born in Danzig in 1788 into a wealthy merchant family. His early life was comfortable and well-traveled, and a family inheritance later freed him from the need to earn a living — a fact his critics would not forget. He studied across several German universities, absorbed the work of Kant, and grew fascinated by Indian thought, particularly the Upanishads and early Buddhist ideas, at a time when such texts were only beginning to reach Europe. In 1818 he published the work that would contain his entire system, The World as Will and Representation, expanded in 1844. For decades it was almost ignored. Only late in his life, through shorter essay collections, did he find the readers and fame he had always believed he deserved. He died in Frankfurt in 1860.

The world as Will

Schopenhauer's starting point is a bold reading of Kant. Behind the world we perceive — the world of objects laid out in space and time, which he calls representation — lies a deeper reality. Kant had said we cannot know that underlying reality. Schopenhauer claimed we can, because we are it: when we act, want, hunger, or fear, we feel from the inside the same force that drives everything else. He named this force the Will — a blind, ceaseless, purposeless striving that underlies all of nature, from gravity to growing plants to human ambition.

The Will — in Schopenhauer, the single, blind, restless striving that is the inner nature of reality. It has no goal it is trying to reach and no satisfaction it can finally arrive at; it simply wills, endlessly. Individual living things are its temporary expressions, each driven by wanting it did not choose.

The crucial point is that the Will has no destination. It does not strive toward anything; it just strives. And because we are expressions of it, our lives inherit its restlessness. We are, in Schopenhauer's picture, wanting machines that can never be switched off.

Suffering, boredom, and the pendulum

From this follows his famous account of why life is hard. To desire something is, by definition, to lack it — and lack is a kind of pain. So as long as we are wanting, we are suffering, at least mildly. Satisfaction, when it comes, is only the brief absence of one particular want; it does not last, because new desires rush in to fill the space. And on those rare occasions when desire is quieted and nothing presses on us, we do not find peace. We find boredom — the dull ache of a wanting creature with nothing to want.

Life, then, swings like a pendulum between two poles: pain when our desires go unmet, and boredom when they are met. Schopenhauer added a deeper claim that many later thinkers borrowed: suffering is positive and pleasure merely negative. By this he meant that pain is the thing we directly feel, while pleasure is only the temporary removal of a pain. We notice the toothache, not the years of painless teeth; we feel the hunger, not the quiet of being fed. On this accounting, the books of a life can never quite balance, because the suffering is real and felt while the satisfactions are mostly the brief silencing of other sufferings.

Life swings between pain and boredom — and pleasure is only a pain briefly stilled.

Compassion and the denial of the Will

It would be a mistake to read Schopenhauer as merely gloomy. His pessimism led him to an ethics of compassion. If every living thing is an expression of one and the same Will, then the boundary between "me" and "you" is, at the deepest level, an illusion. Your suffering and mine are the suffering of the same underlying reality. To grasp this is to feel for others not as a duty imposed from outside but as a recognition of shared condition. Cruelty rests on the illusion of separateness; kindness sees through it.

What, then, can be done? Schopenhauer did not preach despair. He pointed to ways of loosening the Will's grip. Aesthetic contemplation — losing oneself in music or art — lifts us briefly out of wanting, letting us perceive without desiring. More radically, he admired the path of asceticism and the denial of the Will: the saint or renunciate who, recognizing the futility of endless striving, gradually quiets desire itself. Here his debt to Indian and Buddhist thought is plain. Salvation, for Schopenhauer, is not getting what you want; it is being released from the wanting.

His connection to antinatalism lies in this whole architecture. If existence means being a wanting creature, and being a wanting creature means a life of striving and suffering only briefly relieved, then in his bleaker passages he could praise non-existence as preferable, and treat the will to reproduce as the Will's deepest trick for perpetuating itself. He stopped short of the modern argument. But the materials were all there, and Nietzsche, Wagner, Mainländer, Cioran, and Zapffe all built on what he left.

Objection

The whole edifice rests on a contestable metaphysics. Why accept that a single blind "Will" underlies reality, or that pleasure is "merely negative" while pain is "positive"? These are evocative claims, not demonstrated ones — most people directly experience joys, loves, and satisfactions as real positives, not as the mere absence of pain. And critics from his own day onward have noted an awkward gap between the gloom of his philosophy and the comfort of his life: a man who dined well, enjoyed music and his poodle, and guarded his inheritance carefully seems to have found existence rather more livable than his books admit.

Reply

Both points land, but neither sinks the contribution. The metaphysics of the Will can be set aside while keeping the psychological observation underneath it: that desire renews itself faster than it is satisfied, and that we adapt to our pleasures while our pains keep stinging, is something many people recognize whether or not they accept a cosmic Will. As for the gap between the man and the message, a philosopher's habits do not refute his arguments — an unhappy doctor can still diagnose correctly, and a comfortable thinker can still describe suffering accurately. Schopenhauer never claimed his own life was unbearable; he claimed that existence as such carries more pain than is usually admitted, and that one can notice this and still pet one's dog. The fair verdict is to treat him as he is best treated: not as a prophet to be obeyed but as a precursor who put pessimism into rigorous form and handed later thinkers the tools to argue more carefully than he did.

In plain words: Schopenhauer said reality is restless wanting, that wanting means suffering only briefly relieved, and that release comes from quieting desire — not from getting what we want. He didn't argue against having children, but he gave later antinatalists their starting vocabulary.

Chapter in brief

  • Arthur Schopenhauer (1788–1860) gave philosophical pessimism its first complete system in The World as Will and Representation.
  • Reality, for him, is blind Will — endless, goalless striving — and we are its expressions, so our lives inherit its restlessness.
  • Life oscillates between pain (unmet desire) and boredom (met desire); he held suffering to be "positive" and pleasure merely the absence of pain.
  • His response was an ethics of compassion and a path of release through aesthetic contemplation, asceticism, and denial of the Will, drawing on Indian thought.
  • He was a precursor to antinatalism, not a self-described antinatalist, and deeply influenced Nietzsche, Mainländer, Cioran, and Zapffe.
