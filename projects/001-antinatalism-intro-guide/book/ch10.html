Purpose, Duty, and Meaning

Does life's meaning answer the antinatalist, or sit beside the question untouched?

"Even if our lives can have terrestrial meaning, they lack the cosmic meaning we might crave."

— David Benatar

Of all the replies to antinatalism, the most heartfelt is also the simplest. Whatever the arithmetic of suffering, people say, a human life is not a balance sheet. It is a story, a calling, a place in a family and a people and perhaps a faith. Lives have meaning — and meaning is the kind of thing that makes hardship not merely bearable but worthwhile. We do not weigh a child against a column of pains; we offer her a chance at a life that matters. This chapter takes that response seriously, distinguishes the senses of "meaning" at work, and asks whether meaning answers the antinatalist or simply changes the subject.

Two kinds of meaning

In The Human Predicament, Benatar draws a distinction that the conversation badly needs. There is cosmic meaning — the question of whether a life matters from the widest possible standpoint, in the grand scheme of the universe, sub specie aeternitatis. And there is terrestrial meaning — whether a life matters within human horizons: to the person living it, to those who love them, to a community or a cause.

Cosmic vs. terrestrial meaning — Cosmic meaning concerns a life's significance from the standpoint of the universe at large. Terrestrial meaning concerns its significance within human contexts — to oneself, to others, to a community. Benatar argues we plausibly have the second but almost certainly lack the first.

Benatar's view is that terrestrial meaning is real and worth having. A scientist who advances knowledge, a parent who raises a kind child, a friend who shows up — these lives are genuinely meaningful in the only register that is actually available to us. What we lack, he argues, is cosmic meaning. From the standpoint of a vast and indifferent universe, the whole human project leaves no lasting mark; given enough time, every trace of us is erased. Much of our hunger for meaning is really a hunger for the cosmic kind, and that hunger, he thinks, goes permanently unfed. The honest position is to enjoy the terrestrial meaning we can have while not pretending it reaches as far as we wish.

Does meaning offset suffering — or run alongside it?

Here is the pivotal question, and it is easy to slide past. When someone says "a meaningful life is worth its suffering," they may be making either of two very different claims.

The first treats meaning as offsetting — as a good that goes into the same ledger as pain and outweighs it, the way income offsets expense. The second treats meaning as a separate axis — a different dimension of a life's value that does not convert into the currency of pleasure and pain at all. On this second view, a life can be deeply meaningful and contain more suffering than well-being; the meaning does not cancel the suffering, it simply coexists with it.

Meaning may not be the thing that balances suffering, but a different thing that lives beside it.

The distinction matters because the antinatalist's worry survives the second reading. If meaning is a separate axis, then granting that a life will be meaningful does not yet show that it will be good on balance for the one who lives it, or that its harms to others are repaid. A meaningful life can still be a life of net hardship. So "but life has meaning" rebuts the antinatalist only if meaning is the offsetting kind — and that is precisely what has not been shown.

The duty to continue

A stronger version of the reply does not merely permit procreation but commands it. We are told there is a duty to procreate: religious traditions that read fruitfulness as a divine instruction; nationalist or communal arguments that a people must not vanish; and the broad intuition that humanity itself must go on, that to choose extinction would be a kind of betrayal of all who came before.

These claims deserve respect, and the antinatalist response is not mockery but a question about direction. A duty is owed to someone. To existing communities and traditions, the antinatalist can grant real weight — people have genuine stakes in continuity. But the future child is not yet anyone, and so cannot be owed a duty to be created, nor be wronged by remaining unborn. A duty to continue, then, looks like a duty among the living, about the shape of their shared life — not a duty toward a person who would only come to exist if the duty were discharged. That reframing does not refute the religious or communal claim, but it does locate it: it is about us, not about an obligation we owe to the not-yet-born.

Creating a person so that they may find meaning

There is a quieter version still: we should have children so that they can experience the goods of a meaningful life. But this reasoning has an odd structure when examined. Before a person exists, there is no one who is missing out on meaning, no one deprived, no one waiting in the wings to be rescued from meaninglessness by being born. We cannot benefit a person by creating them, because there is no prior person there to benefit. To say "I will make a child so that she can enjoy meaning" treats the future child as someone whose interests already make a claim on us — but those interests come into being only as a result of the act, and so cannot justify it without circularity.

In plain words: Meaning is real and worth having — but you cannot give it to someone who does not yet exist, because there is no one there to receive the gift. Meaning is a reason to live well, not by itself a reason to create a new liver of life.
Objection

This is too clever by half. In every other corner of life we accept — even seek out — suffering for the sake of meaning, and we are right to. People train for years in pain to master an art, endure the exhaustion and heartbreak of raising children, risk their lives for others, grieve deeply because they loved deeply. We do not regard these as bad bargains; we regard the willingness to suffer for what matters as among the noblest things about us. A meaningful human life is obviously worth its hardships — that is the testimony of nearly everyone who has lived one. To set meaning on a separate axis where it conveniently cannot count against suffering is a philosopher's trick that the lived verdict of humanity flatly contradicts.

Reply

The reply agrees entirely that meaning can be worth suffering — for someone who already exists. The musician who suffers for her art has a self, projects, and commitments that make the trade hers to make and her life better for it; the parent who endures hardship is enriched by a love that is already real. That is the strongest case for living well, and antinatalism does not dispute it. But all of it presupposes a person already on the scene, with a life to give meaning to. The question on the table is different: whether to summon a new person into the trade in the first place. Before she exists, there is no one for whom the bargain is worth taking, no self that would be impoverished by never striking it. So the testimony of those glad to have suffered for meaning is real and moving — and it answers a question, "is it worth it once you are here?", that is not the question of whether to bring someone here.

A note on a different debate

It is worth separating one nearby discussion to avoid confusion. The philosopher Julian Savulescu has defended procreative beneficence — the claim that prospective parents have reason to select, among possible children, the one expected to have the best life. Whatever one makes of it, that is a debate about which child to bring into being, taking for granted that some child will be. The antinatalist question is the prior one: whether to bring any child into being at all. The two are easily conflated and important to keep apart.

Chapter in brief

  • Benatar distinguishes cosmic meaning (significance from the universe's standpoint, which he thinks we lack) from terrestrial meaning (significance within human contexts, which is real but limited).
  • "Life has meaning" rebuts antinatalism only if meaning offsets suffering; if meaning is a separate axis, a life can be both meaningful and, on balance, hard.
  • Duty-to-procreate claims (religious, nationalist, "humanity must continue") are treated respectfully but reframed as duties among the living, since the not-yet-born can be owed nothing.
  • Creating a person so that they may find meaning is circular: there is no prior person to benefit by being created.
  • The objection — that we rightly suffer for meaning all the time — is granted for existing people, but that addresses "is it worth it once here?", not "should anyone be brought here?"
  • Savulescu's procreative beneficence is about which child to have, not whether — a separate debate.
