Because It's Here: The Existence Bias in Reproduction

Part I · Cognitive & Psychological Biases · Key terms

The bias: Treating what exists (families, births) as therefore good and necessary.

Whatever is, is right." — Alexander Pope, An Essay on Man (1733), a line the book takes as the very error it examines, not as a creed. --- ## Preface to this volume There is a quiet inference that runs underneath a great deal of reasoning about children, and it is so ordinary that we rarely notice we are making it. The inference goes: this is how it has always been done; families have always formed; people have always had children; therefore having children is good, and right, and more or less obligatory. The premise is a fact about what exists. The conclusion is a judgment about what ought to be. The move from one to the other feels seamless, almost automatic. It is not. This book is about that move. In psychology it has a name, the existence bias, the documented tendency to judge that whatever exists is, for that reason, good, and that whatever has existed longer is better still. The finding is associated most closely with a series of studies by Scott Eidelman, Christian Crandall, and their colleagues, who showed across many experiments that people rate the same policy, the same practice, even the same tree or the same acupuncture treatment more favorably when told it has been around a long time, and that the mere fact of existence confers a presumption of legitimacy and worth. Existence, it turns out, is treated as a weak but persistent argument for goodness. The longer something has existed, the stronger that silent argument becomes. Reproduction is an unusually powerful magnet for this bias, and the reasons are not hard to see. Nothing has existed longer than the practice of having children; it is, quite literally, the reason there is anyone here to have an opinion about it. Families surround us. Births are announced, celebrated, and folded into the deep grammar of ordinary life. The demographic order, the sheer ongoing fact that each generation replaces itself, more or less, has the feel of a natural constant, like weather or gravity, rather than the aggregate of hundreds of millions of individual decisions. When something is that pervasive and that old, existence bias does its work almost invisibly. The practice acquires an aura of goodness and inevitability, and that aura functions as a shield. It makes the practice feel like it does not need to be justified, and it makes the demand for justification feel faintly perverse, even hostile. The stance of this book is critical of that shield, but it is not critical of families, of parents, or of children, and it is emphatically not an argument that anyone should or should not reproduce. It is an argument about a specific error in reasoning. A practice can be good and be shielded from scrutiny by a bias; the two are entirely compatible. To notice that "it has always been done" is not actually a reason for doing it is not to conclude that it should stop being done. It is only to insist that the real reasons, whatever they are, be allowed to do the work, and that the fact of mere existence not be quietly smuggled in to do work it cannot honestly do. Hume's old point, that one cannot derive an ought from an is, is the philosophical spine of the argument. Existence bias is, in effect, the psychological mechanism by which we commit the is–ought error without noticing. The book proceeds in twelve chapters. It begins by naming the bias precisely and distinguishing it from its neighbors in this series, especially default bias (Book 001), which concerns the pre-selected option, and the naturalistic fallacy (Book 015), which concerns the slide from "natural" to "good." Existence bias is narrower and stranger than either: it is about mere being, and about duration, as sources of felt legitimacy. Chapter 2 gives the strongest version of the natalist case, and it is stronger than critics often allow, before any critique begins. Later chapters trace the bias's origins, its everyday mechanisms, the actual evidence for it, the costs it hides, who is most subject to it, and the best objections to this book's own thesis. Two ideas from social psychology recur throughout: Robert Zajonc's mere-exposure effect, the finding that familiarity alone breeds liking, and the system justification theory of John Jost and Mahzarin Banaji, which describes our motivated tendency to defend and rationalize the existing social order, even at our own expense. The aim is not to leave the reader with a conclusion about whether to have children. It is to leave the reader with a cleaner instrument for thinking about it, one in which the antiquity and ubiquity of childbearing are seen for what they are: real facts about the world, and no argument at all. --- ## 1. Naming the bias Begin with a simple test. Suppose you are told that a particular practice, it does not matter which, has been performed by human beings for as long as there have been human beings, that nearly everyone you know participates in it, and that it is woven into the law, the calendar, the language, and the architecture of your society. Before you know anything else about it, how good does it seem? For most people, the honest answer is: it seems good. Not proven good, not examined and found good, but presumptively good, in the way a well-worn path through a field seems trustworthy because so many feet have worn it. The presumption arrives before the evidence. That presumption is the existence bias. Stated formally, the existence bias is the tendency to treat the mere existence of a state of affairs as evidence of its goodness, its rightness, and its legitimacy. In its stronger form, the longevity version of the bias, it treats the duration of a state of affairs as further evidence still: the longer something has existed, the better it is assumed to be. The bias was given this name and much of its experimental grounding by Scott Eidelman, Christian Crandall, and collaborators in the late 2000s. In a representative demonstration, participants were asked to evaluate things, a form of medical treatment, a chocolate, a college requirement, even an unfamiliar tree, under conditions that varied only one detail: how long the thing had supposedly existed. Things described as older were rated as better, more right, and more as they ought to be. The manipulation changed nothing real about the object. It changed only the reported duration of its existence, and that alone moved the evaluation. The one-sentence thesis of this book is this: because childbearing and the family are among the oldest and most pervasive facts of human life, they attract an unusually strong existence bias, which lends them an aura of goodness and necessity that operates independently of, and often in place of, any actual argument for them. The aura is not the argument. The point of the book is to pull them apart. It helps to see immediately what the existence bias is not, because it lives in a crowded neighborhood of related tendencies, several of which have their own books in this series. It is not, first of all, default bias (Book 001). Default bias concerns the pre-selected option, the choice that will occur unless one actively intervenes, and the inertia that keeps us on it. A default is about the path of least resistance. Existence bias is about something subtler: not the ease of an option, but the felt legitimacy conferred by its mere being and its age. The two often travel together, since reproduction is both the default script and an ancient existing practice, but they are distinct. One could imagine a default that is brand-new, a newly pre-checked box on a form, and therefore carries no existence-bias weight at all; and one could imagine an ancient practice that is not anyone's default, an old ritual almost no one performs, yet still commands the "it has been around, so it must be good" intuition. Default bias asks what happens if I do nothing? Existence bias asks is what already is, for that reason, right? It is not, second, the naturalistic fallacy (Book 015), though the two are cousins and often reinforce each other. The naturalistic fallacy slides from natural to good: because reproduction is natural, it must be good. Existence bias slides from existing to good: because reproduction exists and has always existed, it must be good. The categories differ. Something can exist without being natural. Money, marriage, the nation-state all exist and none is a fact of biology, and existence bias attaches to these just as readily. Reproduction happens to trigger both fallacies at once, which is part of why the intuition around it feels so overdetermined and so hard to dislodge. But keeping them separate matters. This book is about existence and duration as sources of felt legitimacy, not about nature. The reader who wants the fuller treatment of the natural-therefore-good move should turn to Book 015; here it appears only where the two mechanisms braid together. It is not, third, mere status quo bias in the narrow economic sense, though it is closely related and the terms are sometimes used loosely as synonyms. Status quo bias, as studied by William Samuelson and Richard Zeckhauser, is a preference for the current state driven substantially by loss aversion: we weight the prospective losses of a change more heavily than the equivalent gains, and so we stay put. Existence bias is broader and does not depend on our having any stake in the outcome at all. In the Eidelman studies, participants judged the goodness of things they had no relationship to and no prospect of losing; the mere fact of existence, not the fear of loss, drove the effect. Existence bias is thus better thought of as one of the deep engines underneath status quo bias, a reason the current state seems safer and better, familiar and right. Two further mechanisms recur throughout the book and deserve naming here, because they are the psychological machinery that gives existence bias its force in the specific domain of reproduction. The first is the mere-exposure effect, demonstrated by Robert Zajonc beginning in the late 1960s: repeated exposure to a stimulus, all else equal, increases our liking for it. We come to prefer the faces, words, shapes, and melodies we have simply encountered more often, with no reasoning involved and often with no memory of the exposure. Reproduction and family life are, for almost everyone, among the most heavily exposed features of the human environment. We are surrounded by families from birth; we were, each of us, produced by one. Mere exposure predicts that this saturation alone should generate a warm, unreasoned preference, a liking that then gets interpreted as evidence of goodness. Existence bias and mere exposure are conceptually distinct, one being about duration and being, the other about frequency of encounter, but in practice they compound. What has existed longest we have usually been exposed to most. The second is system justification theory, developed by John Jost and Mahzarin Banaji beginning in the early 1990s. Its core claim is that people are motivated to defend their own interests and their own groups, and beyond that to defend and rationalize the existing social system as such, to see the prevailing arrangements as fair, legitimate, and natural, sometimes even when those arrangements disadvantage them. System justification supplies the motivational heat that existence bias, on its own, lacks. Existence bias tells us that what is, is probably good. System justification tells us why we want that to be true: because a world whose fundamental arrangements are legitimate is a more reassuring, more orderly, less anxious world to live in. The pervasive practice of reproducing, the very mechanism by which the social system perpetuates itself across time, is close to the center of what system-justifying motives would most want to defend. To question whether people ought to have children is, at some level, to question the continuation of the entire order, and the psychological system recoils. Put these together and the shape of the bias in reproduction becomes clear. Childbearing is the oldest existing human practice; it is the most heavily exposed; and it is the load-bearing wall of the entire social system. Existence bias, mere exposure, and system justification therefore all point in the same direction and all reinforce one another, producing an intuition of goodness and necessity so strong that it can feel less like an intuition than like a perception, like simply seeing that families are good, the way one sees that the sky is blue. The difference between a perception and a judgment matters, because the whole method of the book depends on it. A perception presents itself as a report about the world. The sky is blue, the family is good, and reports about the world do not invite argument; one does not debate a perception, one simply has it or lacks it. A judgment, by contrast, is a conclusion, and conclusions have premises, and premises can be examined, contested, weighed, and revised. The existence bias's most consequential trick is to dress a judgment in the clothing of a perception, so that the conclusion "reproduction is good and necessary" is experienced as though it were simply seen rather than inferred, and therefore as something that could no more be argued with than the color of the sky. Restoring the judgment to visibility as a judgment is not a rhetorical flourish; it is the entire operation the book is trying to perform. Everything that follows, the steelman, the genealogy, the evidence, the costs, is in service of converting a felt perception back into an examinable judgment, so that its premises can be brought into the light and assessed on their merits. Until that conversion happens, there is nothing to reason about, because the bias has hidden the reasoning inside a feeling. After it happens, the real work of deciding can finally begin. The rest of this book is an attempt to convert that perception back into what it actually is: a judgment, made by a mind with known biases, about a practice that may or may not be good for any given person, and whose real merits, where they exist, deserve to be argued for on their own terms. The single discipline the book asks of the reader is to hold apart two questions that the existence bias collapses into one. The first question is: does this exist, and has it existed for a long time? The answer, for reproduction, is plainly yes. The second question is: is it, therefore, good and obligatory? The answer to that is not contained in the first, and pretending it is has consequences, for individuals talked out of examining a life-defining choice, and for institutions that treat the reproduction of the population as beyond the reach of ordinary reasoning. Naming the bias is the first step to declining its unearned conclusion. --- --- Check Yourself — Chapter 1 Quiz. 1. The strongest case for it (steelman) A critique of a bias is worth nothing if it only ever attacks the bias in its dumbest form. The existence bias, applied to reproduction, has a foolish version, "we've always done it, so shut up," that no one needs help refuting. It also has a serious version, and intellectual honesty requires meeting that one first, on its strongest ground, before laying a finger on it. This chapter tries to state the natalist case for taking existence and duration seriously in as good a form as it can be made. The reader should finish the chapter feeling the pull of the argument. If the later critique is going to be worth anything, it has to earn its way past a case this strong. Start with the epistemic humility argument, which is the best one. The claim is not that whatever exists is good by definition. It is that the long persistence of a practice is genuine, if defeasible, evidence of its value, and that a wise person weights such evidence rather than dismissing it. This is essentially a Burkean and Hayekian thought, and it is far from silly. Edmund Burke argued that inherited institutions carry the distilled, largely tacit wisdom of countless people across countless situations, a wisdom no individual reformer, however clever, can reconstruct from first principles. Friedrich Hayek made the parallel argument about spontaneous orders: practices that have survived long competition among alternatives often encode solutions to problems we cannot even fully articulate, let alone solve on a whiteboard. On this view, the existence bias is not a bug but a rough-and-ready heuristic, and a rational one. Given how little any of us knows, "it has worked for a very long time" really is a reason to be cautious about discarding it, and the burden really should sit, at least a little, on the person who wants to change course. Reproduction is the paradigm case: no practice has been tested against reality for longer, and the "test" it has passed is the most demanding one imaginable, the continued existence of the species. A practice with that track record has earned a presumption. To sweep it aside as "mere existence bias" is to mistake accumulated wisdom for prejudice. Add the argument from the conditions of valuing anything at all. There is something self-undermining, the natalist can argue, about using the products of human continuation, language, philosophy, the very concept of a "bias," the leisure to sit and critique reproduction, to argue against the practice that produced them. Every value the critic holds was transmitted through the generational chain. The critic's own existence, capacities, and moral vocabulary are downstream of an unbroken line of people who reproduced. There is at minimum a deep tension, perhaps an incoherence, in standing on the shoulders of that continuity to argue that it carries no special weight. The practice that exists is no ordinary practice. It is the one that produces the valuers and their values. Its existence is therefore not a neutral fact to be bracketed. It is the precondition of there being anyone to do the bracketing. Add the argument from revealed preference and lived testimony. Across virtually every culture, era, and material condition, most people who have had the option have chosen to have children, and a large majority of parents, asked, report that it was among the most meaningful things they ever did. One can try to explain this away as bias all the way down, but at some point the sheer robustness of the preference, across such varied circumstances, becomes evidence in its own right. If a practice consistently produces, in most who undertake it, a sense of deep meaning and connection, and if it has done so across every kind of society, then the burden shifts: the person claiming that all of this is illusion, self-deception, or manufactured false consciousness owes us an account of why so many people, in so many settings, are so systematically wrong about their own lives. The existence and persistence of the practice is not floating free of human welfare; it is entangled with a durable, cross-cultural report that the practice is good. Add the argument from institutional and biological continuity. A society is more than its living members. It is a compact across generations. Many of our most cherished goods, the rule of law, scientific knowledge, cultural inheritance, the care of the vulnerable, are intergenerational projects that presuppose successor generations to inherit and continue them. A generation that declined to reproduce would be doing more than making a private choice; it would be quietly deciding the fate of every future-facing human project. The existing practice of reproduction is what keeps that future open. Its felt necessity, the natalist argues, is not a bias to be dissolved but an accurate perception of a real dependency: almost everything we value requires that there be people later to value it, and there will only be people later if people continue, roughly as they always have, to have children. Finally, add the argument from the asymmetry of error. If the existence bias inclines us, wrongly, to keep having children when we should not, the cost is borne within a continuing human story that can later correct itself. If the opposite error is made, if a generation is talked out of reproduction by clever arguments that later prove mistaken, the error may be irreversible, because there is no later generation to notice and reverse it. Faced with that asymmetry, a rational agent under uncertainty should tilt toward continuation. The existence bias, on this reading, is a prudent thumb on the scale in exactly the direction that a cautious decision-theorist would place it anyway. Taken together, these arguments make a real case, and it is important to state plainly what that case, at its strongest, establishes. It establishes that the long existence of reproduction is not nothing, that it carries real, if fallible, evidential and prudential weight, and that anyone who dismisses it as pure prejudice is being careless. A responsible critique of the existence bias must concede all of this. The persistence of childbearing is a data point. It would be its own kind of bias, call it a reformer's arrogance, to treat the accumulated practice of the entire species as though it contained no information. What the case does not establish, and this is the hinge on which the whole book turns, is that the felt goodness and necessity of reproduction can be read directly off its existence, without further argument. Every one of the strong arguments above is a further argument. The Burkean point is an argument that persistence tracks tacit wisdom, a claim that can be examined, and that holds for some practices and fails for others. The revealed-preference point is an argument from testimony, which can be weighed, and which faces its own well-known biases (Books 003, 008, 009 take these up). The continuity point is an argument about intergenerational goods, a real argument, with real premises that can be true or false. In each case the existence of the practice is doing no work on its own; the work is being done by a substantive claim about why this particular long-lived practice is good. That is exactly as it should be. The existence bias is the error of skipping those arguments, of letting the aura of antiquity stand in for them, so that the practice feels justified without ever having been justified. The steelman, properly understood, does not defend the bias. It defends the reasons the bias impersonates. And once those reasons are on the table as reasons, they can be examined, weighed, and, case by case, life by life, affirmed or found wanting. That examination is what the existence bias forecloses, and what the rest of this book tries to reopen. --- --- Check Yourself — Chapter 2 Quiz. 1. Where it comes from A bias this deep and this widely shared is unlikely to have a single origin. The existence bias in reproduction is better understood as overdetermined: several independent forces, evolutionary, cognitive, religious, economic, and institutional, each push in the same direction, and their convergence is what makes the resulting intuition feel so much like bedrock. This chapter traces the main tributaries. The aim is not to explain the bias away, since showing where an intuition comes from does not show that it is false, but to make visible that the intuition has causes other than the truth of its conclusion. A belief that would be held just as firmly whether or not it were true is a belief we should hold more loosely. The cognitive tributary: a general-purpose shortcut. The existence bias is not specific to reproduction; it is a general feature of human judgment, and its likely function is efficiency. The world presents us with far more objects, practices, and arrangements than we could ever individually evaluate. A cheap heuristic, assume that what exists is roughly okay until shown otherwise, economizes on scarce attention and is right often enough to earn its keep. Eidelman and Crandall have argued that existence bias belongs to a family of "shortcuts to worth," ways the mind assigns value quickly and effortlessly. On this account the bias is not a pathology but a default setting of cognition, applied indiscriminately to trees, treatments, and traditions alike. Reproduction inherits the general setting. What makes reproduction special is not that it triggers a special bias but that it maximizes every input the ordinary bias responds to: maximal duration, maximal ubiquity, maximal exposure. The evolutionary tributary. However one interprets the details, it is uncontroversial that we are the descendants of an unbroken line of ancestors every one of whom reproduced. This has a straightforward consequence, explored more fully in the evolutionary books of this series (015, 017, 018, 023): dispositions that favored reproduction were, on average, differentially represented in subsequent generations. It would be surprising if we did not carry inclinations, toward children, toward the reproductive project, toward regarding it as self-evidently worthwhile, that had been selected precisely for promoting reproduction. For this book, the decisive point is that a disposition selected because it promotes reproduction need not track any independent fact about whether reproduction is good. Natural selection is indifferent to our welfare and blind to our values; it "cares" only about differential replication. So the evolutionary tributary supplies a powerful reason to expect a strong pro-reproduction intuition and a powerful reason to distrust that intuition as a guide to value, because its origin is a process with no interest in value at all. (This is the terrain of the naturalistic fallacy, Book 015, and the book does not re-litigate it here; the point is only that our evolutionary history is one source of the felt legitimacy that existence bias then rationalizes.) The religious tributary. Across the major traditions, reproduction has been sacralized, and the existing practice thereby endowed with cosmic legitimacy. The Hebrew injunction to "be fruitful and multiply" (Genesis 1:28), taken up in detail in Book 041, reframes childbearing not as a choice but as a divine command and blessing, so that the existing practice becomes not old but ordained. Comparable framings appear across traditions, treated in Part IV of this series. For present purposes the mechanism is what matters: religion characteristically takes what exists and roots it in something that by definition cannot be questioned, converting a contingent practice into a sacred order. Once reproduction is sacred, its existence stops being a fact about the world and becomes an expression of the way things are meant to be, the existence bias raised to the level of theology. Even in thoroughly secular societies, the residue of this sacralization persists in the solemnity that still surrounds birth, the language of "blessing" and "miracle," and the moral gravity attached to the reproductive family. The economic and material tributary. For most of human history, and in much of the world still, children were and are an economic and existential asset: additional labor, old-age insurance, the difference between a lineage's survival and its extinction under conditions of high mortality. In a world without pensions, without welfare states, without effective medicine, and with high child mortality, a strong disposition to reproduce, and to regard reproduction as unquestionably good, was not a bias in any pejorative sense; it was closely aligned with survival. The existence bias in reproduction is thus partly a fossil of material conditions that, in the wealthy world, no longer obtain. This matters because biases outlive the environments that made them adaptive. An intuition calibrated to a world of high mortality and no social insurance does not automatically recalibrate when that world gives way to one of low mortality and formal old-age support; the felt necessity persists after the actual necessity has faded. Much of the friction this book examines comes from that lag, a very old intuition running in a very new environment. The institutional tributary and system justification. Finally, and most directly connected to this book's framing, reproduction is the mechanism by which every social system reproduces itself. States need successor citizens, taxpayers, and workers; churches need successor congregants; nations need successor members; families need successor heirs. Every durable institution has a stake in continued reproduction, and institutions are extraordinarily effective at generating the beliefs that serve their continuation, not usually through conspiracy but through the ordinary processes by which the arrangements that persist are the ones whose participants are inclined to reproduce and to valorize reproduction. System justification theory (Jost and Banaji) describes the psychological side of this: our motivated tendency to experience the prevailing order as legitimate and good. Because reproduction sits at the literal foundation of social continuity, it attracts the maximum of system-justifying motivation. To doubt it is to doubt the perpetuation of the whole, and the system-justifying mind treats such doubt the way an immune system treats a pathogen. The institutions that exist today are, definitionally, the ones whose predecessors succeeded in getting their members to reproduce and to feel that reproduction was good. We are, in that sense, the cultural descendants of successful pronatalist systems just as we are the biological descendants of successful reproducers, and both inheritances incline us to mistake the existence bias for insight. The developmental tributary: the bias is installed before reasoning begins. There is one further origin worth isolating, because it explains why the bias feels less like a belief one holds than like a perception one simply has. Every human being acquires the existence bias about reproduction in the same way and at the same time: in childhood, pre-verbally, before the capacity for critical evaluation has formed. A child does not encounter "families exist and reproduction is good" as a proposition to be assessed; the child is born into a family, is cared for by reproducers, and absorbs the entire arrangement as simply the structure of reality, in the same undifferentiated way it absorbs that objects fall and night follows day. By the time a person is old enough to reason about whether reproduction is good, the presumption that it is has already been laid down as part of the felt furniture of the world, beneath the level at which reasons operate. This developmental priority is why the bias is so resistant to argument: one cannot easily reason one's way out of a conviction that was installed before one could reason, because the conviction does not present itself as a conclusion that arguments reached but as a background fact that arguments must accommodate. The other tributaries explain why the belief is widespread and strong; the developmental tributary explains why it is pre-rational, why it arrives wearing the costume of perception rather than judgment, and why naming it as a bias can feel, initially, almost like being told that some obvious feature of the world is not really there. These tributaries have something in common. Not one of them is a reason to think reproduction is good. Each is a reason to think we would believe it is good, feel it, deeply and pre-reflectively, regardless of whether it is. The evolutionary line selected for the belief because it promoted replication; the religious line sacralized it because that stabilized the community; the economic line rewarded it because children were assets; the institutional line propagates it because systems that instill it persist. This is the crucial move of the chapter, and it is a modest one: to observe that the felt obviousness of reproduction's goodness has a rich causal history that runs entirely independent of the question of its actual value. And each installs the belief early, deep, and pre-rationally, so that it arrives feeling like perception. The intuition is manufactured, in the precise sense that there are powerful forces whose job, so to speak, is to produce it. That does not make the intuition false. Manufactured beliefs can be true. But it does strip the intuition of its authority as self-evidence. Once we see how reliably these forces would generate the feeling of obviousness whether or not the underlying claim were correct, we can no longer treat the feeling as its own justification. And that is all existence bias ever offered: the feeling as the justification. --- --- Check Yourself — Chapter 3 Quiz. 1. How it operates today Biases do not float free; they act through concrete channels, through the words people use, the questions they ask, the defaults built into forms and institutions, the images that saturate the environment. This chapter examines the everyday machinery by which the existence bias in reproduction reproduces itself, mostly beneath the threshold of anyone's notice. The recurring pattern is this: at no point does anyone actually argue that reproduction is good. The goodness is presupposed, built into the structure of the interaction so thoroughly that to question it registers not as disagreement but as a kind of malfunction, a failure to understand how the world works. The grammar of the presupposition. The most powerful operation of the bias is linguistic and almost invisible: it lives in the presuppositions of ordinary sentences. Consider the difference between "Are you going to have children?" and "When are you going to have children?" The second, overwhelmingly the more common form, and the subject of Book 027, does not ask whether reproduction will happen; it presupposes that it will and asks only for a date. Presupposition is linguistically hard to challenge: to answer "never" is to reject an expectation and, with it, the entire frame of the question, which is why such answers so often land as awkward or aggressive when nothing aggressive was intended. The existence bias operates here by loading the goodness and inevitability of reproduction into the background of speech, where it functions as shared common ground rather than as a claim on the table. What is common ground does not get argued; it gets assumed. And what gets assumed, over a lifetime of such sentences, comes to feel like a fact about reality rather than a contestable proposition. The narrative default: the unfinished story. Reproduction is encoded as the natural terminus of a life story, so that a life without it reads as a narrative left hanging, a book missing its final chapter. This "life script" (Book 025) supplies a template, school, work, partnership, child, against which individual lives are unconsciously measured. The existence bias reinforces the script by lending each of its stages the authority of what has always been done. A person who departs from the script is not experienced as having chosen a different valid story but as having stalled in the one real story, and this is felt as a lack even by observers who could not articulate what, exactly, is missing. Related devices, the "empty house" metaphor (Book 038), the "completeness" narrative (Book 040), the "missing piece," all do the same structural work: they frame the non-reproductive life as an incomplete version of the reproductive one, rather than as a complete life of a different kind. In each case the existing, populated, child-centered life is the implicit measure of a full life, and everything else is scored as its shortfall. Institutional and material defaults. The bias is built into infrastructure. Tax codes, family-leave policies, housing stock, product design, and the very layout of neighborhoods presuppose and reward the reproductive household (Book 063). The "family-sized" everything, the family car, the family plan, the three-bedroom house sized for children who are assumed to be coming, encodes reproduction into the material world, so that the existing built environment silently confirms that this is how people live. When the physical and legal world is arranged around an assumption, the assumption acquires the mute authority of the concrete: it is simply there, unlabeled, apparently natural, the water everyone swims in. One does not argue with a floor plan. The existence bias here fuses with default bias (Book 001): the reproductive path is both the pre-selected option and the one the existing world is physically built to accommodate, so that choosing otherwise means deciding differently and then working against the grain of one's surroundings. The saturation of imagery: mere exposure at scale. Zajonc's mere-exposure effect predicts that sheer frequency of encounter breeds liking, and the contemporary environment delivers reproductive imagery at a volume no prior era could match. Advertising deploys babies to signal wholesomeness and to sell everything from cars to insurance (Book 067); film and television resolve their plots with pregnancies and births (Book 068); social feeds are dense with curated family content (Book 031); influencers monetize idealized parenthood (Book 073). The cumulative effect is a background hum of pro-reproductive exposure so constant that it becomes ambient, like the temperature of a room. Mere exposure ensures that this constant contact translates, without any argument and often without any awareness, into warmth toward the thing exposed. And because what we are exposed to most is, typically, also what has existed longest, the mere-exposure warmth and the existence-bias presumption reinforce each other in a loop: the practice feels good because it is everywhere, and it is everywhere because it is old, and its being old makes it feel good. The asymmetry of visibility. A subtler mechanism concerns what does not get shown. Contented parents are highly visible; ambivalent or regretful ones are largely silent (Books 008 and 075 treat this at length). Happy families are photographed and posted; the harder realities of care are performed offstage. The existence bias feeds on this asymmetry, because the existing, visible instances of the practice are disproportionately its success stories, and the mind, reading off the visible sample, infers that the practice is as good as its visible instances suggest. The existence of many visibly happy families is taken as evidence about families in general, when in fact it is partly an artifact of which families are visible. Existence bias, in other words, does not evaluate the full population of the practice's outcomes; it evaluates the curated, self-selecting shopfront and then generalizes the shopfront to the whole. The temporal frame: the past as a promissory note on the future. A further mechanism works by projecting the existing pattern forward as though it were a law. Because reproduction has always happened, it is tacitly assumed that it always will and always should; the past's persistence is silently converted into a claim on the future. This is visible in the reflexive force of the objection "but what if everyone stopped?" (examined philosophically in Book 082), which treats the continuation of the existing pattern as a fixed obligation each person inherits, rather than as an open question each generation faces anew. The existence bias supplies the hidden premise: that the pattern which has held is the pattern which must hold. But a practice's having occurred for a long time is a fact about the past and carries, by itself, no obligation into the future; that "carrying" is done by the bias, which treats duration as though it were a promise. The mechanism matters because it is how the existence bias reaches forward in time, converting a track record into a mandate and making any proposed departure feel like the breaking of a vow that no one ever actually made. The conversational immune response. Finally, the bias operates through the way conversations about it are policed. Try, in ordinary company, to ask whether having children is actually good, in general, as a practice, not for you specifically, but as such. The typical response is not counter-argument but a kind of social flinch: the question is heard as strange, cold, possibly hostile, a little bit not-quite-right. This flinch is the existence bias defending itself. Because the goodness of reproduction is held as background common ground rather than as a claim, raising it to the level of a claim feels like a violation of conversational norms, and the violation is met with the mild sanctions reserved for people who say inappropriate things. The effect is that the core proposition, reproduction is good and to be continued, is protected from examination not by winning arguments but by never being allowed onto the field where arguments happen. This is the shield the preface promised to describe, seen in action at the scale of a single dinner conversation. Across all these channels, the common thread is presupposition rather than proof. The existence bias does not win the argument that reproduction is good. It arranges the world, the grammar, the narrative, the infrastructure, the imagery, the visible sample, the conversational norms, so that the argument never has to be had. That is a far more effective way to secure a conclusion than any argument could be, because an argument can be answered and a presupposition mostly cannot. To answer it, one first has to drag it out of the background and into the open, which is difficult, effortful, and mildly transgressive, precisely the work this book is trying to make a little easier. --- --- Check Yourself — Chapter 4 Quiz. 1. The evidence It is one thing to describe a bias plausibly and another to show it is real. This chapter sets out what the empirical record actually supports, and, just as importantly, what it does not. A book critical of a bias has a special obligation here, because the temptation is to overclaim: to treat the bias as more established, more precisely measured, and more directly proven in the reproductive domain than the evidence warrants. The honest picture is a mix of well-supported general findings and reasonable but under-tested extensions to reproduction specifically. Stating the difference is part of the argument, because a critique that relies on overstated evidence would itself be a kind of bias. The existence bias itself is well demonstrated in general. The core finding, that people judge existing and longer-existing states of affairs as better and more right, rests on a body of experimental work by Eidelman, Crandall, and colleagues, published in mainstream social-psychology venues in the late 2000s. The design of these studies is their strength: because the "how long has this existed" information is manipulated experimentally while everything else is held constant, the studies can support a genuinely causal claim. Duration information, and existence information, cause more favorable evaluation. This is not a correlational artifact or a just-so story; it is a repeatable laboratory effect. The effect sizes are moderate rather than enormous, and, as with much of social psychology, a careful reader will want to know how the findings have held up under replication scrutiny; the prudent position is that the basic phenomenon is real and reasonably robust, while the exact magnitude in any given context is uncertain. What the studies establish is the existence (the pun is unavoidable) of the mechanism. What they do not, by themselves, establish is how strongly it operates in the high-stakes, emotionally saturated, real-world domain of reproduction, which no laboratory study directly tests. The supporting mechanisms are each independently well established. The mere-exposure effect (Zajonc) is one of the more robust findings in social psychology, replicated across many stimulus types and cultures over decades; that familiarity breeds liking is not seriously in doubt, though its magnitude varies with conditions. System justification theory (Jost, Banaji, and many collaborators) is supported by a large and varied research program showing that people rationalize and defend existing social arrangements, sometimes against their own interest, and the phenomenon of disadvantaged groups endorsing the legitimacy of the very system that disadvantages them is among its more striking and replicated results. Status quo bias (Samuelson and Zeckhauser) and the endowment effect are staples of behavioral economics. None of these is a fringe claim. The intellectual scaffolding of this book therefore rests on findings that are, individually, about as solid as social science offers. The application to reproduction is the frontier, and it is more inferential. Here candor is required. There is not, to this author's knowledge, a clean body of experiments that manipulates the perceived antiquity or ubiquity of childbearing and measures the resulting shift in how good or obligatory people judge it to be, the direct reproductive analogue of the Eidelman tree-and-treatment studies. The claim that existence bias operates powerfully in reproduction is therefore an inference: it combines (a) the well-established general mechanism with (b) the observation that reproduction maximizes every input that mechanism responds to, maximal duration, ubiquity, and exposure. The inference is reasonable and, in the author's view, strong. But it is an inference, not a directly measured result, and the reader should hold it as such. This is exactly the kind of place where a responsible book flags its own uncertainty rather than dressing a plausible extrapolation as a proven fact. If the general mechanism is real and reproduction maximizes its inputs, the conclusion follows with high probability, but "high probability from strong premises" is not the same as "demonstrated in the specific domain," and pretending otherwise would be to commit, in miniature, the overclaiming this series exists to resist. A note on replication and the credibility of the base findings. Because the existence-bias claim rests on social-psychology research produced in an era now known to have suffered a "replication crisis," a candid book must address the reliability of its own foundations rather than cite them as if all published findings were equally secure. Two things can be said. First, the mechanisms this book leans on hardest are not obscure single studies but large, mature research programs: mere exposure has been probed across five decades and many stimulus classes; system justification has generated hundreds of studies and several convergent lines of evidence, including field data and cross-national comparisons. Programs of that breadth are far more robust than any lone experiment, because their core claim has been approached from many angles. Second, the existence-bias studies specifically are newer and have a correspondingly thinner replication record, so the appropriate confidence is lower, high enough to build an argument on, not so high as to treat the effect size as settled. The book's practice, accordingly, is to state the general mechanism with confidence, the reproductive application as a strong inference, and the precise magnitude as unknown. That calibration is itself an application of the book's method: refusing to let the mere existence of a published finding stand in for a demonstrated one, which would be the existence bias operating on the citation list. The demographic backdrop is solid and relevant. What is firmly established is the demographic context in which the bias now operates, and it sharpens the book's point. Global fertility has fallen dramatically over the past half-century; a large and growing number of countries now have total fertility rates below replacement level; and the direction of travel is well documented by the class of authoritative sources, the United Nations World Population Prospects and national statistical agencies, even where any single projected number carries uncertainty. (Following this series' citation policy, the book attributes these facts to that class of sources rather than inventing a precise figure.) The relevance is this: the existence bias is calibrated to a world in which high fertility was the unbroken norm, and that world is, demographically, ending. The intuition that reproduction is the eternal, universal, self-evident practice is, increasingly, a claim about the past. Many millions of people are already living lives the bias tells them are incomplete or aberrant, and the felt necessity persists even as the statistical universality that once underwrote it erodes. The bias, in short, is running against a moving demographic background, which is one reason its operation is becoming more visible and more contested than it was a generation ago. What the evidence does and does not license us to say. Pulling this together: we are entitled to say, with confidence, that a general existence bias exists, that mere exposure and system justification are real and powerful, and that reproduction has every feature that would maximize their operation. We are entitled to say, with high but not certain probability, that these mechanisms substantially shape how people evaluate reproduction. We are not entitled to say that the strength of the effect in reproduction has been precisely measured, that it explains any particular individual's choices, or that removing the bias would move fertility in any predictable direction. The bias is a distorting lens on the evaluation of reproduction; this book makes no claim to predict what people would choose if the lens were removed, and it is consistent with everything argued here that many or most people, reasoning clearly, would still choose to have children. The point is not the destination but the quality of the reasoning that reaches it. A choice made under a bias may be the same choice one would make without it, but it is a worse-grounded choice, and for a decision this consequential, the grounding matters. One further methodological note belongs here, because it recurs whenever biases about widely shared practices are discussed. It is sometimes objected that any evidence of a pro-reproduction intuition is really evidence that reproduction is good, that people believe it because it is true. This cannot be ruled out a priori, and the steelman of Chapter 2 took it seriously. But the objection cannot be assumed either, and here the evidence helps, because the general existence-bias studies decouple the two: in the laboratory, favorable judgment tracks manipulated duration even when the underlying object is held constant or is arbitrary. That decoupling is the crucial evidential contribution. It shows that the mind assigns extra worth to mere existence and duration as such, independent of the object's actual merits. We know, in other words, that the mechanism can and does produce favorable judgments that the object does not earn. Whether, in the specific case of reproduction, the favorable judgment is fully earned, partly earned, or unearned is exactly the open question, and the value of naming the bias is that it lets us ask that question instead of assuming its answer. --- --- Check Yourself — Chapter 5 Quiz. 1. The hidden costs Every bias has a price, and the price is paid in the things the bias makes it hard to see. The existence bias is a lens that renders the goodness of reproduction so vivid that the costs of the practice, and, more subtly, the costs of the bias itself, fade into the background. This chapter tries to bring those costs into focus. A distinction structures it: there are the costs the bias hides about reproduction, and there is the cost of holding the bias, of reasoning about a life-defining choice with a distorted instrument. Both matter, and they are different. The cost to the quality of individual reasoning. The first and most direct cost is epistemic. A decision about whether to have children is among the most consequential a person can make: it is effectively irreversible, it reshapes every domain of life for decades, and it brings a new person into existence who did not consent and cannot be consulted. A decision of that weight deserves the clearest reasoning a person can bring to it. The existence bias systematically degrades that reasoning by supplying a false sense that the question is already settled, that reproduction's goodness is a given rather than something to be weighed. The cost here is not that people reproduce; it is that many do so, or don't, without ever having genuinely reasoned about it, because the bias told them there was nothing to reason about. A choice can turn out well and still have been made badly, in the sense of made without the deliberation it warranted. The existence bias converts a weighty decision into a non-decision, and the loss of that deliberation is a cost even when the outcome is happy. The opportunity costs the framing suppresses. When a practice is coded as self-evidently good, its opportunity costs go unpriced. Every life is a finite allocation of time, attention, money, energy, and care, and every path taken forecloses others. The reproductive life forecloses certain kinds of freedom, mobility, career concentration, creative or vocational single-mindedness, financial latitude, and forms of relationship and rest that a non-reproductive life keeps open, just as the non-reproductive life forecloses the distinctive goods of parenthood. This is not a claim that one ledger is heavier; it is a claim that the existence bias suppresses the existence of the ledger. Because reproduction is framed as simply what one does, its opportunity costs are not experienced as costs at all but as the ordinary shape of adult life, while the opportunity costs of not reproducing are made lurid and salient (the "empty house," the imagined future regret). The bias does something worse than tilt the scale. It hides one pan of it. Honest reasoning would price both sets of foregone goods. The bias prices only one. The autonomy cost. There is a distinct harm in having one of life's central choices pre-decided by forces one never examined. Autonomy is more than the absence of external coercion; it is the capacity to author one's own life through choices one has actually made one's own. A choice inherited whole from the existence bias, reproduced, as it were, rather than chosen, is autonomy-diminished even when it happens to align with what the person would have chosen anyway, because the person never occupied the position of genuinely choosing. This cost falls on those who reproduce under the bias (who may never know whether they authored the choice or merely inherited it) and on those who do not reproduce and must bear the social friction of visibly departing from a script that is treated as beyond question. In both cases the bias extracts an autonomy toll, and it is a real cost of the bias even for people who are, in the end, glad of where they landed. The welfare costs of unexamined pressure. When reproduction is treated as self-evidently good and obligatory, pressure to reproduce is licensed and its costs are ignored. People have children they are ambivalent about, in circumstances they are not ready for, because the alternative, visibly and permanently declining the one thing "everyone" does, carries a social cost the bias makes steep. The welfare consequences fall on the reluctant parent, on the co-parent, and, not least, on children brought into being more from the gravitational pull of the practice than from a considered desire for this child in this life. This is a delicate point and must be made carefully: the book is not saying that children of ambivalent parents are unwanted or unloved, nor is it passing judgment on any family. It is saying that a bias which converts a weighty choice into a foregone conclusion predictably raises the number of reproductive decisions made under pressure rather than from clear desire, and that the welfare costs of that, borne quietly, across many lives, are a genuine cost of the bias, precisely the kind the bias's framing keeps invisible. The distributive and ecological costs that go unpriced. Because the existence bias frames continued reproduction as simply the natural order, the costs a new person imposes on others and on the biosphere are treated as no one's concern, externalities in the economist's sense, borne by parties who never chose them (Book 065). This is among the most contested terrain in the whole natalism debate, and the book states its claim narrowly and without alarmism: whatever one concludes about the ethics of these costs, the existence bias ensures they are not even entered into the reckoning, because a practice coded as beyond question is a practice whose costs are coded as beyond accounting. One can hold a wide range of views about population and ecology and still accept the narrow point: a bias that forecloses the question forecloses the accounting, and an unpriced cost is not thereby a nonexistent one. The cost of a foreclosed vocabulary. A subtler cost operates at the level of language itself. Because the existence bias holds the goodness of reproduction as background common ground, the culture never had strong reason to develop a rich, neutral vocabulary for reasoning about the choice, and the vocabulary it did develop is loaded. We have warm, ready words for the reproductive life (family, blessing, legacy, complete) and mostly cold or deficient ones for its alternative (childless, barren, empty). The vocabulary itself, examined in Part VI of this series, is an artifact of the bias, and it becomes a further cost because it degrades the very tools a person would need to think clearly. It is difficult to weigh two options evenhandedly when the words available for one are suffused with worth and the words for the other connote lack; the scale is tilted before the weighing begins, in the medium of thought itself. This is a cost the bias hides especially well, because it operates on the instrument of reasoning rather than on any particular conclusion, so that a person can feel they are reasoning freely while the available words quietly do the bias's work for them. The cost to institutions and public reasoning. Finally, existence bias exacts a cost at the level of collective decision-making. When the reproduction of the population is treated as a self-justifying good, public policy inherits the bias: pronatalist measures are adopted with a presumption of legitimacy that other large interventions must earn, and the question of whether a society should be trying to raise its own fertility at all is often skipped in favor of the operational question of how. Demographic panic (Book 051) frequently rests on precisely this unexamined premise, that more births are simply, obviously better, and the existence bias is what makes that premise feel too obvious to state, let alone defend. The cost is a public conversation that debates the means of pronatalism while treating its end as settled, when the end is exactly the thing that a clear-eyed politics would put on the table. A democracy that cannot ask whether a goal is worth pursuing, only how to pursue it, has ceded part of its reasoning to a bias. The unifying theme of these costs is invisibility. The existence bias does not typically produce bad outcomes by making false claims that could be checked and refuted. It produces its costs by removing whole categories of consideration from view: the opportunity costs, the autonomy toll, the welfare of the pressured, the unpriced externalities, the foreclosed public question. What is not seen cannot be weighed, and what cannot be weighed cannot be chosen against. The remedy the book proposes is correspondingly modest and non-directive: not to change anyone's decision, but to make the hidden pans of the scale visible again, so that whatever is chosen is chosen with the full ledger in view. Restoring the costs to visibility is not an argument against reproduction. It is the precondition of the choice being a choice. --- --- Check Yourself — Chapter 6 Quiz. 1. The existence bias in reproduction presents itself as universal, the way things simply are, for everyone, but its pressure falls with very different weight on different people, and the appearance of universality is part of how it works. A pressure that seems to come from nowhere in particular, that seems to be just the shape of reality, is harder to resist than one with a visible source. This chapter maps the uneven incidence of the bias: who is most subject to it, who can most easily escape it, and how the "everyone does this" framing conceals a distribution that is anything but even. Gender: the unequal freight of "what has always been done." No axis of distribution is starker than gender. The existing, longstanding practice whose goodness the existence bias affirms is not a gender-neutral practice; historically and still, it has been organized around women's bodies and women's labor. Pregnancy, birth, and the disproportionate share of care work fall on women, and so the "what has always been done" that the bias sanctifies is, for women, a demand on the body and the life course that men are not asked to meet in the same currency. The existence bias thus does not press equally on the sexes; it presses through a division of reproductive labor that it simultaneously renders invisible by treating it as natural and eternal. The equation of womanhood with motherhood (Book 091), the loaded language of "barrenness" (Book 022), the maternal-instinct framing (Book 017), and the "biological clock" (Book 016) are all, in part, gendered delivery mechanisms for the existence bias: they take the general presumption that reproduction is good and route it, with special force and special deadlines, at women. A man who does not reproduce is often read as merely unmarried or a late bloomer; a woman who does not is more likely to be read as having failed at, or refused, the very thing her existence is presumed to be for. Same bias; heavier freight. Class: the difference between examining a script and being crushed by it. Economic position sharply conditions how the bias operates. Wealth and education buy a degree of insulation: the affluent can more easily access effective contraception, defer childbearing while pursuing education and career, absorb the costs of the choices they make in either direction, and inhabit social milieus where non-reproduction is increasingly unremarkable. They also, notably, have more room to reproduce deliberately, to treat childbearing as a considered project rather than a fate. For people with fewer resources, the script is harder to hold at arm's length: contraceptive access may be worse, the material stakes of any reproductive decision are higher, and the social and institutional supports for departing from the default are thinner. There is a bitter irony worth naming: the existence bias, which presents reproduction as the universal human practice, in fact operates with least friction on those with the fewest resources to examine it, while the very people who most loudly celebrate large families as the natural order are sometimes those most insulated from the costs of the practice they valorize. The distribution of the bias's pressure and the distribution of the practice's costs do not line up, and both fall harder as one moves down the economic gradient. Culture and community: the density of the surrounding consensus. The strength of the existence bias in any given life depends heavily on the density of the surrounding pronatalist consensus. In tightly knit religious and traditional communities where reproduction is sacralized (Part IV of this series), the bias is reinforced at every turn, by scripture, ritual, kin, and the visible example of everyone around, and departing from it can mean genuine exile from one's entire web of relationships, and not merely social awkwardness. In more pluralistic or secular settings, the consensus is thinner and the exits are more available. This means the same underlying bias translates into radically different levels of actual coercion depending on where one stands. For someone embedded in a high-consensus community, the "it has always been done, so it must be done" pressure is close to total; for someone in a low-consensus setting, it is a background hum. Any honest account of the bias must therefore resist the flattening that the word "bias" can encourage: it is one mechanism, but its lived weight ranges from a faint social preference to something approaching compulsion, and where a person falls on that range is largely a matter of the community they happen to inhabit. Ability, health, and the "which children" undercurrent. The existence bias interacts uneasily with disability and health in two opposite-seeming ways that are worth holding together. On one hand, disabled people and those with chronic illness are frequently excluded from the presumption, their reproduction discouraged, questioned, or in the darkest history actively prevented, so that the "everyone reproduces" framing was never extended to them on equal terms (Book 097 treats this fraught terrain directly). On the other hand, the same individuals may face the presumption's full force in the form of grief and stigma when reproduction is difficult or impossible, framed as a bodily failure to perform the practice that existence has decreed (Book 019). The existence bias thus manages to press on the same people from two directions at once: excluded from the practice's presumed universality while still measured against its presumed necessity. This is one of the places where the bias's claim to be a neutral, universal fact of life most obviously breaks down, because its history is full of decisions about whose reproduction counted as part of the sacred existing order and whose did not. Age and the manufactured deadline. Finally, the bias distributes its pressure across the life course in a way that intensifies with age, especially for women, through the "closing window" narrative (Books 016 and 099). The existence bias supplies the background premise, reproduction is what one is here to do, and the biological-clock framing converts that premise into urgency, so that the pressure is not constant but rises toward a manufactured deadline. Younger people can defer the question; as the window is said to close, the accumulated force of a lifetime of presupposition arrives all at once, often at the moment when clear reasoning is hardest because the framing insists there is no time for it. The distribution of the bias's pressure is thus temporal as well as social: it is engineered to peak precisely when deliberation is most rushed. The invisibility of one's own position. A meta-point cuts across all these axes and is worth stating on its own. Whoever is least subject to the existence bias, the affluent, secular, urban person in a low-consensus milieu, is also, characteristically, least able to see how heavily it presses on others, because from a position of relative insulation the pressure looks optional, even mild. This produces a systematic misperception at exactly the wrong place: those with the most social voice, who set much of the tone of public conversation about reproduction, are often those who feel the bias least, and who may therefore underestimate how coercive it remains for the woman in a high-consensus religious community, for the person whose material circumstances leave no room to hold the script at arm's length, or for the individual approaching a manufactured deadline. The result is a debate conducted disproportionately by the insulated about the constrained, in which the real weight of the pressure is chronically discounted by the very people best positioned to describe it. Any honest distributional analysis has to include the analyst's own position in the distribution, and to treat the felt mildness of the bias as itself a datum about privilege rather than as a measure of the bias's true force. The through-line of this chapter is that the existence bias's greatest rhetorical asset, its air of applying to everyone equally, of being simply the human condition, is false, and its falseness is doing work. By presenting itself as universal, the bias obscures that it is a mechanism with a distribution, and that the distribution tracks exactly the lines along which power and vulnerability already run: it presses hardest on women, on the poor, on the embedded, on the ill, and on those approaching a manufactured deadline. Naming the distribution is not a separate project from naming the bias; it is part of the same project, because a pressure cannot be resisted clearly until one can see both that it is a pressure and where, specifically, it bears down. --- --- Check Yourself — Chapter 7 Quiz. 1. The counter-arguments to the critique A book that only presented objections to its opponents and none to itself would be committing, at the level of argument, the very sin it accuses the existence bias of: shielding a conclusion from scrutiny. This chapter turns the critical instrument on the book's own thesis. Each objection below is a real challenge, stated as strongly as the author can state it, and each is answered honestly, which sometimes means conceding ground. The reader who finishes this chapter thinking the book's thesis has survived should think so because it was genuinely tested, not because the tests were rigged. Objection one: you are proving too much, this dissolves every institution. If "it exists and has existed a long time, therefore it is good" is a fallacy, then the same demolition applies to language, law, morality, science, cooperation, and every other long-standing human practice. But surely we do not want to say that the antiquity of, say, the prohibition on murder gives us no reason at all to respect it. If the argument works against reproduction, it works against everything, and an argument that proves everything proves nothing. This is the most serious objection, and answering it requires a real concession. The book does not claim that duration and persistence carry no evidential weight whatsoever. Chapter 2's steelman granted, sincerely, that the long survival of a practice can be defeasible evidence of its value; the Burkean point is a good one. The claim is narrower and, properly stated, survives the objection: it is that existence and duration are weak, defeasible evidence that must be supplemented by actual argument about the specific practice, and that the existence bias is the error of treating them as sufficient, of letting the aura of antiquity substitute for the argument rather than merely open it. The prohibition on murder is well supported by antiquity and by a mountain of independent argument about harm, autonomy, and the conditions of social life; strip away the antiquity and the prohibition still stands on those arguments. The test the book proposes is exactly this: for any long-standing practice, can it be supported by reasons other than its longevity? Where the answer is yes, longevity is a bonus, not the load-bearing wall. Where the answer is no, where the practice is defended only by its own persistence, the existence bias is doing all the work, and that is where the critique bites. Reproduction can be examined by that test like anything else; the book does not prejudge the result. So the objection fails as stated, but only because the thesis, correctly bounded, was never the universal solvent the objection imagines. Objection two: the persistence of reproduction is different, because it is the condition of everything else. Reproduction is no ordinary old practice. It is the practice without which there is no language, no law, no science, no anyone. Its existence is presupposed by the existence of every valuer and every value, so it cannot be bracketed the way an ordinary practice can. To question it is quasi-self-refuting. This is the strongest form of Chapter 2's continuity argument, and it deserves a careful answer, because it contains a real insight and a real overreach. The insight: yes, reproduction is uniquely foundational, and that is a reason to treat questions about it with special gravity and special care, which this book does. The overreach: "the practice that produced the valuers" is not thereby exempt from evaluation by those valuers. That a capacity was produced by some process does not make the process immune to the capacity's judgment; our moral reasoning was produced by evolution, and we nonetheless use it to judge evolution's products, including many of our own selected impulses. There is no incoherence in a being who owes its existence to a practice asking whether that practice, going forward, is good, any more than there is incoherence in a person raised by a particular tradition coming to assess it. The foundational status of reproduction raises the stakes of the question; it does not answer it, and treating "it made us" as if it settled "we must continue it" is, precisely, the existence bias wearing its most dignified clothes. Objection three: revealed preference is stronger evidence than you allow. You concede that most people, across most cultures, choose reproduction and report it as meaningful, and then you explain this away as bias. But at some point the robustness of a cross-cultural preference is just evidence that the thing is good. Your framework lets you dismiss any amount of testimony as "bias," which makes it unfalsifiable. This objection has teeth, and the honest response has two parts. First, the concern about unfalsifiability is legitimate and the book must not do what it warns against. The framework is not meant to license dismissing all pro-reproduction testimony as bias; Chapter 5 was explicit that the evidence does not license predicting what people would choose absent the bias, and that many might well still choose reproduction. The bias claim is about the quality of the reasoning, not a claim that the conclusion is wrong. Second, the reason testimony cannot simply be read as truth is that we have independent evidence, the general existence-bias studies, mere exposure, system justification, that these exact mechanisms produce favorable judgments that objects do not earn. That independent evidence is what stops the framework from being unfalsifiable: it gives a specific, testable reason to discount testimony by the amount attributable to known distorting mechanisms, no more. What remains after that discount may still be substantial, and may well vindicate reproduction for most people. The book's claim is only that the discount is real and should be applied, not that it consumes the whole of the testimony. Conceding that is not weakness; it is the difference between a critique and a crusade. Objection four: naming a bias is a cheap rhetorical trick. For any conclusion you dislike, you can invent or invoke a "bias" that supposedly explains why people hold it, and thereby avoid engaging the reasons they actually give. This is ad hominem dressed as psychology. The mere existence of a bias that could explain a belief does not show the belief is false or unjustified. Correct, and the book agrees, which is why it has been at pains throughout to say that identifying the causal origin of a belief does not refute the belief (Chapter 3 made this explicit), and to engage the actual natalist arguments on their merits (Chapter 2 gave them their strongest form). The genetic fallacy, inferring that a belief is false from facts about its origin, is a real fallacy, and a critique of a bias that committed it would be worthless. The book's move is different and legitimate: it is to show that a belief which feels self-evident has a causal history sufficient to explain the feeling without the belief's truth, and therefore that the feeling of self-evidence cannot be used as evidence. That does not refute the belief. It removes one particular bad argument for it, "it's just obvious," and demands that the belief be supported by the other, better arguments instead. Far from avoiding the reasons people give, the book insists those reasons be given, because it denies the bias's shortcut of not having to give them. The objection would land against a lazier book; against this one it actually describes the book's own method for staying honest. Objection five: existence bias might be correct in expectation, a good bet, not a mistake. A decision-theorist could press the point sharpened in Chapter 2: perhaps the existence bias is not an error at all but a well-calibrated prior. If long-persisting practices are, on average, more likely to be beneficial than randomly chosen novel ones, and there is a real evolutionary and cultural-selection argument that they are, then a mind that assigns extra credence to what has endured is not committing a fallacy but tracking a genuine regularity in the world. On this view "existence bias" is a misnomer for a rational Bayesian update, and calling it a bias smuggles in the conclusion that it is wrong. This is the most intellectually serious version of the challenge and it forces a precise reply. The book grants the premise for a wide class of practices: yes, persistence is correlated with value often enough that a mild prior in its favor is defensible, and Chapter 2 said exactly this. But three things convert a defensible prior into a bias in the case at hand. First, the laboratory evidence (Chapter 5) shows the mind applies the duration premium indiscriminately, to arbitrary objects with no selection history at all, which means the mechanism is not a careful Bayesian tracking selection pressure but a crude heuristic that fires whether or not the correlation it assumes actually obtains. A rational prior would discriminate; the bias does not. Second, the correlation between persistence and value is itself conditional on a stable environment, and Chapter 3 argued that the environment reproduction was calibrated to, high mortality, no social insurance, children as assets, has changed, which is exactly the condition under which a once-reliable prior becomes stale and misleading. Third, and decisively, even where the prior is well-calibrated it is only a prior: it licenses a starting presumption, not a refusal to update on the specific evidence. The bias's actual work, as documented throughout the book, is to foreclose the update, to treat the presumption as a conclusion and shield the specific case from the very examination a good Bayesian would insist on. So the objection succeeds in showing that a mild pro-persistence prior can be rational, and fails to show that what operates around reproduction is that disciplined prior rather than its crude, indiscriminate, environment-blind, examination-foreclosing cousin. The name "bias" is earned not by the prior but by the foreclosure. Objection six: this will be weaponized as pressure not to reproduce. Whatever the author intends, a book like this will be read by some as an argument that clear-thinking people don't have children, as the childfree mirror image of the pronatalist pressure it criticizes. It replaces one bias with another. This is a fair worry about reception, and it is the reason the book states its non-directive stance so often that a reader may find the repetition tiresome. The repetition is deliberate. The thesis is symmetrical in a way that resists weaponization: the existence bias distorts the evaluation of reproduction, and a distorted evaluation can be corrected toward either choice. Someone who reproduces after genuinely weighing the ledger has done exactly what the book asks; someone who declines to reproduce out of a new, equal-and-opposite ideological reflex has not; they have merely swapped one unexamined script for another. The book's target is the unexamined quality of the choice, not its content, and a childfree life adopted as an unexamined identity would be as much a failure of the book's aim as a reproductive life adopted as an unexamined default. That the argument can be misread as coercion is a cost of writing about the subject at all; the remedy is to be relentlessly clear that the only thing being argued for is the reopening of a question, never a particular answer to it. If a reader closes the book more certain of what they should do, the book has partly failed; if they close it better able to reason about it, whatever they decide, it has done its work. Taking these objections together, the thesis that survives is narrower and more defensible than a careless reader might expect, and that narrowing is a feature rather than a retreat. The book does not claim that persistence is worthless evidence, that reproduction is bad, that pro-reproduction testimony is illusion, that biases refute beliefs, or that anyone should decline to have children. It claims only this: that the existence and antiquity of reproduction generate a feeling of self-evident goodness that outruns the actual evidence, that this feeling functions to foreclose examination of a life-defining choice, and that the choice deserves examination on its real merits, wherever that examination leads. Every serious objection either misses that narrowed claim or, in pressing it, helps to sharpen it. A thesis that gets clearer under attack is one worth keeping. --- --- Check Yourself — Chapter 8 Quiz. 1. Case studies / illustrations Argument in the abstract can float free of the world it describes. This chapter grounds the existence bias in concrete instances, some historical, some contemporary, where the machinery described in earlier chapters can be watched in operation. None of these cases is offered as decisive proof; each is an illustration, meant to make the mechanism visible and recognizable. The reader is invited to test the framework against them and to notice, in each, the signature move: the goodness or necessity of the existing arrangement is treated as beyond question because it exists, and the demand for justification is redirected onto whoever proposes to depart from it. Illustration one: the reversed burden of proof in ordinary conversation. Consider the asymmetry, examined more fully in Book 086, in who is expected to justify themselves. A person who has children is essentially never asked to explain why; the choice is treated as self-explanatory, as needing no account. A person who does not have children, by contrast, is routinely asked to account for the absence, often repeatedly, and is expected to have reasons ready. This asymmetry is the existence bias made social. The existing, longstanding practice occupies the position of the unmarked default that requires no defense; departure from it occupies the position of the marked exception that must be defended. And this is backwards from the perspective of consequence: reproduction is the choice that is irreversible, that creates a new person, and that reshapes a life for decades, and so if any choice ought to require articulated reasons, it is that one. The existence bias inverts the burden, demanding justification from the low-stakes, reversible option (remaining as one is) and waiving it for the high-stakes, irreversible one (bringing a new person into being). Once seen, the inversion is hard to unsee, and it recurs in countless small interactions: the raised eyebrow, the "but why not?", the assumption that childlessness is a problem in search of an explanation while parenthood is an answer in need of none. Illustration two: pronatalist policy and the unexamined premise. When governments respond to falling fertility with baby bonuses, tax incentives, and public campaigns (Books 051 through 066 treat this domain at length), the public debate almost always concerns the effectiveness of the measures, will the subsidy actually raise the birth rate, is it worth the cost, which policy lever works best, and almost never concerns the goal itself. The premise that a society ought to be raising its own fertility is typically not defended; it is assumed, and the entire debate is conducted downstream of the assumption. This is the existence bias operating at the scale of the state. The continued reproduction of the population is treated as a self-evident good, so obviously worth pursuing that the only remaining questions are technical. But "should a society try to increase its own birth rate at all?" is a substantive question with substantive arguments on multiple sides, and the striking thing is how rarely it is even posed. The existence bias supplies the missing premise silently, and a public conversation that could have interrogated the end instead confines itself to the means. Whatever one's own view on the merits, the structure is diagnostic: an entire policy field built atop a premise that is presupposed rather than argued. Illustration three: the demographic-decline panic and the language of catastrophe. Falling birth rates are routinely reported in the register of emergency: "crisis," "winter," "collapse," "timebomb" (Books 051, 057, and 069 examine the rhetoric directly). What makes this an illustration of the existence bias, rather than simply a policy disagreement, is the automaticity of the alarm. The mere fact that the number of births is declining from its historical norm is treated as self-evidently bad, requiring no argument, because the historical norm, the existing, longstanding pattern of continuous replacement, is coded as the way things ought to be. Deviation from the existing pattern is experienced as deterioration by definition. Yet whether a given demographic trajectory is good or bad depends on a host of contestable premises about economics, ecology, welfare, and value, none of which the panic pauses to examine. The existence bias converts a change from the status quo directly into a catastrophe, skipping the argument that would be needed to establish that the change is actually bad. And the same commentators are rarely troubled by the far larger historical increase in human numbers, which is a comparable deviation from earlier norms in the opposite direction, a selectivity that reveals the operative principle is not "stability is good" but "the recent existing order is good," which is the existence bias exactly. Illustration four: the family photograph and the naturalized order. Consider a smaller, domestic instance, because the existence bias operates at the scale of the mantelpiece as surely as at the scale of the state. In most homes, the visual record on display, framed photographs, albums, the images chosen for walls, is overwhelmingly a record of the reproductive family: weddings, births, children at successive ages, generations arranged in descent. This is not sinister; it is love, and the book means no criticism of it. But consider its effect as an environment. A person raised amid such a record grows up inside a continuous visual argument that this is what a life is, what a family is, what the passage of time is for, an argument made not in propositions, which could be examined, but in images, which are simply absorbed. The existence bias and mere exposure work here in concert: the reproductive life is the one that has always been documented, and being the one documented, it is the one encountered, and being the one encountered, it acquires the warmth that familiarity confers. The lives not on the mantelpiece, the sibling who did not marry, the aunt who had no children, the roads not taken, leave no comparable visual trace, and so the record silently confirms that the reproductive path is the real substance of a family's story and the rest is background. No one decided this; it is simply what accumulates when the existing practice is the one that gets photographed. The illustration matters because it shows the bias reproducing itself through the most ordinary and well-meant of materials, entirely beneath the level at which anyone could be said to be making a claim, let alone defending one. Illustration five: the experimental decoupling in the laboratory. The final illustration is not from the reproductive domain at all, and that is the point. Recall the general existence-bias experiments described in Chapter 5, in which participants rated an object, a course requirement, a form of treatment, an arbitrary object, as better and more right when told it had existed longer, with nothing else changed. This is the mechanism in its purest, most controlled form, stripped of everything specific to reproduction. It matters as an illustration precisely because it cannot be explained by any special feature of childbearing: there is no evolutionary drive toward a tree, no sacred injunction to prefer an old college requirement, no economic asset in an arbitrary object. The favorable judgment tracks duration as such. When we then observe the same pattern of reasoning around reproduction, older is better, existing is right, deviation requires justification, we are entitled to suspect that at least part of what we are seeing is the same domain-general mechanism, dressed in the local costume of family and birth. The laboratory case is the control condition for all the others: it shows the mechanism operating where none of reproduction's special pleadings apply, which is what licenses the inference that the mechanism is also operating, alongside whatever else is true, in the reproductive case. Read together, these illustrations trace the existence bias from the smallest scale to the largest and back to its experimental core: from the raised eyebrow at a childless dinner guest, through the mantelpiece and the structure of national policy, to the vocabulary of demographic catastrophe, and finally to the controlled laboratory demonstration that anchors the whole account. In each, the diagnostic signature is the same: the existing arrangement is exempted from the demand for justification, and that demand is redirected onto whoever would depart from it. The illustrations do not, by themselves, establish that reproduction is not good; nothing in this book aims to establish that. What they establish is that the feeling of its self-evident goodness behaves exactly as the existence bias predicts it would, arising automatically, resisting examination, inverting burdens of proof, and treating deviation as decline, whether or not the underlying practice is, on the merits, as good as it feels. And that gap, between how good it feels and how good it has been shown to be, is the whole subject of the book. --- --- Check Yourself — Chapter 9 Quiz. 1. The existence bias is one strand in a dense weave of mutually reinforcing tendencies, and part of what gives the pronatalist intuition its remarkable resilience is that when one strand is pulled loose, several others hold. This chapter maps how the existence bias interlocks with its neighbors in this series, taking care to mark the boundaries so that this book covers its own territory and no more. The aim is to show the reader how the biases combine, because it is the combination, not any single mechanism, that makes the intuition feel like bedrock, and because seeing the seams is the first step to working any one of them loose. Default bias (Book 001): the pre-selected option. The closest neighbor, and the one most easily confused with this book's subject. Default bias concerns inertia, the tendency to stay on whatever option requires no action. Reproduction is, for many, the pre-selected life path, the thing that happens unless one actively decides otherwise. Existence bias concerns something adjacent but distinct: not the ease of the option but the felt legitimacy conferred by its being and its age. They compound powerfully, since reproduction is both the default and the ancient existing practice, but they are separable, and separating them clarifies the remedy. Against default bias, the corrective is to convert a non-choice into an active choice, to make the person actually decide rather than drift. Against existence bias, the corrective is different: it is to strip the option of its unearned aura of goodness, so that when the person does actively choose, they are not choosing under the illusion that antiquity has already settled the matter. A person could defeat their default bias, genuinely, effortfully decide, while still laboring under existence bias, deciding "yes" partly because the practice's sheer pastness made "yes" feel self-evidently right. Both corrections are needed, and this book supplies only the second. The naturalistic fallacy (Book 015): from natural to good. The other close cousin. The naturalistic fallacy slides from natural to good; existence bias slides from existing to good. Reproduction triggers both at once, since it is both natural and ancient, and the two fallacies are so frequently braided together in ordinary speech ("it's natural, it's what we've always done") that they can seem like one. Keeping them distinct matters because they have different scopes and different counters. Many things exist and have long existed that are not natural at all, money, writing, the state, and the existence bias attaches to these just as firmly, which shows the existence mechanism is not reducible to the nature mechanism. This book handles the existing-therefore-good move and leaves the natural-therefore-good move to Book 015; where they braid, this book notes the braid but does not unpick the other strand. Hume's is–ought gap sits behind both, the common philosophical root from which each fallacy grows a different branch. Terror management and symbolic immortality (Book 012). System justification, central to this book, has a sibling in terror management theory, which holds that awareness of our own mortality generates anxiety we manage through symbolic structures that promise a kind of permanence, and children are among the most potent such structures, a way of continuing past one's own death. This connects to existence bias through the motivational undercurrent both share: a hunger for the reassurance of an order that endures. Where terror management explains part of why we want the reproductive project to be good and necessary (it defends us against oblivion), existence bias explains how the wanting gets rationalized into a judgment (the practice's antiquity supplies the alibi). Book 012 owns the mortality-anxiety mechanism; this book borrows only the observation that the desire for an enduring order supplies heat to the existence bias's light. System-justification's political cousins (Books 085, 086, 089). In the philosophical part of this series, several biases are close analytic relatives of the existence bias's system-justifying core. Book 085 examines the "baseline trick," naturalizing the status quo as though it were the neutral ethical default, which is the existence bias translated into moral philosophy. Book 086 examines the reversal of the burden of proof, the demand that the childfree justify themselves while reproduction goes unquestioned, which is the social face of the same asymmetry (illustrated in Chapter 9 above). Book 089 examines selective invocation of future generations, called upon only when they support more births. These share with the existence bias the structural move of exempting the existing pronatalist order from the scrutiny applied to any departure from it. This book covers the psychological mechanism, the felt legitimacy of what exists; those books cover its argumentative deployments in ethics and rhetoric. The division of labor is deliberate, and readers wanting the philosophical machinery should turn there. The perception-and-testimony family (Books 003, 007, 008, 009). A cluster of biases governs how we read the evidence about parenthood, and each compounds with the existence bias by ensuring that the visible, remembered, and reported instances of reproduction are systematically rosier than the reality. Optimism bias (Book 003) inflates expected joys and deflates expected costs. The availability heuristic (Book 007) lets vivid happy-family images crowd out the harder data. Survivorship bias (Book 008) means we hear mostly from those glad they reproduced. Hedonic adaptation (Book 009) obscures how quickly both the joys and the burdens normalize. The existence bias sits beneath all of these: it supplies the prior presumption of goodness that the perceptual biases then confirm with a curated stream of evidence. The relationship is symbiotic: existence bias tells us the practice is good, and the perceptual biases arrange for us to keep encountering the evidence that says so, while filtering out the evidence that does not. This book does not re-examine those perceptual mechanisms in detail; it notes that they form the evidential machinery through which the existence bias's presumption is perpetually re-confirmed, so that the loop never has to open. The completeness and lack cluster (Books 038, 040, 090). Finally, a family of framings casts the non-reproductive life as incomplete: the empty house (Book 038), the completeness narrative (Book 040), the equivocation that slides from "life needs meaning" to "meaning needs children" (Book 090). These connect to the existence bias through the mechanism described in Chapter 4: because the existing, populated, child-centered life is the implicit measure of a full life, everything else is scored as its shortfall. The existence bias establishes the reproductive life as the standard; the completeness cluster then applies the standard, reading any departure as deficiency. This book establishes the standard-setting move and leaves the detailed anatomy of the "lack" framings to those volumes. The lesson of this map is not that the existence bias is more important than its neighbors; it is that no single bias, this one included, does the whole job, and that the pronatalist intuition owes its strength to redundancy. Pull the default bias and the existence bias still holds; answer the existence bias and the optimism bias still inflates the picture; correct the optimism bias and the completeness narrative still frames the corrected picture as lack. This redundancy is why the intuition feels like bedrock and why no single book in this series claims to dislodge it alone. Each book loosens one strand. This one loosens the strand that runs: it exists, it has always existed, therefore it is good and necessary. Loosening that strand does not unravel the weave. But it is one fewer thread holding a conclusion in place that deserves, whatever it turns out to be, to be reached by reasoning rather than by inheritance. --- --- Check Yourself — Chapter 10 Quiz. 1. Seeing past it To criticize a bias is easy; to say what clear reasoning looks like once the bias is named is harder, and it is the part most likely to slide, unnoticed, into prescription. This chapter tries to describe the corrected instrument without dictating what should be measured with it. Everything that follows is method, not conclusion. The test of whether the reader has taken the book's point is whether these tools feel equally available to someone who ends up wanting children and someone who does not, because if they only serve one answer, they are not tools for clear thinking but arguments in disguise, and the book will have betrayed its own thesis. For the individual: separate the two questions, always. The single most useful discipline is the one named at the very start: hold apart does this exist and has it existed a long time? from is it therefore good, and good for me? The first question, for reproduction, is trivially answered yes; the second is genuinely open and personal. A person reasoning clearly notices the moment the first answer is being quietly used to settle the second, the moment "people have always had children" is functioning as though it were a reason, and declines the substitution. This is not cynicism about tradition; it is insistence that whatever real reasons exist be allowed to speak for themselves. If the reasons for reproducing, or for not, are good, they lose nothing by being examined; only a conclusion that cannot survive examination needs the existence bias to protect it. Ask what the choice would look like stripped of its aura. A practical exercise: imagine that reproduction were a new option, invented last year, with exactly its actual features, the joys, the labor, the irreversibility, the creation of a new person, the decades of care, the costs and the meanings, but none of its antiquity, ubiquity, or sacred weight. Would you choose it, on its merits, for your life? The point of the exercise is not that reproduction is new, since it obviously is not, but that the thought-experiment quarantines the existence bias so the merits can be seen on their own. Some people, running it, will find that the practice looks just as compelling stripped of its aura as clothed in it; their "yes" was not resting on the bias, and they can hold it with a cleaner conscience. Others will find that a good deal of the practice's felt necessity evaporates when its antiquity is bracketed, which tells them something worth knowing about the sources of their own inclination. The exercise dictates no answer. It only reveals how much of the answer was the bias. Notice the burden of proof and refuse its inversion. Chapter 9 described how the existence bias inverts the burden of proof, demanding justification from the reversible, low-stakes option and waiving it for the irreversible, high-stakes one. Seeing past the bias means restoring the burden to where consequence puts it: the choice that creates a new person, reshapes a life for decades, and cannot be undone is the choice that most deserves articulated reasons, not as an obstacle to it, but as the ordinary due care any grave decision warrants. This is emphatically not a demand that prospective parents justify themselves to anyone else; it is an invitation to justify the choice to oneself, with the seriousness it merits, in either direction. A person who declines to reproduce owes an account only to themselves as well. The corrective is symmetry: both choices are choices, both deserve reasons, and neither is exempt. Watch for the tells of the bias in your own speech and thought. Certain phrases are reliable markers that the existence bias is doing work that argument should be doing: it's just what people do; it's natural; it's always been this way; can you imagine if everyone stopped; that's just life. None of these is an argument; each is the aura standing in for one. Noticing them, in others, and more importantly in oneself, is a practical skill. When one catches oneself reaching for "it's just what people do," the useful next move is to ask: and if it weren't, would the thing still be good? If yes, name the reason it is good and lean on that instead. If no, one has learned that the practice was being supported by its own persistence and nothing else, which is precisely the case the existence bias exists to conceal. For institutions: put the end back on the table. At the collective level, seeing past the existence bias means allowing the goals of reproductive policy to be examined, not only the means. When a society debates how to raise its birth rate, a clear-eyed politics first asks whether it should be trying to, and requires that the answer be argued rather than presupposed. This does not predetermine the answer, there may be strong arguments for a given fertility goal, but it insists the arguments be made, in public, and be open to reply. The same discipline applies to demographic reporting: a change from the historical norm should be described as a change, and the case that the change is bad should be made explicitly, rather than smuggled in by the loaded vocabulary of crisis and winter. Institutions cannot easily rid themselves of a bias, but they can build in the habit of stating premises that would otherwise stay buried, and a premise stated is a premise that can be contested. Distinguish confidence from clarity. One caution belongs here, because seeing past a bias can produce a false sense of having transcended it. Recognizing the existence bias does not make a person immune to it; the mechanisms are deep, pre-rational, and continuously reinforced by an environment that will not stop supplying them. The realistic goal is not to eliminate the bias, since no one can, but to develop the habit of noticing it, the way one learns to recognize an optical illusion without ceasing to see it. The two lines that still look unequal even after one has measured them are a fair model: the bias remains, but its authority is broken, because one no longer mistakes the appearance for a fact. A person who believes they have fully escaped the existence bias has most likely just relocated it, perhaps into a new certainty that clear thinkers reach some particular conclusion, which is the bias's mirror image. Clarity, in the sense this book means, is not a destination one arrives at and then possesses; it is an ongoing practice of catching the bias in the act, again and again, in a mind and a culture that will keep producing it. The modesty of that goal is not a weakness of the account. It is the account being honest about what naming a bias can and cannot do. For institutions: design for genuine choice, not for a preferred outcome. An institution serious about non-coercion would arrange defaults, information, and support so that reproducing and not reproducing are both live, visible, and materially feasible options, rather than arranging the world so that one is the frictionless path and the other a swim upstream. This is not the same as promoting non-reproduction, which would be the mirror-image bias; it is the removal of the thumb from the scale in either direction. A society passes this test not when its birth rate hits some target but when its members can reason about reproduction freely, with the ledger's costs visible, the burden of proof placed by consequence rather than convention, and neither answer treated as the one that needs no explanation. What clear reasoning looks like, then, is not a particular decision but a particular quality of deciding: the two questions held apart, the aura bracketed so the merits can be seen, the burden of proof restored to where consequence puts it, the tell-tale phrases noticed and cashed out into real reasons or discarded, and, at the level of institutions, the ends put back on the table beside the means. None of this tells anyone what to choose. All of it makes the choosing more genuinely a choice. On one side is a fact: reproduction exists, has existed for as long as there have been humans, is nearly ubiquitous, and is woven into the deepest structures of language, law, and life. What the book has argued, it has tried to argue proportionately. It does not claim that the persistence of reproduction is worthless as evidence; Chapter 2 granted, sincerely, that long survival can carry real if defeasible weight, and that a wise person attends to it. It does not claim that reproduction is bad, or that anyone should decline it, or that the cross-cultural testimony of meaning is illusion. It does not claim that the strength of the bias in the specific domain of reproduction has been precisely measured; Chapter 5 was explicit that this is a strong inference from well-established general mechanisms rather than a directly demonstrated result, and that the honest word for the difference is uncertainty. The book's actual claim is narrower and, the author hopes, harder to dislodge: that the existence and antiquity of reproduction generate a powerful feeling of self-evident goodness that outruns the evidence and functions to foreclose examination of a genuinely open, genuinely consequential, and deeply personal choice. What remains contested is real and should be stated without spin. Reasonable people disagree about how much evidential weight the persistence of a practice deserves; the Burkean case that it deserves quite a lot is serious and was given its due. Reasonable people disagree about how strongly the bias actually operates in reproduction as opposed to other domains, and the direct evidence is thinner than one would like. Reasonable people disagree about whether the costs the bias hides, the opportunity costs, the ecological externalities, the welfare of the pressured, are as weighty as Chapter 6 suggested, and about how they net out against the goods of parenthood that the book has taken care not to deny. Reasonable people will disagree, too, about the decision-theoretic objection of Chapter 8, whether the pro-persistence prior is rational enough, often enough, that calling the tendency a "bias" overstates the case; the book's answer, that the bias lies not in the prior but in the foreclosure of the specific update, is defensible but not beyond dispute. And reasonable people will disagree about whether a book like this, whatever its stated intentions, can avoid being read as pressure in the opposite direction. These are not loose ends the book has failed to tie; they are the honest boundaries of what can be claimed, and marking them is part of the argument rather than a retreat from it. The measured takeaway is correspondingly modest. Naming the existence bias does not tell anyone whether to have children. It changes the character of the question, from one that feels answered before it is asked into one that is genuinely open. A person who, having seen the bias clearly, still chooses to reproduce has done everything the book asks; so has a person who, having seen it, chooses not to. What the book asks is not a particular answer but that the answer be reached by reasoning that the person can recognize as their own, with the aura of antiquity set aside, the hidden costs restored to the ledger, the burden of proof placed where consequence puts it, and the felt obviousness of the whole thing recognized for what it is: not a perception of goodness, but a judgment produced by a mind with a known and describable bias. There is no triumph in this conclusion, and there should not be. The existence bias is not a villain; it is a shortcut that has served our species tolerably well across a long history in which "keep doing what has kept us going" was rarely bad advice. Its costs become visible mainly now, in a world where mortality is low, old age is socially insured, the ecological accounts are strained, and the demographic norm the bias was calibrated to is itself changing, a world, in short, where the shortcut increasingly runs off the map it was drawn for. In such a world the most useful thing one can do with an old and honorable heuristic is not to denounce it but to see it: to know it is operating, to know what it conceals, and to keep one's hand on the wheel where it would prefer to steer for us. The practice of reproduction may well deserve much of the honor it receives. ① In brief

100 Biases of Natalism — Book 002

There is a quiet inference beneath much of our thinking about children: families have always formed, people have always reproduced, therefore having children is good, right, and more or less obligatory. The premise is a fact about what exists; the conclusion is a judgment about what ought to be. The slide between them feels seamless. It is not. This book is about that slide.

In psychology it has a name, the existence bias, the documented tendency, associated with the work of Scott Eidelman, Christian Crandall, and colleagues, to judge that whatever exists is for that reason good, and that whatever has existed longer is better still. In controlled experiments, people rate the same object, policy, or practice more favorably when told it has been around longer, with nothing else changed. Existence and duration function as a silent, unearned argument for worth.

Read the rest of the overview

Reproduction is an unusually strong magnet for this bias. Nothing has existed longer than childbearing; nothing is more ubiquitous; nothing is more heavily built into language, law, ritual, and the physical world. Three mechanisms compound in the reproductive case: the existence bias itself, Robert Zajonc's mere-exposure effect (familiarity breeds liking), and the system justification theory of John Jost and Mahzarin Banaji (we are motivated to defend the existing social order as legitimate). Because reproduction is the mechanism by which the whole social system perpetuates itself, all three point the same way, producing an intuition of goodness so strong it feels less like a judgment than like a perception.

The book's stance is critical of the bias, never of families, parents, or children, and it argues for no particular reproductive choice. Its thesis is narrow: the existence and antiquity of reproduction generate a feeling of self-evident goodness that outruns the evidence and forecloses examination of a genuinely open, consequential, and personal choice. A practice can be good and be shielded from scrutiny by a bias; the two are compatible. The point is only to let the real reasons, whatever they are, do the work, rather than letting the aura of antiquity do it for them. Hume's is–ought gap is the philosophical spine; the existence bias is the psychological machinery by which we cross that gap without noticing.

Following the series template, the book steelmans the natalist case first and at full strength, including the serious Burkean and Hayekian argument that long-surviving practices encode tacit wisdom, and a decision-theoretic argument that a mild pro-persistence prior may be rational. It then traces the bias's overdetermined origins (cognitive, evolutionary, religious, economic, institutional, and developmental), its everyday mechanisms (linguistic presupposition, the life-script narrative, built infrastructure, saturating imagery, and conversational policing), and the actual evidence, stating candidly that the general mechanism is well established while its precise strength in reproduction is a strong inference rather than a directly measured result.

Later chapters examine the hidden costs the bias obscures (to individual reasoning, autonomy, welfare, and public deliberation), the uneven distribution of its pressure (across gender, class, community, ability, and age), and the strongest objections to the book's own thesis, conceding real ground where the objections land. It maps how the existence bias interlocks with neighboring biases in the series, distinguishing itself carefully from default bias (Book 001) and the naturalistic fallacy (Book 015). It closes with what clear, non-coerced reasoning looks like once the bias is named, for individuals and institutions, and a deliberately modest conclusion that declines to say whether anyone should have children.

The aim is not a verdict but a cleaner instrument: to convert a felt perception back into an examinable judgment, so that whatever is chosen is chosen with the full ledger in view.

② The book in twelve chapters

Each chapter opens with its own summary. Note chapter 2: every volume states the strongest case for the thing it criticises before laying a finger on it — and chapter 8 turns the critique back on itself.

CH 1Naming the bias

Begin with a simple test. Suppose you are told that a particular practice, it does not matter which, has been performed by human beings for as long as there have been human beings, that nearly everyone you know participates in it, and that it is woven into the law, the calendar, the language, and the architecture of your society. Before you know anything else about it, how good does it seem? For most people, the honest answer is: it seems good. Not proven good, not examined and found good, but presumptively good, in the way a well-worn path through a field seems trustworthy because so many feet have worn it. The presumption arrives before the evidence. That presumption is the existence bias.

CH 2The strongest case for it (steelman)The strongest case against this book

A critique of a bias is worth nothing if it only ever attacks the bias in its dumbest form. The existence bias, applied to reproduction, has a foolish version, "we've always done it, so shut up," that no one needs help refuting. It also has a serious version, and intellectual honesty requires meeting that one first, on its strongest ground, before laying a finger on it. This chapter tries to state the natalist case for taking existence and duration seriously in as good a form as it can be made. The reader should finish the chapter feeling the pull of the argument. If the later critique is going to be worth anything, it has to earn its way past a case this strong.

CH 3Where it comes from

A bias this deep and this widely shared is unlikely to have a single origin. The existence bias in reproduction is better understood as overdetermined: several independent forces, evolutionary, cognitive, religious, economic, and institutional, each push in the same direction, and their convergence is what makes the resulting intuition feel so much like bedrock. This chapter traces the main tributaries. The aim is not to explain the bias away, since showing where an intuition comes from does not show that it is false, but to make visible that the intuition has causes other than the truth of its conclusion. A belief that would be held just as firmly whether or not it were true is a belief we should hold more loosely.

CH 4How it operates today

Biases do not float free; they act through concrete channels, through the words people use, the questions they ask, the defaults built into forms and institutions, the images that saturate the environment. This chapter examines the everyday machinery by which the existence bias in reproduction reproduces itself, mostly beneath the threshold of anyone's notice. The recurring pattern is this: at no point does anyone actually argue that reproduction is good. The goodness is presupposed, built into the structure of the interaction so thoroughly that to question it registers not as disagreement but as a kind of malfunction, a failure to understand how the world works.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

It is one thing to describe a bias plausibly and another to show it is real. This chapter sets out what the empirical record actually supports, and, just as importantly, what it does not. A book critical of a bias has a special obligation here, because the temptation is to overclaim: to treat the bias as more established, more precisely measured, and more directly proven in the reproductive domain than the evidence warrants. The honest picture is a mix of well-supported general findings and reasonable but under-tested extensions to reproduction specifically. Stating the difference is part of the argument, because a critique that relies on overstated evidence would itself be a kind of bias.

CH 6The hidden costs

Every bias has a price, and the price is paid in the things the bias makes it hard to see. The existence bias is a lens that renders the goodness of reproduction so vivid that the costs of the practice, and, more subtly, the costs of the bias itself, fade into the background. This chapter tries to bring those costs into focus. A distinction structures it: there are the costs the bias hides about reproduction, and there is the cost of holding the bias, of reasoning about a life-defining choice with a distorted instrument. Both matter, and they are different.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

Biases are not distributed evenly. The existence bias in reproduction presents itself as universal, the way things simply are, for everyone, but its pressure falls with very different weight on different people, and the appearance of universality is part of how it works. A pressure that seems to come from nowhere in particular, that seems to be just the shape of reality, is harder to resist than one with a visible source. This chapter maps the uneven incidence of the bias: who is most subject to it, who can most easily escape it, and how the "everyone does this" framing conceals a distribution that is anything but even.

CH 8The counter-arguments to the critiqueObjections to the critique

A book that only presented objections to its opponents and none to itself would be committing, at the level of argument, the very sin it accuses the existence bias of: shielding a conclusion from scrutiny. This chapter turns the critical instrument on the book's own thesis. Each objection below is a real challenge, stated as strongly as the author can state it, and each is answered honestly, which sometimes means conceding ground. The reader who finishes this chapter thinking the book's thesis has survived should think so because it was genuinely tested, not because the tests were rigged.

CH 9Case studies / illustrations

Argument in the abstract can float free of the world it describes. This chapter grounds the existence bias in concrete instances, some historical, some contemporary, where the machinery described in earlier chapters can be watched in operation. None of these cases is offered as decisive proof; each is an illustration, meant to make the mechanism visible and recognizable. The reader is invited to test the framework against them and to notice, in each, the signature move: the goodness or necessity of the existing arrangement is treated as beyond question because it exists, and the demand for justification is redirected onto whoever proposes to depart from it.

CH 10Adjacent biases

No bias operates alone. The existence bias is one strand in a dense weave of mutually reinforcing tendencies, and part of what gives the pronatalist intuition its remarkable resilience is that when one strand is pulled loose, several others hold. This chapter maps how the existence bias interlocks with its neighbors in this series, taking care to mark the boundaries so that this book covers its own territory and no more. The aim is to show the reader how the biases combine, because it is the combination, not any single mechanism, that makes the intuition feel like bedrock, and because seeing the seams is the first step to working any one of them loose.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

To criticize a bias is easy; to say what clear reasoning looks like once the bias is named is harder, and it is the part most likely to slide, unnoticed, into prescription. This chapter tries to describe the corrected instrument without dictating what should be measured with it. Everything that follows is method, not conclusion. The test of whether the reader has taken the book's point is whether these tools feel equally available to someone who ends up wanting children and someone who does not, because if they only serve one answer, they are not tools for clear thinking but arguments in disguise, and the book will have betrayed its own thesis.
