② Build the argument — and try to break it

Accept or reject each premise. The conclusion updates to show where you land. No setting is “correct” — the point is to feel which premises carry the weight.

P1. The presence of pain is bad.
P2. The presence of pleasure is good.
P3. The absence of pain is good, even with no one to enjoy it.
P4. The absence of pleasure is not bad, unless someone is deprived.

④ The non-identity puzzle

Why is this genuinely hard, and not obviously settled either way? Here is the knot underneath every position in the debate.

A couple can conceive now — the child will have a hard but worthwhile life — or wait, and have a different child with an easier one. If they conceive now, have they harmed that child?

Yes — that child would have been better off if they’d waited
No — its only alternative was never existing, and its life is worth living
The question is confused — “better off” needs someone to compare

⑤ Where the arguments are vulnerable

A foundation you have never stress-tested is not one you can build on. Chapter Eight gathers the open problems into one place, with no closing reassurance. Tap each to read it honestly.

In the group: most fights start when someone defends the grand claim (“every life is bad,” “the living would be better off dead”) that the book itself sets aside. What survives the attack is the modest, sturdier claim: procreation is a morally serious act, not a weightless default. Defend that, and you are on ground the book has held.

Key takeaway

The grandest version of the thesis is not the one that survives. What survives every chapter of attack is smaller and sturdier: procreation is a morally serious act — an unconsented imposition answering no prior need — not the obvious, weightless default it is nearly always assumed to be. That assumption does not survive contact with this book, and its destruction is enough to require everything the next nine disciplines examine.

From The Asymmetry (Book 1 of the Antinatalist Education). Education and reflection, not medical, legal, financial, or mental-health advice. The position is universal in its reasoning and voluntary in its application, and concerns starting lives, never ending them.
