BIAS 015

It's Only Natural: The Naturalistic Fallacy of Childbearing

Part II · Evolutionary & Biological Framings · The Naturalistic Fallacy of Childbearing

The bias: Sliding from "reproduction is natural" to "reproduction is good and obligatory."

The word Nature has two principal meanings: it either denotes the entire system of things... or it denotes things as they would be, apart from human intervention. In the first sense... it is unmeaning to enjoin us to conform to it. In the second sense... the doctrine that man ought to follow nature is equally irrational and immoral.

① In brief

100 Biases of Natalism — Book 015 · Opens Part II: Evolutionary & Biological Framings

Few phrases end a conversation about children as quietly as "it's only natural." This book isolates the move hidden inside that phrase: the inference from reproduction is natural to reproduction is good, right, and obligatory. Its single thesis is that this inference does not go through. Naturalness is a descriptive property; goodness is an evaluative one; and no purely descriptive premise entails an evaluative conclusion without a further, unstated value premise, which the appeal to nature characteristically smuggles in rather than defends.

The book is philosophically careful and deliberately modest. It keeps three related-but-distinct tools apart: David Hume's is–ought gap (evaluative conclusions cannot be derived from facts alone); G. E. Moore's technical "naturalistic fallacy" (the error of defining goodness in natural terms, defended by the open-question argument); and the everyday "appeal to nature." Popular criticism tends to collapse these into one club; this book refuses to, and does not claim more of Moore than Moore established. Its core rests only on the widely shared observation that a description does not by itself entail a value.

Read the rest of the overview

Because it opens Part II, the volume is a keystone. Later books apply the same inference pattern to specific materials, the "maternal instinct" (017), selfish-gene teleology (018), the survival of the species, and inherit the tool sharpened here. The book also distinguishes its target from the existence bias (002, from exists to good) and the broader worship of the natural (016).

Chapter 2 gives the natalist side its strongest hearing first, and does so generously. The steelman includes the wisdom-of-nature argument (evolved dispositions encode tested wisdom, so naturalness is a strong prior, not a proof), the teleological-goods tradition of natural-law and virtue ethics (flourishing consists in fulfilling one's natural ends, so goodness is not inferred from naturalness but partly constituted by it), the functional-fit argument (our psychology really is organized around reproduction, and fit with our own nature plausibly aids well-being), and the prudential-humility argument (defer to deep defaults given our fallibility). These are the real reasons thoughtful people have in mind, not a straw fallacy, and the book engages them.

Subsequent chapters trace the bias's evolutionary, religious, economic, institutional, and philosophical origins; show how it operates today through honorific vocabulary, the design idiom ("what your body is for"), default-and-script framing, and the "why not?" asymmetry; weigh the evidence (the descriptive premise is well supported; naturalness is a weak and unreliable proxy for welfare, since evolution optimizes reproductive success, not happiness or morality); and count the hidden costs, chiefly that the framing forecloses the actual question, converts a genuine choice into a non-choice, and lands unequally.

The distributional chapter shows the "natural" reproductive duty proclaimed universally but enforced selectively: hardest on women, on those without resources, on communities that police reproduction, and on those whose bodies or families do not fit the assumed template. The book then fairly presents objections to its own thesis, above all that the fact–value gap is itself contested, and answers them narrowly rather than overclaiming.

The takeaway is non-coercive and precise. The critique is not that childbearing is bad, that natural inclinations are untrustworthy, or that anyone who wants children has been fooled. It is that "it's only natural" is not by itself a reason; it names where a reason is needed. Removing the bias pushes no one toward or away from children; it only lets the choice be a reasoned, un-coerced one. The transferable skill: whenever nature is invoked as an argument, supply the missing premise aloud, natural, and therefore \_\_\_, because \_\_\_, and see whether anything plausible fills the blank.

② The book in twelve chapters

Each chapter opens with its own summary. Note chapter 2: every volume states the strongest case for the thing it criticises before laying a finger on it — and chapter 8 turns the critique back on itself.

CH 1Naming the bias

The bias this book examines is the naturalistic bias in reproduction: the tendency to treat the naturalness of childbearing as though it settled the value of childbearing, as though showing that reproduction is biologically typical, evolutionarily favored, or bodily "designed for" thereby showed that it is good, right, and something one ought to do.

CH 2The strongest case for it (steelman)The strongest case against this book

The appeal to nature is easy to caricature, and caricaturing it would be a disservice. There is a serious position here, several in fact, and they deserve to be stated in their most compelling form before any objection is raised. A critique that only ever engages the crude bumper-sticker version — "natural, therefore good, full stop" — wins cheaply and teaches nothing, because no reflective person actually holds the bumper-sticker version. The interesting positions are the ones a thoughtful defender would retreat to under pressure, and it is those we set out here at their strongest.

CH 3Where it comes from

The naturalistic bias in reproduction has deep and overdetermined roots. No single origin explains it; several reinforce one another. This overdetermination matters, because a bias with one cause might be dislodged by removing that cause, whereas a bias supported from five directions at once will feel, to the person who holds it, simply like seeing reality clearly. When evolution, religion, economics, politics, and inherited philosophy all point the same way, the resulting conviction does not present itself as a conclusion one has reached but as a fact one has noticed. Tracing the sources is therefore part of loosening the grip: it lets us see the intuition as produced rather than merely given.

CH 4How it operates today

In contemporary life the naturalistic bias rarely appears as a formal syllogism. It works through language, defaults, and framing, often below the level of explicit argument. This matters for how one fights it. A bias that announced itself as an argument could be met with a counter-argument; a bias that lives in vocabulary and defaults has to be met by noticing, by developing an ear for the moment when a description has quietly turned into a recommendation. The examples below are meant to train that ear.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

What can evidence even contribute to a book about an inference? A logical gap is not an empirical finding; you cannot run a study to show that ought does not follow from is. But several bodies of evidence bear on the surrounding claims that give the naturalistic bias its force, and clarity here matters as much as data. In particular, evidence is decisive against the strongest form of the natalist case, the wisdom-of-nature argument, which makes an empirical wager: that naturalness reliably tracks what is good for us. That wager can be tested, and the results discipline how much weight the prior can bear.

CH 6The hidden costs

If the inference were merely invalid but inert, it would be a curiosity for logic textbooks. It is worth a book because leaving it unexamined has real costs. These costs are not evenly visible; some fall on the quality of our reasoning, some on individuals making the most consequential decision of their lives, and some on whole groups who bear the "natural" duty disproportionately. Cataloguing them is not a way of arguing against reproduction — a life-choice cannot be refuted by listing the costs of a bad argument for it — but a way of showing why an apparently harmless figure of speech is worth the effort of dismantling.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

Biases are not felt equally. The naturalistic framing of reproduction presses hardest on some people and lightly on others, and the pattern is not random. Tracing who bears the weight is not a change of subject from the logical critique; it is a deepening of it. An invalid inference that pressed on everyone equally would be an intellectual nuisance. An invalid inference that is proclaimed universally but enforced selectively is something more: a mechanism by which a logical error is put to work sorting people, assigning burdens, and marking some lives as deficient. The distribution is part of what the bias is.

CH 8The counter-arguments to the critiqueObjections to the critique

A book that steelmanned the natalist case in Chapter 2 owes the same fairness to the objections against its own thesis. Several are serious. Taking them seriously is not a rhetorical courtesy; it is what keeps the critique honest and, frankly, what keeps it accurate. Each objection below identifies a way the critique could overreach, and the right response in each case is not to swat the objection away but to let it discipline the claim — to concede exactly as much as the objection is owed and to locate precisely what survives.

CH 9Case studies / illustrations

The design idiom in everyday speech. Consider the common remark, offered to someone unsure about children: "Well, it's what your body is for." The sentence has the grammar of a fact and the force of a command. Its factual content, that the reproductive system has reproduction as a biological function, is unobjectionable. But the intended payload is normative: therefore you have reason, even an obligation, to use it so. Laid bare, the leap is obvious; a kidney has a function too, and no one feels a duty to their kidney's telos. The example shows the fallacy at its most ordinary and most invisible, doing evaluative work while wearing the costume of biology. Push the parallel and it only sharpens. The reproductive system's "purpose" is a fact of the same kind as the purpose of the sweat glands, the startle reflex, or the capacity to form scar tissue — each selected because it did something useful for survival or reproduction. We feel no obligation to sweat as much as possible, to be startled as often as we can, or to maximize our scarring, because we correctly treat these as capacities we may use or not as circumstances warrant. The reproductive case feels different only because a mass of independent cultural and emotional weight has been loaded onto it, not because the underlying "for" is any more command-like. Strip that weight away and "it's what your body is for" is exactly as normative as "your legs are for walking" — which is to say, not at all, until a further reason is added.

CH 10Adjacent biases

The naturalistic bias does not stand alone. It interlocks with several others in this series, and part of seeing it clearly is seeing where it ends and its neighbors begin. Mapping the boundaries is not idle taxonomy. Biases that are allowed to blur into one another become harder to answer, because an objection that defeats one can be deflected by quietly sliding to another; keeping them distinct means each can be met on its own terms.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

What does clear-eyed reasoning about reproduction look like once the naturalistic bias is named? Not a rejection of childbearing, which would repeat the very error in reverse, deriving ought not from some other description. Rather, it looks like restoring the missing premise to view and letting the question be a real question. The temptation to overcorrect is genuine and worth naming at the outset: having seen through "natural, therefore good," a reader might be tempted to conclude "natural, therefore suspect," treating reproductive desire as a trap to be resisted. That would be the same fallacy wearing the opposite coat. The disposition this chapter recommends is not a new verdict but the retirement of a whole class of shortcut, in both directions.

CH 12Conclusion

This book has tried to do one thing carefully: to isolate the inference from reproduction is natural to reproduction is good, right, and obligatory, and to show that the inference, as ordinarily made, does not go through. Naturalness is a descriptive property; goodness is an evaluative one; and no purely descriptive premise entails an evaluative conclusion without a further evaluative premise, which the appeal to nature characteristically leaves unstated. That is the entire, modest core of the critique. It is worth restating how deliberately modest it is. The book has not argued that reproduction is bad, that natural inclinations are untrustworthy, or that the many people who find deep meaning in raising children are deceived. It has argued something far smaller and, for that reason, far harder to evade: that one particular and extremely common route to the pro-reproduction conclusion — the route that runs through the word "natural" — is missing a premise, and that the missing premise is precisely the one that would have to be argued for rather than assumed.

Show all 17 terms as a list
Appeal to nature
The informal inference from something's being natural to its being good, right, or obligatory; the everyday pattern this book examines.
Naturalistic bias in reproduction
The specific tendency to treat the naturalness of childbearing as though it settled its value.
Is–ought gap (Hume's gap)
David Hume's observation (A Treatise of Human Nature, 1739–40) that evaluative conclusions (ought) cannot be derived from purely factual premises (is) without a further evaluative premise.
Naturalistic fallacy (Moore's)
G. E. Moore's technical thesis (Principia Ethica, 1903) that it is a mistake to define goodness in terms of any natural property. Distinct from, and narrower than, the everyday appeal to nature; the book does not conflate them.
Open-question argument
Moore's argument that for any proposed natural definition of "good" (e.g. "the pleasant"), it remains an open, intelligible question whether that thing is in fact good — indicating goodness is not identical to any natural property.
Fact–value distinction
The claimed separation between descriptive and evaluative statements; the hinge of the critique, and itself philosophically contested.
Descriptive premise
A premise stating what is the case (e.g. "reproduction is natural"), carrying no evaluative content on its own.
Evaluative (normative) premise
A premise stating what is good or ought to be done; required to reach any evaluative conclusion.
Design idiom / teleological language
Talk of organs, drives, or bodies as being for an end. Harmless as biological shorthand; misleading when read as a personal instruction or duty.
Wisdom-of-nature argument
The steelman that treats evolved dispositions as encoding tested, distributed wisdom, so that naturalness functions as a strong prior for value rather than a proof of it.
Teleological goods
In natural-law and neo-Aristotelian ethics, the characteristic ends (telē) of a nature, whose fulfilment is held to constitute flourishing (eudaimonia).
Functional-fit argument
The steelman claim that human psychology and social life are really organized around reproduction, so that fit with one's own nature is a plausible ingredient in well-being.
Prudential-humility argument
The steelman claim that, given human fallibility, deferring to deep natural defaults is wiser than trusting each generation's cleverest counter-argument.
Naturalistic honorific
The quietly approving force the word "natural" carries while appearing to be merely descriptive.
Burden-of-proof asymmetry
The framing effect by which the "natural" option (having children) needs no justification while the departure from it must be defended ("why not?").
Fallacy
A reliable pattern of invalid inference that nonetheless feels compelling; naming one describes a shape of reasoning, not a defect in the reasoner.
Overcorrection
The mirror-image error of inferring ought not from some other description; rejecting the appeal to nature does not license anti-natalist conclusions either.

⑥ Sources

What this volume draws on. Where the evidence is thin or contested, the book says so rather than reaching for a number.

  • Hume, David. A Treatise of Human Nature (1739–1740). The is–ought passage appears in Book III, Part I, Section I.
  • Moore, G. E. Principia Ethica (1903). Source of the technical "naturalistic fallacy" and the open-question argument.
  • Mill, John Stuart. "Nature," in Three Essays on Religion (published posthumously, 1874). Mill's critique of "follow nature" as a moral maxim. The source of this volume's epigraph, condensed from Mill's argument.
  • Aristotle. Nicomachean Ethics. Classic source for the doctrine of ends (telē) and flourishing (eudaimonia), background to the teleological-goods steelman.
  • Aquinas, Thomas. Summa Theologiae. Principal source for natural-law ethics and the ends written into human nature.
Directions the volume points to (not pinned citations)
  • Foot, Philippa. Natural Goodness (2001). Neo-Aristotelian attempt to ground value in facts about the human form of life; directly relevant to the strongest objection to the book's thesis.
  • Hursthouse, Rosalind. On Virtue Ethics (1999). Naturalistic virtue ethics; bears on the functional-fit and teleological-goods steelmans.
  • Reference-work entries on "the naturalistic fallacy" and "the appeal to nature" in the standard encyclopedias of philosophy, for the distinction among Hume's gap, Moore's fallacy, and the everyday appeal.
  • Scholarship on the evolutionary explanation of moral intuitions, and on the distinction between adaptiveness and individual welfare.
  • Historical scholarship on the rhetoric of "natural motherhood" in population and pronatalist policy.
