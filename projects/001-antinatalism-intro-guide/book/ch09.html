The Misanthropic Argument

A separate route: the harm a new person will go on to cause others.

"Humans are responsible for the suffering and deaths of billions of other humans and non-human animals."

— David Benatar

So far the antinatalist case has looked inward, toward the person who would be born — asking whether existence is good for them, whether they could consent, whether their life would contain more harm than they would ever admit. David Benatar offers a second argument that turns the camera around. It asks not only what a new person will suffer, but what a new person will do. He calls it the misanthropic argument, and despite its forbidding name it carries no hatred of anyone. This chapter explains why a thinker can press it and still hold human beings, including himself, in something close to tenderness.

Misanthropic argument — the claim that creating a new person is wrong partly because that person will, over a lifetime, become a cause of significant harm to others — to animals, to the environment, and to other humans. The wrong lies in what the new person will inflict, not only in what they will endure.

Two arguments, pointing opposite ways

It helps to set this beside the argument it is most often confused with. The philanthropic argument — the asymmetry and the quality-of-life case from earlier chapters — is moved by love of the future child. Its worry is that we would be placing an innocent into a life of harm for the child's own sake. It is, at heart, protective.

The misanthropic argument runs the other way. Its worry is for everyone the new person will affect. Each human life, lived out in an ordinary modern way, leaves a long wake: animals raised and killed for food, ecosystems drawn down, resources consumed, and a share — sometimes small, sometimes large — in the cruelties humans visit upon one another. The philanthropic argument says do not create a victim. The misanthropic argument says do not create a perpetrator. Benatar's striking move is that both can be true at once, and that each new life sits at the meeting point of the two.

One argument fears creating a victim; the other fears creating, however unwillingly, a cause of harm.

The ordinary, well-meaning footprint

It is essential to see that the argument does not depend on anyone being wicked. The person it describes is decent, ordinary, and trying to live well. The harms accumulate anyway, through ordinary participation in ordinary systems. A typical life in an industrialized society involves the consumption of animals reared in conditions most people would rather not picture; a steady draw on a strained natural world; and an unavoidable entanglement in economies and histories that grind on others somewhere out of sight. Benatar's claim is statistical and structural, not a charge against any individual's character. The point is that to add a person is, predictably, to add another node through which such harms will flow — not because the person chooses harm, but because being human in the present world routes harm through almost everyone.

Notice, too, that the harms here fall mostly on parties who never agreed to bear them: the farmed animal, the future generation inheriting a depleted environment, the distant person at the wrong end of a supply chain. This is what distinguishes the argument from the inward case. The suffering it weighs is suffering done to others, and so it cannot be answered, as the personal case sometimes can, by saying the new person might be glad to be alive.

Why this is not contempt for humanity

The word misanthropic suggests a sneer, and that impression must be cleared away, because it is exactly backward. Benatar is not saying humans are loathsome or that existing people should be despised. The argument is fully compatible with admiring human kindness, art, courage, and love — and Benatar is careful to acknowledge the good humans also do. Its claim is comparative and forward-looking: that the harm a new human life will cause is large enough to count as a serious reason against starting one, given that the alternative — never bringing that person into being — harms no one.

In plain words: The argument says creating a person predictably adds to the harm done in the world — to animals, nature, and other people — and that this counts as a reason not to do it. It does not say people are bad, and it asks nothing of people who already exist except, perhaps, to do less harm.

And like every argument in this book, its conclusion bears only on future births. It implies no judgment whatsoever on the worth of those already here, who have interests, relationships, and a stake in continuing. To refrain from creating a new source of harm is not to wish away the people who currently are one. The misanthropic argument is, if anything, an unusually demanding form of concern for the world the new person would enter.

Objection

This is a one-sided ledger again, and a sterner one. Humans do not only consume and harm; they cure diseases, build shelters, write symphonies, rescue strangers, plant forests, and care for the very animals and people the argument says they injure. To "charge" an unborn child with humanity's collective harms is grotesquely unfair — the child has done nothing, and may grow up to be a vegan conservationist who leaves the world better than she found it. Worse, the argument proves too much: if causing some harm is a reason not to exist, then existing virtuous people should also stop, which no one accepts. And the harms it names are contingent. Factory farming, fossil fuels, and exploitation can be reduced or abolished by reform. The answer to a harmful system is to fix the system, not to stop making people.

Reply

The reply concedes much of this and narrows accordingly. Yes, humans create immense good — but good done for other people mostly benefits beings who would not have needed benefiting had they not been created; it does not obviously cancel harm done to third parties like animals, who gain nothing from the arrangement. The "unfair charge" objection misreads the structure: no one blames the unborn, who are blameless by definition; the reason weighs on the creator's decision, made with foreknowledge of the predictable wake. The "proves too much" worry is blocked by the asymmetry the rest of the book defends — declining to start a life harms no one, whereas asking existing people to end theirs harms them gravely; the cases are not parallel. The contingency point is the strongest, and Benatar grants it real force: insofar as the harms are reformable, the argument's grip loosens exactly as reform succeeds. But reform has been slow, partial, and is itself uncertain — so the argument stands as a weighty reason here and now, even if it is one a better world could someday answer.

Where it leaves us

The misanthropic argument is best understood as a second, independent line of support rather than a replacement for the personal case. One can accept it and reject the asymmetry, or the reverse. Its distinctive contribution is to widen the moral frame: the question of whether to create a person is not only a question about that person's welfare, but about the welfare of everyone and everything that person's existence will touch. Even readers who find it ultimately unpersuasive often grant that it names something true — that a human life is not only a recipient of the world's goods and harms, but a source of them.

Chapter in brief

  • The misanthropic argument is Benatar's second, independent argument: creating a person is wrong partly because of the harm that person will cause others — animals, the environment, other humans.
  • It is the mirror image of the philanthropic argument: that one fears creating a victim, this one fears creating a cause of harm. Both can be true together.
  • The harms accumulate through ordinary participation in ordinary systems; the argument is structural, not a charge against anyone's character.
  • "Misanthropic" does not mean hatred of people. It implies no judgment on existing lives and concerns only future births.
  • The main objection — that humans do great good, that the unborn are blameless, and that the harms are reformable — is partly conceded; the contingency point is the strongest, loosening the argument's grip exactly as reform succeeds.
