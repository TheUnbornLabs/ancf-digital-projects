"The care of human life and happiness is the first and only legitimate object of good government — and, one might add, of a good life."

— after Thomas Jefferson

Suppose the arguments of this book have moved you. Suppose you find the antinatalist case serious, even persuasive — and yet you also feel, deeply, a wish to care for children, to give to the next generation, to spend your life in the company of the young. These two things are not in contradiction, and this chapter is for the many readers who hold both. It is about the paths people discuss when they want to direct their care toward children without creating new ones: adoption, fostering, and the wider work of mentoring, teaching, and caregiving.

A word of caution before we begin, because it shapes everything that follows. None of these paths is a tidy "solution" to the antinatalist question, and none should be pressed on anyone. They are options worth knowing about — not obligations, and not a test of sincerity. A person who finds antinatalism compelling and adopts no one, fosters no one, and simply lives a quiet, kind life has done nothing wrong.

Adoption and fostering: real, and really complicated

Adoption is the path most often raised in antinatalist conversation, and for an understandable reason. There are children already here who need families, and there are people who long to parent. Bringing the two together can be a genuine good — for the child, for the parent, and for everyone around them. Many adoptive and foster families describe the bond as no less real, no less loving, than any other.

But honesty requires saying plainly that adoption is not simple. It carries its own ethical weight and its own hard questions. Adoption can involve loss as well as love — a child separated from birth family, culture, and origins, sometimes carrying grief that lasts a lifetime. Some adoption systems, historically and today, have involved coercion, poverty, or inequality between the families who place children and the families who receive them. International and transracial adoption raise further questions about identity and consent. None of this means adoption is wrong; it means adoption is a serious undertaking that deserves the same careful thought antinatalism asks us to bring to creation itself.

Fostering — providing temporary care for a child who cannot, for the time being, live with their birth family. Unlike adoption, it usually does not transfer permanent legal parenthood, and it is often oriented toward eventual reunification. It can be a profound way to help a child already in need, while making no claim to permanence.

It is also worth saying that adoption is not always available. The path can be long, expensive, intrusive, and uncertain; eligibility rules vary widely by place; and the number of people hoping to adopt does not always match the children who need homes, in either direction. Treating adoption as the obvious "answer" for antinatalists can quietly burden people with an expectation that the world will not let them meet.

In plain words: Adoption and fostering can be deeply good — and are also hard, complex, and not always possible. They are options to consider, never duties to fulfill.

The many other forms of care

Parenthood is far from the only way to pour yourself into the lives of the young, and antinatalist writers often point this out with real warmth. Most of the people who shape a child are not that child's parents.

  • Teaching — spending years helping young minds grow, in classrooms, workshops, music rooms, and sports fields.
  • Mentoring — guiding a young person through a craft, a hard season, or the passage into adulthood.
  • Caregiving — nursing, social work, pediatric medicine, foster support, and the countless roles that tend to the vulnerable.
  • Being an aunt, uncle, godparent, or family friend — the steady, loving adult who is not the parent but is unmistakably there.
  • Building a better world for those already in it — work that reduces suffering for children who exist now, from public health to poverty relief.

For a great many people, these forms of care are not consolation prizes. They are rich, meaningful lives in their own right, and they answer a real human need to nurture without raising the philosophical question of creation at all.

Most of the adults who shape a child were never that child's parents.

What these paths do and don't settle

Here is the honest philosophical point, and it deserves to be stated rather than blurred. Caring for a child who already exists is a different matter from creating a child who does not. Adoption, fostering, mentoring, and teaching all address the well-being of people who are already here, with interests, needs, and a stake in being loved. They do not, by themselves, answer the core antinatalist question — whether it is right to bring a new person into existence — because that question is about creation, and these paths are about care.

That is not a flaw in these paths; it is simply what they are. They can be wonderful expressions of the very concern for suffering that drew a person to antinatalism in the first place. But a reader should not feel that adopting "resolves" the argument, or that declining to adopt "fails" it. The two live on different levels.

Objection

If antinatalists really believed their own arguments, wouldn't they all adopt? Telling people not to have children while doing nothing for the children who exist looks like an empty, even self-serving, philosophy.

Reply

The challenge has a point worth honoring: a concern for suffering that never moves toward action can ring hollow, and many antinatalists do feel a strong pull toward caregiving for exactly this reason. But the objection asks for more than fairness allows. We do not require people who decline to have biological children for any other reason — health, circumstance, simple preference — to adopt as proof of sincerity, and it would be strange to single out antinatalists for that test. Adoption is costly, gatekept, and not always available; making it a moral entrance fee would punish the willing for barriers outside their control. More deeply, the antinatalist argument is about creation, and refraining from creating is itself a complete action — it needs no supplement to count. Adoption and care are admirable extensions of the same compassion, freely chosen. They are not a debt the philosophy collects.

A gentle word to the reader weighing this

If you are turning these paths over in your own mind, take your time, and take the pressure off. Adoption and fostering deserve to be entered for the child's sake and your own, with clear eyes — not undertaken to settle a philosophical score or to prove a point. The same is true of every other form of care. Do the good that fits your life, your means, and your heart. That is enough.

Chapter in brief

  • For readers moved by antinatalism who still wish to care for children, adoption, fostering, mentoring, teaching, and caregiving are often discussed as paths.
  • Adoption can be a real good but is not a simple "solution": it carries its own ethical complexity, can involve loss, and is not always available.
  • Many other forms of care — teaching, mentoring, being a present adult — are meaningful lives in their own right, not consolation prizes.
  • These paths address caring for those already here, which is a different question from whether to create new life; they don't settle the core argument, and don't need to.
  • None of these is a duty or a test of sincerity. Do the good that fits your life, without pressure.
