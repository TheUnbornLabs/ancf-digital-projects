PROJECT 007

Consent and Birth Philosophy Page

Philosophy · Difficulty: Intermediate

A recurring question in antinatalist philosophy is whether consent can matter when no one yet exists to give it. This page lays the argument out step by step, lets you test which steps you accept, and gives the strongest replies a fair hearing.

1. The argument, step by step

Philosophers often write arguments as numbered premises leading to a conclusion. Here is one common version of the consent argument.

P1A future person cannot consent to being born — there is no one there yet to be asked. P2Being born reliably exposes that person to some risk of serious harm (pain, loss, illness, grief). P3Imposing an un-consented risk of serious harm on someone normally requires a strong justification. P4"It will probably be a good life" may not, by itself, justify imposing that risk on a person who could not agree to it. CTherefore, creating a new person is ethically weighty — not automatically permissible, and worth careful thought.

3. The strongest replies

A fair page presents the best objections, not just the argument. Tap each to expand.

Consent only applies to existing people

You cannot wrong someone who does not yet exist; there is no one being denied a choice. The whole debate turns on whether "potential persons" have any moral standing.

The non-identity problem

A particular person only exists because they were born. Compared to never existing at all, it is hard to say they were made worse off — so "harm" may not apply in the usual way.

We routinely act for future people

We make many un-consented decisions for people who will exist — schooling, vaccination, environmental policy — and call them clearly good. Why should birth be different?

Hypothetical consent

Perhaps the relevant test is whether a reasonable person, looking back, would endorse having been born. Many people clearly do — which may supply a kind of after-the-fact consent.

4. Key thinkers

Seana ShiffrinArgues that imposing the risks of existence on a non-consenting person needs justification we cannot easily give. David BenatarFrames birth as a harm via the asymmetry of pleasure and suffering, complementing the consent line of thought. Derek ParfitSet out the non-identity problem, the most influential objection to "birth harms the person born".

Why it stays unresolved

Reasonable, thoughtful people land on different sides of this — often because they accept the same premises but weigh them differently. Holding the question open, without rushing to a verdict, is itself a philosophical skill. The goal here is not to win the argument, but to understand it well enough to disagree carefully.
