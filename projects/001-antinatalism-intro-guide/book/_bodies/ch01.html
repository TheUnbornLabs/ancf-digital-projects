"It would be better if there were nothing. Since there is more pain than pleasure on earth, every satisfaction is only transitory, creating new desires and new distresses."

— Arthur Schopenhauer

Few words in philosophy are as quickly misread as antinatalism. The shape of the word — that hard prefix, anti-, pressed against the warm idea of birth — makes people brace for hostility before they have heard a single argument. They expect a creed that hates children, despises parents, or wishes the human story would simply stop. It is none of those things, and one purpose of this book is to clear that ground carefully before we build anything on it.

At its plainest, antinatalism is the view that bringing a new person into existence is an ethical decision that deserves serious thought, rather than an automatic good or an unquestioned duty. Some antinatalists go further and argue that procreation is always wrong; others hold only that it is far less obviously right than our culture assumes. What unites them is not a feeling but a question — and a refusal to treat that question as already answered by instinct, tradition, or the simple fact that humans have always had children.

Antinatalism — from the Latin natalis ("of birth"). The ethical position that assigns a negative value to coming into existence, and therefore holds that procreation requires justification it may not be able to give. The strong version says creating a life is always a harm to the one created; weaker versions say only that the decision is morally weighty and too often made thoughtlessly.

Two ideas at the root

Most antinatalist thinking grows from two observations that are, on their own, hard to deny. The first is about consent. Almost every significant thing we do to another person, we ideally ask them first — and where we cannot ask, we proceed with great caution and only for their benefit. Birth is the one event that happens to a person before they exist to be asked. The future child is not consulted, cannot be consulted, and yet the entire weight of a human life is placed upon them.

The second observation is about risk. However well a life turns out, it is never guaranteed to be free of pain, loss, illness, grief, or hardship. To create a person is to enter them, without their agreement, into a game whose worst outcomes can be very bad indeed and whose dice are thrown only once. From these two starting points the antinatalist asks: if a future person cannot agree, and is certain to face at least some suffering, can we really say that creating that life is automatically and obviously good for that person?

You do not have to answer "no" to find the question worth taking seriously. That distinction — between taking a question seriously and accepting a particular answer — runs through every chapter that follows.

Birth is the one thing we do to a person before there is a person to ask.

Six things antinatalism is not

Because the misunderstandings are so persistent, it is worth naming them directly. Antinatalism, as serious thinkers have defended it, is:

Not hatred of children. The opposite is usually true. Concern for the well-being of any future child — the wish that no child be born into avoidable suffering — is often the very thing that draws a person to the view. Many antinatalists are devoted aunts, uncles, teachers, and carers.

Not contempt for parents. Antinatalism questions one specific decision; it does not look down on the billions of people who have made the opposite choice, usually with love and hope. Disagreeing with a choice is not the same as despising those who made it.

Not a demand that existing people disappear. This is the most important confusion to dispel. Antinatalism is concerned entirely with future births — with whether to create new people. It says nothing for, and nothing against, the lives of people who already exist. The ethics of those already here, who have interests, attachments, and a powerful stake in continuing, is a completely different matter.

Not a call for harm of any kind. Nothing in the argument licenses coercion, violence, or pressure. The classic antinatalist conclusion is simply to not do one thing — to refrain — which is about as far from harm as an action can be.

Not the same as depression or misanthropy. It is an ethical argument, and a cheerful, people-loving person can hold it for entirely reasoned motives, just as a miserable person might reject it. We will treat the relationship between the philosophy and a person's mood with real care in a later chapter, because the two are easy to conflate and important to keep apart.

Not merely a dislike of family life. One can adore family, marriage, and the company of children and still believe that creating a new person is an ethical decision that has to clear a high bar.

In plain words: Antinatalism is an argument about whether to start a new life — not a verdict on the lives already being lived, and not an emotion dressed up as a philosophy.

Why bother with the question at all?

A fair response at this point is impatience: people have always had children, the species depends on it, so why pick at something so basic? The antinatalist answer is that "we have always done it" is not, by itself, a moral justification — humanity has always done many things it later examined and revised. The decision to create a person is among the most consequential any of us can make, since it determines whether there will be a whole new center of experience in the world, with everything good and bad that entails. A decision that large, the argument runs, deserves at least as much reflection as we give to far smaller ones.

Notice that this is a modest claim. It does not yet tell you what to do. It says only that the question is real, that it is not absurd, and that it has occupied serious minds across many cultures and centuries. The chapters ahead will sharpen the arguments, give each its strongest form, and then — just as deliberately — hand the microphone to the critics.

How this book treats the question

This is a teaching book written from inside the antinatalist conversation, which means it will explain the arguments sympathetically and in their best light. But a sympathetic guide is not a salesman. Every major argument here is paired with the most serious objection to it, because an idea you cannot see the weaknesses of is an idea you do not really understand. By the end you should be able to state the antinatalist case clearly enough to defend it — and clearly enough to argue against it. What you conclude is yours to decide.

Chapter in brief

  • Antinatalism is the view that creating a new person is a weighty ethical decision, not an automatic good — in its strong form, that procreation is always a harm to the one created.
  • It grows from two hard-to-deny observations: a future person cannot consent to being born, and every life carries some risk of suffering.
  • It is not hatred of children or parents, not about existing people, not a call for harm, and not the same as depression.
  • The position asks us to take a question seriously; it does not force a particular answer.
  • This book presents each argument sympathetically and then squarely faces its strongest objection.
