① The core thesis

Everything in the series rests on one argument, owed in its modern form to David Benatar: the asymmetry of pain and pleasure. It proceeds from four premises.

  1. The presence of pain is bad.
  2. The presence of pleasure is good.
  3. The absence of pain is good, even if there is no one to enjoy that absence.
  4. The absence of pleasure is not bad, unless there is someone for whom that absence is a deprivation.

The whole argument lives in the bottom pair: a missing pain counts as good, but a missing pleasure counts only as not bad — so non-existence never loses a category.

In one sentence: procreation imposes guaranteed, unconsented harm on a being who had no need of the goods offered in compensation.

Two boundary markers — every misreading crosses one

It is about starting lives, never ending them.

Existing people have interests — in living, in being loved, in not being harmed — and those carry full moral weight. The comparison is never between living and dying, only between starting and never starting.

It is universal and voluntary.

The reasoning applies to every prospective birth identically. There is no version that applies to some populations and not others; any selective or coercive application is not a variant of the position but its negation.

Read it twice

This primer is deliberately incomplete. Its job is to show you the whole before you study the parts — so read it once now, and again after you finish the series. It will be a different book the second time. The destination was never an argument won; it was a person who can hold a heavy truth and remain whole, kind, and at peace.

This is education and reflection, not medical, legal, financial, or mental-health advice. It is a sensitive topic; if it stirs distress, consider reaching out to someone you trust. The position defended here is universal in its reasoning and voluntary in its application.
