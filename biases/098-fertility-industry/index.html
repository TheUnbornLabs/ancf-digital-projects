Manufacturing Demand: The Fertility Industry

Part VIII · Gender, Body, Identity & Autonomy · How the commercial assisted-reproduction sector profits from and amplifies the drive to conceive

The bias: How a commercial sector profits from and amplifies the drive to conceive.

The market does not create the desire to have a child. But it learns, refines, and sells the fear of not having one." — paraphrase of a common critique in the bioethics literature (idea attributed, wording not a direct quotation) --- ## Preface to this volume Infertility is a real grief. For many who live through it, the wish for a child is among the most sincere and least manufactured desires a person can hold. Assisted reproductive technology — in vitro fertilization, intracytoplasmic sperm injection, donor gametes, and the practices that surround them — has brought children, and with them profound joy, to people who would otherwise never have had them. Any honest book about the fertility industry must begin by conceding all of this without reservation. The technology is a genuine good. The suffering it addresses is genuine. And the clinicians who do this work are, in the main, sincere. It would be a poor and cruel critique that lost sight of the tearful reunion of a parent with a longed-for infant, and this book intends never to lose sight of it. This book is not about the technology and not about the patients. It is about the industry — the commercial structure that has grown up around assisted reproduction — and a specific structural fact about that industry: it is, in most of the world, a for-profit market, and for-profit markets have an incentive to meet demand and, beyond that, to expand it. The bias this volume names is the tendency of that industry to amplify the pronatalist pressures already present in a culture: to convert an anxiety into a diagnosis, a diagnosis into a treatment cycle, and a treatment cycle into hope sold at a premium. The industry did not invent the drive to conceive. But it has learned to trade on it, and in trading on it, it makes that drive louder. The distinction matters because it is easy to hear a critique of the industry as a critique of the people it serves, or of the science it uses. It is neither. A person who freezes her eggs at thirty-eight because she is not ready to have a child is making a reasonable decision under uncertainty. A couple who spends a decade and a great deal of money on IVF is not naive. The question this book asks is a different one: what does the surrounding commercial apparatus do to the information environment in which such decisions are made? When success rates are reported in the most flattering possible frame, when egg freezing is marketed as "insurance," when clinics advertise hope rather than odds, when corporations offer fertility benefits that quietly signal an expectation about the timing of a woman's family — the individual decisions may still be free, but the field on which they are made has been tilted. A word on why the distinction between meeting and amplifying demand is not a semantic quibble. Almost every business hopes to sell more of what it makes, and there is nothing sinister in that. What sets the fertility market apart is the conjunction of three features that rarely coincide elsewhere: the product is sold at a moment of acute emotional need, the seller wears the authority of medicine, and the "quantity" a buyer can be induced to purchase — more cycles, more add-ons, more years of storage — has no natural ceiling. Where those three meet, the ordinary commercial impulse to grow demand acquires a force it does not have when the product is a refrigerator. Naming that force, and separating it from the genuine good it rides upon, is the whole task of this volume. The book proceeds in the series' standard order. Chapter 1 names the bias precisely and separates it from its neighbors. Chapter 2 — before any criticism — gives the strongest possible case for the industry and for a permissive, market-based approach to assisted reproduction; this is not a formality, and readers who want only the critique should read it anyway. Chapters 3 through 7 trace where the bias comes from, how it operates, what the evidence shows, what it costs, and who bears those costs. Chapter 8 turns the critique on itself and takes the objections seriously. Chapter 9 offers concrete illustrations. Chapter 10 connects this bias to its neighbors in the series — the medicalization of ordinary life (019), the corporate consumer-market interest (062), and the sunk-cost dynamics of reproductive investment (016) — without duplicating them. Chapter 11 asks what clear-eyed reasoning looks like once the bias is named, and Chapter 12 draws the measured conclusion. Throughout, the stance is critical of pronatalist bias but non-coercive. Nothing here argues that anyone should not use ART, should not freeze eggs, or should not want a child. The argument is narrower and, I hope, more useful: that an industry with a financial stake in your wanting a child badly enough to pay for it is not a neutral source of information about whether, when, or how you should have one — and that naming this lets you use its genuine gifts with clearer eyes. Where the evidence supports only a general claim, this book makes only the general claim, and where figures are contested it says so rather than picking the most striking one. A book that criticizes an industry for over-claiming cannot itself trade in unverified specifics. --- ## 1. Naming the bias The bias named in this book is demand amplification by the fertility industry: the tendency of the commercial assisted-reproduction sector to intensify, rather than merely serve, the drive to conceive — by shaping the information, urgency, and emotional framing in which reproductive decisions are made, in directions that increase the uptake of its own products and services. Stated as a single thesis: the fertility industry is not a neutral responder to a fixed demand for children; it is an economic actor with a structural incentive to enlarge that demand, and in doing so it amplifies the surrounding culture's pronatalist bias — converting anxiety into diagnosis, hope into a product, and the passage of time into a marketable emergency. It is worth pausing on the word amplifies, because it carries the whole argument. An amplifier does not generate a signal; it takes a signal already present and increases its power. The industry, on this account, is precisely an amplifier: the drive to have children, the cultural premium on biological parenthood, the fear of a childless future — all of these exist before any clinic opens its doors. What the industry adds is gain. It takes an ambient anxiety and, through marketing, urgency framing, and the authority of medicine, makes it louder, more insistent, more likely to issue in a purchase. To call the bias "amplification" rather than "manufacture" is therefore not a softening; it is an accuracy. The industry is not accused of inventing a desire that was not there, which would be implausible. It is described as intensifying a desire that was, and profiting from the intensification. Precision requires separating this from three neighbors. First, it is not the same as the general medicalization of reproduction (Book 019), which concerns how ordinary life stages — pregnancy, menopause, the childless state — get reframed as medical conditions requiring professional management. Medicalization is the broader cultural process; the fertility industry is one powerful actor within it, and this book is about that actor's specific commercial incentives, not the process as a whole. A public health system could medicalize infertility without any profit motive at all; what distinguishes the industry is the profit motive layered on top. One can imagine a fully socialized fertility service that still treated childlessness as a condition to be cured — that would be medicalization without demand amplification. The two come apart, and it is the second that this book isolates. Second, it is not the same as the general corporate and consumer-market interest in a growing population (Book 062), which concerns how businesses of every kind — from formula manufacturers to homebuilders — benefit from more babies and quietly favor pronatalist norms. The fertility industry is unusual, and worth its own volume, because its stake goes past benefiting from births in the aggregate. It sells the means of conception itself, directly to the person trying to conceive, at the most emotionally charged moment of the decision. Its product is not diapers, which are bought after the fact by someone who has already decided. Its product is hope, bought before the fact by someone still deciding — and the industry participates in the deciding. Third, it is distinct from the sunk-cost and escalation dynamics of reproductive investment (Book 016), though the two interlock tightly. Sunk-cost reasoning is a bias in the patient — the pull to continue a costly course because so much has already been spent. Demand amplification is a bias in the market — the way the industry's framing feeds that patient-side dynamic. This book focuses on the supply side; Book 016 focuses on the demand side of the same transaction. The distinction is not academic: a remedy aimed at the patient's psychology (a pre-committed stopping rule, say) is different from a remedy aimed at the market's incentives (standardized success-rate reporting), and confusing the two produces muddled policy. What all four share is a pronatalist tilt: each, in its own register, makes having a child feel more necessary, more urgent, or more within reach than a neutral analysis would support. The specific contribution of the fertility industry is that it operates at the point of maximum vulnerability — a person confronting the possibility that they cannot have the child they want — and that at that point it is simultaneously a source of medical authority, a source of hope, and a seller with something to gain. Those three roles are not automatically in conflict. But they are not automatically aligned either, and the burden of this book is to show where they come apart. One clarification guards against caricature. To say the industry has an incentive to amplify demand is not to say every clinic, every advertisement, or every clinician does so, still less that they do so cynically. Structural incentives operate through averages and pressures, not through the villainy of individuals. A conscientious clinic can exist inside a market that, in aggregate, over-sells; indeed most clinicians would be dismayed to see their vocation described as a sales operation, and rightly, for it is not one to them. The bias lives in the system's tendencies — in how success is measured, marketed, and reimbursed — and it shapes behavior even among people of complete good faith. That is precisely why naming it matters: because it is not reducible to bad actors who could simply be removed. Remove them, and the incentive would remain, quietly re-shaping the conduct of their replacements. Check Yourself — Chapter 1 Quiz. 1. The strongest case for it (steelman) Before any criticism, the case for the fertility industry — and for a permissive, market-based approach to it — deserves to be made at full strength, because it is strong. A steelman that a proponent would not recognize as their own best argument is worthless; what follows aims to be one they would sign. Begin with the suffering. Infertility affects a large share of people who want children: a WHO report published in April 2023 put the lifetime prevalence at 17.5 percent of adults — roughly one in six — with rates broadly similar across high-, middle-, and low-income countries. For those affected, the experience is not a mild disappointment. Longitudinal and clinical studies have repeatedly found that the distress of involuntary childlessness is comparable to that of serious illness; the benchmark study, Domar and colleagues' 1993 comparison in the Journal of Psychosomatic Obstetrics and Gynaecology, found that women with infertility had global symptom scores equivalent to those of cancer, cardiac-rehabilitation, and hypertension patients. This is a genuine hardship, and it is one that medicine can, in many cases, actually relieve. Since the birth of Louise Brown on 25 July 1978, IVF and its descendant technologies have led to the birth of at least twelve million children worldwide, on the estimate of the International Committee for Monitoring Assisted Reproductive Technologies. For each of those children's parents, the industry did not manufacture a want — it answered one. It is worth holding that number, millions, steadily in mind: whatever follows about incentives and framing, this is not a phantom benefit but an enormous and concrete one. Now the market case. A permissive market in assisted reproduction has real virtues. Competition drives innovation: ICSI, blastocyst culture, vitrification for egg and embryo freezing, and preimplantation genetic testing all emerged and improved rapidly in a competitive commercial environment. Competition also drives access in one important sense — where clinics compete, prices can fall and services proliferate, reaching populations that a rationed public system might make wait for years or exclude entirely. Many national health systems ration IVF severely, funding only a small number of cycles, or none, and only up to a certain maternal age. A private market lets a person who is willing to pay pursue a child on her own timeline rather than the state's. That is a form of reproductive autonomy, and autonomy is a value this very series takes seriously. Indeed, a series devoted to resisting pronatalist coercion cannot casually endorse the state deciding who may attempt to conceive and when. The autonomy argument deserves to be pressed further, because it cuts against the reflex to regulate. The same anti-paternalist logic that says no one should pressure a woman into motherhood also says no one should stand between her and the means of achieving it. If a thirty-five-year-old wants to freeze her eggs to keep her options open, telling her the odds are worse than the brochure implies is fair; telling her she may not do it, or that the service should not exist, is not. Egg freezing in particular can be framed not as a manufactured anxiety but as a rational hedge against a real biological asymmetry — the fact that female fertility declines with age in a way that male fertility largely does not, and that careers, relationships, and finances often do not resolve until later. On this view, the industry is not amplifying a false fear; it is offering a genuine tool for managing a true constraint, and the "insurance" framing, whatever its marketing excesses, points at something real. A woman who freezes at thirty-two and never needs the eggs has not been defrauded; she has bought and used peace of mind, which is a real good even when the underlying risk does not materialize — exactly as with any insurance. Corporate fertility benefits, similarly, can be read generously. When an employer covers egg freezing or IVF, it extends an expensive service to people who could not otherwise afford it and signals that family-building is compatible with a career. For a lower-paid worker, such a benefit may be the only route to treatment at all. To read it purely as a device for extracting more working years before childbearing is to assume bad faith where mixed motives, including genuine ones, are more likely. Many of the people who designed and championed such benefits did so precisely because they had watched colleagues struggle, and wanted to help; the cynical reading is not obviously the true one, and a fair critique must say so. Finally, the honest steelman must concede that much of the industry's information is not false. Clinics that report success rates are, in many jurisdictions, required to submit them to registries. The hope they sell is, for a meaningful fraction of patients, delivered. And the alternative to an imperfect market is not a perfect one but often no service at all. A person who needed IVF in 1975 simply could not have a biological child; the industry, for all its incentives, changed that. Any critique that forgets this has lost the plot. There is, too, a quieter virtue worth naming: the industry has been a powerful engine of destigmatization, dragging infertility out of the shadows of private shame and into the domain of ordinary medical problems that people may discuss, treat, and survive. That cultural shift, whatever its commercial motive, has spared many people a lonely grief. The remainder of this book criticizes the industry's framing and incentives — but it does so on the far side of conceding, fully, that the thing being criticized is also, in large part, a gift. Where it comes from The fertility industry's demand-amplifying tendency has several roots, and untangling them shows why the bias is structural rather than incidental. Each root, taken alone, might seem innocuous; it is their combination that makes amplification not a choice but a gravitational pull. The first root is technological and recent. Before 1978 there was no IVF and therefore no IVF market. The birth of Louise Brown created a medical capability and, with it, a product category — something that could be offered, priced, and sold. From the start, this category had an unusual feature: its buyers were, almost by definition, people in acute distress and hope, and its product had no ceiling on desirability. There is a limit to how many cars a person will buy; there is, for someone who desperately wants a child and has not yet succeeded, no natural limit to how many cycles they will attempt. A market whose product is hope, sold to people who cannot easily say "enough," is structurally prone to expansion. The economist's usual assumption — that demand falls as price rises — bends here, because the good being purchased is not a commodity but a chance at the thing a person may want most in the world. The second root is the commercialization of medicine more broadly, and here the industry sits at an extreme. In many countries, assisted reproduction is delivered predominantly or entirely by private, for-profit clinics — including, increasingly, clinics owned by private-equity firms and consolidated into chains. In the United States the private-equity-affiliated share of fertility clinics rose from around 4 percent in 2013 to roughly 32 percent a decade later, and by 2022 more than half of all American IVF cycles were performed at clinics affiliated with private-equity or venture-capital owners; the largest buyout in the sector to date, KKR's acquisition of the Spain-headquartered IVI-RMA Global, was valued at some $3 billion. Private-equity ownership sharpens the incentive: returns depend on growing volume, cross-selling adjacent services (genetic testing, storage fees, add-on procedures), and maximizing the revenue per patient. Even where clinicians resist, the ownership structure exerts a steady pressure toward treating more, testing more, storing more. The clinician who wishes to counsel a patient to stop, or to skip an unproven add-on, is quietly swimming against the tide of the enterprise that employs her — not because anyone has instructed her to sell, but because the metrics by which the business is judged reward volume, and metrics have a way of becoming culture. The third root is the pre-existing pronatalist culture the industry inherited. It did not have to create the belief that a life without biological children is incomplete; that belief was already ambient, reinforced by religion, family expectation, and the general natalist tilt this series documents book by book. The industry's move was to attach itself to that belief and offer a technological resolution of it. This is why the industry amplifies rather than originates: it plugs into an existing emotional current and increases its voltage. The cultural script "a real family has children" pre-existed the clinic; the clinic added "and here is how, for a price, we can get you one." The genius, if that is the word, of the arrangement is that the industry never has to argue the premise. It arrives after the premise is already installed by a thousand other forces, and simply offers to satisfy it. A fourth root is the biology of female fertility and the way modern life has widened the gap between when people are ready to have children and when it is easiest to. As education and careers extend the age of first attempted childbearing, the number of people encountering age-related fertility decline grows. This is a real demographic change, not a manufactured one — but it creates a large, anxious, and paying market, and the industry has strong reasons to frame that anxiety in the way most conducive to treatment: as an emergency with a technological solution, rather than as one consideration among many in a complex life. The industry, in other words, did not create the tension between biological and biographical time; but it stands to gain from every way of resolving that tension that routes through a clinic, and to gain nothing from resolutions that route through, say, having children earlier or accepting a smaller family. A fifth root is regulatory and informational. In many jurisdictions the industry has been left to report its own success rates and to advertise with relatively light oversight. Where the seller controls the scoreboard, the scoreboard tends to flatter the seller. Success-rate reporting can be framed per-cycle or per-transfer, for younger or older patients, including or excluding the hardest cases — and each choice can make a clinic look better without any single false statement — a latitude that has drawn sustained regulatory attention, though the rules vary widely by country. The absence, historically, of strong constraints on how hope is priced and advertised is not a minor detail; it is one of the conditions that let the amplifying tendency flourish. An industry that must publish standardized, audited outcomes behaves differently from one that may choose its own denominators, even when the people running both are equally honest. Together these roots explain why the bias is not an accident of a few aggressive clinics but a predictable product of the field's structure: a hope-based product with no natural ceiling, sold under a for-profit ownership model, attached to a pre-existing pronatalist culture, into a growing anxious market, under light informational oversight. Any industry built on those five conditions would tend to amplify demand. This one does. How it operates today The bias acts through concrete, observable mechanisms — the everyday grammar of how fertility services are marketed, measured, and sold. What follows is a taxonomy of that grammar, and the striking thing about it is how ordinary each item looks in isolation and how systematically they point one way in combination. The framing of urgency. The most pervasive mechanism is the conversion of a gradual biological process into a countdown. Female fertility does decline with age, but the decline is a curve, not a cliff; marketing tends to render it as a cliff. The "biological clock" is a real phenomenon rendered as a ticking bomb, because urgency sells. A person who believes she has a shrinking window acts now; a person who understands the true, gentler slope of her odds might wait, or might not treat at all. The industry's interest lies unambiguously with the first framing. Urgency is commercially valuable for a further reason: it forecloses deliberation. A decision made under a felt emergency is a decision made without the slow comparison of alternatives — including the alternative of not treating — that a calmer timeline would permit. Hope over odds. Fertility advertising characteristically foregrounds success stories — the smiling baby, the tearful parents — and backgrounds the base rate. This is not lying; it is selection. Every clinic has success stories, and every clinic also has a larger number of patients who did not conceive and do not appear in the brochure. When hope is the product, the marketing shows the hope realized, not the modal outcome. The result is a systematic mismatch between the emotional impression a patient forms and the statistical reality she faces. The photographs are all real; the composite they create is not, because the many who left without a baby were never photographed. Success-rate presentation. Because success can be reported per cycle started, per egg retrieval, per embryo transfer, or per patient — and because it can be stratified by age or reported in aggregate — clinics have wide latitude to present the same underlying performance in the most favorable light. A "60% success rate" might mean something very different from what a reasonable reader assumes. Regulators have flagged this directly: in June 2021 the UK's Advertising Standards Authority, together with the Human Fertilisation and Embryology Authority and the Competition and Markets Authority, issued an enforcement notice to every HFEA-licensed clinic in the country setting out how success rates, prices, and add-ons must be advertised, after finding ads that quoted percentages without the number of patients treated or that reported clinical pregnancy rates in place of live birth rates. The mechanism is not fraud; it is the strategic choice among true denominators. A clinic can report its live-birth rate per embryo transfer in women under thirty-five and state nothing false, while a patient of forty hears a promise the number does not contain. Add-ons. A large and growing part of the transaction is optional extras layered onto the base cycle: assisted hatching, endometrial scratching, immune therapies, time-lapse imaging, various genetic screens. Independent reviews — including the HFEA's published ratings of the evidence for common IVF add-ons, launched in 2017 and rebuilt in October 2023 into a five-category scheme — have concluded that many of these have little or no high-quality evidence of improving live birth rates for most patients. Of the thirteen add-ons the HFEA rated, not one qualified as "green," the rating reserved for treatments with clear evidence that they improve outcomes. Yet they are offered, often at additional cost, to patients primed by hope and urgency to try anything. The add-on economy is demand amplification in miniature: selling more procedure per patient regardless of marginal benefit. It thrives on a particular emotional logic — the patient who has already committed to a cycle, and who cannot bear the thought of failing for want of some extra step, is exquisitely susceptible to "it might help, and you don't want to look back with regret.

① In brief

The bias. This volume names demand amplification by the fertility industry: the tendency of the commercial assisted-reproduction sector — IVF and egg-freezing clinics, gamete and surrogacy markets, fertility testing — to intensify, rather than merely serve, the drive to conceive. The industry did not invent the wish for a child; it inherited a pronatalist culture and learned to trade on it, converting anxiety into diagnosis, hope into a product, and the passage of time into a marketable emergency.

The stance, stated carefully. Infertility is a real grief, and assisted reproductive technology is a genuine good that has brought children and joy to millions since the birth of Louise Brown in 1978. The clinicians who provide it are, in the main, sincere. This book criticizes neither the patients nor the technology. Its target is the structure: a predominantly for-profit market whose product is hope, sold to people in acute need. For-profit markets have an incentive not only to meet demand but to expand it, and a market built on hope — with no natural ceiling on how many cycles a desperate buyer will attempt — is structurally prone to amplification.

How the bias operates. The book documents concrete mechanisms rather than accusing anyone of fraud. Urgency framing renders the gentle curve of age-related fertility decline as a cliff, because urgency sells. Hope-forward marketing foregrounds success stories and backgrounds base rates. Success rates can be reported per cycle, per transfer, or per patient, and by age or in aggregate — letting the same performance be framed favorably. An add-on economy sells costly extras that often lack good evidence of improving live-birth rates. Egg freezing is marketed as "insurance," a metaphor implying a payout the biology does not guarantee, while generating recurring storage fees. Corporate fertility benefits expand access and simultaneously send a timing signal — postpone now, conceive later with our help.

What the evidence shows. The distress of involuntary childlessness is real and serious — the industry's strongest ground. But per-cycle IVF success rates are lower than public perception and fall sharply with maternal age; egg freezing improves options only at the margin and depends heavily on age and egg count; and many IVF add-ons lack good evidence of benefit. Across success rates, egg freezing, and add-ons, there is a consistent gap between marketing impression and statistical reality — the footprint of the bias. The book is honest about a key limit: the counterfactual (how much treatment the framing actually adds) cannot be measured, so the argument rests on documented incentives and mechanisms, not a quantified harm.

Hidden costs and who bears them. The default framing prices only the benefit — a wanted child — and hides the rest: the true cost of a live birth across multiple cycles, the compounding grief of "just one more cycle," the distortion of life timing under false urgency, the reinforcement of the childless state as a problem to be solved, and the commodification questions of gamete and surrogacy markets. These pressures fall unevenly — hardest on women, on the less advantaged (sometimes on the supply side of egg and surrogacy markets), on the culturally exposed, and above all on those already struggling to conceive, who meet the industry at their most hopeful and most vulnerable.

Seeing past it. The response is non-coercive: not rejecting the industry's gifts but receiving them with better information. Individuals can separate the tool from the seller, ask for the right denominator, treat "insurance" language skeptically, name the sunk-cost moment in advance, and keep a real place for the childless outcome. Institutions can standardize success-rate reporting, require honest add-on disclosure, and mandate clear conditional information for egg freezing — regulating the framing, not the choice. Employers can offer benefits without the timing signal.

The takeaway. The bias is not villainy or a scam; it is a structural conflict of interest. An actor with a financial stake in your wanting a child badly enough to pay for it is not a neutral source of information about whether, when, or how to have one. The technology can be a blessing — the point is to meet it with clear eyes, so the blessing is chosen and not sold. The book relates to but stays distinct from medicalization (019), corporate pronatalism (062), and sunk-cost escalation (016).

They fall along the familiar axes of gender, class, culture, and age, and the distribution is itself revealing — because a pressure that lands hardest on those least able to resist it is, by that fact, more troubling than one borne by those with resources to spare.

CH 12Conclusion

The argument of this book has been narrow and, I hope, fair. Infertility is a real grief; assisted reproductive technology is a genuine good that has brought children and joy to millions; the clinicians who provide it are, in the main, sincere. None of that is in question. What is in question is the commercial structure that has grown up around the technology — a for-profit market whose product is hope, sold to people in acute need, attached to a pre-existing pronatalist culture, under historically light oversight. Any industry built on those conditions would tend to amplify demand rather than merely serve it, and this one does: through urgency framing, hope-forward marketing, strategic success-rate reporting, an add-on economy that outruns the evidence, "insurance" metaphors that oversell egg freezing, and corporate benefits that double as demand channels.

Show all 18 terms as a list
Assisted reproductive technology (ART)
Medical techniques for achieving pregnancy by artificial means, including IVF, ICSI, and donor-gamete procedures.
In vitro fertilization (IVF)
Fertilization of an egg by sperm outside the body, followed by transfer of an embryo to the uterus; the core ART procedure since the birth of Louise Brown in 1978.
Demand amplification
The tendency of a for-profit industry to enlarge, not merely meet, demand for its products; here, the fertility industry's tendency to intensify the drive to conceive.
Egg freezing (oocyte cryopreservation)
Extraction and frozen storage of a woman's eggs for possible later use, marketed heavily as fertility "insurance" and generating recurring storage fees.
Add-ons
Optional extra procedures layered onto a standard IVF cycle (e.g., assisted hatching, endometrial scratching, various screens), many of which lack strong evidence of improving live-birth rates.
Success-rate framing
The strategic choice among true denominators (per cycle started, per transfer, per patient, by age band) to present a clinic's performance in the most favorable light without stating a falsehood.
Base rate
The underlying probability of an outcome across all relevant cases, often obscured by the selection of success stories in marketing.
Biological clock
The real decline of female fertility with age, frequently rendered in marketing as a cliff rather than a curve to heighten urgency.
Cumulative success rate
The probability of a live birth across multiple treatment cycles; a genuine and important nuance that also happens to favor selling additional cycles.
Conditional probability
The chance of an outcome given specific circumstances (such as age at freezing or number of eggs stored), which "insurance" framing tends to flatten into a false guarantee.
Commodification
The transformation of reproductive capacity (eggs, sperm, gestation) into priced goods and services in gamete and surrogacy markets, raising distributive and ethical questions.
Corporate fertility benefits
Employer-funded egg-freezing or IVF coverage, which extends access while sending an implicit signal about the acceptable timing of childbearing.
Sunk-cost escalation
The pull to continue a costly reproductive course because of prior investment; the patient-side dynamic that the industry's framing feeds (see Book 016).
Medicalization
The reframing of ordinary life stages, including the childless state, as medical conditions requiring professional management (see Book 019).
Structural incentive
A pressure built into a system's design that shapes behavior on average, independently of any individual actor's intentions or good faith.
Strategic denominator
The particular case of success-rate framing in which the choice of what a rate is "per" (cycle, transfer, patient) changes the impression while every stated number remains true.
Traffic-light rating
A regulatory approach (associated with UK oversight of IVF add-ons) that classifies extra procedures by the strength of evidence that they improve outcomes, without banning any of them.
Counterfactual (in this critique)
The unobservable state of decision-making in an information environment not shaped by a seller's incentives; its unmeasurability limits how strongly the harm can be quantified.

⑥ Sources

What this volume draws on. Where the evidence is thin or contested, the book says so rather than reaching for a number.

  • Louise Brown, the first person born through in vitro fertilization, was born on 25 July 1978. This is a widely documented fact of medical history and marks the beginning of the clinical IVF era.
  • United Nations, World Population Prospects — the standard reference class for global and national fertility trends. Cited here for demographic context: falling fertility and sub-replacement TFR across many countries.
  • Peter Singer and Deane Wells, The Reproduction Revolution — an early work in the bioethics of assisted reproduction, written when the questions were still hypothetical.
  • The general finding that involuntary childlessness causes serious psychological distress is well established across the infertility-psychology literature. This book states it at that general level; the more dramatic magnitude comparisons are a separate matter, treated below.
Directions the volume points to (not pinned citations)
  • Debora Spar, The Baby Business — the standard analysis of the commercial market in reproduction, and the closest thing this subject has to a founding text.
  • Human Fertilisation and Embryology Authority (HFEA) guidance on IVF add-ons, and its "traffic light" system rating add-ons by strength of evidence. Consult the current ratings; they change as the evidence does, and the direction of change is instructive on its own.
  • Cochrane and other systematic reviews on the efficacy of common IVF add-ons — assisted hatching, endometrial scratching, immune therapies, time-lapse imaging.
  • See the literature on elective (social) egg freezing: utilization rates, the proportion of women who return to use stored eggs, and live-birth probabilities by age at freezing and number of eggs stored.
  • Registry data on IVF live-birth rates by maternal age — the HFEA in the UK, the CDC and SART reports in the US, ESHRE in Europe. Worth reading against the figures clinics advertise.
  • See the record of advertising-standards and consumer-regulator rulings against clinics over misleading success-rate claims.
  • The reporting on corporate egg-freezing benefits introduced by major technology employers in the mid-2010s, and the argument it set off about what such a benefit actually offers.
  • See the analyses of private-equity ownership and consolidation in the fertility-clinic sector, and their effect on volume, pricing, and cross-selling.
  • Estimates of infertility prevalence, including the WHO's. The headline figures move with the definition used and the population sampled, which is itself part of this book's subject.
  • The studies comparing the psychological distress of infertility to that of serious illness. The comparison is widely repeated in marketing material, and reads more cautiously in the original.
  • See the bioethical literature on commodification in gamete and surrogacy markets: the pricing of donors, the sourcing of eggs and gestational labour, and cross-border reproduction.
