Part VII · Living With the QuestionCHAPTER 36

Reproductive Autonomy

Why the decision belongs to each person — including the freedom to say no.

"No one consents to be born. But each of us, once here, has the right to decide whether to pass that fact along."

— a common reflection in reproductive-ethics writing

One of the quieter consequences of taking antinatalism seriously is that it turns procreation back into a question — and a question is something a person answers for themselves. This chapter is about that act of answering: the principle that each person has the right to decide whether and when to bring a new life into the world, including the freedom to say no. Philosophers and ethicists call this reproductive autonomy, and it is one of the few places where antinatalists and their critics often find common ground.

It is worth being clear at the outset. Whatever you make of the antinatalist arguments in earlier chapters, almost no serious antinatalist wants to force anyone to do anything. The classic conclusion of the view is a refraining, not a command — and a refraining that one person urges on the world is very different from a rule that a state imposes on a body. Holding that procreation needs justification is compatible with holding, just as firmly, that the decision belongs to the person who would carry and raise the child, and to no one else.

Reproductive autonomy — the moral and legal principle that each person has authority over their own reproductive choices: whether to have children, when, how many, and with whom. It protects the decision to have children just as much as the decision to decline. Coercion in either direction — forcing births or forbidding them — is a violation of it.

Choice, not duty

For much of history, having children was treated less as a choice than as a default — something one simply did, the way one grew up and grew old. Tradition, family expectation, religion, and economics all pressed in the same direction, and the question "should I?" was rarely allowed to fully form. Whatever else it does, antinatalism reopens that question. It insists that creating a person is a decision, and a weighty one, which means it can be decided either way.

This cuts in a direction people sometimes miss. By framing procreation as a genuine choice, antinatalism also affirms the legitimacy of choosing to have children. A decision is only meaningful if more than one answer is permitted. The person who reflects carefully, weighs the arguments, and decides to become a parent has exercised exactly the autonomy this chapter defends — and deserves the same respect as the person who decides the opposite.

A real choice is one that can be answered either way — and respected either way.

Persuasion is not coercion

It matters enormously where one draws the line between persuasion and coercion. To make an argument, write a book, or share a worry about future suffering is persuasion: it leaves the listener free to disagree and walk away. To use law, force, money, or social punishment to override someone's decision is coercion: it removes the freedom that makes the choice a choice at all.

Antinatalism, as a philosophy, lives entirely on the persuasion side of that line. It offers reasons. Reasons can be examined, weighed, and rejected. A person who hears the antinatalist case and decides to have children anyway has done nothing wrong by the lights of the view itself — they have simply reached a different conclusion, which is their right.

In plain words: Antinatalism asks you to think. It does not — and on its own terms cannot — make you obey.

The cautionary history of coercion

History gives us hard reasons to keep the state out of the womb in both directions. Coercive natalist policies — bans on contraception, criminalized abortion, pressure or rewards to raise birth rates — have repeatedly treated people, especially women, as instruments for producing population. And coercive anti-natalist policies have been just as cruel: forced sterilization under twentieth-century eugenics programs in several countries, and coercive elements within population-control campaigns, inflicted real and lasting harm on the people subjected to them.

The lesson points the same way each time. The wrong is not which direction the coercion pushed; the wrong is the coercion. This is why thoughtful antinatalists are, overwhelmingly, opponents of any coercive population policy whatsoever. A view built on concern for a future person's well-being has no business running roughshod over the autonomy of the people who already exist.

Respect runs in every direction

It is easy, in a book that explains antinatalist arguments sympathetically, to forget how much of the world chooses parenthood with love, hope, and open eyes — and how good a choice that can be. Reproductive autonomy is not a polite cover for nudging everyone toward childlessness. It is a genuine commitment to the principle that this decision is personal, and that people of good will reach opposite answers.

So the antinatalist who truly values autonomy will hold two things at once: a serious argument about creation, and a deep respect for the parents who heard it and chose otherwise. Disagreement about a choice is not contempt for the chooser. That distinction, first drawn in this book's opening pages, is nowhere more important than here.

Objection

Talking about procreation as a free "choice" is naive. Real decisions are shaped by enormous social and economic pressure — religious expectation, family demands, the cost or scarcity of contraception, gendered assumptions, financial precarity. Calling all of that a "choice" papers over the forces that constrain people and lets society off the hook.

Reply

This is a fair and important challenge, and the right response is not to abandon autonomy but to take it more seriously. Autonomy is not just a label we stamp on whatever decision a person happens to make; it is something a society can either protect or undermine. Recognizing the pressures the objection names is part of honoring autonomy, because real freedom of choice requires access to contraception, accurate information, economic security, and freedom from coercion. Antinatalists who care about reproductive autonomy should therefore be among the strongest supporters of the conditions that make a free decision possible — and should be honest that, for many people, those conditions are not yet in place. The point of naming procreation as a choice is not to pretend the constraints away. It is to insist that, ideally, the decision should belong to the person living it.

Why this principle comes first

Reproductive autonomy is the soil in which everything else in this final part of the book grows. The chapters ahead are about caring for children already here, about discussing a hard idea without quarrel, and about living with a question you may never fully close. None of that works without the prior conviction that these are your questions to answer. Antinatalism, at its best, does not narrow your freedom. It hands you back a decision that culture had quietly made for you, and trusts you to make it yourself.

Chapter in brief

  • Reproductive autonomy is the right of each person to decide whether and when to have children — protecting the freedom to decline and the freedom to choose parenthood.
  • By treating procreation as a question, antinatalism reinforces that it is a genuine choice, not an automatic duty — which equally legitimizes choosing to have children.
  • Antinatalism lives on the side of persuasion, not coercion; it offers reasons that anyone is free to reject.
  • History's coercive policies — both pro-birth and anti-birth — are cautionary; the wrong was the coercion itself, which is why antinatalists overwhelmingly reject coercive population measures.
  • Respect runs in every direction: disagreeing with a choice is not disrespecting the person who made it.
