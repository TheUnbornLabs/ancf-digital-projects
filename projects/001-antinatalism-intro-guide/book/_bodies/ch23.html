This chapter goes to the far edge of the territory. The positions we have studied so far — Benatar's asymmetry, the consent argument, suffering-focused ethics — are debated within mainstream philosophy and stop, deliberately, at the conclusion that we should not create new lives. Efilism goes further. It is the most radical idea connected to the antinatalist conversation, and precisely because it is radical, it deserves to be described carefully, neutrally, and with its weaknesses fully on display. A reader who only ever hears such a view caricatured cannot judge it; a reader who hears it cheerled is being misled. The aim here is neither.

It also matters to say plainly, from the start, where this author stands. Nothing in this book — and least of all this chapter — endorses harming, coercing, or ending anyone. Mainstream antinatalism is about voluntary non-procreation: a decision not to do one future thing. Efilism, in some of its statements, reaches past that, and where it does, it leaves the careful, non-coercive ethics this book defends. We will mark that line clearly.

What the word means

"Efilism" is simply the word life spelled backwards, with an -ism attached. The term emerged not from academic philosophy but from online discussion, particularly around the YouTube figure who posts as "inmendham" (Gary). As an internet-born position it has no canonical text, no peer-reviewed statement, and considerable internal disagreement — which is part of why it is so easily misrepresented in every direction.

Efilism — a radical online position that extends antinatalist reasoning beyond humans to all sentient life, treating the capacity to suffer that comes with sentience as the core problem. In some formulations it endorses the hypothetical, painless end of sentience as a way to stop suffering. It is fringe and widely criticized, including by many antinatalists. Closely related "extinctionist" or "pro-extinction" views overlap with it.

How it extends the argument

The reasoning that efilism builds on is recognizable. Antinatalism observes that sentient existence guarantees some suffering and that the unborn cannot consent to bearing it. Efilism takes that observation and refuses to confine it to human beings. Animals, it points out, suffer too — often terribly, in predation, disease, starvation, and parasitism — and the vast bulk of this happens in the wild, with no human involved to blame or to stop it. If the badness of suffering is what drives antinatalism, the efilist asks, why should the argument halt at the boundary of our own species? Sentience itself, on this view, is the source of the trouble, because sentience is what makes suffering possible.

From there, the most radical efilist statements take a further and far more controversial step: that the painless cessation of all sentient life would, in principle, end all suffering, and so would be — considered purely as an outcome — a kind of mercy. This is where efilism crosses well beyond mainstream antinatalism, and it lands squarely in the territory of R. N. Smart's "benevolent world-exploder," the thought experiment that most philosophers, including most antinatalists, treat as a reductio — a sign a theory has gone wrong, not a plan to enact.

To name a harm is not to license a remedy that would be a far greater harm.

Why mainstream antinatalists reject it

It would be a serious mistake to treat efilism as simply "antinatalism, more so." Many antinatalists reject it firmly, and for reasons internal to their own position. Three are worth naming.

It abandons the focus on future births. The whole moral cleanliness of antinatalism, as this book has presented it, lies in its being about refraining — declining to start a new life. The moment a view contemplates ending existing sentient lives, it is doing something categorically different: acting on beings who already exist, have interests, and overwhelmingly wish to continue. Refraining and exterminating are opposites, not points on a single scale.

It overrides consent in the very way antinatalism objected to. The antinatalist complaint was that birth imposes a momentous fate on someone who cannot agree. A program that ended sentient life would impose an even more momentous fate on beings who not only can be asked but, if asked, would almost all say no. The argument turned against itself.

It is unworkable and dangerous as anything but a thought experiment. There is no humane, consensual mechanism by which "ending all sentience" could even be approached, and treating it as a goal invites exactly the romanticizing of harm that the rest of antinatalist ethics is at pains to avoid.

Objection Efilism takes a careful, non-coercive ethical view — antinatalism — and collapses it into something that licenses or even romanticizes catastrophic harm. By extending "it would be better not to suffer" into "it would be better for sentience to end," it turns restraint into destruction and hands critics a reason to dismiss antinatalism wholesale.
Reply A defender of the view would answer that it is only following the logic of suffering consistently: if wild-animal suffering is real and vast, looking away from it is not obviously more virtuous than facing it, and the "end of sentience" is offered as a hypothetical about outcomes, not a call to action — most who use the label still oppose any actual coercion or violence and frame it as a grim thought experiment about a perfect, painless, impossible scenario. But this book does not endorse that step. Even granting the reality of animal suffering — which is a serious moral problem worth real attention — the move from "suffering is bad" to "sentience should be ended" is exactly the inference that the world-exploder objection shows to be a reductio. The humane response to vast suffering is to reduce it where we can and to stop adding to it by not procreating thoughtlessly — not to fantasize about a button. On the question of harm to existing beings, the author's voice is unambiguous: no.

Holding the line

Why include such a view in a sympathetic book at all? Because honesty requires it. Antinatalism is sometimes attacked by being conflated with its most extreme online cousin, and the only defense against that conflation is to understand the cousin clearly and to mark the boundary out loud. Efilism shows what happens when a real moral observation — that sentience brings suffering — is pushed past the point where consent, the interests of the living, and basic feasibility can restrain it. Mainstream antinatalism stops at non-procreation for precisely these reasons. That stopping point is not a failure of nerve; it is the whole ethical content of the view.

In plain words: Efilism extends antinatalist logic to all sentient life and, at its edge, imagines a painless end to sentience. It goes far beyond mainstream antinatalism, is rejected by many antinatalists, and this book does not endorse it. The serious, defensible position is simple: don't add new suffering by procreating — never harm those already here.
