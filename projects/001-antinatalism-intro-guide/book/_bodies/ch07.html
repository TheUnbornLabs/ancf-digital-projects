"To procreate is to gamble with a stranger's whole existence — and the stranger you are gambling with is the very person the bet creates."

— a paraphrase of the modern risk argument

Imagine being offered a wager. The stakes are an entire human life — every joy and every sorrow it will ever contain. You may not see the odds in advance, you cannot buy insurance against a bad result, and most importantly, the person whose life is on the table is not you. You place the bet on their behalf, before they exist, and once it is placed it can never be unmade. This is the antinatalist picture of procreation as risk-imposition: a one-time, uninsurable gamble staking someone else's whole existence on odds they never chose. This chapter takes that picture seriously, gives the strongest reply, and weighs the two.

Two ways to reason about risk

There are broadly two ways to think about a risky decision. The first is expected value: you tally the possible outcomes, weight each by its probability, and act if the average looks good. By this light, if most lives are net positive, procreation is on average a winning bet. The second is precautionary or worst-case reasoning: when an outcome could be catastrophic and irreversible, and when the person bearing the risk did not consent, you give special weight to avoiding the worst case rather than chasing the average.

Risk-imposition — exposing someone to the chance of harm through your choice. It matters here because procreation imposes the full range of life's risks on a new person who had no say in accepting them.

Antinatalists tend to favor the precautionary frame for procreation, and they have a specific reason. We normally let people run their own risks because they consented and can bail out — quit the job, leave the city, end the venture. A created life offers no such exit on the same terms: the person cannot have agreed to the odds beforehand, and the worst outcomes (severe illness, profound loss, a life dominated by suffering) cannot be returned or refunded.

The compensation problem

In most risky arrangements between adults, we soften the gamble with consent and compensation. You sign a waiver; you are paid a premium for hazardous work; if something goes wrong, there may be redress. None of this is available before birth. There is no one yet to consent to the odds, and no way to compensate a person in advance for a hardship they have not yet been brought into being to suffer.

In plain words: When we take risks for someone who can't agree, we usually do it only for their benefit and with great care. Procreation is unusual: the risk is imposed for reasons that are partly the parents' own, on a person who cannot be consulted and cannot be made whole if it goes badly.

This is where a non-antinatalist thinker becomes a useful companion to the argument. Rivka Weinberg, in The Risk of a Lifetime (2016), agrees that procreation is fundamentally a matter of imposing serious, unconsented risk on a future person. But she does not conclude that it is therefore forbidden. Instead she argues that the risk-imposition can be permissible when certain conditions are met — roughly, that prospective parents take on the role responsibly and the risks are kept within reasonable bounds. Weinberg is not an antinatalist; she is a careful theorist of when the gamble is acceptable. Citing her fairly shows that taking the risk framing seriously does not automatically land you at "never procreate." It is a fork in the road, and reasonable people take different branches.

"They'll probably be glad they were born"

The most natural rejoinder is that this all overdramatizes a bet that usually pays off. Most people, after all, are glad to be alive and would not undo their existence. Doesn't that retroactively justify the gamble?

The antinatalist asks us to examine that gladness rather than simply trust it. Several familiar mechanisms can inflate it. People adapt to their circumstances, recalibrating expectations so that even hard lives are reported as satisfactory. Survivorship bias means the people we hear from are, by definition, those who came through; the worst-off may be silent, diminished, or gone. And the optimism bias discussed earlier — Benatar's "Pollyannaism" — suggests our retrospective verdicts on life run systematically rosy. None of this proves people are wrong to be glad; it only warns that "most are glad" is weaker evidence than it feels, and cannot by itself license imposing the risk in the first place.

A bet can be unfair even when it happens to win.

There is also a subtler point about the timing of consent. Even if a grown person endorses their existence, that endorsement comes after they were given preferences, attachments, and a powerful stake in continuing. The bet had already been placed and the person already shaped by it; their later approval cannot reach back and supply the consent that was missing at the moment of decision.

Objection

Most lives genuinely go well, and most people, asked directly, affirm that they are glad to exist — that is revealed preference, not bias. And if the mere possibility of a catastrophic outcome forbids an action, then almost nothing is permissible: driving a child to school, performing surgery, even raising a family all impose serious unconsented risks. A principle that forbids procreation would forbid most worthwhile human action along with it.

Reply

The reply turns on two distinctions. First, the biases are not a way of calling people liars; they are reasons that aggregate self-reports are a noisy guide to whether imposing the risk was right. Adaptation and survivorship don't erase real gladness, but they do blunt its evidential force. Second, the parallel to ordinary risky acts misses an asymmetry the antinatalist leans on. Driving a child to school risks harm to a person who already exists and has an overriding interest in living their life; the risk serves their existing ends. Procreation has no such person to serve in advance — and on the asymmetry, the catastrophe avoided by not creating a life is a genuine bad prevented, while the joys foregone are not a loss to anyone, since there is no one deprived. That is why an avoidable catastrophe and a forgone joy do not weigh equally here. Whether the asymmetry truly holds is the deeper question; if it does, the risk argument has real force, and if it doesn't, the precautionary frame loses much of its grip.

Notice what the chapter does and does not claim. It does not say every life is bad, or that anyone now living should regret existing, or that the gamble always loses. It says that procreation is a serious, irreversible, unconsented risk imposed on someone else, and that this fact deserves real moral weight rather than the cheerful shrug our culture usually gives it. You can accept the description and still, like Weinberg, judge the gamble permissible. What the antinatalist asks is only that you not pretend there is no gamble at all.

Chapter in brief

  • Procreation can be seen as a one-time, uninsurable bet staking another person's whole life on odds they never chose.
  • Expected-value reasoning favors the bet if most lives go well; precautionary reasoning stresses the catastrophic, irreversible, unconsented worst case.
  • There is no way to obtain advance consent or offer advance compensation to a person who does not yet exist.
  • Rivka Weinberg accepts the risk framing but argues the gamble can be permissible under conditions — a non-antinatalist cousin, showing the framing doesn't force the conclusion.
  • "Most people are glad" is real but weakened by adaptation, survivorship bias, and optimism bias; on the asymmetry, an avoidable catastrophe and a forgone joy do not weigh the same.
