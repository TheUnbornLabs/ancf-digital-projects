BIAS 080

The Consent No One Can Give

Part VII · Philosophical & Ethical Fallacies · Procreative Ethics and the Problem of Imposing Existence

The bias: The created person cannot agree to be created.

To be able to speak of consent, there must first be someone to consent." — a common objection to the procreative-consent problem, stated here as the challenge this book must answer. --- ## Preface to this volume There is a peculiar structural fact at the heart of every act of procreation. The person most affected by the decision does not exist at the time it is made, and therefore cannot be consulted about it. We bring someone into being who will experience joys and injuries, satisfactions and losses, and who will eventually die, and we do all of this without, and necessarily without, their agreement. In no other domain do we so casually impose a lifelong and unrenounceable condition on a person who had no say. Some of us, on noticing this, feel a flicker of unease. This book is about that flicker: what it is, whether it tracks anything real, and why the dominant pronatalist culture treats it as too obviously confused to take seriously. The bias this volume examines is not the belief that procreation is permissible. It is the reflex that treats the consent question itself as a non-question, a category error, a philosopher's trick, something no reasonable person need pause over. That reflex is worth examining because it is so automatic. When a consideration is dismissed before it is heard, the dismissal deserves scrutiny at least as much as the consideration does. There is a general lesson in intellectual life here: the speed of a dismissal is often inversely related to its warrant. Claims we have genuinely thought through we can defend slowly, patiently, with reasons we can lay out. Claims we have merely inherited we defend fast, with a reflex that fires before the question has finished being asked. The consent question is dismissed fast. That alone does not show the dismissal to be wrong, but it should make us suspicious enough to look. Let me be plain about the stance. This book does not argue that the impossibility of prenatal consent makes procreation wrong. I think the strongest versions of that inference fail, and I will say why, at length and in their own strongest form, before I say anything in the worry's favor. What I will argue is narrower and, I hope, harder to dismiss. The consent worry identifies a real moral asymmetry, since creating a person imposes a serious, unchosen, unrenounceable condition on them, and this asymmetry generates responsibilities that pronatalist common sense obscures. The worry is not a proof. It is a pressure. And the bias is the habit of relieving that pressure too quickly, by means of arguments that, examined closely, prove less than they are asked to prove. The philosophical anchor here is the family of arguments around what is often called the procreative-consent problem, and in particular Seana Shiffrin's influential 1999 essay "Wrongful Life, Procreative Responsibility, and the Significance of Harm." Shiffrin does not argue that procreation is wrong. She argues, more subtly, that imposing existence is imposing a mixed condition, one carrying great goods but also serious, non-trivial harms and risks, on someone who cannot consent and who cannot even, in the ordinary case, be asked whether they would have. From this she draws conclusions about responsibility and about the significance of the harm involved, not a blanket prohibition. Her care is a model for this book. It is worth pausing on how unusual her care is in this debate, which tends to polarize between those who think coming into existence is a catastrophe and those who think the whole question is a joke. Shiffrin occupies the difficult middle: she takes the imposition seriously without concluding that it must not be done. That middle is exactly where this book tries to stand. I want to be equally clear about method. The steelman comes first, in Chapter 2, and it is not a straw effigy set up to be knocked down. The most powerful objection to the consent worry is that it is incoherent: you cannot secure consent from someone who does not yet exist, so demanding it is like demanding that we consult the unborn about the color of their nursery, impractical, yes, but also conceptually void. Worse, the demand seems to prove too much. If valid, it forbids all procreation, and any premise with that consequence is under suspicion. And we routinely, rightly, make momentous unconsented decisions for children, vaccinating them, schooling them, naming them, on the basis of hypothetical or proxy consent. Why should creation be different? These objections have real force. A reader who finishes Chapter 2 unpersuaded that the consent worry is dead has been failed by Chapter 2. I have tried to write that chapter as the ablest opponent of this book would write it, and then to spend the rest of the book showing not that the opponent is confused but that the opponent has proven less than the dismissal assumes. The rest of the book then asks whether anything survives. I will argue that something does: not a prohibition, but a reoriented sense of what procreation is, a serious imposition made on behalf of another, which places the burden of justification on the one who imposes, and which is not discharged merely by pointing out that the imposed-upon cannot complain. The measured conclusion is that the consent worry is best read not as an argument against having children but as an argument against a certain complacency about having them. If that sounds deflationary, so be it. Philosophy earns its keep more often by dissolving false confidence than by delivering dramatic verdicts, and the confidence dissolved here, that creation is self-evidently exempt from the moral scrutiny we bring to every other serious thing we do to another person, is worth dissolving. --- ## 1. Naming the bias The bias named in this book is the consent-dismissal reflex: the near-automatic treatment of the procreative-consent problem as a pseudo-problem, unworthy of moral attention, because the person whose consent is at issue does not yet exist. It is the move from "consent is impossible here" to "consent is therefore irrelevant here," a move that feels like logic but is in fact a substantive and contestable ethical claim smuggled in as an obvious one. Let me first say exactly what the underlying consent worry is, so that the bias against it can be seen clearly. The consent worry runs like this. In ordinary moral life, we recognize a strong presumption against imposing serious risks, burdens, or unrenounceable conditions on another person without their consent. I may not enroll you in a dangerous experiment, sign you up for a lifelong obligation, or alter your body without asking, however confident I am that you will benefit. Now consider procreation. To create a person is to impose on them the entire condition of existence: a life containing, with certainty, vulnerability, aging, loss, and death, and, with high probability, some measure of serious suffering. This is imposed on someone who could not agree to it. If the presumption against unconsented serious impositions is genuine, then procreation appears to be a very large instance of exactly the kind of act that presumption governs, and yet we treat it as needing no justification of the consent-respecting kind at all. It is worth dwelling on how strong the ordinary presumption is, because its strength is what makes procreation's exemption from it look so strange. The presumption against unconsented imposition is not a marginal ethical nicety; it is close to the center of how modern morality understands persons at all. It is why medicine requires informed consent, why research on human subjects is hedged with elaborate protections, why we recoil from paternalism even when the paternalist is right, why "it was for your own good" is an excuse rather than a justification. We treat the capacity to consent, or to refuse, as partly constitutive of what it is to respect someone as a person rather than to manage them as a thing. Against that background, an act that imposes not a single procedure but an entire life, and that no one thinks even needs a consent-respecting justification, is an anomaly. The consent worry is, at its most basic, the refusal to stop noticing the anomaly. That is the worry. It matters to see what it is not. It is not the claim that the created person is harmed on balance by being created; that is the axiological asymmetry associated with David Benatar (Better Never to Have Been, 2006), examined in Book 079, and it stands or falls independently. One can hold that most lives are well worth living and still ask whether it was permissible to impose one without consent. The consent worry is also distinct from the thought that life is a gift that grounds gratitude or obligation, examined in Book 081; indeed the consent worry is in tension with gift-talk, since a gift imposed on an unconsulted recipient is a strange sort of gift. The consent worry is narrower and more structural than either. It concerns the procedural fact that a momentous, self-affecting decision is made for a person, by others, without any possibility of their input. The one-sentence thesis of this book is this: The impossibility of prenatal consent does not make procreation wrong, but it does make procreation a serious imposition on another person that must be justified as such, and the bias worth naming is the reflex that treats the very impossibility of consent as though it dissolved, rather than deepened, the demand for justification. Consider the shape of the alleged fallacy. From "X's consent cannot be obtained" two very different conclusions might be drawn. One: "therefore the norm requiring consent does not apply, and we are free to act as we see fit." Two: "therefore the norm requiring consent cannot be satisfied, and we must ask what, if anything, could take its place, whether proxy judgment, restraint, or heightened responsibility." The consent-dismissal reflex leaps to the first. But impossibility of consent is, in every other domain, treated as a reason for heightened care, not diminished care. When a patient is unconscious and cannot consent, we do not conclude that anything may be done to them; we conclude that whoever acts bears a special burden to act in their interest. The bias consists in reversing this logic exactly where the stakes, the imposition of an entire existence, are highest. The reversal deserves a name of its own, because it is the engine of the whole bias. Call it the exculpatory reading of impossibility: the reading on which the fact that consent cannot be obtained lets the imposer off the hook rather than binding him more tightly. In no other serious case do we accept the exculpatory reading. A guardian who spent a ward's money freely, on the ground that the ward was too young to be consulted, would be understood to have things exactly backwards; the ward's inability to consent is precisely why the guardian's duty is stringent. The exculpatory reading, applied to procreation, would be recognized as monstrous if applied anywhere else. The consent-dismissal reflex is the local, and only local, acceptance of a reading we reject everywhere else, and it is applied at the very point where the imposition is largest and most irreversible. This book does not claim the reversal is never defensible. Creation may indeed be relevantly different from the unconscious-patient case, and Chapters 2 and 8 will press exactly that disanalogy. The claim is only that the difference must be argued, not assumed; that the leap from impossibility to irrelevance is where the bias lives; and that a culture which finds procreation self-evidently exempt from consent-talk has stopped noticing a question it ought at least to feel. Naming the reflex is the first move, because a reflex, unlike an argument, does its work in the dark. Once it is named, it can at least be made to appear in the light and answer for itself, which is all this chapter asks and all the bias most fears. --- --- Check Yourself — Chapter 1 Quiz. 1. ① In brief

Book 080 · 100 Biases of Natalism

Every act of procreation has a strange structure: the person most affected by it does not exist when the decision is made and so cannot be consulted. To create someone is to impose on them an entire existence, vulnerability, aging, loss, and certain death, along with the risk of serious suffering, without, and necessarily without, their agreement. In no other domain do we so casually settle a lifelong, unrenounceable condition on a person who had no say. This book examines the procreative-consent problem and, more precisely, the bias that keeps us from taking it seriously.

That bias is the consent-dismissal reflex: the near-automatic treatment of the consent question as a pseudo-problem, on the grounds that the person whose consent is at issue does not yet exist. The reflex slides from a true premise, consent is impossible here, to a much stronger and contestable conclusion, consent is therefore irrelevant here. The book's central observation is that this slide runs backward from how we reason everywhere else: when consent is impossible (an unconscious patient, future generations), we normally conclude that a heightened justificatory burden applies, not that anything goes.

Read the rest of the overview

The stance is deliberately modest and stated up front. The book does not argue that the impossibility of consent makes procreation wrong. Following the method of the series, Chapter 2 gives the strongest case against the consent worry first and in full: consent presupposes a subject and there is none before conception (the coherence objection); the worry, if valid, would condemn every birth including happy ones (the reductio); we legitimately make unconsented decisions for children all the time via proxy and hypothetical consent; and there is no comparatively-wronged victim (the non-identity "no one is wronged" reply). These objections are treated as largely sound. A reader who finishes Chapter 2 should feel the consent worry has been genuinely pressed, not staged.

What survives, the book argues, is narrower but real. Drawing on Seana Shiffrin's 1999 essay "Wrongful Life, Procreative Responsibility, and the Significance of Harm," it distinguishes the demand to obtain consent (impossible, conceded) from the recognition that procreation imposes a serious condition on another in the absence of any possible consent. That recognition does not forbid the act. It does two things: it places the burden of justification on the one who imposes, to be discharged by honestly working to make the imposed life one the person will have reason to endorse, not by noting that no one can complain, and it grounds a distinctive parental responsibility that arises precisely because the child could not consent.

The book keeps carefully in its lane. It is distinct from Benatar's axiological asymmetry (Book 079), which claims existence is a net harm; the consent worry is neutral on whether life is good. It is distinct from, and opposed to, the gift-of-life framing (Book 081), which dissolves the consent question in advance. And it uses the non-identity problem (Book 095) only insofar as it bears on consent. Along the way it maps how the reflex operates today, through defaults, beneficiary framing, the incredulous stare, and institutional pre-emption, and shows that its pressure falls hardest on women and on those least free to decline, while its history includes real coercive abuses the book firmly repudiates.

The measured takeaway is a change of posture, not of policy. Non-coercion is a fixed point: the worry, if it applies, applies to everyone equally and licenses no policy singling out any group. To see past the bias is not to become an antinatalist but to lose a certain complacency, the sense that bringing a person into being is self-evidently a benefit conferred, needing no justification of the kind we demand for every other serious imposition on another. The person who cannot give consent cannot, for that reason, be forgotten.

② The book in twelve chapters

Each chapter opens with its own summary. Note chapter 2: every volume states the strongest case for the thing it criticises before laying a finger on it — and chapter 8 turns the critique back on itself.

CH 1Naming the bias

The bias named in this book is the consent-dismissal reflex: the near-automatic treatment of the procreative-consent problem as a pseudo-problem, unworthy of moral attention, because the person whose consent is at issue does not yet exist. It is the move from "consent is impossible here" to "consent is therefore irrelevant here," a move that feels like logic but is in fact a substantive and contestable ethical claim smuggled in as an obvious one.

CH 2The strongest case for it (steelman)The strongest case against this book

I now state, as forcefully as I can and before any reply, the case that the consent worry is confused rather than merely weak, that the pronatalist dismissal is correct, and the flicker of unease that motivates this book is a philosopher's illusion. If the steelman is right, this book's thesis collapses. I do not think it is fully right, but I think it is close, and honesty requires giving it its full strength.

CH 3Where it comes from

If the consent-dismissal reflex is so quick, where does its confidence come from? Its sources are less philosophical than historical and structural. It is overdetermined by the conditions under which human beings have always reproduced. Overdetermination matters here, because it means the reflex would be firmly in place even if every single one of its intellectual supports were removed. A belief held for one reason can be dislodged by refuting that reason; a belief held for six reasons at once, none of them examined, is held not by argument but by inertia, and inertia is not moved by objections.

CH 4How it operates today

The consent-dismissal reflex is rarely stated as a thesis. It operates, instead, through defaults, scripts, and turns of phrase that make the consent question disappear before it can be asked. Naming these mechanisms is how the bias is brought into view. What follows is, in effect, a field guide to the ways a serious question gets processed out of existence without ever being answered.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

The consent worry is a normative claim, and normative claims are not settled by data. But several bodies of evidence bear on its premises: on how bad the imposed condition typically is, on whether the imposed-upon endorse it, and on the reliability of the intuitions the dismissal rests upon. Honesty requires saying what the evidence does and does not show, and, in particular, resisting the temptation, endemic on both sides of this debate, to let a normative conclusion masquerade as an empirical finding.

CH 6The hidden costs

What does the consent-dismissal reflex obscure? What goes unnoticed, or unpriced, when the consent question is processed out of the picture? The costs are not primarily to demographic aggregates; they are to the moral quality of how we think about, and undertake, the making of persons. It is characteristic of a bias, as opposed to an ordinary error, that its costs are borne quietly and diffusely, spread across countless decisions and lives, so that no single failure ever announces itself as the bias's fault. The point of this chapter is to gather those diffuse costs and give them a name.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

Biases are never evenly distributed, and the consent-dismissal reflex presses differently on different people. Because it functions to keep procreation unquestioned, its burden falls hardest on those for whom procreation is least free, and its blind spots fall hardest on those most likely to inherit a heavy existence. A bias that looks like a neutral intellectual error from the standpoint of the comfortable turns out, from other standpoints, to be an instrument that presses on some far harder than on others.

CH 8The counter-arguments to the critiqueObjections to the critique

This book's critique is that the consent worry deserves a hearing the dismissal denies it. The strongest objections to that critique were laid out in Chapter 2; here I engage them directly, and I concede more than a partisan would. The aim is not to score points but to establish exactly how much survives the objections, on the principle that a position which has honestly absorbed the strongest case against it is worth more than one that has only ever met weak versions of its opponents.

CH 9Case studies / illustrations

Abstractions about imposition and consent take on definite shape in particular cases. Four illustrations, drawn from law, medicine, philosophy, and ordinary life, show the consent worry, and its dismissal, at work. Each is chosen because it is a place where the structure the book has been describing becomes visible in a concrete institution or predicament, rather than remaining an abstraction that can be waved away.

CH 10Adjacent biases

The consent-dismissal reflex does not stand alone. It interlocks with several other biases in this series, borrowing strength from them and lending strength in return. Mapping the connections keeps this book in its lane while showing where the lane runs. A bias rarely operates in isolation; more often a cluster of mutually reinforcing biases surrounds a practice, each covering the others' weaknesses, so that an objection turned aside by one is caught by the next. The consent-dismissal reflex sits inside such a cluster, and part of understanding it is understanding how it hands off to its neighbors.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

Suppose the consent-dismissal reflex is named and set aside. What does clear-eyed reasoning about the consent question then look like, for individuals contemplating procreation, and for the institutions that shape it? Seeing past the bias is not arriving at a prohibition. It is arriving at a different quality of attention. The distinction between a change of verdict and a change of attention is the hinge of this whole chapter, and it is easy to miss: readers expecting an ethics to issue in a rule are apt to hear "no prohibition" as "no upshot," when the upshot is real but lies in how the decision is made rather than in which decision is reached.

CH 12Conclusion

I have argued for a deliberately modest conclusion, and it is worth stating exactly what it is and is not. It is not that the impossibility of prenatal consent makes procreation wrong. The strongest objections to that inference (that consent presupposes a subject and there is none, that the argument would condemn every birth, that proxy and hypothetical consent do real justificatory work, that there is no comparatively-wronged victim) are, I have conceded, largely sound. Anyone who came to this book hoping for a consent-based proof that having children is impermissible should leave without it. That proof, I think, does not exist, and I have tried to show why by giving its refutation first and in full. I have not hedged those concessions or held anything in reserve; the book means them. The strong consent argument fails, and it fails for the reasons its ablest critics have given.

Procreative-consent problem
The moral difficulty that the person created by an act of procreation cannot consent to being created, since they do not exist when the decision is made.
Consent-dismissal reflex
The bias named in this book: the automatic treatment of the procreative-consent problem as a pseudo-problem, inferring from the impossibility of consent that consent is morally irrelevant.
Imposition of existence
The bringing of a person into being, understood as settling upon them an entire life-condition (including mortality and risk) without their agreement.
Hypothetical consent
Justification of an unconsented act by appeal to what the affected person would, or would have reason to, agree to; central both to the pronatalist reply and to the consent worry's constructive residue.
Proxy consent
Consent given on another's behalf by someone empowered to judge their interests; legitimate for existing children, but of contested application to the founding decision to create a person.
Non-comparative harm
A harm understood as a bad condition imposed on a person, not as a person's being made worse off than they otherwise would have been; invoked (after Shiffrin) to preserve the worry against the non-identity reply.
Non-identity structure
The feature of procreative acts whereby the act said to wrong a person is the very act that constitutes them, so that no possible person is made worse off.
"No one is wronged" reply
The objection that, since there is no pre-existing subject and the existing person endorses their life, there is no victim and hence no wrong.
Coherence objection
The objection that consent requires a subject who can be asked, and none exists before conception, so a consent requirement has an empty domain and cannot be what makes procreation wrong.
The reductio
The objection that the consent worry, if valid, condemns all procreation including happy lives — an implausible conclusion said to discredit the premise.
Beneficiary framing
The linguistic and cultural casting of the created person as a lucky recipient of the gift of life, which suppresses the description of creation as imposition.
Incredulous stare
The social mechanism by which the consent worry is dismissed through expressed disbelief rather than argument, imposing a cost on merely entertaining the question.
Adaptive preference
The tendency to reconcile one's preferences to one's actual circumstances, which complicates the use of retrospective life-endorsement as evidence of hypothetical consent.
Reproductive autonomy
The value protecting individuals' freedom over their own procreative choices; invoked here as a fixed constraint ruling out any coercive use of the consent worry.
Wrongful-life claim
A legal action alleging that a person was harmed by being born into a severely impaired condition; the paradigm case where courts confront both the consent worry and the non-identity problem.
"Ought implies can"
The principle that one cannot be morally required to do what is impossible; deployed by the steelman to argue that an unsatisfiable consent requirement cannot be a genuine requirement.
