"For any possible population of at least ten billion people, all with a very high quality of life, there must be some much larger imaginable population whose existence... would be better, even though its members have lives that are barely worth living."

— Derek Parfit, stating the Repugnant Conclusion

So far we have weighed the value of a single life — whether to begin one, and whether the one begun can be wronged by its beginning. But ethics also has to reckon with numbers. How many people should there be? Is a world with more happy people better than a world with fewer? Questions like these belong to a field called population ethics, and they turn out to be among the hardest in all of moral philosophy. The good news, for our purposes, is that they are hard for everyone — not only for antinatalists. The puzzles below trouble the cheerful optimist who thinks more happy people is always better just as much as they trouble anyone else.

Parfit and the Repugnant Conclusion

The modern study of these questions begins, for most readers, with Derek Parfit's 1984 masterwork Reasons and Persons. Parfit asked a deceptively simple question: if adding a happy person to the world makes the world better, where does that reasoning end? Follow it carefully and it leads somewhere unsettling. Imagine a world of ten billion people, all flourishing. Now imagine a far larger world — hundreds of billions — whose members have lives only barely worth living, lives that contain just a sliver more good than bad. If we simply add up the total amount of good, the second world can come out ahead, because there is so much more of it. The theory says the vast, dreary multitude is better than the smaller paradise. Parfit called this the Repugnant Conclusion, and he meant the name.

The Repugnant Conclusion — Derek Parfit's result that, on theories which value the total sum of well-being, a sufficiently enormous population of lives "barely worth living" must count as better than a smaller population of genuinely flourishing lives. It is widely felt to be false, yet it is remarkably hard to escape without giving up some very plausible assumption.

The mere addition paradox

How does the reasoning get there? Through what Parfit called the mere addition paradox. Start with a flourishing population, call it A. Now add a second, separate group of people whose lives are good but somewhat less so — they take nothing away from A; they are simply extra lives worth living. This larger world, A+, seems no worse than A: how could adding more good lives, harming no one, make things worse? Next, level things out so everyone is equal but at a slightly lower average — a world that contains more total well-being than A and distributes it fairly, which looks like an improvement. Repeat the steps, and each small move seems reasonable, yet the chain marches us straight to the repugnant world of barely-worth-living lives. Every individual step looks acceptable; the destination looks monstrous. That gap is the paradox.

The Procreation Asymmetry

There is a second puzzle, closer to the heart of this book, and it cuts in the antinatalist's favor. Most people feel a powerful intuition that we have a strong duty not to create a person who will be miserable — bringing a doomed, suffering child into the world would be deeply wrong. Yet hardly anyone feels a matching duty to create a person who would be happy. We do not think a couple does something wrong by choosing not to have a child who, had they existed, would have lived a wonderful life. This lopsidedness — strong reason against creating misery, little or no reason to create happiness — is the Procreation Asymmetry, discussed by philosophers such as Jan Narveson and Jeff McMahan.

The Asymmetry matters because it is hard to explain on the view that making happy people is simply good. If adding happiness were a benefit we owed the future, failing to have the happy child should feel like a loss, even a wrong — but it does not. Antinatalists, including David Benatar, take this widely shared intuition as a clue: perhaps creating a happy life is not, in fact, a gift we are obligated to give, while creating a suffering life is genuinely a harm. The Asymmetry does not by itself prove antinatalism, but it shows that the pole opposite to "more happy people is always better" is not some bizarre invention. It tracks something most people already believe.

We feel we must not create the miserable, yet feel no duty to create the happy. Why the difference?

Where antinatalism sits

Population ethics can be pictured as a spectrum. At one end stands total utilitarianism, which says the more well-being the better and so leans toward creating as many worthwhile lives as possible — and which is precisely what runs aground on the Repugnant Conclusion. At the other end sits antinatalism, which holds that adding people is not required, and in its strong form is bad. Most people live somewhere in the muddled middle, wanting to say that some additions are good and others are not, without a tidy rule for which. The lesson is that there is no comfortable, paradox-free home anywhere on this spectrum. Counting future people resists every neat theory we have tried.

And the Asymmetry, however intuitive, is just one intuition among many; you cannot leap from "we have no duty to create happy people" to "we have a duty not to create people at all." Puzzles in population ethics show that the math is hard. They do not show that we should stop having children.
A replyThis is right, and the honest antinatalist grants it: none of these puzzles vindicates antinatalism on its own. The Asymmetry is consistent with antinatalism but does not entail it, and the Repugnant Conclusion is a problem for totalism specifically. But notice the modest work the chapter is doing. The puzzles dismantle the most common reply to antinatalism — the cheerful assumption that adding happy people is plainly good, so of course we should keep having children. That assumption is exactly what the Repugnant Conclusion and the mere addition paradox put under unbearable strain. So while population ethics does not hand antinatalism a victory, it removes the easy confidence of its opponents. It shows that the antinatalist's hardest questions — does adding a life make the world better? do we owe the future its happy people? — are questions no one has answered cleanly. Difficulty shared is not difficulty disproved, but it does end the pretense that the antinatalist alone has a counting problem.

In plain words: Working out how many people there should be breaks almost every moral theory we try. "More happy people is always better" leads to a world we find repugnant; "we owe the future happy children" clashes with the strong sense that we don't. These puzzles don't prove antinatalism, but they do strip away the breezy confidence that having children is obviously, costlessly good.

Chapter in brief

  • Population ethics asks how the number of future people bears on how good a world is — and it stumps nearly every theory.
  • Parfit's Repugnant Conclusion (1984) shows that valuing total well-being leads to favoring vast populations of lives "barely worth living."
  • The mere addition paradox reaches that result through a chain of individually reasonable steps.
  • The Procreation Asymmetry — strong reason not to create misery, little reason to create happiness — is intuitive and friendly to antinatalism, though it does not prove it.
  • Antinatalism sits at the pole opposite total utilitarianism; the real point is that counting future people is hard for everyone, which deflates the easy reply that having children is obviously good.
