"The best of all things is not to be born; the second best is, having been born, to die as soon as possible."

— the wisdom of Silenus, as reported in Greek tradition

Long before antinatalism had a name, it had a voice in literature. Poets, playwrights, and novelists have given shape to the thought "it would have been better not to be born" far more often, and far more vividly, than philosophers have. This chapter walks through some of those moments — not to claim that art proves the antinatalist case, but to show how persistently the human imagination has circled the same dark question, and how seriously great writers have taken it.

Silenus and the ancient world

The oldest and starkest expression comes from Greek myth. Silenus, companion of the god Dionysus, is said to have answered the question of what is best for human beings with the bleak counsel quoted above: best never to be born, and next best to die soon. The "wisdom of Silenus" echoes through Greek tragedy, where the chorus more than once observes that the unborn are spared what the living must endure. Friedrich Nietzsche later made this saying central to his reading of Greek culture in The Birth of Tragedy (1872), treating it as the pessimistic truth that art was invented to make bearable.

A related world-weariness runs through the Hebrew book of Ecclesiastes, whose narrator surveys human striving and pronounces much of it "vanity," at one point suggesting that those who have not yet been are better off than both the living and the dead. These are not systematic arguments. They are something older: the recurring human suspicion that existence is a heavier gift than it looks.

Philosophical pessimism — the broad tradition holding that suffering outweighs or undermines the goods of existence, so that life is, on balance, not the benefit it is assumed to be. Antinatalism is one possible conclusion of this tradition, but the two are not identical: one can be a pessimist without being an antinatalist, and vice versa.

Schopenhauer's long shadow

The nineteenth-century philosopher Arthur Schopenhauer turned this mood into a system, and his influence on literature was immense. Writers across Europe absorbed his picture of a world driven by a blind, insatiable will that guarantees frustration. Through him, pessimism became a literary current rather than a private complaint, shaping novelists and poets who never read a page of formal philosophy but breathed its atmosphere.

One of the most harrowing fictional descendants of that mood is Thomas Hardy's novel Jude the Obscure (1895). The book is famous, among other reasons, for an episode involving a despairing child nicknamed "Father Time," who voices the conviction that children should never have been born. Without dwelling on the details, which are genuinely painful, it is enough to say that Hardy used the scene to dramatize the thought at the heart of this book — "we should not have been born" — and that the public outcry it provoked was so fierce it helped drive Hardy away from novel-writing altogether. It remains one of literature's most direct, and most disturbing, expressions of an antinatalist sentiment.

Cioran and Ligotti: pessimism as literature

In the twentieth century the line between philosophy and literature blurs in two figures worth knowing. The Romanian-French writer E. M. Cioran composed aphorisms — short, polished, mordant — that return again and again to the misfortune of having been born. His book The Trouble with Being Born (1973) is exactly what its title promises: a literary meditation, not a treatise, on the thought that coming into existence is the original calamity. Cioran is read as much for his style as for his ideas; he is a stylist of despair.

The American horror writer Thomas Ligotti carried the tradition into our own time with The Conspiracy Against the Human Race (2010), a nonfiction work of philosophical horror that draws explicitly on antinatalist and pessimist sources. Ligotti treats consciousness itself as a kind of cruel accident, and his bleak vision would soon reach a far larger audience than any book of his ever had.

The same dark question keeps finding new voices — chorus, prophet, novelist, screenwriter.

Pessimism on the screen

That larger audience arrived through television. The first season of True Detective (2014) gave its detective Rust Cohle a series of monologues steeped in Ligotti's pessimism — most famously the claim that human self-awareness is a kind of evolutionary mistake and that the most ethical thing our species could do is decline to reproduce. The show's writer openly acknowledged Ligotti's influence, and overnight, ideas that had lived in obscure books were being quoted by millions. Cohle is a character, not a spokesman, and the series treats his bleakness as something to wrestle with rather than simply endorse — but it put antinatalist language into mainstream culture as nothing before it had.

Cinema offers its own version in Lars von Trier's Melancholia (2011), in which a depressed protagonist faces the end of the world with an eerie calm, declaring at one point that life on Earth is bad and will not be missed. The film is not an argument; it is a mood rendered in image and music, a portrait of pessimism that asks the viewer to sit inside it for a while. Like Hardy's novel and Cohle's monologues, it dramatizes a feeling with great force — which is exactly where the honest objection arises.

Objection

"All of this is art dramatizing a mood, not establishing a truth. A harrowing novel or a charismatic detective makes pessimism feel profound, but feeling is not proof. You cannot argue from Melancholia to a moral conclusion."

Reply

This is correct, and an honest antinatalist should concede it cheerfully. Hardy's "Father Time" and Rust Cohle prove nothing; they express something. Art's job here is not to demonstrate but to make vivid — to show that the thought "better not to have been born" is not a fringe glitch but a recurring response to the human condition, voiced by serious imaginations across millennia. The actual case for antinatalism rests on the arguments about consent, risk, and asymmetry made elsewhere in this book, and those must stand or fall on their own. Literature does two useful things alongside them: it keeps the question from being dismissed as merely modern or merely morbid, and it reminds us that whatever conclusion we reach should be able to face the full weight of what these writers saw. A philosophy that cannot survive contact with Hardy is not worth much. But a novel is not a syllogism, and we should never pretend it is one.

Chapter in brief

  • The "wisdom of Silenus" and the world-weariness of Ecclesiastes show the thought "better not to be born" is ancient, not modern.
  • Schopenhauer turned pessimism into a system and seeded a literary current; Thomas Hardy's Jude the Obscure (1895) gave it one of fiction's most harrowing expressions.
  • Cioran and Ligotti wrote pessimism as literature — aphorism and philosophical horror respectively.
  • True Detective season 1 (2014) carried Ligotti's ideas to a mass audience; von Trier's Melancholia (2011) rendered pessimism on film.
  • Art makes the question vivid, not proven; the case still rests on the arguments, not the drama.
