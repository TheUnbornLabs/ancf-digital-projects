"Birth is suffering, aging is suffering, illness is suffering, death is suffering."

— from the Buddha's first discourse (traditional)

Long before European pessimism gave the modern argument its shape, contemplative traditions in Asia were thinking carefully about suffering, rebirth, and the value of coming into existence. It is natural for readers of antinatalism to feel a flicker of recognition when they first encounter Buddhist and Jain teachings, and that recognition is not imaginary. But it must be handled with care. These are vast, living religions with their own ends, their own ethics, and their own joys — not early drafts of a modern thesis. This chapter aims to describe them accurately and respectfully, and then to ask honestly what the resonance does and does not mean.

Buddhism: dukkha, samsara, and release

The Buddha's teaching begins with what are called the Four Noble Truths, and the first of them concerns dukkha — a word usually translated "suffering" but better understood as "unsatisfactoriness." The claim is not that every moment is agony, but that ordinary existence is shot through with a subtle dis-ease: things change, pleasures fade, and we are never finally at rest. Birth, aging, illness, and death are all named as forms of dukkha.

This dis-ease unfolds across samsara, the cycle of rebirth. In the Buddhist vision a being is reborn again and again, life after life, and what keeps the wheel turning is tanha — craving or thirst — together with ignorance about the true nature of things. We grasp, we cling, we want existence to be other than it is, and that very grasping pulls us back into another birth.

Dukkha — the first of the Four Noble Truths: the pervasive unsatisfactoriness of conditioned existence. Samsara — the beginningless cycle of birth, death, and rebirth. Tanha — craving, the thirst that drives the cycle onward. Nirvana — the "blowing out" of that craving, and so the cessation of the cycle.

The Buddhist goal is therefore not a better birth but liberation from the cycle altogether: nirvana, the "blowing out" of the fires of craving, aversion, and delusion. In many traditions the monastic path, including celibacy, supports this aim — a life arranged so as not to be pulled back into the round of becoming. Here the parallel with antinatalism feels closest: a tradition that treats continued birth as something to be transcended rather than simply celebrated.

Jainism: ahimsa, karma, and the bound soul

Jainism, an ancient Indian tradition contemporaneous with early Buddhism, shares the framework of rebirth but develops it in its own striking direction. At its moral heart is ahimsa — non-harm — held with a thoroughness that few other traditions approach. The devout Jain takes extraordinary care not to injure any living being, down to the smallest creature, because every jiva, every soul, is precious.

The Jain account of why we are bound to the world is distinctive. Karma is understood almost physically, as a kind of subtle matter that flows into the soul and weighs it down, keeping it trapped in the cycle of rebirth. The spiritual task is to stop this influx and burn off what has accumulated, and the chosen means is severe asceticism — disciplined restraint of action, appetite, and harm, including monastic celibacy. The aim is moksha: the liberation of the soul from matter and from rebirth entirely.

A tradition may treat rebirth as a burden without treating any living being as a mistake.

Notice what is and is not being said. The Jain reveres life so deeply that he will sweep the path before he steps; yet he also seeks release from the cycle of birth. The combination is not a contradiction. It reflects a worldview in which existing beings are owed the utmost care and in which the endless round of becoming is a condition to be transcended. Reverence for the living and a longing for liberation sit together.

In plain words: Buddhism and Jainism both see ordinary existence as bound up with suffering and rebirth, and both offer a path to liberation from the cycle — often including celibacy. But each is a complete religion with its own goal (nirvana, moksha) and its own ethics, not a program for ending births.

Resonance is not identity

It is tempting, having seen all this, to declare these traditions "antinatalist." That would be a mistake, and an unfair one. The differences are not minor. Antinatalism, as a modern secular argument, concerns whether to bring a particular future child into this one earthly life, and it rests on claims about consent and harm that make no reference to rebirth. Buddhism and Jainism concern liberation across many lives from a metaphysical cycle, and their celibacy is a spiritual discipline aimed at moksha or nirvana, not a population conclusion or a verdict that procreation wrongs the unborn.

Moreover, neither tradition condemns the householder. Most Buddhists and Jains marry and have children, and their traditions honor that life while reserving the monastic path for those called to it. The renunciate's celibacy is one road among several, undertaken for liberation — not a rule that birth itself is an injury to be prevented.

ObjectionTo read modern antinatalism into Buddhism and Jainism is an appropriation. These are contemplative traditions with aims utterly different from a secular argument about consent and harm — their whole framework of rebirth, karma, and liberation is foreign to it. Lifting a few resonant phrases out of that framework and recruiting ancient religions as ancestors of a contemporary thesis distorts them and flatters the thesis.
ReplyThe objection is right to insist on the difference, and this chapter has tried to honor it: resonance is not identity, and these traditions are not antinatalist programs. But noticing a resonance is not the same as claiming descent. The honest observation is narrower and survives the objection: across very different metaphysics, serious and humane traditions have independently concluded that ordinary existence is deeply bound up with suffering, and that continued birth is not a self-evident good to be assumed without thought. That convergence is interesting precisely because the frameworks differ — it suggests the underlying intuition is not a quirk of one modern mood. What we must not do is collapse the traditions into the thesis or use them as decoration. Treated with respect, they are interlocutors, not ancestors — voices worth listening to on their own terms, which sometimes rhyme with ours.

Listening on their own terms

The right posture, then, is one of attentive humility. A modern antinatalist can learn from how these traditions describe the texture of dukkha, from the Jain's uncompromising care for every living thing, and from a vision in which liberation matters more than continuation. But learning is not annexing. These are paths people walk for their own reasons, toward their own goals, sustained by communities and practices that long predate the word "antinatalism." To meet them with genuine respect is to let them keep their own meaning — and to be glad, rather than possessive, when their wisdom touches our own questions.

Chapter in brief

  • Buddhism begins with dukkha (the unsatisfactoriness of existence) and sees rebirth (samsara) as driven by craving (tanha); liberation is nirvana, often supported by monastic celibacy.
  • Jainism centers on ahimsa (radical non-harm), understands karma as matter binding the soul, and pursues moksha through severe asceticism, including celibacy.
  • Both treat ordinary existence and continued rebirth as conditions to be transcended — a genuine resonance with antinatalism.
  • But resonance is not identity: these are complete religions with their own metaphysics and goals, and neither condemns the householder or frames birth as a wrong against the unborn.
  • The respectful posture is to treat them as interlocutors, not ancestors — learning from them without recruiting them.
