Every serious debate has a working vocabulary, and arguments stall when the words slide around. Antinatalism leans on a small cluster of terms drawn from ethics, decision theory, and the philosophy of birth — most of them ordinary words used with unusual precision. This chapter lays them out one at a time. You do not need to memorize them; you need only to recognize them when they appear in later chapters, so that the arguments land cleanly. We will keep each definition short and add a sentence on why it matters, because a term you cannot use is a term you do not really have.

The core moral terms

Consent — agreement to something that affects you, given by you, in advance. It matters here because the person a birth affects most cannot give it: they do not yet exist to be asked, which makes procreation a decision taken entirely on someone else's behalf.
Harm and benefit — to harm is to set back someone's interests; to benefit is to advance them. These matter because the whole debate turns on whether coming into existence can sensibly be called a harm or a benefit to the one who comes to exist.
The asymmetry — David Benatar's claim that the absence of pain is good even when no one enjoys that good, while the absence of pleasure is not bad unless someone is thereby deprived. It matters because, if true, it implies that not creating a person avoids a real bad (their future pain) without incurring a real loss (no one is around to miss the foregone pleasure).

The asymmetry is the engine of much modern antinatalism, so it is worth seeing its four parts together: the presence of pain is bad; the presence of pleasure is good; the absence of pain is good even if no one enjoys that absence; but the absence of pleasure is not bad unless there is someone for whom it is a deprivation. The contested move is between those last two claims, and we examine it closely in its own chapter. For now, just hold the shape of it.

Terms about future and possible people

The non-identity problem — the puzzle, sharpened by Derek Parfit in Reasons and Persons (1984), that many of our choices determine which person comes to exist, so a person born of such a choice cannot usually claim they were made worse off than they would otherwise have been. It matters because it complicates the simple idea that procreation "harms" a specific child.
Person-affecting views — the principle that an act can be good or bad only insofar as it is good or bad for someone. It matters because, on a strict reading, it is hard to say a possible person is wronged by never existing — there is no one there to wrong — which both helps and complicates the antinatalist case.
Deprivation — being denied a good one would otherwise have had. It matters because the asymmetry rests on the idea that you can only be deprived of a pleasure if you exist; an uncreated person misses nothing, because there is no one to do the missing.

Terms about freedom and society

Autonomy (and reproductive autonomy) — the right to govern your own life and body, including decisions about whether to have children. It matters because antinatalism is an ethical argument addressed to free choosers, not a call for anyone to be coerced; reproductive autonomy cuts in several directions at once.
Pronatalism — the broad social presumption that having children is good, expected, and admirable, often invisible precisely because it is everywhere. It matters because antinatalists argue that this background pressure lets the procreation decision go unexamined, treating an open question as already settled.
Suffering-focused ethics — an approach that gives special moral weight to preventing suffering, more than to producing additional happiness. It matters because much antinatalist reasoning runs on this asymmetric concern: a serious bad avoided counts for more than a good not created.
Optimism bias ("Pollyannaism") — the well-documented human tendency to expect better outcomes than the evidence warrants and to remember life as rosier than it was; Benatar calls it "Pollyannaism." It matters because, if our self-reports about how good life is are systematically inflated, then the cheerful consensus in favor of birth may be partly an artifact of the bias.

A deliberate contrast

Not every neighbor of antinatalism is an ally, and one term is included precisely to mark a boundary.

Procreative beneficence — Julian Savulescu's proposed principle that, if one is going to have a child, one has reason to choose the child expected to have the best life. It matters as a contrast: it is a view about which child to have, not about whether to have one at all, and so it is not antinatalism — it actually presupposes procreation.

Keeping that contrast clear prevents a common confusion. Many positions in the philosophy of birth are concerned with doing reproduction well; antinatalism is concerned with whether to do it at all. They can share a vocabulary without sharing a conclusion. A related non-antinatalist cousin, which we meet later, treats procreation as a permissible risk-imposition under conditions rather than as something always wrong.

In plain words: Most of these words are everyday words used carefully. The trick is noticing when an argument quietly switches between "harm to an existing person" and "harm to a merely possible one" — that switch is where most confusion lives.

One small puzzle, before we go on

Objection

Several of these terms assume you can "harm" or "benefit" someone who never exists. But harm has to happen to a person. There is no one in the void waiting to be spared, so talk of helping or hurting the never-born is just confused.

Reply

This is a fair pressure, and good antinatalists feel its weight. The careful reply is that the relevant comparison is not "the never-born versus a happier never-born," but a judgment about states of affairs: a world with a given suffering in it versus a world without that suffering and without anyone who misses the foregone goods. On that framing — the heart of the asymmetry — we are not claiming to benefit a ghost, only to avoid producing a real harm at no real cost. Whether that framing succeeds is exactly what later chapters test.

That is the toolkit. With these terms in hand, the arguments ahead can be stated in their strongest form and questioned just as strongly. When a later chapter says "person-affecting" or "the asymmetry" or "Pollyannaism," you will know which gear is turning.

Chapter in brief

  • Consent, harm, and benefit are the moral anchors: a future person cannot consent, and the debate hinges on whether existence harms or benefits them.
  • The asymmetry (absence of pain is good; absence of pleasure is not bad unless someone is deprived) is the engine of much modern antinatalism.
  • The non-identity problem, person-affecting views, and deprivation govern how we talk about future and merely possible people.
  • Reproductive autonomy, pronatalism, suffering-focused ethics, and optimism bias / Pollyannaism frame the freedom-and-society side of the debate.
  • Procreative beneficence (Savulescu) is included as a contrast — a view about which child to have, not whether, and therefore not antinatalism.
