The Symmetry Objection and the Formal Attacks

T he last chapter built the asymmetry's defenses. This one turns them around and opens fire, because a foundation that has only ever been admired has never been tested, and an untested foundation is the one that fails when the weight goes on. The objections here are not straw men. They are the actual reasons actual philosophers, Harman, DeGrazia, Smuts, and others by name, reject the asymmetry, set out as strongly as their authors press them. One of them, the symmetry objection, is presented as what this volume takes it to be: the single best reason a thoughtful person has to decline the whole argument. Finish this chapter still holding the asymmetry and you should hold it differently than you did at the start. Not as a proof you own, but as a position you have watched its ablest critics attack and judged, on balance, to survive, while knowing what that "on balance" is hiding.

The chapter takes six attacks, deepest first: the symmetry objection, the formal attack from neutrality, the no-subject objection, the extreme-case objection, the retrospective-endorsement objection, and then the named critics whose lines do not reduce to the others. Each gets stated at strength, answered as well as it can honestly be answered, and graded.

The Symmetry Objection

This is the one that matters most. It is sometimes called the consistency objection, and its logic fits in a paragraph, though it has occupied the literature since the year Benatar's book came out.

The asymmetry treats Premises Three and Four differently. Absent pain is good, by Three. Absent pleasure is merely not bad, by Four. The objection asks by what right the difference is kept. Look at how Four is justified. Absent pleasure is "not bad," it says, because there is no one deprived of it; the badness of the missing pleasure is made to depend on a subject who suffers the want. That justification is person-affecting through and through. So apply the same standard to Three. Absent pain should then be "not good," because there is no one who benefits from its absence, exactly as there is no one deprived by the missing pleasure. If absent pleasure cannot be bad without a subject to be deprived, then by parity absent pain cannot be good without a subject to be relieved. Hold both premises to one standard and the asymmetry evaporates. Either both absences get the person-affecting treatment, in which case absent pain is not good and absent pleasure is not bad, a symmetry of two zeros that yields no verdict; or both get the impersonal treatment, in which case absent pain is good and absent pleasure is bad, a symmetry of a good and a bad that again yields no verdict against existence. The asymmetry survives, the critic concludes, only by switching standards midstream: impersonal generosity for Three, person-affecting strictness for Four. Name the switch and the structure flattens into symmetry, and a symmetric ledger condemns nothing.

This is a formidable objection, and softening it is the very temptation this series exists to resist. It denies no premise outright. It accepts the whole framework and finds an inconsistency inside it, which is the most dangerous kind of objection, because you cannot answer it by insisting harder on the intuitions. The intuitions are not in dispute. The even-handedness of their treatment is. Versions of this objection have been pressed by many of Benatar's critics; it is the common engine under a large share of the professional literature against the asymmetry, and a defender who has not felt its force has not understood it.

The best answer runs straight through the previous chapter's strongest move, which is why that move was built before this objection was raised. Premises Three and Four are not assigned their values by two inconsistent standards picked arbitrarily and then noticed after the fact. They are assigned their values by whatever standard best explains the four sub-asymmetries, and that standard, whatever its ultimate character, delivers "good" for absent pain and "not bad" for absent pleasure because that is the only pairing that makes sense of the four judgments we hold independently and stubbornly. The defender does not stipulate the asymmetric values first and then trip over an embarrassing inconsistency. He reads the values off the four asymmetries, which were fixed by common moral sense before the premises were ever written. So the apparent inconsistency is not arbitrary. It is the asymmetry of our considered judgments about procreational duty, prospective reasons, retrospective regret, and distant absence, transcribed faithfully into the language of value. To "restore symmetry," as the objection demands, you would have to revise those four judgments: accept a duty to create happy people, find it sensible to create a child for its own sake, grieve on behalf of the uncreated, mourn the empty planet. Almost no one will revise even one of them, let alone all four.

That is a real answer. But here the chapter has to do the thing the series was built to do, the thing that separates an education from a recruitment. The answer is not decisive, and the reason is that the critic has a reply, and the reply is good. The four sub-asymmetries, the critic says, can be explained without the impersonal Premise Three, by a wide person-affecting principle:

roughly, that in comparing two outcomes we count the welfare of anyone who exists in either one, which gives a reason not to create the miserable, who exist in the create-outcome and suffer there, without giving any reason to create the happy, since there is no prior claimant whose deprivation could count against not creating them. Frick's conditional-reasons account, mentioned a moment ago, makes a related move from another angle: the reason to confer a good life is conditional on the person's existing, and so cannot be a reason to bring them into existence, while the reason not to impose a bad life is unconditional. If the wide principle or Frick's structure explains all four asymmetries, and their defenders argue with real ingenuity that they do, then the basic asymmetry is not the best explanation after all. It is merely one explanation, and one that drags in the heavy, contested machinery of impersonal value when a lighter person-affecting rival does the same work. At that point the symmetry objection has not been refuted. It has been fought to a draw, serious philosophers on each side, no decisive consideration in view, and the verdict is that the question is open. The defender can fairly point out that the rivals carry their own well-known troubles: wide person-affecting principles are hard to state without either collapsing into impersonalism or giving wrong verdicts in "mere addition" and mirror cases; conditional-reasons accounts must say why the conditional runs one way and not the reverse. Those troubles are real and should be pressed. But the defender cannot say the rivals are beaten, and he discredits the series the moment he pretends they are. The most this volume will claim is that the asymmetry remains a strong contender among live options, with one clearly marked spot, the even-handedness of Premises Three and Four, where a reasonable, informed person may get off the train. That marked exit is not a confession dragged out reluctantly. It is the coordinate every reader needs in order to know where their own weight rests.

The Formal Attack: Neutrality and Transitivity

The symmetry objection is a charge of inconsistency, pressed in ordinary moral language. There is a sharper version, made in the formal language of betterness, and it is the most rigorous attack the asymmetry faces. It targets Premise Four, and its author is John Broome.

Recast the second intuition as a claim not about reasons but about value, the way Benatar's axiology invites. Premise Four says the absence of pleasure is not bad. Read evaluatively, that becomes a claim about whole worlds: a world with an extra happy person added is neither better nor worse than the world without them, other things equal. The added life, being good for the one who lives it but answering no prior deprivation, leaves the world's value unchanged. Broome calls this the intuition of neutrality, and it is the same thought as Narveson's dictum, that morality favours making people happy but stays neutral about making happy people, now stated as a relation between outcomes. It is exactly what Premise Four needs if the asymmetry is to be an axiology, a ranking of states of the world.

Broome's argument, in Weighing Lives, is that neutrality so understood cannot be sustained, because it collides with transitivity, one of the most basic formal features of the relation "better than." The collision is easy to see. Take a world with no extra person. Add a person at a modest but positive level of wellbeing, and by neutrality the result is neither better nor worse, equally good. Now imagine that same added person at a higher level of wellbeing; again, by neutrality, neither better nor worse than the world without them, equally good. But the high-wellbeing world is plainly better than the low- wellbeing one, since it is the same person living a better life. So we have two worlds equally good with a third, which by transitivity should be equally good with each other, and yet one is clearly better than the other. Neutrality, read as a relation of equal goodness, breaks the transitivity of betterness.

Broome concludes that the intuition, however attractive, must be given up. The judgment of the field is that this is the strongest argument against the evaluative asymmetry; even Jeff McMahan, who has done as much as anyone to articulate the asymmetry, calls Broome's the best argument he knows against its evaluative form, and on its strength rejects that form himself.

This is a deep problem, and it cannot be waved off, because it does not turn on any contested intuition. It turns on transitivity, which almost no one wants to surrender. The defender has three routes, and each is a real retreat to higher ground rather than a refutation. The first keeps neutrality but reinterprets "neither better nor worse" as incommensurable rather than equally good. On this view, defended by Wlodek Rabinowicz among others, the world with the added happy life and the world without it are not equal in value but incomparable, related by parity rather than equality, and parity does not transmit across the chain the way equality does, so the transitivity argument never gets traction. This rescues neutrality at the price of admitting incommensurable value into the theory, which has costs of its own elsewhere. The second route abandons the evaluative reading altogether and states the asymmetry as a claim about reasons, not betterness: there is no reason to add the happy life, though the world with it is not thereby ranked equal to, better than, or worse than the world without. This is Frick's strategy, and Broome's formal argument, being about the ordering of outcomes, simply does not reach a claim cast in terms of reasons. The third route is the consent argument and the priority of suffering, the two pillars of Part Two, which never needed the axiology in the first place.

The retreat has a name in the literature, and naming it sharpens the whole chapter. McMahan distinguishes a strong and a weak version of the asymmetry. The strong version, which is Benatar's, says the absence of the happy life is positively no loss and the world without it is in that respect better, that not creating is to be preferred. The weak version says only that we have strong moral reason not to create a miserable life and no moral reason to create a happy one, while declining to say that the world is better for the happy life's absence or that not creating is positively preferred. The weak version is what survives Broome, because it makes no claim about the betterness ordering that transitivity could break; it speaks only of the presence or absence of a reason. But the weak version is also less powerful. It grounds the conclusion that there is no duty to procreate, which almost everyone already accepts, far more securely than it grounds the conclusion that there is a duty not to. So the antinatalist who reaches for the weak asymmetry to escape Broome buys formal safety with argumentative reach, and must make up the lost ground elsewhere, in the consent argument or the priority of suffering, rather than from the asymmetry alone. That McMahan, having drawn the distinction, still rejects the asymmetry's evaluative form is a fact the honest defender reports rather than hides. He is not an ally to be conscripted. He is a careful philosopher who went where this chapter's argument cannot follow without cost, and marking the spot where he turned off is more useful to the reader than pretending the road runs straight.

The No-Subject Objection

The second attack is older than Benatar by two thousand years and more metaphysical than the first. It presses the worry Chapter Two raised under the comparative reading, and widens it. Non-existence, the objection holds, cannot be better or worse for anyone, because better-for and worse-for are relations that need a subject for whom the state obtains, and in non-existence there is no such subject. To say "non-existence is better for the person who would otherwise have suffered" is a category mistake. There is no person in non-existence to occupy the better-for side, so the comparison is not false but malformed, like asking whether silence is louder than the color blue.

The lineage is Epicurean. Epicurus argued that death is nothing to us, because where death is, we are not, and where we are, death is not; there is never a subject who bears death's supposed badness, so death cannot be bad for the one who dies. The no-subject objection runs the same shape in reverse. Where non-existence is, the never-created person is not, so non- existence cannot be good for them. If the antinatalist wants to resist the Epicurean conclusion about death, and most do, holding that death is bad for the one who dies by depriving them of future goods, then the antinatalist seems to owe a deprivation account of death's badness, on which death is bad for a person because it deprives a subject who exists at the time of death of goods they would otherwise have had. But that very account, with its insistence on a subject, is what makes Premise Three hard, because in non- existence there is no subject to bear any good, deprivation or otherwise. The antinatalist risks being caught between two stools: a deprivation account of death's badness that needs a subject, and a Premise Three about non- existence's goodness that has none.

The objection bites hardest, as Chapter Two said, on the comparative reading, the one that tried to dodge impersonal value by talking about better- for. If better-for needs a subject in the compared state, the comparative reading cannot get off the ground. The defender's options are few, and naming them exactly helps more than pretending one is free. He can fall back on the impersonal reading, giving up better-for in exchange for plain impersonal good, but then he is back in the war over impersonal value, the trade Chapter Two described, and the no-subject objection simply turns into the person-affecting denial of impersonal value. Or he can argue that comparisons of existence with non-existence make sense even with no subject standing in the void, that "better for a person never to have existed" is well- formed shorthand for a coherent comparison of two whole world-states, one holding the person and their suffering and one not, where "better for her"

picks out the world without her suffering and does not require her present in it to enjoy the lack. This second move is the one most defenders make, and it is the one the non-identity problem of Chapter Six dissects at length, because the same question, can we coherently compare existence with non-existence "for" a person, is the hinge of that problem too. The fair statement is that the no-subject objection is not fatal, and not dissolved either. It forces a permanent choice between two readings of Premise Three, each carrying a metaphysical cost, and it does not vanish with careful wording. It is a tax on the argument, paid in full every time the argument runs, and a responsible defender records the tax rather than hiding it.

The Extreme-Case Objection

The third attack is less metaphysical and more about method, and it returns to a worry Chapter One conceded in passing. The first intuition was pumped with a deliberately extreme case, the child certain to be born into constant suffering, and the critic charges that the extremity does the work illegitimately. Of course we judge it good that that child was not conceived; the case was built so existence offered the child nothing but suffering, so naturally non-existence comes out ahead. But the asymmetry's conclusion is not about lives of unrelieved suffering. It is a claim about all lives, the happy ones included. The objection: an intuition reliable in the extreme case is being stretched illegitimately to the ordinary case, where existence offers a great deal besides suffering, and where our intuitions in fact run the other way. We do not generally judge it good that some particular happy person was never conceived, and we do not congratulate the parents of a healthy child on the harm they would have avoided by abstaining.

The answer concedes the point about method and denies that it touches the argument, and the concession should be made cleanly. Yes, the case was chosen for vividness, and vividness can mislead; an intuition pumped by an extreme example deserves suspicion when it is asked to travel. But the asymmetry does not rest on the extreme case as evidence. It rests on it as illustration. The actual support for Premise Three, built in Chapter Two, is the fourfold structural argument, the four sub-asymmetries, and not one of them turns on extremity. The distant empty planet is not a planet of agony; it is a planet of merely absent happiness, and we still feel no grief at it. The missing duty to procreate is not waived because the possible children would suffer; it is waived even for the possible children who would flourish. The asymmetry of prospective reasons holds for happy lives as much as miserable ones: we cannot create even a happy child for its own sake. The four asymmetries hold across the whole range of possible lives, joyful and wretched alike, and it is they, not the extreme case, that carry Premise Three into the ordinary case. The extreme case opens the door; the four asymmetries hold the room in every kind of weather. A critic who has beaten only the extreme case has beaten only the illustration and left the load-bearing structure standing. Still, the residue has to go in the ledger. That the illustration and the underlying premise come apart so visibly in ordinary lives is a real reason the argument feels steadily less compelling the further you move from the extreme. That felt loss of grip is something candid defenders report and evasive ones suppress. The four asymmetries answer the objection at the level of logic. They do not fully restore, at the level of felt force, what the move away from the extreme case costs.

The Retrospective-Endorsement Objection

The fourth attack comes not from metaphysics or method but from the massed testimony of the living. The great majority of people, asked whether they are glad to have been born, say yes, and not as a passing mood but as a settled judgment, reaffirmed across illness, loss, and hardship. If coming into existence were the harm the asymmetry claims, the objection runs, we should expect this near-universal verdict to be a systematic error, and a theory that convicts nearly everyone who has ever lived of a deep mistake about the worth of their own life carries a burden it has not discharged. Aaron Smuts and others press versions of this. A life worth living just is, more or less by definition, one its liver has reason to be glad of, and the near-universality of such gladness is strong evidence that coming to exist is, for most, no net harm.

The reply has two parts, and the first is far stronger than the second; the order matters, because leaning on the weaker part is one of the standard ways antinatalists wreck their own credibility. The first part: the comparison the endorsing person makes is not the comparison the asymmetry makes. When you say you are glad to exist, you are an existing being, with interests and attachments and a history, comparing your actual life favorably against losing it, against the deprivation that ceasing to exist would now be. The argument grants that comparison whole. For an existing being, continuing is generally far better than dying, and gladness about your life, understood that way, is fitting and uncontested. But that gladness is evidence about the value of continuing an existing life, not about the value of having been started, and the two come apart exactly as Chapter One's clarification insisted. Retrospective endorsement answers the question "given that I exist, am I glad to go on?" It does not, and from the inside cannot, answer the question "was it better for me to be started than never to have been?", because the person giving the verdict has no access to the never-started alternative, there being no one in it to have access.

The second part is weaker, and it should be offered with care and never made to carry weight, because pressed hard it slides toward the very contempt for the living that the series treats as antinatalism's characteristic failure. This part points to the psychology of adaptation and positive bias, what Benatar discusses as Pollyannaism: the human tendency to recalibrate expectations, to adapt to circumstances one would have called unbearable in advance, to remember the good and discount the bad, and so to report high satisfaction across an astonishing range of objective conditions. There is real and well- replicated psychology here. But a defender who leans on it ends up telling contented people they are deceived about the one thing they might be thought to know best, the worth of their own lives, and doing it selectively, trusting the reports of suffering that help the thesis while discounting the reports of satisfaction that embarrass it. Worse, the move cuts both ways. The same adaptive faculties that supposedly inflate the natalist's contentment also shape the antinatalist's judgments, so a thoroughgoing distrust of self-report cannot be aimed at one side only. The disciplined version of the reply stops, for its real weight, at the first part: the comparison is the wrong one. It uses the second part only to blunt the objection, never to override the testimony wholesale. The residue: retrospective endorsement does not refute the asymmetry, because it answers a different question. But a theory that has to explain why the considered, reaffirmed self-assessment of nearly everyone who has ever lived does not bear on its central claim is carrying a real weight, evidential and moral both, and the lighter and less condescending the explaining, the more the theory keeps its integrity.

The Named Critics: Harman, DeGrazia, Smuts

Three of the asymmetry's most cited professional critics deserve naming, without caricature, because a reader who goes to the literature will meet them and should know in advance that their objections are versions of the structures already laid out, pressed with particular rigor.

Elizabeth Harman, in her 2009 critical study of Benatar, takes aim at the third premise directly. The alleged impersonal goodness of absent pain, she argues, is not something we are really committed to once the cases are described with care, and the intuitions Benatar leans on can be redescribed without granting Premise Three. At bottom it is the symmetry and no- subject objections combined, executed with unusual attention to what the cases do and do not show.

David DeGrazia, in his work on creation ethics, grants that procreation raises real moral questions but resists the step from "imposes harm" to "impermissible," pressing the whole-life and benefit-conferring considerations this volume takes up in Chapter Seven. His is, in effect, an early and careful statement of the whole-life evaluation challenge, and his refusal of the final inference is the refusal Part Four concedes a reasonable person may make.

Aaron Smuts argues, against Benatar, that a life worth living is good for the one who lives it, and that the asymmetry mislocates the standard for a life worth starting. His objection blends the retrospective-endorsement and whole-life lines, and it is among the most readable entries into the debate for a newcomer.

Naming them serves one structural point, and it should reassure rather than alarm. The professional case against the asymmetry, formidable as it is, reduces in the main to a small number of deep objections: the symmetry or consistency objection, the no-subject objection, and the whole-life and endorsement family, pressed by able people in careful ways. There is no secret further objection, no hidden knock-down the defender failed to see coming. The terrain is mapped. What divides the field is not the discovery of new attacks but the weighing of known ones, and that weighing is unsettled.

Where the Chapter Leaves the Argument

Six attacks, six answers, and the answers are not equal. Grading them without flattery is the point of the whole exercise, because the calibration is what the reader takes away.

The symmetry objection is the deepest, and its answer the least conclusive. The asymmetry survives it only if the basic asymmetry really is a better explanation of the four sub-asymmetries than the wide person-affecting principle and the conditional-reasons account, and that comparison is, in the present state of the field, contested by serious people with no resolution in sight. A reader who comes away judging that the symmetry objection wins has not blundered. They have backed one side of a live dispute, and a responsible defender grants exactly this.

The formal attack from neutrality is the most rigorous, and against the evaluative reading of the asymmetry it may well succeed; transitivity is not a thing one gives up lightly. Its force is also its limit. It defeats only the version of Premise Four that ranks whole worlds, and the asymmetry has two escape routes that Broome's argument cannot follow, into incommensurable value or into a claim about reasons rather than betterness. The cost of taking either route is McMahan's: the asymmetry that survives is the weak one, which secures that there is no duty to procreate far better than it secures a duty not to, and which therefore leans more of the antinatalist conclusion onto the two pillars of Part Two. A reader who concludes that the evaluative asymmetry fails has, again, backed a respectable side, and the position was built to lose this and stand.

The no-subject objection is permanent rather than fatal. It taxes the argument by forcing a choice between two costly readings of Premise Three, and the tax is paid every time the argument runs. A standing cost, never a clean win.

The extreme-case objection is answered cleanly at the level of logic, since the four asymmetries carry Premise Three without the extreme case, but it leaves a real residue at the level of felt force, which honest defenders acknowledge.

The retrospective-endorsement objection is answered well by the comparative clarification, which shows it targets the wrong comparison, and badly by the adaptation move, which corrodes the position's own compassion and cuts both ways. The defender should lean wholly on the first and distrust the second.

The named critics press refined versions of all this, not new attacks. The terrain holds no ambush.

So what stands? The asymmetry stands, but as a strong, contested position rather than a demonstration. It is the best-developed and best- defended argument in its field, with one clearly marked point of greatest vulnerability, the even-handedness of Premises Three and Four, where reasonable, informed philosophers get off. This is why the introduction promised three pillars and not one, and why the next two chapters matter as much as these three. Suppose a reader, having worked honestly through this chapter, decides the symmetry objection is decisive and the asymmetry fails. That reader has not left the position this series defends. They have shifted their weight onto the two columns the asymmetry never supported, the columns Part Two now builds: the consent argument, which Benatar's asymmetry does not touch and which stands even if Premises Three and Four fall together, and the priority of preventing suffering, which is older than the asymmetry and survives its fall. The foundation was designed to lose its most elegant column and still bear the load. Part Two is where that design is tested under weight.
