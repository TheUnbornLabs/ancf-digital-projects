Antinatalism Intro Guide

Philosophy · Difficulty: Beginner

Antinatalism is the philosophical view that bringing new people into existence is ethically worth questioning. This guide explains the idea respectfully and in plain language — and gives the strongest objections a fair hearing too, because an honest introduction should let you make up your own mind.

The core idea in one minute

Most decisions ask for the consent of the people they affect. Coming into existence is the one major event that happens to a person before they can agree to it. Antinatalism takes that seriously and asks: if a future person cannot consent, and is certain to experience at least some suffering, can we say that creating that life is automatically good for them? You do not have to answer "no" to find the question worth sitting with.

What antinatalism actually claims

At its core, antinatalism asks a single careful question: given that a future person cannot consent to being born and will certainly face some suffering, is creating that life automatically good for that person? Philosophers answer in different ways. A common argument is the asymmetry associated with David Benatar: the absence of suffering is good even if no one exists to enjoy that good, while the absence of pleasure is only "bad" if there is someone who is deprived of it. If that asymmetry holds, never being born cannot harm anyone, but being born always carries some guaranteed harm.

What it is not

It is not hatred of children, parents, or families. It is not a call to harm anyone who already exists, and it is the opposite of any "kill people" caricature — almost every antinatalist position is fiercely pro the welfare of everyone already here. People who hold this view often care deeply about reducing suffering for those already alive, and support reproductive autonomy in both directions: the freedom to have children and the freedom not to.

Main flavours
  • Philosophical / consent-based — focused on the asymmetry of pleasure and suffering and the impossibility of consent (Benatar, and echoes of Schopenhauer).
  • Misanthropic vs. compassionate — most modern writers reject the misanthropic strand and frame antinatalism entirely around compassion and harm-reduction.
  • Ecological — focused on resource limits, climate, and the welfare of future generations.
  • Personal / childfree overlap — focused on individual autonomy; a childfree life is a personal choice that does not require agreeing with any philosophical argument.
Key thinkers & texts (to read further)
  • David Benatar — Better Never to Have Been (2006), the modern asymmetry argument.
  • Arthur Schopenhauer — 19th-century pessimism about the balance of suffering in a life.
  • Seana Shiffrin — work on the ethics of imposing risk on a non-consenting future person.

Reading the primary sources is the best way to judge the argument for yourself.

The strongest objections (steelmanned)

A fair introduction has to present the best counter-arguments, not just the view it describes:

  • Lives contain real goods. Many people judge their own existence as overwhelmingly worth it; the asymmetry may undervalue the genuine joy, love, and meaning a life can hold.
  • The non-identity problem. If a particular person only exists because they were born, it is hard to say they were "harmed" by birth compared to a non-existence in which they never are.
  • Consent isn't always required. We make many unconsented decisions for future people (vaccinations, education) that we consider clearly good.
  • Practical optimism. Suffering can be reduced; a future person may help reduce it.

Whether these defeat the antinatalist argument is exactly what the debate is about — and what you get to decide.
