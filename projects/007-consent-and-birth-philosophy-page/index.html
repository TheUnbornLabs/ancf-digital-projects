PROJECT 007

Consent & Birth Philosophy

Philosophy · Difficulty: Intermediate · ~22 min · interactive

One of the oldest puzzles in birth ethics: can consent matter when no one yet exists to give it? This page builds the consent argument with you, one premise at a time, so you can see exactly where it is strong and where it can be resisted. It explains three senses of consent, gives the best objections a fair hearing, and helps you find your own position. It argues for no conclusion — only for understanding.

① The puzzle

The consent intuition — for almost everything significant we do to another person, we seek their agreement first. Birth is the one act that happens to someone before they exist to be asked. The consent argument asks what, if anything, follows from that strange fact.

It is easy to misread this as a gotcha ("you never asked to be born!"). Taken seriously, it is subtler: it points at a genuine gap in how we usually justify imposing things on people. We can't get prior consent from a future person — so what justifies the imposition of an entire existence, with all its risks, upon them? Defenders of procreation have real answers; the point of this page is to see the question and the answers clearly.

④ The strongest version: Shiffrin's hybrid

The philosopher Seana Shiffrin (1999) gave the consent worry its most careful form. She doesn't say procreation is always wrong; she points out a structural difficulty.

The gold-coins analogy

Imagine a wealthy person who wants to benefit their neighbors, so they fly over and drop heavy cubes of gold into people's yards. One cube lands on someone, breaking their arm — but they're now $5 million richer. Were they wronged?

Most of us feel yes, somewhat — even though they ended up better off overall. We're uneasy about imposing serious burdens on someone, without their consent, for a benefit they never asked for — especially when the burden couldn't be avoided in the giving. Shiffrin argues birth has this shape: existence is a profound benefit that also unavoidably imposes serious harms (pain, loss, mortality), and it is done without consent.

Crucially, Shiffrin herself is cautious — she draws out the difficulty, not a blanket prohibition. It shows why "but life is a gift!" doesn't fully dissolve the consent worry: gifts that break your arm are still complicated.

Key takeaway

The consent argument is neither a knockout nor a trick. It points at a real gap — we cannot ask a future person — and reasonable people fill that gap differently. You can find the question genuinely hard without accepting any single answer, and understanding exactly where you get off the train is worth more than a verdict.
