The childfree conversation borrows words from medicine, law, demography, and everyday speech, and the same term can mean slightly different things in different rooms. This glossary gathers the words that appear most often throughout the book and gives each a short, plain definition. It is meant as a quick reference, not the last word: where a term is medical or legal, the brief definition here is a starting point only, and the details that matter for your situation should be confirmed with a qualified professional.

C

Childfree — a person who does not have children and does not want them. The choice is active and deliberate, and the "-free" framing treats the absence as openness rather than lack.
Childless — a person who does not have children but wanted them, or remains open to them. The same outward fact as childfree, but a very different inner experience, often involving grief.
Childless by circumstance / not by choice — a person whose situation, rather than their preference, decided the matter: infertility, the absence of a willing partner, timing, illness, or loss. Sometimes called the "circumstantially childless."
Chosen family — the network of friends, partners, mentors, and loved ones a person deliberately builds, as distinct from biological or legal kin. For many childfree people it carries the weight that an extended family of descendants might otherwise hold.

D

DINK — short for "dual income, no kids," describing a couple with two earners and no children. The term is descriptive of a household's economics and says nothing about whether the absence of children is by choice.

F

Fence-sitter — an informal term for someone who has not yet decided whether to have children and is weighing the options. It is a normal and often lengthy stage, not a failure to decide.

L

LARC (long-acting reversible contraception) — methods such as intrauterine devices (IUDs) and contraceptive implants that prevent pregnancy for years yet can be removed when desired. They are reversible, in contrast to sterilization. Suitability is a question for a clinician.
Living will / advance directive — a legal document stating your wishes for medical care if you become unable to speak for yourself. It is one of the planning tools that quietly matters more when you do not have adult children. Confirm the requirements for your jurisdiction with a professional.

N

Natalism — a general orientation that values and encourages having children, whether as a personal outlook or a broad cultural attitude.

P

PANK / PUNK — "professional aunt, no kids" and "professional uncle, no kids": affectionate labels for childfree adults who are closely and lovingly involved in the lives of other people's children.
Power of attorney (durable / medical) — a legal document authorizing someone you trust to make decisions on your behalf. A durable power of attorney typically covers finances and remains valid if you become incapacitated; a medical power of attorney names someone to make health decisions. The exact forms and terms vary by jurisdiction; consult a legal professional.
Pronatalism — the assumption or active promotion of parenthood as the expected, default, or superior path, often built into laws, workplaces, and social expectations. Recognizing pronatalism helps explain much of the pressure childfree people encounter.

R

Regret asymmetry — the observation that the possibility of regret runs in both directions: one can regret not having children, but one can also regret having them. The two risks are rarely weighed evenly in public conversation.
Reproductive autonomy — the principle that decisions about whether, when, and how to have children belong to the individual. It underpins the case that choosing to be childfree needs no external justification.

S

Salpingectomy / tubal ligation — surgical methods of female sterilization. Tubal ligation blocks or seals the fallopian tubes; salpingectomy removes them. Both are generally intended to be permanent. Risks, reversibility, and suitability are matters for a medical professional.
Sterilization — a permanent surgical method of preventing pregnancy, by procedures on the fallopian tubes in women or the vas deferens in men. It is meant to be irreversible and should be discussed thoroughly with a clinician.

V

Vasectomy — a minor surgical procedure that interrupts the vas deferens to provide permanent male sterilization. It is generally simpler and lower-risk than female sterilization, but is still intended to be permanent; consult a medical professional.

Chapter in brief

  • Childfree (not wanting children) and childless (wanting but not having) describe the same outward fact with very different inner experiences.
  • Pronatalism and natalism name the cultural pressure toward parenthood; reproductive autonomy is the principle that the choice is yours.
  • Contraception terms run from reversible (LARC) to permanent (sterilization, vasectomy, tubal ligation, salpingectomy).
  • Legal-planning terms — living will, power of attorney — matter more without adult children to lean on.
  • Any medical or legal term here is a starting point only; confirm the specifics with a qualified professional.
