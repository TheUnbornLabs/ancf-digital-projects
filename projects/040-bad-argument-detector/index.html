PROJECT 040

Bad Argument Detector

Learning Tools · Difficulty: Intermediate · ~15 min · detector + library

Paste any argument and spot the classic logical fallacies hiding inside — each with a plain explanation. It's a study aid for learning to reason well, not a verdict on people: a sentence can contain a fallacy and still come from someone who means well. Below the scanner is a field guide to fifteen common fallacies.

① Scan an argument② Fallacy field guide③ Quick check④ Reflection

① Scan an argument

Paste a message, or try a sample. The scanner looks for possible fallacy patterns — always use your own judgement on what it flags.

② Fallacy field guide

Filter by family or search. Each entry has a plain definition and an everyday example.

③ Quick check

Two quick questions — educational, not a test.

1. "You only think that because you're bitter" is an example of…
Ad hominem — attacking the person, not the argument
A strong rebuttal
A false dilemma

2. Spotting a fallacy in someone's argument means…
That one step of their reasoning is weak — not that they're a bad person
They are stupid
Their conclusion must be false

④ Your reflection

Saved on this device only.

Key takeaway

A fallacy is a flaw in the reasoning, not a measure of the person. Learning to name them sharpens your own thinking and helps you reply to the weak step instead of getting swept along. Note, too: an argument can be full of fallacies and still reach a true conclusion — so spotting one is an invitation to think harder, not a mic-drop.
