Benatar Argument Explainer

Philosophy · Difficulty: Advanced

David Benatar's "asymmetry argument" (from Better Never to Have Been, 2006) is the most cited case for antinatalism. This page walks through it in plain terms, shows the famous four-box table, lets you test which step you doubt, and gives the strongest objections — so you can judge for yourself.

1. The asymmetry, in plain terms

Benatar argues that pain and pleasure are not symmetrical when we compare existing with never existing:

  • The presence of pain is bad.
  • The presence of pleasure is good.
  • The absence of pain is good — even if no one exists to enjoy that good.
  • The absence of pleasure is not bad — unless there is someone for whom that absence is a deprivation.

Because a never-existent person misses out on nothing (no one is deprived), but is also spared all pain, Benatar concludes that coming into existence is always a harm compared with never existing.

2. The four-box table

Scenario A: a person existsScenario B: the person never exists
Pain(1) Bad(3) Good (even with no one to enjoy it)
Pleasure(2) Good(4) Not bad (no one is deprived)

The engine of the argument is the contrast between box (3) Good and box (4) Not bad: non-existence has an advantage (3) with no matching disadvantage (4).

4. The strongest objections

"Good for no one" may be incoherent

Critics argue a state can't be good if there is literally no subject for whom it is good. If box (3) collapses, the asymmetry loses its engine.

The asymmetry is question-begging

Some say Benatar simply builds the conclusion into how he rates the four boxes; a symmetric rating (absence of pleasure is bad) would dissolve the argument.

Lives can be net positive

Many hold that a good life's pleasures genuinely outweigh its pains, so existence is not automatically a harm — directly denying the conclusion.

It proves too much

If sound, the argument implies it is always wrong to create any life, however happy — a conclusion many take as a reason to suspect a hidden flaw.
