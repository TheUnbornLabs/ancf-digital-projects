The Priority of Preventing Suffering

T he third pillar is the oldest of the three, the broadest, and in some ways the most intuitive. It also carries the greatest danger. Misapplied, it produces the worst caricature of the whole position, the charge that antinatalism is a death cult that would extinguish life to extinguish suffering. So this chapter builds the pillar and then builds, with equal care, the wall that separates it from that conclusion. A pillar that cannot be told apart from pro- mortalism is not an asset. It is the gift that hands opponents their easiest win.

The pillar is a claim about urgency. Preventing suffering takes moral priority over creating happiness. The asymmetry here is not in the value of pain and pleasure as such, but in the force of our reasons concerning them. We have a strong, recognizable duty to relieve an existing person's agony. We have no comparable duty to manufacture an existing person's bliss, and certainly none to manufacture a new person in order to stock the world with bliss. The reason to prevent suffering presses on us. The reason to create happiness, where it exists at all, does not press the same way. This is a claim about the structure of moral reasons, and it is held, in some form, far beyond antinatalism. It is one of the most widely shared asymmetries in all of ethics.

The Asymmetry of Moral Reasons

The claim shows best through cases that have nothing to do with procreation, because its independence from the procreative conclusion is exactly what makes it a separate pillar and not a restatement of Part One.

Take Peter Singer's man who walks past a child drowning in a shallow pond. We judge his failure to wade in and pull her out a grave wrong. The reason to prevent her suffering and death was binding, and he flouted it. Now take a man who declines a chance to bring a new happy person into the world, who could have conceived a child likely to flourish and chose, for ordinary reasons, not to. We do not judge his omission a wrong at all, let alone a grave one. In both cases there is suffering one man could have prevented and joy another could have created, and our moral assessment of the two failures runs wildly apart. The first is monstrous. The second is unremarkable. The asymmetry of reasons names that difference. The reason to prevent suffering binds. The reason to create happiness, in the procreative case, barely registers.

Notice that someone who rejects the whole apparatus of Part One can still affirm this. You need not accept that absent pain is impersonally good, nor that the four premises hold, to feel the gap between the pond and the unconceived child. The asymmetry of reasons is a datum of ordinary moral life, visible to common sense before any theory shows up. What antinatalism adds is one observation. Procreation is the deliberate creation of guaranteed future suffering, since every life holds some, undertaken to deliver goods that, by the asymmetry of reasons, set up no comparable duty to deliver. If the urgency of preventing suffering really does outweigh the urgency of creating happiness, then the procreative act, which creates both, is an act whose certain harm is morally weighty and whose hoped-for goods are morally optional. That imbalance is the pillar. It does not prove the conclusion with the geometric neatness the asymmetry attempts. It presses the conclusion by appeal to a priority almost everyone already grants in the non-procreative case.

A Respectable Pedigree: The Duty to Relieve Suffering

The priority of preventing suffering is neither an antinatalist invention nor a utilitarian eccentricity, and the pillar is steadier once that is seen. It has been defended at book length, on explicitly non-utilitarian grounds, by Jamie Mayerfeld. In Suffering and Moral Responsibility, Mayerfeld argues that the reduction of suffering is morally more important than the promotion of happiness, and that the duty to relieve suffering is far stronger than most of us acknowledge. He reaches this not from a single theory but from convergent intuitions, drawing on utilitarians, deontologists, Aristotelians, and ordinary moral feeling alike, which is part of the point: the priority of suffering is a fixed point that many theories must accommodate rather than a deliverance of one contested theory.

Mayerfeld's sharpest illustration is worth carrying. Set a world in which one person endures a lifetime of torment while countless others enjoy a lifetime of bliss against a world in which everyone lives near the neutral line, neither tormented nor blissful. Mayerfeld judges, and invites us to judge, that the bliss of the many, however many, cannot justify allowing the lifelong torture of the one. The judgment is not that happiness is worthless; he grants it real and substantial value. It is that the two are asymmetric, that a measure of suffering weighs more heavily in the moral scale than a like measure of happiness, and that no sum of the second redeems enough of the first.

This bears on the pillar in two ways. It shows the priority of suffering to be a serious, developed position in mainstream moral philosophy rather than a flag of convenience. And it shows, which is the whole burden of the next section, that the priority can be held without negative utilitarianism. Mayerfeld is no negative utilitarian. He does not hold that only suffering counts, and his view licenses none of the catastrophes that sink the strict doctrine. He holds the modest, asymmetric claim, suffering first, happiness real but second, which is exactly the pillar this chapter needs and exactly what the firewall protects.

One open question comes with the inheritance, and honesty requires setting it down. How much priority? An absolute priority, on which no quantity of happiness ever offsets any suffering, slides toward negative utilitarianism and its troubles. A weighted priority, on which suffering counts for more but a great deal of happiness can sometimes outweigh a little suffering, is safer, but it leaves the natalist room to argue that a flourishing life's happiness does outweigh its suffering for the purpose of the decision to create. Where to set that dial is unsettled, and the setting governs how hard the pillar presses toward the antinatalist conclusion. The chapter's final accounting returns to it.

Negative Utilitarianism, and Why the Position Does Not Need It

The priority of preventing suffering has a famous and far more radical cousin: negative utilitarianism, the view that the sole or primary aim of morality is to minimize suffering, with the promotion of happiness carrying little or no independent weight. Negative utilitarianism delivers the antinatalist conclusion fast. If minimizing suffering is what matters, and creating people creates suffering, then not creating them is plainly better. For that reason the two positions get run together, by critics keen to saddle antinatalism with negative utilitarianism's notorious problems, and by some proponents careless enough to accept the package.

The central strategic claim of this chapter is that the package should be refused, and refused on principle, not for convenience. The antinatalist position does not require negative utilitarianism, and chaining it to that foundation imports a set of vulnerabilities the position need not carry. What the position requires is only the priority of preventing suffering, the asymmetry of reasons set out above, which is far weaker than full negative utilitarianism and far more widely held. You can believe that preventing suffering is more urgent than creating happiness while also believing that happiness has real, substantial, independent value. The two sit together without strain. The asymmetry of reasons says preventing suffering takes priority. It does not say happiness counts for nothing. Negative utilitarianism says the second, stronger thing, and the second, stronger thing is what produces the catastrophe everyone fears.

Here is why the difference matters, and the reason for the firewall this chapter is about to build. Negative utilitarianism, taken strictly, faces the world-destruction objection, pressed in its classic form by R. N. Smart in 1958. If minimizing suffering is the overriding aim, then painlessly ending all sentient life, which would end all future suffering forever, looks like the morally optimal act, since a universe with no sentient beings holds no suffering. This is the conclusion every opponent of antinatalism reaches for. Against strict negative utilitarianism it has real bite. The decisive point is that antinatalism does not entail it, because antinatalism does not require strict negative utilitarianism, and the priority-of-suffering pillar, stated properly, blocks it outright.

The objection is older and better developed than a single paper, and seeing its full shape shows why the whole package is worth refusing rather than some favored member of it kept. The view that morality's task is to minimize suffering rather than to maximize happiness received its best- known early statement from Karl Popper in The Open Society and Its En emies, who held that the relief of suffering makes a more urgent claim on us than the promotion of happiness. Sympathisers alarmed by the world- destruction result have built ever more qualified versions to escape it: absolute negative utilitarianism, which counts only suffering; lexical versions, on which suffering always outweighs happiness while happiness still counts for something; and lexical-threshold versions, on which happiness can offset suffering up to some level of severity, beyond which it cannot. Toby Ord, surveying these, argues that the maneuver fails in a predictable pattern. The versions strong enough to preserve negative utilitarianism's animating thought still imply that a painless end to all life would be best or permissible, while the versions weak enough to avoid that result have surrendered what made negative utilitarianism distinctive and slid back toward ordinary utilitarianism. Either the catastrophe returns or the view dissolves.

That pattern is the decisive reason to refuse the entire family rather than shop within it. The priority of preventing suffering, as Mayerfeld holds it and as this chapter holds it, is not a negative utilitarianism at all, in any of its grades. It does not say only suffering counts, so it never generates the world- destruction result that every grade of negative utilitarianism must somehow wrestle with. It says suffering takes priority among the things that count, the living and their happiness emphatically included, which is why the firewall of the next section holds without any of Ord's contortions.

The Firewall

The firewall has two walls, and the position needs only one of them to hold. That it has both is what makes the separation from pro-mortalism secure rather than merely asserted.

The first wall is the distinction between starting a life and ending one, set as the permanent boundary marker of this series back in Chapter One, and carrying real weight here. Antinatalism is a claim about the ethics of starting lives. Pro-mortalism is a claim about the ethics of ending them. These are different acts with different moral structures, and the priority of preventing suffering bears on them differently. Ending an existing life does not prevent that being's suffering in the clean way that never starting it would have. It inflicts a fresh and grievous harm: the destruction of a being who now has every interest in continuing, projects underway, attachments that matter, a will to live that is itself a powerful moral fact. The existing person is not a candidate for costless suffering-prevention. They are a subject whose interests, including their overwhelming interest in not being killed, carry full moral weight. So the priority of preventing suffering, far from licensing their destruction, counts against it, because killing them is among the gravest harms one can impose on a being who exists. Preventing suffering means preventing the suffering of those who would otherwise undergo it. It does not mean manufacturing the catastrophic suffering and loss that ending existing lives entails.

The second wall is the asymmetry of reasons itself, read correctly. The pond case taught that we have a stringent duty to prevent suffering and no comparable duty to create happiness. It did not teach that we have a duty to prevent suffering by any means whatever, including means that themselves inflict massive suffering and override the strongest interests of existing beings. The priority of preventing suffering is a priority among the reasons that bear on our choices. It is not a license to treat existing persons as containers of suffering to be emptied. A view that authorized killing the living to spare them and their possible descendants future pain would not be an application of the priority of preventing suffering. It would be a gross violation of it, since it would deliberately inflict the most extreme suffering and deprivation on beings who exist and refuse it. The pro-mortalist conclusion does not follow from the pillar. It contradicts the pillar.

This is why the chapter insisted on refusing the negative-utilitarian package. The world-destruction objection is powerful against strict negative utilitarianism, which counts only suffering and so, in principle, sanctions ending whatever suffers. It is powerless against the priority of preventing suffering, which counts the interests of the living at full weight and so forbids the very act the objection imagines. An antinatalist who has been careful about which claim they hold is simply not exposed to the objection. An antinatalist who has been careless, who has let "preventing suffering matters most" slide into "only suffering matters," has handed the opponent a real target and earned the hit. The discipline here is the discipline the series teaches everywhere. Hold the modest claim that is true and defensible. Refuse the stronger claim that is neither.

The Objection That Matters

The most serious objection to this pillar is not the pro-mortalist charge, which the firewall defeats, but a quieter and more principled one: the denial of the asymmetry of reasons itself. A committed classical utilitarian, anyone who holds that happiness and suffering carry equal and opposite moral weight, will simply reject the claim that preventing suffering takes priority over creating happiness. On their view the pond and the unconceived-child cases differ not because of an asymmetry of reasons but because of confounding features, the identifiability of the drowning child, the certainty of the harm, the special obligations of proximity. Control for those, and the underlying reasons are symmetric: a unit of prevented suffering and a unit of created happiness count the same. If that is right, the pillar's central premise is false, and the procreative ledger balances suffering against happiness with no thumb on the scale.

The reply cannot be a knock-down proof, because the asymmetry of reasons is, like the intuitions of Part One, a foundational judgment rather than a derived theorem. That is precisely why it is offered as a pillar and not as a deduction. What the reply can do is push the explanatory burden back onto the symmetrist. The asymmetry of reasons explains an enormous range of ordinary judgments the symmetric view strains against: that we owe far more to the suffering than to the merely-not-yet-happy; that there is no duty of procreative beneficence; that a world with less suffering can be better even with somewhat less happiness; that the repugnant conclusion, the symmetric utilitarian's standing embarrassment, on which a vast population of barely- worth-living lives outranks a small flourishing one, really is repugnant. The symmetrist must either explain these away or swallow conclusions most people reject. That is not a refutation of symmetry. It is a demonstration that the asymmetry of reasons is well-supported and that its denial carries heavy costs of its own. As with the asymmetry of Part One, the verdict is that this pillar rests on a contested foundational judgment which is, on balance, better-supported than its rival, and that "on balance" is again doing real work.

What This Chapter Leaves Open

This pillar's distinctive strength is its breadth and its independence. The priority of preventing suffering is held far beyond antinatalism, it does not depend on the asymmetry of Part One, and it does not depend on the consent argument of Chapter Four. Its distinctive risk is the pro-mortalist caricature, which the firewall defeats, provided the position is stated as the priority of preventing suffering and not as strict negative utilitarianism. This chapter made that proviso central precisely because so much rides on it.

Three things remain open. First, the pillar rests on the asymmetry of reasons, a foundational judgment a symmetric utilitarian can coherently reject, which leaves the pillar strong but not coercive. Second, the firewall's first wall, the starting-versus-ending distinction, does heavy work, and a critic who challenges that distinction, a challenge the position has to meet anyway, since it is the boundary marker the whole series leans on, presses on this pillar too. The firewall is secure. It is not free-standing; it borrows the most important load-bearing distinction in the series. Third, and most candidly, the move from "preventing suffering takes priority" to "therefore do not create the person whose life will contain suffering" requires that the suffering created not be outweighed, for the purposes of the creative decision, by the happiness also created. A critic will say that for a life rich in goods the happiness does outweigh the suffering, priority or no priority, so that creating that life is not the kind of suffering-creation the pillar condemns. That is the whole-life evaluation challenge arriving from a third direction, and it is the reason Chapter Seven exists. All three pillars, pressed far enough, arrive at the same two questions. How do moral claims attach to people who do not yet exist? And may the goods of a whole life justify the harms imposed in creating it? Part Three takes those two questions head-on, because they are where the field, antinatalist and natalist alike, finally stands or falls.
