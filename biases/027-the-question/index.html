① In brief

So, When Are You Having Kids? The Interrogation Norm

Part III — Cultural & Social Scripts · 100 Biases of Natalism

Almost everyone who reaches adulthood without children fields a recurring question: So, when are you having kids?, along with its variants, Don't you want children? Isn't it time? You're not getting any younger. It arrives warmly, at weddings and dinners and in office kitchens, asked by relatives, colleagues, and strangers alike. This book examines not any single asker, and not any specific source of pressure, but the norm of asking itself: the fact that in most societies it is socially licensed, even friendly, to interrogate another adult about their reproductive plans, and that this questioning runs in only one direction.

Read the rest of the overview

The bias named here is not a belief but a speech practice. Its critique rests on three load-bearing observations. First, a grammatical one: when are you having kids? is a question about timing, and questions about timing presuppose the event they time. The word smuggled in is when, not whether: to ask when is to treat the whether as already settled. Linguists call this a presupposition: information a sentence takes for granted rather than asserts, which survives even negation and cannot easily be argued with. The person asked is placed, before they speak, inside a story whose ending is assumed. Second, an asymmetry: no one is routinely asked to justify having children. The questioning flows toward the childless and childfree and stops at the parents' door, so that reproduction becomes the one life-path exempt from having to explain itself. Third, an accumulation: any single instance is trivial and easily deflected, but the questioning is repeated across a life by dozens of unrelated askers, and its structural effect is cumulative. It keeps the pronatalist default tilted and the childfree perpetually on the defensive.

The stance is the series' stance, applied strictly. The critique is of the mechanism, never of the people who ask (most mean well) and never of parents, children, or anyone's answer. Chapter 2 makes the strongest case for the question first, before any criticism: it is usually an act of love, a normal move in the grammar of intimacy, a useful and safe conversational opener, sometimes genuinely welcome, and grounded in a real statistical prior rather than in coercion. The book then traces the norm's origins in pronatalism, in the historical treatment of reproduction as a communal matter, in heteronormativity, and in the social clock; it shows how the norm operates today through presupposition, felicity conditions, politeness and face, repetition, and asymmetric accountability; and it separates the firm evidence (the linguistics, the observable asymmetry) from the softer (the qualitative burden, the analogical microaggression framing).

The hidden costs are diffuse but real: the presumption itself, the unreciprocated labour of answering, the ambushing of the grieving and the infertile, the entrenchment of the default, and a degrading of the very conditions under which a free reproductive decision could be made, for future parents as much as for the childfree. Chapter 7 maps who is asked most: women, earlier and against a clock; married couples, from the wedding onward; same-sex and single people, for whom the buried assumptions surface; and the bereaved, who field a light question that lands on a wound.

The book fairly states the strongest objections (that it pathologises friendliness, that the presupposition is usually true, that any single question is easily dodged) and answers them by keeping its thesis narrow: a practice can have a structure that does work no participant intends. What it asks of individuals is only, optionally, a small shift from when to whether. What it asks of the culture is to notice the asymmetry it maintains one dinner at a time. The hope is not for silence and not for fewer families, but for a restoration of the whether: so that everyone answers from a decision that is theirs, rather than from a presupposition placed in the question before they opened their mouths.

② The book in twelve chapters

Each chapter opens with its own summary. Note chapter 2: every volume states the strongest case for the thing it criticises before laying a finger on it — and chapter 8 turns the critique back on itself.

CH 1Naming the bias

The interrogation norm is the socially licensed, widely practised pattern of questioning adults about their reproductive plans (When are you having kids? Don't you want children? Isn't it time?) in a form that presupposes reproduction is expected and owed, and that asks this only of those who have not (yet) reproduced.

CH 2The strongest case for it (steelman)The strongest case against this book

Before any criticism, the honest work: the question deserves a real defence, and it has one. I will make it as strongly as I can, because most people who ask when are you having kids? would recognise themselves here, and they are not wrong to.

CH 3Where it comes from

The interrogation norm feels timeless, but it is assembled from several older structures, and seeing them dismantles its air of naturalness. A thing that feels like it has always been asked, and always will be, is a thing whose history has gone invisible; recovering the history is how the feeling of inevitability comes apart.

CH 4How it operates today

The interrogation norm works through several concrete mechanisms, most of them invisible to the people using them. This chapter names the machinery. The invisibility is not incidental; it is precisely what lets the machinery keep running. A mechanism you could see and object to could be resisted. These operate below the threshold of notice, which is why they are so durable.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

What can actually be shown here, and what must be held with appropriate uncertainty? This chapter separates the firm from the suggestive. The discipline of the series is to claim exactly as much as the evidence supports and not a word more, because a critique of pronatalism that inflates its own evidence forfeits the one advantage it has over the thing it criticises: honesty about what is known.

CH 6The hidden costs

If the question is usually warm, where is the harm? The costs are real but diffuse, which is exactly why the default framing misses them. Diffuse costs are systematically underweighted by human judgement, which is tuned to notice sharp, local, attributable harms and to overlook harms that are spread thin across time, persons, and instances. The interrogation norm's costs are of the second kind, and so they hide in plain sight.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

The interrogation norm does not fall evenly. Its weight is distributed along familiar lines, and naming the distribution is part of naming the bias. A pressure that presents itself as universal small talk is in fact sharply targeted, and its targeting follows the same contours as every other pronatalist pressure in this series, which is itself a clue that the friendly question and the harder pressures are branches of one root.

CH 10Adjacent biases

The interrogation norm interlocks with several other biases in this series. Mapping the joints keeps this book in its lane and shows how the pressures compound. Boundary-drawing of this kind is not bookkeeping; it is part of the argument, because a bias understood in isolation looks smaller and stranger than it is, while a bias located within its family of neighbours reveals its true function as one organ in a larger system.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

What does non-coerced reasoning, and non-coercive conversation, look like once the interrogation norm is named? The aim of this chapter is not a rulebook but a clearer field of choice, for askers and answerers and institutions alike. Nothing here is a prohibition, and nothing here demands that anyone monitor their speech anxiously; the whole spirit of the chapter is that seeing a mechanism clearly is most of what is needed to be free in relation to it, and that the freedom, once gained, makes the small adjustments feel like relief rather than restriction.

CH 12Conclusion

This book has argued that one of the most ordinary sentences in social life, So, when are you having kids?, is not the neutral pleasantry it appears to be, but a low-grade enforcement mechanism for the pronatalist default. The argument had three load-bearing parts. First, a grammatical one: the question presupposes reproduction rather than asking about it; when smuggles in whether, and the presupposition, being structural, resists the debate that an assertion would invite. Second, an asymmetry: the question flows toward the childless and childfree and never toward parents, so that reproduction is exempted from having to explain itself while its absence is perpetually called to account. Third, an accumulation: any single instance is trivial and warm, but the practice is repeated across a life by dozens of unrelated askers, and its structural effect is cumulative. It keeps the default tilted and the exception on the defensive, through a mechanism no individual operates on purpose. These three are not independent complaints but a single argument in three movements: the grammar installs the frame, the asymmetry ensures the frame presses on only one group, and the accumulation converts a thousand light presses into a standing weight. Remove any one and the mechanism loses its force; hold all three together and an invisible sentence turns out to be doing real cultural work.

Interrogation norm
the socially licensed, widespread practice of questioning adults about their reproductive plans in a form that presupposes reproduction is expected and owed, and that asks this only of those who have not (yet) reproduced.
Presupposition
a proposition that a sentence takes for granted rather than asserts; it survives negation and questioning. When are you having kids? presupposes that you will have kids.
Presupposition cancellation
the conversationally costly, "marked" act of rejecting a sentence's built-in assumption rather than answering within it (e.g. replying "actually, we're not planning to" instead of "not yet").
"When" vs "whether"
the central distinction of the book: when asks about timing and thereby presupposes the event; whether leaves the event genuinely open. The critique is that the norm licenses when and rarely whether.
Asymmetry (of accountability)
the imbalance whereby non-reproduction must be justified but reproduction need not; the interrogation norm's core payload.
Ambient accumulation
the property whereby each instance of the question is trivial but the sum, across many unrelated askers over years, becomes a real cumulative burden.
Felicity conditions
in speech-act theory, the conditions under which an utterance (such as a question) counts as appropriate. The norm makes the reproduction question felicitous by default, shifting onto the person asked the burden of signalling that it does not apply.
Face
in politeness theory (Brown & Levinson), a person's public self-image: positive face (the wish to be approved of) and negative face (the wish to be unimpeded). The question threatens the answerer's negative face; declining threatens the asker's positive face.
Microaggression
a brief, commonplace, often unintended slight communicating a negating message (Sue et al.). Used in this book analogically and modestly — for the shared structure (unintended, ambient, cumulative), not as a claim that the childfree face the same marginalisation the concept was built to describe.
Pronatalist default
the background cultural assumption that adults will and should reproduce, from which the reproduction question inherits its presupposition.
Heteronormativity
the default assumption that adult life follows heterosexual pairing toward biological children; one source of the norm's buried assumptions, which surface when the question meets a case it was not built for.
Social clock
Bernice Neugarten's concept of shared age norms specifying when life events "should" occur; invoked directly by the urgent forms isn't it time? and you're not getting any younger.
Collective entitlement to know
the residual assumption, inherited from kin-based societies where fertility affected inheritance and lineage, that a couple's reproduction is a communal rather than purely private matter.
Speech act
an utterance that performs an action (asking, asserting, promising); the unit of analysis for this book's critique of the practice of asking.
Steelman
the strongest, most sympathetic version of an opposing view, stated before it is criticised; here, the case that the question is usually loving, normal, useful, welcome, and statistically grounded.

⑥ Sources

What this volume draws on. Where the evidence is thin or contested, the book says so rather than reaching for a number.

  • Blackstone, Amy. Childfree by Choice: The Movement Redefining Family and Creating a New Age of Independence. Dutton, 2019. On voluntary childlessness and the recurrent social questioning of childfree adults.
  • Brown, Penelope, and Stephen C. Levinson. Politeness: Some Universals in Language Usage. Cambridge University Press, 1987. The canonical framework for "face" and politeness, used in Chapters 4–5.
  • Sue, Derald Wing, et al. "Racial Microaggressions in Everyday Life: Implications for Clinical Practice." American Psychologist, 2007. Origin of the microaggression concept; applied in this book analogically and with explicit caution, for its structure rather than as an identity-equivalence claim.
  • Neugarten, Bernice L. Research on age norms and the "social clock" (1960s–1970s). Widely cited for the idea that cultures carry shared expectations about the proper timing of life events; invoked here for the urgent forms of the question (isn't it time?). Attributed as a class of well-known work rather than to a single precise page.
  • Adichie, Chimamanda Ngozi. The Danger of a Single Story. TED talk, 2009. Source of the epigraph on stereotypes as incomplete rather than untrue.
  • Strawson, P. F. "On Referring." Mind, 1950. A foundational treatment of presupposition and referring expressions in the philosophy of language; the general framework (presupposition as distinct from assertion) is well established and safe to attribute at this level of generality.
Directions the volume points to (not pinned citations)
  • Park, Kristin. "Stigma Management Among the Voluntarily Childless." Sociological Perspectives, 2002. On the techniques — passing, identity substitution, condemning the condemnors, asserting a right to self-fulfillment — by which childfree adults manage questioning and stigma.
  • On the frequency of reproductive questioning, the reader will find the literature thinner than the phenomenon deserves. The burden is well attested in interview and testimony work; it has not been finely measured. This book states it accordingly.
  • Formal-semantic and philosophical treatments of presupposition beyond Strawson: Frege's remarks on presupposition, and the later formal-semantics tradition associated with Robert Stalnaker, Lauri Karttunen, and Irene Heim.
  • The sociological literature on pronatalism as a cultural default, including the work of Rosemary Gillespie and Carolyn Morell on voluntary childlessness and pronatalist norms.
  • Speech-act theory primary sources: J. L. Austin, How to Do Things with Words (1962), and John Searle, Speech Acts (1969), for the "felicity conditions" framing.
  • A note on the evidence: The strongest claim of this book — that when are you having kids? grammatically presupposes that the addressee will have kids — is a matter of standard presupposition theory and needs no empirical study to support it. The claims about the burden of the questioning are of a different kind. They rest on testimony, and testimony is qualitative. That is a real difference in evidential weight, and the argument is built so as not to lean on the softer material where the harder material will do.
