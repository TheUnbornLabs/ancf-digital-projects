Risk Argument Explainer

Philosophy · Difficulty: Intermediate · ~10 min

The risk argument says: creating a person imposes serious, unavoidable risk on someone who cannot agree — and that needs justifying. This page makes the factors concrete with an interactive tool, then shows the main ethical lenses and the leading replies.

What you will learn

  • The four factors that make a risk harder to justify.
  • How the same situation looks under expected-value, precautionary, and non-aggregation lenses.
  • Why "deciding for another" raises the bar.
  • The strongest replies to the argument.

2. Three lenses

Expected valueWeigh outcomes by probability and sum; a likely good life can outweigh a small bad chance. PrecautionSome harms are severe enough to avoid imposing any real risk of, whatever the upside. Non-aggregationOne person's severe suffering may not be cancelled by another's joy, so totals can mislead.

3. The leading replies

Life is usually worth the risk

Most lives contain real goods, and people endorse them. Reply: a gamble paying off often doesn't settle whether it was permissible to take for someone else.

We take risks for future people constantly

Parents choose schools, diets, places to live. Reply: those decisions shape an existing or certain-to-exist child; birth creates the very subject of the risk.

Without risk, nothing good happens

Refusing all risk would forbid most of life. Reply: the question is where to draw the line for risks imposed on a non-consenting other, not whether to draw one.

Key takeaway

Risk arguments don't claim life is bad — they ask what justifies imposing serious, un-consented risk on another. Where you set that bar, and how much weight you give to deciding for someone else, shapes your whole answer.
