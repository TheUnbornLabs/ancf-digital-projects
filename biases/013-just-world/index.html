Who Deserves a Child: Just-World Reasoning and Reproduction

Part I · Cognitive & Psychological Biases · Just-World Reasoning and Reproduction

The bias: The belief that good people get and want children, and the reverse.

We do not believe that we live in a world where good things happen to bad people or bad things to good; we work very hard to keep from believing it.

① In brief

Just-World Reasoning and Reproduction

This volume examines a quiet piece of moral arithmetic that runs beneath much of how people talk about having children: the assumption that the world is fundamentally fair, and therefore that reproductive outcomes are somehow deserved. On this frame, wanted parenthood becomes a reward for goodness, childlessness becomes a shortfall that must reflect a deficiency in the person, and infertility or loss gets read as a punishment, a test, or a meaningful lesson rather than as morally blank misfortune.

The engine behind this arithmetic is the belief in a just world, first studied systematically by Melvin Lerner in the 1960s and 1970s. Lerner's classic experiments showed that people are so motivated to believe outcomes are deserved that, faced with an innocent victim they cannot help, they will devalue the victim, deciding the sufferer must have brought it on. The world is kept fair by revising the person rather than accepting that suffering can be undeserved. This book applies that mechanism to reproduction and argues that it does three things: it frames parenthood as earned goodness, frames childlessness as deserved lack, and thereby generates a pronatalist pressure that has nothing to do with whether a given person wants or should have children, the pressure to stay on the "deserving" side of a moral ledger that does not actually exist.

Read the rest of the overview

The book is careful about its lane. It is distinct from Book 028 (the selfish stereotype) and Book 029 (the pity script), both of which it treats as outputs of the deeper fairness cognition it studies here. Its subject is the desert-inference itself: the move from an outcome to a moral verdict, made with no evidence of any act.

The stance is critical of pronatalist bias but evidence-first, and Chapter 2 belongs entirely to the other side: a genuinely strong steelman holding that a just-world orientation is good for mental health, that reproductive readiness really is partly earned, that honoring good parenting protects children, that meaning-making comforts the grieving, and that fairness intuitions fund compassion for the childless. The book grants each of these as partly true, then shows where each, pressed past its warrant, curdles into the cruelty of a verdict.

On evidence, the book is scrupulous about its seam. The just-world motive is real and well-replicated; victim-derogation and self-blame are documented; infertility stigma is real and harmful. But the specific claim that just-world reasoning is the engine of reproductive stigma is offered as a strong, illuminating hypothesis rather than a proven experimental result: a lens to try, not a theorem.

The hidden costs are heaviest on the involuntarily childless, who carry not only grief but the gratuitous suspicion that the grief is deserved; on those pushed toward parenthood to affirm their own worth; on honest grief and honest joy, both flattened into ledger entries; and on collective remedy, since reading misfortune as desert discourages funding clinics and support. The pressure falls hardest on women, on those already marked "less deserving," and, most quietly, on the self, in the sleepless what did I do to deserve this.

The book handles infertility with deliberate gentleness. Its target is never the person who longs for a child, but the surrounding culture, and each of us, that reads such longing's non-fulfillment as a comment on worth. Its corrective is to decouple fortune from desert, to let some misfortune be meaningless rather than assign it false purpose, and to choose about children from wanting and circumstance rather than from moral scoring. The measured takeaway: there is no cosmic ledger recording who deserves a child. Seeing that clearly does not argue anyone into or out of parenthood; it removes an auditor who was never there.

② The book in twelve chapters

Each chapter opens with its own summary. Note chapter 2: every volume states the strongest case for the thing it criticises before laying a finger on it — and chapter 8 turns the critique back on itself.

CH 1Naming the bias

The bias this book names is just-world reasoning applied to reproduction: the tendency to treat having, wanting, or being able to have children as morally deserved, as evidence of the parent's goodness, and to treat childlessness as a deficit that must reflect something lacking in the childless person.

CH 2The strongest case for it (steelman)The strongest case against this book

Before criticizing this way of thinking, we owe it the strongest version anyone actually holds, stated not as a straw man but as a serious position that thoughtful people defend. Here it is, as sympathetically as it can be put.

CH 3Where it comes from

If just-world reasoning about reproduction were merely a modern quirk, it would be easier to shrug off. It is not. It draws on some of the deepest currents in human cognition and culture, and understanding its sources is the first step to loosening its grip. A bias with a single, shallow root can be pulled out; this one has many roots, running down through psychology, evolution, religion, economics, and institutions, which is why it holds so firmly and why loosening it takes patience rather than a single insight.

CH 4How it operates today

The bias rarely announces itself. It works through defaults, scripts, and small linguistic habits that feel like common sense. Cataloguing them is the point of this chapter, because a bias you can hear is a bias you can start to disarm. The examples below are deliberately ordinary; the whole argument of this book is that the bias lives not in extraordinary cruelty but in the texture of ordinary, well-meant speech, and so learning to notice it requires attending to exactly the phrases that usually pass unexamined.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

What does the research actually establish, and what does it not? Honesty here matters, because the just-world literature is robust in some respects and thin or contested in others. A book that critiques others for reasoning backward from conclusions to evidence must be scrupulous about not doing the same, which means stating plainly where the science is solid, where it is suggestive, and where this book is offering an interpretation that outruns the data.

CH 6The hidden costs

If the just-world frame around reproduction were merely inaccurate but harmless, it could be left alone. It is not harmless. Its costs are largely invisible because the frame itself hides them, presenting each as either deserved or as nobody's fault. A cost that is experienced as deserved is not registered as a cost at all, which is precisely why these harms can persist at scale without provoking the outrage they warrant; the bias launders its own damage into the appearance of justice.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

Biases are not distributed evenly. The reproductive just-world frame falls hardest on some shoulders, and tracing the distribution is part of taking it seriously. A bias that fell equally on everyone would at least be impartial in its injustice; this one is not, and its unevenness compounds inequalities that already exist, which is why any honest account of its costs must also be an account of who pays them.

CH 8The counter-arguments to the critiqueObjections to the critique

A book that steelmans its opponent in Chapter 2 owes it a fair hearing again here, against its own conclusions. Several objections deserve honest engagement, and the strongest of them are not easily dismissed; a critique that could not survive its own best objections would not be worth making.

CH 9Case studies / illustrations

Abstractions convince less than instances. Four illustrations show the bias at work, some drawn from broad cultural patterns rather than named individuals, in keeping with this series' practice of not turning real sufferers into examples. The composites below are built from patterns documented across the qualitative literature and ordinary observation; no single real person is depicted, and the point of each is not the particular story but the machinery it makes visible.

CH 10Adjacent biases

This bias does not stand alone. It interlocks with others in the series, and mapping the connections keeps each book in its lane while showing the larger structure. Understanding the map matters because it clarifies what this book is not claiming: it is not the theory of all reproductive stigma but the account of one specific cognitive engine that helps drive several of the biases treated elsewhere.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

What does clear-eyed, non-coerced reasoning look like once this bias is named? The goal is not to replace one verdict with another, not to declare parenthood undeserved or childlessness noble, but to remove the ledger entirely and let people think about children without a cosmic auditor looking over their shoulder. The work is largely subtractive: not adding a new belief but retiring an old one, and then noticing how much lighter the decision becomes once the false premise is gone.

CH 12Conclusion

This book has argued that a specific cognitive habit, the belief in a just world applied to reproduction, quietly shapes how we score having and not having children. The habit reads reproductive fortune as desert: parenthood as earned goodness, childlessness as deserved lack, infertility and loss as meaningful rather than merely misfortunate. Because the belief in a just world is self-protective and largely unconscious, this scoring operates even in kind people who intend no harm, and it operates hardest inside the very people it wounds. Its pronatalist force lies in the reward it dangles, confirmed virtue, and the shadow it casts: the fear of being judged, by others or by oneself, as one of the undeserving.

Show all 16 terms as a list
Belief in a just world (BJW)
The largely unconscious conviction that the world is fundamentally fair, so people tend to get what they deserve and deserve what they get; named and studied by Melvin Lerner.
Just-world reasoning about reproduction
The bias examined in this book: reading reproductive outcomes — having, wanting, or being able to have children — as morally deserved.
Derogation of victims
The just-world-driven tendency to devalue an innocent sufferer one cannot help, so as to preserve the belief that suffering is deserved (Lerner & Simmons, 1966).
Immanent-justice reasoning
Interpreting a misfortune as a punishment, test, or meaningful consequence of the sufferer's earlier conduct or character.
Immanent vs. ultimate justice
Immanent justice locates desert in the present outcome; ultimate-justice belief defers the fair reckoning to the future or an afterlife, sustaining the just-world belief when present outcomes look unfair.
The personal contract
Lerner's term for the childhood bargain (forgo present gratification for future reward) that grounds and motivates the belief in a just world.
BJW-self vs. BJW-others
The distinction between believing the world is fair to oneself (linked to well-being) and to others (linked more to harsh judgment of the unfortunate).
Victim self-blame
The internalized form of the bias, in which a sufferer searches their own past for a fault that would make their misfortune deserved.
Desert inference
The core reasoning move of the bias: inferring a person's moral worth backward from their fortune, absent any evidence of an act.
Moral halo (of parenthood)
The unearned upgrade in perceived virtue conferred by becoming a parent; the positive face of the reproductive just-world frame.
Reproductive stigma
The social devaluation of the childless, much of it plausibly generated by just-world reasoning (the broader subject of Book 050).
Consolation script
Reflexive comforting phrases ("everything happens for a reason") that preserve the speaker's just-world belief by implying the sufferer's misfortune is purposeful.
System justification
Jost's theory, a close cousin of the just-world hypothesis, describing the tendency to defend existing social arrangements as fair even when they disadvantage oneself.
Decoupling fortune from desert
The corrective practice of separating what happens to a person from any judgment about what they deserve.
Presence-without-explanation
The humane alternative to the consolation script: accompanying a grieving person without assigning meaning or blame to their loss.
Care as virtue vs. fertility as virtue
The proposed redirection of moral esteem from the mere fact of having children toward the actual work of raising them well.

⑥ Sources

What this volume draws on. Where the evidence is thin or contested, the book says so rather than reaching for a number.

  • Melvin J. Lerner, The Belief in a Just World: A Fundamental Delusion. Plenum Press, 1980. The foundational book-length statement of just-world theory, including the "personal contract" account of its origins.
  • Melvin J. Lerner and Carolyn H. Simmons, "Observer's Reaction to the 'Innocent Victim': Compassion or Rejection?" Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 1966. The classic derogation-of-victims experiment.
  • John T. Jost and colleagues, system justification theory (developed from the 1990s onward) — the just-world hypothesis's close theoretical cousin, explaining why people defend social arrangements that disadvantage them.
  • David Benatar, Better Never to Have Been: The Harm of Coming into Existence. Oxford University Press, 2006. Cited for the broader philosophical backdrop of the series rather than for this specific bias.
  • Headline demographic facts used in the text — falling global fertility and sub-replacement total fertility rates across much of the world — are attributed to the class of authoritative sources (UN World Population Prospects; national statistical agencies) rather than to any invented table.
Directions the volume points to (not pinned citations)
  • Zick Rubin and Letitia Anne Peplau, whose 1970s work developed and validated the Just World Scale.
  • Carolyn L. Hafer and colleagues, whose review articles distinguish the just-world motive from mere belief and summarize the experimental literature.
  • Claudia Dalbert, on belief in a just world and psychological well-being, including the BJW-self / BJW-others distinction.
  • Adrian Furnham, whose review articles survey belief in a just world across cultures.
  • The qualitative and survey literature on infertility stigma and the psychological burden of involuntary childlessness, a large and well-developed body of work. The book relies on its general findings and cites no figure.
  • World Health Organization and national statistical estimates of infertility prevalence. Definitions differ across sources, and the book therefore describes the scale of the problem without pinning it to a rate.
  • The literature connecting just-world belief to opposition to redistribution and to structural remedy, invoked in Chapter 6.
