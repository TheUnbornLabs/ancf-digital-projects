Housing and Where You Live

Choosing a home and a place around your actual life, not a hypothetical family.

"We shape our dwellings, and afterwards our dwellings shape us."

— often attributed to Winston Churchill

Where and how you live is one of the largest decisions a person makes, and most of the default advice about it is written for families. Buy in a good school district. Get the extra bedroom. Prioritize the yard. For a childfree person, much of that script simply does not apply — which is liberating once you notice it, and quietly disorienting if you do not. This chapter is about choosing a home around the life you actually live, not the hypothetical one a culture built for families assumes you must be heading toward.

Designing for your real life, not a hypothetical one

The most common housing mistake childfree people make is buying for a family that does not exist. The "spare bedroom for when kids come," the big house in a quiet suburb, the layout optimized for a stage of life you have chosen not to enter — these can quietly impose the costs of parenthood (a larger mortgage, more cleaning, more maintenance, more distance from the things you enjoy) without any of the reasons for them.

The alternative is to start from your actual days. Do you work from home and need a real office more than a nursery? Do you host friends and want a generous kitchen and a guest room that earns its keep? Do you crave a studio for a hobby, a garage for a project, a reading nook, room for pets? Designing for the life you genuinely live tends to produce a home that fits like a good coat — and often a smaller, cheaper, easier one than the family template would have pushed you toward.

In plain words: Don't buy bedrooms for children you've decided not to have. Buy the rooms your life uses — an office, a guest room, a studio, a workshop — and let the rest go.

The freedom of location

Here is an advantage that is easy to underrate until you feel it: you are released from the single most powerful constraint on where families can live — the school catchment. Parents routinely pay a premium, accept long commutes, or rule out whole neighborhoods to land in a particular school zone. None of that binds you.

That freedom opens real options. You can live closer to work, in a walkable neighborhood, near the cultural life or landscape you love, in a smaller town or a denser city, or somewhere simply cheaper — and you can move more easily when your life changes, unencumbered by the disruption of pulling a child out of school mid-year. Location can be chosen for your priorities: commute, community, climate, cost, proximity to friends and aging parents, or the things you actually do on a Saturday.

Released from the school catchment, you can choose a home for your commute, your community, and your weekends.

Right-sizing

"Right-sizing" simply means matching your home to your needs rather than to a status template or a family-shaped default. For many childfree people that means choosing something smaller than convention expects — and discovering it is a gift rather than a sacrifice. A smaller, well-chosen home generally costs less to buy and to maintain, frees up money for the savings goals from Chapter 10, takes less time to clean and care for, and leaves more of your life for living rather than for upkeep.

Right-sizing is not a rule that smaller is always better; it is a habit of asking what each square foot is for. A childfree couple who loves to entertain may rightly want more space; a solo minimalist may want far less. The point is to choose deliberately rather than to inherit a family's footprint by default.

Community and walkability

For families, a great deal of community arrives automatically through children — school gates, sports teams, birthday parties, other parents. Childfree people do not get that built-in social infrastructure, which makes the surrounding environment matter more, not less. A walkable neighborhood, proximity to friends, access to the places where your kind of community gathers — a gym, a maker space, a café, a place of worship, a trailhead — can do real work in keeping a life connected.

Worth knowing

  • Walkability and density tend to support more spontaneous social contact, which matters when you are not getting it through a child's social world.
  • Proximity to friends and chosen family is a legitimate, even high, priority — closeness is a form of long-term insurance, not just convenience.
  • "Aging in place" is easier in a home and neighborhood designed for it; the choices you make at fifty shape your options at eighty.
  • Renting versus buying is a personal and financial question, not a moral one; the right answer depends on your circumstances and local market, and is worth discussing with a qualified financial professional.

Planning a home that works as you age

This is the dimension the family script never has to think hard about, because it quietly assumes grown children nearby. Without that default, it is worth being deliberate, earlier than feels necessary, about a home that will still serve you when mobility, energy, or health change. The unglamorous design choices made in your fifties can be exactly what keeps you independent in your eighties.

Consider single-level living, or at least a layout where a full life is possible without stairs, for the long term.
Favor low-maintenance materials and systems, so upkeep does not become a burden as energy declines.
Prioritize proximity to healthcare, transport, and daily errands you could reach without driving.
Weigh nearness to friends, chosen family, or supportive community against a larger or cheaper place farther away.
Think about how the home would adapt to in-home care or reduced mobility, since you may be arranging support rather than relying on adult children (see Chapter 10).
Avoid over-housing yourself for a hypothetical family; redirect what you save toward security and the things you love.
Common worry

"If I buy a small place now, won't I regret it if my life changes — a partner, a relative who needs to move in, or a change of heart?"

In reality

Flexibility is a feature you can design for without over-buying. A guest room, an adaptable layout, or simply the financial breathing room that comes from not over-housing yourself all leave you free to respond to change — including a change of plans. Choosing a home for your life now is not the same as locking yourself out of a different future; it usually leaves you with more options, not fewer.

A balanced view

There is no single childfree way to live — some want a city apartment within walking distance of everything, others a quiet cottage with a workshop and a garden. What unites the good versions is honesty: a home chosen for the life genuinely being lived, freed from a template designed for a different one, and planned with enough foresight that it will still be a good home decades from now. That is not a smaller ambition than the family dream. It is simply a different one, fitted to a different life.

Chapter in brief

  • Choose a home for the life you actually live — an office, guest room, or studio — rather than spare bedrooms for hypothetical children.
  • Freedom from school catchments opens real choice in location: commute, walkability, community, climate, and cost.
  • Right-sizing matches a home to your needs, often saving money, time, and upkeep — though "right" sometimes means more space, not less.
  • Community does not arrive automatically without children, so walkability and proximity to friends matter more.
  • Plan early for aging in place, since you may be arranging support rather than relying on adult children nearby.
