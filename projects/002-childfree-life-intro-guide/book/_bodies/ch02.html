"The desire to have children is not innate; it is a social construction reinforced by countless small pressures."

— a paraphrase of Leta Hollingworth's argument, 1916

The childfree choice can feel brand new — a product of the modern age, of birth control and careers and crowded cities. In one sense it is: the word "childfree" and the open, public version of the choice are recent. But the impulse behind it is old, and the story of how a private decision became a recognizable movement is worth knowing. It helps to understand that you are not the first person to feel this way, and that a long line of thinkers, writers, and ordinary people cleared the ground you now stand on.

When parenthood was simply assumed

For most of recorded history, having children was not a decision in any meaningful sense. It was the expected outcome of marriage, often a religious duty, frequently an economic necessity, and — without reliable contraception — usually unavoidable for anyone who was sexually active. Children were labor on a farm, security in old age, and the only widely available way to leave something of yourself behind. People who never had children were usually understood through that lens: the childless widow, the "spinster," the celibate clergy. To choose a life without children, and to say so out loud, was nearly unthinkable.

That does not mean no one did it. Across cultures and centuries there were always people who quietly preferred other lives — religious vocations, artistic devotion, devoted aunthood and uncle-hood, partnerships that simply never turned toward children. But there was no shared language for the preference and no public movement behind it.

An early voice: Leta Hollingworth

One of the first people to name the machinery of pressure was the American psychologist Leta Hollingworth. In a 1916 paper she argued that society did not leave childbearing to instinct at all; instead it deployed an array of "social devices" — law, religion, public opinion, art, and education — precisely because the desire to bear and raise children was not automatic. If it were truly instinctive, she pointed out, no such pressure would be needed. Her insight was quietly radical: it reframed parenthood as something society compels rather than something every person naturally craves. That reframing sits at the root of nearly every childfree argument made since.

Pronatalism — the cultural and structural assumption that having children is the normal, expected, and superior choice, built into everything from tax codes to small talk. Naming it was a turning point: once you can see the pressure, you can ask whether to obey it.

The 1970s turning point

The modern movement crystallized in the United States in the early 1970s, riding the energy of second-wave feminism, the spread of effective contraception, and rising environmental awareness. Two events stand out.

In 1971, writer Ellen Peck published The Baby Trap, a blunt, popular argument that not everyone should have children and that many women were being pushed into motherhood against their real interests. It was provocative by design, and it put the idea on the cultural map.

The following year, 1972, Peck and Shirley Radl founded the National Organization for Non-Parents (NON) in Palo Alto, California. NON's mission was striking for its time: to make choosing not to parent respectable and responsible, to support people who made that choice, and to challenge pronatalist discrimination. The group later renamed itself the National Alliance for Optional Parenthood and continued into the early 1980s. The very phrase "optional parenthood" captured the whole shift — parenthood reframed as a genuine option rather than a default.

In plain words: The 1970s gave the choice three things it had never had — a popular book, an organized group, and a name for the pressure it was pushing back against.

From "non-parent" to "childfree"

Notice the language of that era: "non-parent," "childless," "optional parenthood." Each defines the life by what is missing — a parent who isn't, a child who isn't there. Over the following decades, communities gravitated toward a different word: childfree. The "-free" did real work. Like "carefree" or "debt-free," it framed the absence as openness rather than lack. The shift from "childless" to "childfree" was small in letters and large in meaning, and it remains one of the clearest markers of the movement's confidence.

"Childless" names what is missing. "Childfree" names what is chosen.

The choice becomes a subject of study

As the choice grew more visible, scholars began to study it seriously rather than treat it as a curiosity. Canadian sociologist Jean Veevers conducted interviews with voluntarily childless couples and published Childless by Choice in 1980, one of the first sustained academic looks at how people arrive at and live out the decision. Around the same period, sociologist Sharon Houseknecht reviewed the research and helped distinguish those who chose childlessness from those who did not. This scholarship mattered because it replaced stereotype with evidence: childfree people, it turned out, were not a single sad or selfish type but a varied group reaching a considered decision.

The internet era

If the 1970s gave the movement a name and the 1980s gave it study, the internet gave it community. For the first time, people who felt isolated in their own towns could find thousands of others who felt the same. Online forums and mailing lists appeared in the 1990s and 2000s; the r/childfree community on Reddit grew into one of the largest gathering places of its kind. Alongside it came lighter cultural shorthand — DINK, "dual income, no kids," a phrase that turned a demographic category into something close to a lifestyle identity. The tone of these spaces varies from joyful to venting, but their existence changed the experience of the choice from lonely to shared.

DINK — "dual income, no kids," a couple without children and with two earners. The term began as marketing demographics and became popular shorthand for a particular childfree lifestyle.

Modern books and rising numbers

The twenty-first century brought a wave of thoughtful writing. Laura Carroll documented happily childfree couples in Families of Two (2000) and examined cultural pressure in The Baby Matrix (2012). In France, Corinne Maier's provocative No Kid (2007) stirred debate across Europe. And sociologist Amy Blackstone, herself childfree, brought research and warmth together in Childfree by Choice (2019), perhaps the most balanced popular account to date.

Meanwhile the numbers shifted. The Pew Research Center has reported that a growing share of U.S. adults say they are unlikely ever to have children, and that the most common reason given is simply that they do not want to. Without inventing precise figures, the direction is clear: what was once a fringe choice has become a familiar one. The movement's threads — feminism's insistence on reproductive autonomy, environmental concern about population and resources, and a broader cultural permission to design your own life — have woven the childfree choice into the mainstream.

International Childfree Day, observed each year on August 1, honors Ellen Peck and marks how far the conversation has come: from unthinkable, to organized, to studied, to ordinary.

Chapter in brief

  • For most of history, parenthood was assumed rather than chosen; there was no public language for opting out.
  • Leta Hollingworth (1916) argued that society pressures people into childbearing precisely because it is not purely instinctive.
  • The 1970s were the turning point: The Baby Trap (1971) and the founding of NON (1972, Peck & Radl) named and challenged pronatalism.
  • Language shifted from "non-parent"/"childless" to childfree; Veevers (1980) and others studied the choice seriously.
  • The internet built community (forums, r/childfree, DINK culture), and modern authors — Carroll, Maier, Blackstone — broadened the conversation.
  • Linked to feminism, environmentalism, and reproductive autonomy, the choice has grown common, as Pew's research on declining intentions reflects.
