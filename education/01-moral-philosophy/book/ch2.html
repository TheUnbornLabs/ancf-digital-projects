Why the Absence of Pain Is Good

C hapter One set the asymmetry out and then walked away from its hardest moment on purpose. It asked you to grant Premise Three, that absent pain is good with no one to enjoy it, on the strength of a vivid case and a courtroom's unease, and it admitted that neither one is a defense. This chapter pays the debt. It asks the question you have been holding since the table first appeared. How can an absence be good when there is no one for whom it is good? That is not a hostile question. It is the most natural question there is, and the credibility of the asymmetry rides on whether it can be answered without a trick.

To answer it we first need an account of what harm and benefit even are, because the whole quarrel over Premise Three is, underneath, a quarrel about that. The chapter moves in four steps. It sets out the dominant account of harm, the counterfactual comparative account, and shows why Premise Three looks suspect under it. It develops the first defense of Premise Three, the impersonal reading, and names what that costs. It develops the second defense, the comparative reading, and names its different cost. Then it sets out the move that does the most work, and that gets the least credit: the claim that the asymmetry need not be defended head-on at all, because it is the best explanation of four other asymmetries that almost everyone already accepts. By the end you should be able to say which reading of Premise Three you are leaning on, what it costs you, and why those four other asymmetries, not the lone intuition about absent pain, are the real ground beneath the premise.

What Harm Is

The reigning account of harm in analytic philosophy is counterfactual and comparative. An event harms a person when it makes them worse off than they would otherwise have been: when their welfare given the event is lower than their welfare in the nearest world without it. To be harmed is to be set below your own counterfactual baseline. The account is intuitive and widely held, and it explains why harm matters morally. A harm is a setback to someone, measured against how that same someone would otherwise have fared, and the someone has a complaint precisely because their own counterfactual was better.

Now carry the account to Premise Three and watch the trouble surface. Premise Three says the absence of pain in non-existence is good. Good for whom, and against what baseline? On the counterfactual account, a state is good for a person when it leaves them better off than they would otherwise have been. In the case of the never-created person there is no one whose welfare can be plotted, no baseline to measure against, no subject to carry the comparison. The account seems to make Premise Three not just false but ill- formed. It predicts that non-existence is neither good nor bad for anyone, because there is no one there to hold a level of welfare at all. The very framework that makes harm intelligible appears to make Premise Three unintelligible. That is the difficulty at full strength, and it does have decisive force against the naive reading of Premise Three, the reading on which some particular non-existent person enjoys, in the void, the benefit of their own missing suffering. There is no such person. A benefit needs a beneficiary. A defender who reaches for that reading has already lost, and both serious defenses below begin by refusing it.

One thing should be said, since the counterfactual account is not the only account of harm, and the antinatalist is sometimes accused of leaning on it selectively. There are non-comparative accounts too, on which certain states, agony, mutilation, severe deprivation, are harms in themselves, with no baseline required. If a non-comparative account is right, the antinatalist's road is in one way smoother: the suffering created by procreation is a harm intrinsically, not because it makes the created person worse off than non- existence. But in another way it gets rougher, because the benefit side of the asymmetry then needs its own non-comparative treatment, and the symmetry between the two sides becomes harder to break. The correct theory of harm is unsettled, and the asymmetry's fate is tangled up with it. Different theories of harm make Premise Three easier or harder to defend, and none of them owns the field. The defender's task is not to pretend the question is settled in their favor. It is to show that on the most plausible theories Premise Three can be read coherently. The next two sections try.

Three Theories of Harm, and Why the Choice Matters

We should slow down here, because the dispute over Premise Three is, underneath, a dispute about what harm is, and the philosophy of harm is its own developed field with its own factions. Matthew Hanser, in a careful survey, sorts the serious accounts into three families, and seeing all three keeps the antinatalist from being accused of cherry-picking the one that suits.

The first is the counterfactual comparative account just described, whose canonical statement is Joel Feinberg's: to be harmed is to be made worse off than one would otherwise have been. The baseline is the nearest possible world in which the harmful event did not occur. The second is the temporal comparative account: to be harmed is to be made worse off than one was before, the baseline being one's own prior condition rather than a counterfactual. The third is the non-comparative account, associated with Seana Shiffrin: to be harmed is to be put into a certain kind of bad state, pain, disability, deprivation, the badness of which does not depend on any comparison at all. The first two are comparative; they locate harm in a gap between two levels of welfare. The third locates it in the intrinsic character of a state.

None of the three is free of trouble, and the trouble is instructive, because it shows the antinatalist is not exploiting a defect peculiar to the account they need. The counterfactual comparative account, the most popular of the three, faces two well-known problems. The first is that it cannot cleanly distinguish harming someone from merely failing to benefit them, since both leave the person worse off than they would otherwise have been; preserving the moral difference between the two, which almost everyone wants, seems to require smuggling in an extra baseline the account does not supply. The second is the preemption problem: if some other cause would have produced the same bad state anyway, the counterfactual account says no harm was done, since the victim is no worse off than they would have been, which gives the wrong verdict in cases of overdetermined injury. These problems are serious enough that Ben Bradley has argued, in a much-cited paper, that the concept of harm is too defective to do real philosophical work and should be set aside altogether. That is a striking position for a moral philosopher to reach, and it should temper any confidence that "harm" is a stable foundation for anyone, antinatalist or natalist.

Now watch how the choice among the three plays out for Premise Three, because this is the payoff. On the counterfactual comparative account, Premise Three is hardest, for the reason already given: there is no baseline for the never-created person, no counterfactual welfare level to fall below, so the account struggles to call non-existence good or bad for them at all. On the temporal account it is no easier, since there is no prior condition of the never- created person to compare against either. The non-comparative account, by contrast, changes the board. If suffering is a harm in itself, with no baseline needed, then the suffering procreation creates is a genuine harm regardless of any comparison with non-existence, and the antinatalist gains the harm side of the ledger without having to defend a comparison involving a missing subject. This looks like a gift. But the gift comes wrapped in a cost, and the cost is the whole game. If goods and bads can be non-comparative, then the goods of existence are non-comparative too, real benefits intrinsically, and the asymmetry between Premise Three and Premise Four, the claim that absent pain is good while absent pleasure is merely not-bad, becomes much harder to sustain, because the natural symmetric move is to say that just as the suffering is intrinsically bad, the joy is intrinsically good, and the two offset. The non-comparative account helps the antinatalist establish that creation harms and hurts the symmetry argument that the harm is not outweighed.

Elizabeth Harman pressed exactly this region in an early paper asking whether we can harm or benefit a person by creating them at all. Her answer cuts against the easy antinatalist line: if any account on which creation harms is also an account on which creation benefits, then the bare fact that existence contains harm settles nothing, since it equally contains benefit, and the verdict turns entirely on how the two are weighed, which is the contested aggregation of Chapter One, not a result the theory of harm delivers on its own. The honest conclusion of this section is therefore a modest one, and it is the conclusion the whole chapter is built to earn. The theory of harm is unsettled to the point that a serious philosopher proposes abandoning the concept. Every account carries costs. No account hands the antinatalist a free victory, and none hands the natalist one either. The antinatalist must choose a theory of harm and pay its price in the open, and the most defensible choice is one on which Premise Three can be read coherently, which the next two sections supply, while conceding that a reader who holds a different theory of harm may reasonably resist it. That concession is not a weakness in the presentation. It is the actual state of the field, reported without flattery.

The First Defense: Impersonal Goodness

The first defense takes the hit and defends impersonal value directly. Some states of affairs, it holds, are good or bad full stop, impersonally, not good-or- bad for any subject. And far from being an oddity invented to save the asymmetry, this is something we already believe across a wide range of ordinary cases that have nothing to do with procreation.

We say without hesitation that a world with less suffering in it is, other things equal, a better world than one with more. Not better for any named person. Better. We are glad a forest fire burned out before it reached a sleeping town, even in the version where everyone would have woken and escaped unharmed; the gladness does not seem to wait on our naming who it is for. Many of us judge the suffering of wild animals who will never be known or helped to be bad, and its absence good, without supposing the goodness has to be cashed as good-for some creature we could pick out. Most telling of all: a universe of nothing but eons of pointless agony seems worse than an empty one, though in neither case is there anyone for whom the empty universe goes better. If those judgments are coherent, and most people make them with no strain, then impersonal goodness is not special pleading on antinatalism's behalf. It is already in wide use, and Premise Three only extends it one more step. Absent suffering is impersonally good, and the missing beneficiary cancels that goodness no more than the missing beneficiary cancels the badness of the agony-filled universe.

The strength of this defense is its candor. It claims exactly what it claims and pretends the claim is no smaller than it is. The cost is just as clear, and the literature names it precisely. It commits the defender to impersonal value, which the person-affecting tradition flatly denies. That tradition, in various forms from Narveson, Roberts, and others, holds that an outcome can only be better if it is better for someone, that there is no such thing as a benefit or harm with no subject, and that "good, but good for no one" is a confusion, not a deep truth. To the person-affecting theorist, Premise Three on the impersonal reading is false from the start, and no pile of vivid cases will move them, because they hold a principled reason to reject the whole category the cases trade on. The impersonal defense does not refute that theorist. It trades blows with them. And the result of that exchange, impersonalism against the person-affecting restriction, is one of the open questions of value theory, a question the asymmetry's fate partly rides on, and one this volume will not pretend to settle. What can be said is that impersonalism is a respectable and widely held position in its own right, not a desperate invention, and that the person-affecting denial of it carries its own heavy costs, which the next chapter surveys when the symmetry objection comes due.

The Second Defense: The Comparative Reading

The second defense is subtler, and to many readers more satisfying, because it steps around impersonal value instead of embracing it. On this reading Premise Three does not say absent pain is good in itself, for no one. It says absent pain is good comparatively, better than the relevant alternative, which is the pain the created being would have undergone. The judgment is not "this absence is a free-floating good." It is "this absence is better than what existence would have delivered." And better than is a relation whose two terms are both present: pain-free non-existence on one side, pain-containing existence on the other.

The advantage is real. It locates the goodness of absent pain in a comparison rather than in a lonely impersonal fact, and the argument is already committed to comparisons of existence with non-existence; Chapter One built the whole asymmetry as one, the table being a comparison of two scenarios. On the comparative reading, Premise Three says only this: for the being who might have been created, non-existence is better with respect to pain than existence would have been, since existence guarantees some pain and non-existence guarantees none. A clean comparative judgment, and one that does not obviously demand a beneficiary installed in the void. It demands only that two scenarios can be compared and ranked on a given dimension.

The pressure point has to be named, because there is one, and a sharp critic finds it in seconds. To say non-existence is better for the being who might have existed is to hang a comparative welfare relation, better-for, on someone who does not exist in the scenario where the relation is supposed to hold. The critic presses right there. Better for whom? In Scenario B there is no one for whom anything is better or worse, so "better for X" has no X on the winning side. The comparative reading answers the impersonal-value worry, since it need posit no free-floating value, but it inherits a version of the no-subject worry in its place, now pinned to better-for rather than to the bare word good. Whether it truly escapes the problem or only moves it is contested. Defenders say comparisons of existence with non-existence make sense as comparisons of two whole world-states, and that "better for her never to have existed" is shorthand for a coherent ranking of those states that does not need her present in both. Critics, following Epicurus, answer that welfare comparisons need a subject in both states being compared, and that the shorthand papers over an incoherence. This is the same dispute the non- identity problem of Chapter Six takes up in full, so the comparative reading hands the argument forward to that chapter rather than closing the matter here. The two readings of Premise Three, then, do not merely meet different objections. They send the argument down different roads: the impersonal reading toward the war over impersonal value, the comparative reading toward the war over subjectless comparison.

A word on Benatar himself, since the comparative reading is sometimes laid at his door. Defenders reach for the comparative gloss to escape the charge that the asymmetry trades in benefits with no beneficiaries. But Benatar's texts are read both ways, and on the more common reading he insists that the absence of pain is genuinely good, not merely "better than," which pulls his stated view back toward the impersonal reading of the previous section. The comparative reading is best treated as one available development of Premise Three, attractive for its lighter metaphysics, rather than as a settled report of what Benatar held. Which reading the historical Benatar took matters less, for us, than that both are on the table and each carries a price.

The Move That Does the Work: Four Other Asymmetries

There is a third line, and it is the strongest of the three, because it does not argue for Premise Three head-on at all. It argues that the asymmetry, the very pairing of Premises Three and Four, is the best explanation of four other asymmetries that almost everyone accepts on reflection. To reject Three and Four you would have to give up these four judgments too, or find some other account of them that no one has yet made work as cheaply. This is the move that has kept the asymmetry alive through twenty years of assault, and it is worth taking one asymmetry at a time, because the force is cumulative and a reader who sees only one will undervalue it.

The first is the asymmetry of procreational duties. There is a duty not to bring into being a person whose life would be sheer suffering; the couple of Chapter One's case would do wrong to conceive. There is no matching duty to bring into being a person whose life would be full of happiness; the secure couple who decline to conceive a flourishing child do no wrong. One is a real obligation. The other is, to almost everyone, not even an option, let alone a duty. Why the gap? The asymmetry explains it cleanly. The suffering of the possible miserable person counts decisively against creating them, by Premises One and Three; its presence would be bad and its absence good. The happiness of the possible joyful person sets up no duty to create them, by Premises Two and Four; its presence would be good, but its absence is not bad, so nothing is lost by the omission and no duty is broken. This is Narveson's asymmetry, make people happy but stay neutral about making happy people, and it is the most secure of the four, because denying it commits you to a duty of procreative beneficence almost no one accepts: a standing obligation to conceive as many happy people as you can, on pain of moral failure. Reject Premise Four and you owe an account of why we are not all derelict every night we do not procreate. No one has made that account plausible.

The second is the asymmetry of prospective reasons. When you are deciding whether to have a child, it makes plain sense to cite the prospect that the child would suffer as a reason not to. It sounds off-key, not just weak but conceptually strange, to cite the prospect that the child would be happy as a reason for creating it for the child's own sake. We can decline to create a person to spare them a bad life. We cannot create a person to benefit them with a good one, because before creation there is no them to be the intended beneficiary. The asymmetry predicts exactly this lopsidedness in what counts as a reason. A natalist who insists we create happy children for their own sakes has to explain how an act can be done for the sake of someone who does not yet exist and whose existence the act itself produces. The explanation, once attempted, tends to fold back into the language of impersonal good, or of the parents' own wishes, and neither of those is the child's interest.

The third is the asymmetry of retrospective accounts. We can regret, for the sake of a person we created, that we brought them into a life of suffering. The regret is genuinely on their behalf, and it makes full sense; a parent who conceived knowing the child would suffer terribly, and who later grieves for that child, is intelligible to us. We cannot regret, for the sake of a person we did not create, that we failed to bring them into a happy life, because there is no one whose behalf the regret could be on. About the children we chose not to have we may feel many things, wistfulness, curiosity, relief, but grief on their behalf is not among the coherent ones. The asymmetry predicts the pattern again: the suffering of the created counts for them, while the absent happiness of the uncreated counts for no one.

The fourth is the asymmetry of distant absence. We are rightly saddened by the suffering of people in places we will never see; their pain is bad, and the badness does not shrink with distance or with our ignorance of their names. We are not, and need not be, saddened by the happy people who could exist on some far, habitable, empty planet and do not. The empty planet, able to hold flourishing lives and holding none, is no tragedy. The suffering continent is. If absent happiness were bad the way present suffering is bad, the empty planet should grieve us as the suffering continent does; we should mourn the unborn happy multitudes the cosmos fails to produce. We do not. The asymmetry says why, with no special pleading. Absent suffering is good, so the lifeless planet, holding no suffering, is to that extent fine; absent happiness is merely not bad, so the planet's failure to hold happy lives is no loss to mourn. The natalist who denies Premise Four owes us an account of why the empty galaxy is not one vast running catastrophe of unborn joy, and no such account has ever felt like anything but absurd.

Now step back and see the shape of the move, because the shape is the point. Any one of the four asymmetries might be explained away by a clever critic with a tailored story for that case alone. But the basic asymmetry explains all four at once, with one mechanism, the pairing of Premises Three and Four, and no rival on offer matches that economy across all four. This is what a good theoretical posit looks like in any field. Not a premise defended in lonely isolation, but a small assumption that earns its keep by making unified sense of a wide range of judgments we already hold and would hate to give up. This, and not the solitary intuition about a spared child, is the real ground of the asymmetry. The intuition opens the door. The four asymmetries are what stand inside, and what pay the rent.

What This Chapter Leaves Open

The four-asymmetries defense is strong. It has survived twenty years of professional fire. It does not close the case, and the next chapter is largely the working-out of why.

The first limit is structural. A best-explanation argument is only as safe as the absence of a rival, and the absence of a rival is a temporary state in philosophy, never a permanent one. Critics have proposed person-affecting accounts of all four asymmetries, accounts that draw the same four judgments from a wide person-affecting principle, or from Johann Frick's idea that the reason to give someone a good life is conditional on their coming to exist, without invoking Premise Three's impersonal goodness at all. On Frick's approach the reason to provide a good life is conditional on there being a person whose life it is, while the reason to avoid imposing a bad life is not so conditional, and that alone reproduces the procreation asymmetry on person-affecting ground, threatening to make Benatar's impersonal Premise Three explanatorily idle. Frick presents this conditional-reasons view as a middle way between the narrow person-affecting theories and the wide or totalist ones, which is why it counts as a distinct rival rather than a restatement of the wide principle. Whether those rivals really capture all four asymmetries, without either collapsing into impersonalism or giving wrong verdicts elsewhere, is the live dispute of Chapter Three. So the claim here has to stay conditional. The basic asymmetry is the best explanation of the four sub-asymmetries if the person-affecting rivals fail, and whether they fail is not yet settled, by the field or by this book.

The second limit is the menu itself. Two readings of Premise Three were laid out, impersonal and comparative, and the chapter never told you which to order. That was not evasion. Each reading answers one objection and inherits another, and the choice between them is a choice about which objection you would rather spend the rest of the argument facing. The impersonal reading must defend impersonal value against the person- affecting theorist. The comparative reading must defend better-for-a-non- existent-being against the no-subject worry that runs back to Epicurus. No reading of Premise Three escapes both, and a defender who claims to have one will be caught. What the defender can say, and say with force, is this. Premise Three is not arbitrary. It earns its place by explaining four judgments we already make and would hate to surrender. Its leftover difficulties are shared, in mirror image, by every rival account of those same four judgments, including the accounts that reject it. And the position has been built so that even if Premise Three falls outright, two further pillars carry the conclusion. That is not a proof. It is a strong, self-aware position in a live debate, which is the most any foundational premise in this field can claim, and exactly what the next chapter puts under maximum load.
