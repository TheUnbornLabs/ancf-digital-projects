"The absence of pain is good, even if there is no one to enjoy that good; but the absence of pleasure is bad only if there is someone for whom this absence is a deprivation."

— David Benatar (paraphrasing the asymmetry)

The single most influential argument in modern antinatalism is also its most slippery on first contact. It comes from the South African philosopher David Benatar (born 1966, University of Cape Town), whose 2006 book Better Never to Have Been set out a claim that startles almost everyone who meets it: that coming into existence is always a serious harm to the one who comes into existence, no matter how good that life later turns out to be. Not usually a harm. Not a harm for the unlucky. Always, and for everyone.

That conclusion sounds extreme, and it is meant to. But Benatar does not arrive at it by claiming that life is mostly misery, or that the pleasures of living are worthless. He grants that lives contain real joy. His argument turns instead on a quiet structural point about how good and bad things weigh up when we compare existing with never existing at all. The whole case rests on what he calls the asymmetry — and the work of this chapter is to walk through it slowly enough that you can see both why it persuades and where you might resist.

The four boxes

Benatar asks us to compare two scenarios for a single possible person. In Scenario A, that person exists. In Scenario B, that person never exists — they are not born, and there is simply no one there. He then lays out four claims, which it helps to picture as a small grid.

For the person who exists, two things are true. (a) The presence of pain is bad. Their headaches, heartbreaks, illnesses, and griefs are genuinely bad things. And (b) the presence of pleasure is good. Their laughter, love, meals, and music are genuinely good things. So far this is just common sense; no one disputes it.

For the person who never exists, Benatar claims two further things, and here is where the argument earns its name. (c) The absence of pain is good — and crucially, it is good even though there is no one who enjoys that absence. Where there is no person, there is no suffering, and the avoidance of that suffering counts in the plus column. But (d) the absence of pleasure is not bad — unless there is some existing person for whom that absence is a deprivation. Where there is no person, the missing pleasures are missed by no one, and so they count as neither bad nor a loss.

The Asymmetry — Benatar's claim that the absence of pain and the absence of pleasure are not evaluated in the same way. Absent pain is good even with no subject to benefit; absent pleasure is merely "not bad" unless a real subject is deprived. This unevenness is the engine of the entire argument.

How the boxes combine

Now compare the columns. The existing person's life offers good things, yes — but it also imposes bad things, real pain that is genuinely against their interests. The never-existing alternative offers a good (no pain) and pays no price for it (the absent pleasure is no loss to anyone). When you set Scenario A beside Scenario B, existence brings a guaranteed harm that nonexistence entirely avoids, while existence's pleasures are not, on this accounting, any advantage over never having been — because the pleasures that go missing in nonexistence are missed by no one and so cost nothing.

The result is that nonexistence is never worse than existence, and is always at least somewhat better, because it spares a real harm at no real cost. Existence, by contrast, always carries a harm that could simply have not been imposed. Every coming-into-existence is therefore a net harm.

In plain words: If you are born, you get some good and some bad. If you are never born, you miss the good — but no one is there to miss it, so that is no loss. Meanwhile you escape all the bad, which is a real gain. So never being born comes out ahead, every time.

Why believe claim (c)? Three intuitions

The move from (c) to (d) is the controversial heart of the asymmetry, and Benatar does not just assert it. He argues that it best explains several judgments most of us already make.

The first is about duties. We generally agree that there is a strong duty not to bring into existence a child whose life would be full of suffering. But we feel no corresponding duty to bring into existence a child merely because that child would be happy. We do not wrong a possible happy person by declining to create them. This lopsidedness — a duty to avoid suffering people, no duty to produce happy ones — is exactly what (c) and (d) predict.

The second is about regret. A person can sensibly look at a child born into terrible suffering and judge that it would have been better had that child never been conceived — we can regret a birth for the sake of the one born. But we do not mourn, on their own behalf, the "missing" happy lives of the children we chose not to have. The empty crib is not a victim. We do not lie awake grieving for the joys that some never-conceived person is failing to enjoy.

The third is about distance. We feel real moral concern for people suffering far away, in places we will never visit. But we feel nothing of the kind for uninhabited regions — a barren planet, an empty island — on the grounds that no happy people exist there to enjoy them. We do not pity Mars for its lack of contented Martians. Absent suffering elsewhere matters to us; absent pleasure in empty places does not.

Each intuition, Benatar argues, fits the asymmetry and is awkward to explain without it. Together they are meant to make (c) and (d) feel less like a trick and more like something we already believe.

The empty crib is not a victim. No one is harmed by the joys they never get to have because they never were.

The "good for no one" objection

The most natural pushback strikes directly at claim (c).

Objection

How can the absence of pain be good if there is nobody for whom it is good? Goodness and badness seem to need a subject — a someone who is better or worse off. In nonexistence there is no such someone. So when you say "no pain is good here," that good is good for no one, which looks like no good at all. And if (c) collapses, the asymmetry is just the ordinary, symmetrical truth that for a nonexistent person there is neither pain nor pleasure to weigh — and the whole argument dissolves.

Reply

Benatar answers that the goodness here is not the warm, felt good of someone enjoying relief; it is a comparative or impersonal good. We are comparing a possible person's nonexistence with the harms that that very person would have suffered had they existed. Relative to those harms, their absence is better — and we can call it good in that relational sense without needing a present subject to bask in it. We routinely judge that a possible state of affairs is better than another; saying "it is better that this suffering not occur" need not name a beneficiary standing by to thank us. This response is contested, and the no-subject objection is serious enough that Chapter 26 returns to it in depth. For now, notice only that the asymmetry survives if comparative betterness can be good without a subject — a claim many find at least defensible.

A second worry: must every life be net-negative?

Objection

Grant the asymmetry for the sake of argument. It still seems wild to conclude that every life — including a long, loving, fulfilled, fortunate one — is a net harm to its owner. Surely the asymmetry shows at most that we should not create miserable lives, not that a wonderful life is worse than no life. The lucky person, asked, would surely say their existence was a gift.

Reply

This is the right place to feel the bite of the argument. Benatar's point is that the comparison is not "good life versus bad life" but "any life versus no life," and on those terms the pleasures of even a wonderful life buy nothing, because their absence in nonexistence was never a loss. So however high the column of goods rises, it cannot offset the column of harms, since the alternative carried no deficit to make up. The fortunate person's gratitude, meanwhile, is real but does not settle the matter: they are glad now, as an existing being with interests in continuing — but the question was whether starting them was a benefit to them, and a person who never existed has no thwarted preference to be grateful about. One may still reject the asymmetry; what one cannot do is accept it and then quarantine its conclusion to unhappy lives only.

Whether the asymmetry is true is among the most argued questions in this whole field. You are not obliged to be convinced. But you should be able to see that it is a careful argument, not a mood — and that its force, if it has any, reaches further than we would like.

Chapter in brief

  • Benatar's asymmetry rests on four claims: present pain is bad, present pleasure is good, absent pain is good even with no one to enjoy it, but absent pleasure is not bad unless someone is deprived.
  • Combined, these make nonexistence never worse and always at least somewhat better than existence — so coming into existence is always a net harm.
  • He defends the key step with three intuitions: a duty not to create suffering but none to create happiness; sensible regret over a suffering birth but no grief for "missing" happy lives; concern for distant suffering but none for empty, joyless places.
  • The chief objection — that absent pain cannot be good for no one — meets a comparative/impersonal reply, explored further in Chapter 26.
  • A second objection asks whether even wonderful lives are net-negative; the asymmetry, if accepted, says yes, because the goods of existence are no advantage over never having been.
