Prove You Shouldn't: Reversing the Burden of Proof

Part VII · Philosophical & Ethical Fallacies · Reversing the Burden of Proof

The bias: Demanding the childfree justify themselves while natalism goes unquestioned.

He who asserts must prove.

① In brief

Book 086 · 100 Biases of Natalism

This volume examines a quiet asymmetry in how we argue about having children. Ask a childfree person "Why did you make that choice?" and they are expected to have reasons ready: an account, a defense. Ask a parent the same question and they are expected to produce nothing at all; their choice is simply what people do. One party stands in the dock; the other never enters the courtroom. The book calls this the reversal of the burden of proof: the tacit rule that the person who declines to have children must justify that decision while the person who has children need justify nothing.

This is a claim about the dialectic of the debate, not about the merits. The book does not argue that having children is wrong. It argues something narrower and prior: that in ordinary and public discourse about reproduction, the burden of proof has been allocated to the wrong party. Drawing on argumentation theory, it distinguishes three terms: the burden of proof (the obligation to support one's position, on pain of losing by default), the presumption (the correlative advantage of provisional acceptance), and allocation (the logically prior decision of who bears which). Because in most disputes neither side proves its case to certainty, allocation frequently decides the outcome. Where we set the burden shapes every argument that follows, invisibly, before a single reason is offered.

Read the rest of the overview

Chapter 1 names the bias and distinguishes it from three neighbors: Book 085 (the moral-baseline bias, about where the normative zero-point sits), Book 027 (the social-interrogation norm, about the etiquette of who gets asked what), and Book 079 (the treatment of the childfree choice as inherently needing explanation). Chapter 2 steelmans the case for granting reproduction a presumption, the arguments from near-universality, from the continuation of value, from the absurdity of justifying everything, and from the protection of intimacy, conceding these have genuine force.

The remaining chapters trace the reversed allocation to its origins in evolution, scarcity, doctrine, and state interest (Chapter 3), showing these are causes, not reasons. Chapter 4 maps the mechanisms by which it operates today: the asymmetry of the question, the default reading of silence, the vocabulary of deviation, the structure of persuasion, and the pre-emption of the counterfactual. Chapter 5 surveys what argumentation theory, chiefly Douglas Walton's work, establishes: that allocation must be reasoned, and that the two central principles, he who asserts must prove and the stakes principle, both point toward placing the burden on reproduction, the positive, irreversible act performed on behalf of a non-consenting party. It cites David Benatar's asymmetry argument for the structural point without endorsing its strong conclusion.

Chapters 6 through 8 count the hidden costs, ask who bears the pressure most heavily (women, the embedded, the poor, the disabled), and answer the strongest objections, narrowing the thesis to its defensible core. Chapters 9 through 11 illustrate the bias in concrete cases, map adjacent biases, and describe what a fairly allocated burden would look like.

The corrected allocation is not a verdict that reproduction is wrong, not a claim that every grave act bears a burden, and not a license to interrogate parents. It is only this: in the public argument, the party proposing to create a new, unconsenting subject is the party that owes reasons, and the childfree default of perpetual explanation has no warrant the theory of proof can supply. Correcting the allocation removes a thumb from the scale, so that whatever each person decides, they decide it having actually been asked to think, and are left free to answer to no one but the gravity of the choice itself.

② The book in twelve chapters

Each chapter opens with its own summary. Note chapter 2: every volume states the strongest case for the thing it criticises before laying a finger on it — and chapter 8 turns the critique back on itself.

CH 1Naming the bias

The bias this book examines is a move in the logic of debate, not a claim about the world. It is the reversal of the burden of proof in disputes about reproduction: the tacit rule that the person who declines to have children must justify that decision, while the person who has children need justify nothing.

CH 2The strongest case for it (steelman)The strongest case against this book

Before I criticize the reversal, I must state the case for it as forcefully as its ablest defender would. The case is not silly. There are real reasons, several of them, why reproduction might legitimately enjoy a presumption, such that it is the childfree choice, and not the choice to have children, that properly bears the burden of explanation. I will give four, and I will not straw-man any of them.

CH 3Where it comes from

A misallocated burden of proof does not arise from an argument; if it did, it could be answered by a better argument. It arises from the deeper strata of custom, survival, doctrine, and law that lay down what "goes without saying." To understand why the childfree, rather than the parent, came to bear the onus, we have to look at where presumptions in general come from, because presumptions, unlike proofs, are inherited rather than earned.

CH 4How it operates today

The reversed burden is not, for most people, a proposition they would assent to if asked. It lives instead in the grammar of ordinary life, in defaults, scripts, and turns of phrase that quietly assign the onus without anyone deciding to. This chapter traces the concrete mechanisms.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

The subject of this book is not an empirical regularity but a normative allocation, and so "the evidence" here is of a particular kind. It is not demographic data about who has children; that would answer a different question. It is the body of principled work, chiefly in argumentation theory and moral philosophy, that bears on where a burden of proof ought to lie. This chapter sets out what that literature does and does not establish, and it states honestly the limits of what any such literature can settle.

CH 6The hidden costs

If a burden of proof is misallocated, the cost is more than logical untidiness. Where the onus lands determines what gets scrutinized and what slips through unexamined, and things that slip through unexamined tend to be things that, examined, would have looked different. This chapter names the costs that the reversed burden hides, costs that go unpriced precisely because the party who would have to answer for them is exempted from answering.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

A misallocated burden of proof is not distributed evenly across those it touches. The demand to justify oneself falls hardest on those already positioned by other structures to owe accounts, and the exemption from justification is enjoyed most fully by those already positioned to be believed without argument. This chapter maps who actually bears the reversed burden.

CH 8The counter-arguments to the critiqueObjections to the critique

I have argued that the burden of proof belongs on reproduction rather than on its avoidance. I now turn against my own thesis and state the strongest objections to it, engaging each honestly. If the critique cannot survive its best objections, it does not deserve to stand.

CH 9Case studies / illustrations

Abstractions about allocation become clearer in concrete instances. Here are four illustrations of the reversed burden operating, each chosen to show a different face of the same asymmetry, and each read carefully so as not to overclaim.

CH 10Adjacent biases

The reversed burden of proof does not stand alone. It interlocks with several other biases catalogued in this series, drawing strength from them and lending strength in return. Mapping the connections keeps this book in its lane while showing how the lanes run together.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

What would it look like to reason about reproduction with the burden fairly allocated, neither exempting reproduction from scrutiny nor persecuting those who reproduce? This chapter is constructive. Having named the bias, I want to describe the clear-eyed, non-coerced reasoning that becomes available once it is named, for individuals and for institutions.

CH 12Conclusion

This book has argued for a single, deliberately narrow claim. In our ordinary and our public arguments about reproduction, the burden of proof has been placed on the wrong party, resting on the person who declines to create a new life when, by the structure of the act, it belongs on the person who proposes to create one. I have tried to establish this without the stronger and separate claim that reproduction is wrong, and to be clear at every step about the difference between the two.

③ Key terms

Show all 17 terms as a list
Burden of proof (onus probandi)
The obligation, in a dispute or deliberation, to provide adequate support for one's position; the party bearing it loses by default if it is not discharged.
Presumption
The correlative advantage to a burden: a position accepted provisionally, without argument, until sufficient reason is given against it.
Allocation
The logically prior decision of which party bears the burden and which enjoys the presumption, before any evidence on the merits is weighed.
Reversal of the burden
The specific bias of this book: placing the onus on the choice to remain childfree rather than on the choice to create a new person.
"He who asserts must prove"
The maxim that the party advancing a positive claim, or proposing to alter the state of affairs, ordinarily bears the burden, while the party who merely declines does not.
Stakes principle
The principle that the graver, more irreversible, and less remediable an action, the heavier the justificatory burden it should carry.
Defeasible principle
A principle that holds by default but can be overridden by countervailing considerations in particular cases.
Presume the standard
The (defeasible) principle that near-universal practices earn a presumption, so that departures from them bear the burden.
Non-consensual imposition
The feature of reproduction whereby a whole life, with its risks, is imposed on a party who does not yet exist and cannot be consulted.
Counterfactual invisibility
The fact that the person who would have been created, but is not, is no one, and so cannot supply a vivid subject or stakes for the case against reproduction.
Pre-emption of the counterfactual
The mechanism by which the invisibility of the un-created person makes reproduction seem to have a subject and stakes while its avoidance seems to have none, sliding the burden toward the childfree.
Vocabulary of deviation
Language ("childless," "barren") that frames non-reproduction as a lack, encoding the burden in grammar; no parallel word marks parenthood as a departure.
Presumption tracking power
The observation that where presumptions actually fall follows social force and normativity rather than the logical structure of the claims, which is why the burden falls unequally by gender, class, community, and ability.
Dialectical vs. substantive
The distinction between a claim about who must argue (dialectical) and a claim about whether reproduction is justified (substantive); this book advances only the former.
Symmetry of scrutiny
The corrected condition in which the public argument scrutinizes reproduction and its avoidance alike, and private life relieves both of unwarranted interrogation.
Default reading of silence
The mechanism whereby a parent's failure to explain their choice is read as "no explanation needed," while the childfree person's identical silence is read as evasion — the party with the presumption may be silent, the party with the burden is convicted by silence.
Overdetermination (of the reversed burden)
The condition in which multiple independent causes — evolution, scarcity, doctrine, state interest — each on their own place the onus on the non-reproducer, so the bias is sustained even as any one cause fades.

⑥ Sources

What this volume draws on. Where the evidence is thin or contested, the book says so rather than reaching for a number.

  • David Benatar, Better Never to Have Been: The Harm of Coming into Existence (Oxford University Press, 2006). The canonical statement of the asymmetry argument and of the claim that the burden lies with those who would create a new sufferer. The book cites the structural point — that the party proposing to bring a new bearer of risks into existence owes the justification — without endorsing Benatar's strong conclusion that reproduction is always wrong.
  • Douglas Walton, Burden of Proof, Presumption and Argumentation (Cambridge University Press, 2014). A systematic treatment of allocation, presumption, and the dependence of burden on dialogue type; the principal source for the book's claim that allocation is a reasoned choice rather than a natural constant.
  • Douglas Walton, Informal Logic: A Pragmatic Approach, 2nd ed. (Cambridge University Press, 2008). Accessible background on burden of proof within argumentation theory.
Directions the volume points to (not pinned citations)
  • Christine Overall, Why Have Children? The Ethical Debate (Cambridge, MA: MIT Press, 2012) — argues that the decision to procreate stands in need of justification in a way the decision not to does not. The nearest thing in the literature to this book's thesis, argued substantively rather than dialectically.
  • David Benatar and David Wasserman, Debating Procreation: Is It Wrong to Reproduce? (Oxford University Press, 2015) — a structured exchange between an antinatalist and his most careful critic, in which the question of who owes the argument surfaces repeatedly without ever quite being settled.
  • Seana Valentine Shiffrin, "Wrongful Life, Procreative Responsibility, and the Significance of Harm," Legal Theory 5 (1999) — on non-consensual imposition and the special justificatory demands of creating a person.
  • Rivka Weinberg, The Risk of a Lifetime: How, When, and Why Procreation May Be Permissible (Oxford University Press, 2016) — on the risks imposed on the created person and what would be required to justify imposing them.
  • Elizabeth Brake and Joseph Millum, "Parenthood and Procreation," Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — a reliable overview of the philosophical terrain around procreation and parental obligation.
