BIAS 026

Proof of Adulthood: Children as the Milestone That Counts

Part III · Cultural & Social Scripts · Children as the Milestone That Counts

The bias: Parenthood treated as the credential that certifies you as a grown-up.

Nobody is ever quite grown up." — a common folk saying; the sentiment recurs across cultures without a single confirmed author. ## Preface to this volume There is a particular sentence that people say to new parents, and it repays close listening because it carries a whole worldview in a few words: "You're really an adult now." Sometimes it is said with warmth, sometimes with a wry sympathy, sometimes as congratulation. But whatever the tone, the content is constant. It asserts that something has been conferred: that the person who was, until recently, perhaps thirty-five years old, employed, married, a homeowner, a taxpayer, a caregiver to aging parents, was somehow not yet fully an adult, and has now, by having a child, become one. This book is about that assertion and the bias behind it. Listen to the sentence a second time and notice what it does not say. It does not say "your life has changed," which would be uncontroversial. It does not say "you have taken on a hard new responsibility," which would be true. It says that a category has changed, that the person has crossed from one kind of being into another, and that the crossing was accomplished by the birth itself rather than by anything the person has yet done. That is a remarkable thing to assert about a stranger at a baby shower, and it is asserted constantly, cheerfully, and without evidence. The whole apparatus of this book is an attempt to slow that sentence down enough to see what it is claiming. The bias is milestone framing: the treatment of parenthood as the credential that certifies a person as a genuinely grown, serious adult, the achievement that "counts" above career, autonomy, independence, or self-development, none of which, on this view, quite finishes the job. It is not the claim that parenthood changes people; often it does. It is the stronger and stranger claim that parenthood is what makes you an adult, as though maturity were a status awarded upon reproduction rather than a capacity a person may or may not develop, at any point, with or without children. Let me be exact about the stance, because a series like this one lives or dies by its precision. The claim here is not that parents are not mature, that non-parents are more mature, or that raising a child teaches nothing. Raising a child plainly teaches a great deal, and many parents describe real growth. The claim is narrower. Treating parenthood as the proof, the credential, the milestone that alone certifies adulthood, does two quiet harms at once. It casts the childless, including those who have made every other transition into adult life, as perpetually unfinished, arrested, not-quite-grown. And it flatters reproduction as an automatic engine of character, as if the biological fact of having a child did the maturing on its own. Both moves substitute a status for a capacity. That substitution is the bias. Chapter 2 will make the natalist case as strongly as I can make it, and it will make it first, because a critique that has not honestly felt the pull of what it criticizes is worthless. There is a serious argument that parenthood matures people, that the sheer non-negotiable demand of a dependent forces a growth that comfort never would. The natalist reader should find that argument stated there without caricature, and should find that this book concedes a great deal of it. What it does not concede is the leap from "parenthood often occasions growth" to "parenthood is the milestone that certifies adulthood, and its absence marks a person as unfinished." That leap is the whole quarrel, and I want the reader to feel that it is a leap and not a small step, a move from an observation about many individuals to a standard applied to everyone. This book must also stay in its lane, because three neighbors sit very close. Book 025, the life script, concerns the ordered sequence of milestones and childbearing as its scheduled final act; that book is about sequence and timing. Book 040, the completeness narrative, concerns the story that a child is what makes a person, couple, or life whole. And Book 050, moral signalling, concerns parenthood as evidence of virtue: unselfishness, seriousness, moral weight. This book is about none of those exactly. It is about parenthood as a maturity credential: not the ending of a sequence, not emotional completeness, not moral goodness, but the specific badge that says you are now a grown-up. Where these overlap, I will mark the seam and move on. The book proceeds along the series template. After naming the bias and steelmanning it, we trace its origins in rites of passage and the history of adulthood markers; we examine how it operates today through language, defaults, and the "adulting" discourse; we weigh the evidence, including the striking research finding that young people themselves rank self-reliance and independent decision-making above role transitions like parenthood when asked what makes an adult; we count the hidden costs, ask who bears the pressure, and give the strongest objections to our own case their due. The aim is modest and, I hope, freeing: to separate the capacity of maturity from the status of parenthood, so that neither parents nor the childless are measured by a credential that was never a credential at all. ## 1. Naming the bias Milestone framing is the tendency to treat parenthood as the milestone that certifies a person as a fully grown, serious adult, the achievement that confers adult status, and whose absence leaves a person, however accomplished otherwise, as not-quite-finished. The one-sentence thesis of this book: Treating children as the proof of adulthood mistakes a status for a capacity. It flatters reproduction as automatic character growth and casts the childless as perpetually immature, when maturity is something a person develops or fails to develop independently of whether they reproduce. Three features distinguish this bias and mark its borders. The first concerns maturity as a credential, not maturity as a change. Everyone agrees that becoming a parent is a large life change that can occasion growth. The bias is the further claim that the change constitutes adulthood: that before it you were, in some load-bearing sense, still a child, and that after it you have graduated. It is the credentialing move, not the observation of change, that we are naming. The distinction can be felt in a single substitution. "Having a child changed her" is a claim almost no one would dispute and this book does not dispute. "Having a child made her an adult" is a different claim entirely, and it is the second, not the first, that the bias smuggles in under cover of the first. Second, it treats parenthood as the milestone that counts above the rest. Modern life offers many candidate markers of adulthood: finishing education, earning a living, leaving home, forming a household, taking legal and civic responsibility, caring for others. Milestone framing does more than add parenthood to that list. It elevates parenthood over the list, so that a person who has done everything else is still, on this framing, missing the decisive one. "But when are you going to grow up and have kids?" is a question that treats the other markers as provisional. Notice the word "but": it concedes the career, the mortgage, the caregiving, and then discounts all of it, as though these were preliminaries and the real examination were still to come. Third, it runs in both directions, and this is what makes it a bias rather than a mere opinion. Toward the childless, it withholds full adult standing: they are "still young," "not settled," "not there yet," even at fifty. Toward the parent, it grants full adult standing automatically, by the fact of reproduction, whether or not any actual maturing has occurred. A twenty-year-old who has a child is often spoken of as having been "made to grow up," while a childless forty-year-old with two decades of independent, responsible life is spoken of as not yet grown. The credential is issued and withheld by reproductive status, not by any measured capacity. A test that both awards passes to people who have not demonstrated the trait and fails people who plainly have it is not measuring the trait at all; it is measuring something else and borrowing the trait's good name. Mark carefully what is not being claimed. Not that parents are immature; most are perfectly mature adults, as most non-parents are. Not that parenthood never matures anyone; it plainly matures many people. Not that maturity does not exist or does not matter; it exists, matters greatly, and is exactly the thing being defended here from a false proxy. The claim is only that maturity is a capacity, not a status conferred by reproduction, and that the milestone framing systematically confuses the two. I labor the negations because critiques of natalism are routinely misheard as attacks on parents, and this one is not. It is a defense of a concept, maturity, from a measuring instrument that does it a disservice. We should also mark the difference from mere growing-up-in-general. Every culture has some notion that people should mature. The bias is not that notion. It is the specific attachment of that notion to one biological event, so that the event becomes the gate through which adulthood is entered and without which it is, supposedly, never fully entered. To name the bias is to pry the concept of maturity loose from that single gate, and to ask whether a gate is the right image at all for something that, on reflection, looks far more like a slope one climbs gradually and unevenly, at one's own pace, sometimes without ever noticing the moment of arrival. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 1 Quiz. 1. ① In brief

100 Biases of Natalism · Part III — Cultural & Social Scripts

This book examines milestone framing: the treatment of parenthood as the credential that certifies a person as a fully grown, serious adult, the achievement that supposedly "counts" above career, autonomy, independence, or self-development, none of which, on this view, quite finishes the job. When someone tells a new parent "you're really an adult now," they assert that adulthood has been conferred by reproduction, that a thirty-five-year-old who was employed, partnered, and independent was somehow not yet fully grown, and now is.

The book's one-sentence thesis: treating children as the proof of adulthood mistakes a status for a capacity. It flatters reproduction as automatic character growth and casts the childless as perpetually immature, when maturity is something a person develops or fails to develop independently of whether they reproduce.

Read the rest of the overview

The stance is careful and non-coercive. It is not claimed that parents are immature, that non-parents are more mature, or that raising a child teaches nothing; raising a child plainly teaches a great deal. The claim is narrower: that treating parenthood as the credential of adulthood does two quiet harms. It casts the childless, including those who have made every other adult transition, as permanently unfinished. And it flatters reproduction as an automatic engine of character, crediting the biological event rather than the difficult work of parenting well.

Chapter 2 steelmans the natalist case first and concedes much of it: parenthood supplies a forced, high-stakes, self-decentering experience that genuinely matures many who undergo it, and may even be a reliable route to that growth. What the book contests is the leap from matures to certifies, from "a route to maturity" to "the milestone without which one is not fully grown."

The evidence chapter is the book's spine. Drawing on Jeffrey Arnett's research on emerging adulthood and the markers of adulthood, it reports the striking, repeatedly confirmed finding that people themselves, asked what makes an adult, rank individualistic criteria (accepting responsibility for oneself, making independent decisions, financial independence) above role transitions like parenthood. Adulthood, on people's own considered view, lives in capacities, not reproduction. The sociology of the transition to adulthood (Furstenberg and colleagues) shows the classic markers have de-standardized until no single one can be the gate. And the causal claim that parenthood manufactures maturity is, at the population level, far softer than ordinary speech assumes, confounded by the selection problem that people who become parents differ in advance from those who do not.

The book traces the bias to rites of passage (van Gennep's separation–liminality–incorporation), to an economic past in which household and parenthood arrived together, and to the modern elongation of the road to adulthood that has concentrated credentialing weight on the one marker delay has not eroded. It shows the bias operating today through the language of "settling down" and "growing up," the ironic "adulting" discourse, conferral rituals, asymmetric commentary, and institutional defaults that treat adult-as-parent. It counts the hidden costs: a standing verdict of immaturity on the childless, a badge handed to parents before the maturing work is done, the degradation of "maturity" as a concept, and the decisional distortion of having children to close a status gap. It maps who bears the pressure most (women, older childless adults, the out-of-sequence, the involuntarily childless), and it engages five serious counter-arguments, granting the "reliability" objection real weight while showing that a reliable route is still not a proof.

The takeaway is measured and freeing in both directions: to the childless, that adulthood was never contingent on a credential they were told they lacked; to parents, that the growth they may have earned is theirs, earned through the work rather than conferred at the birth. Maturity is a capacity, intact and available to everyone, parent or not, on the strength of what they can actually do.

CH 2The strongest case for it (steelman)The strongest case against this book

The honest work comes first. The intuition that parenthood makes you grow up is not foolish, and here is the strongest version of it.

CH 3Where it comes from

Milestone framing has deep roots, and seeing them helps explain both its power and its contingency. Power, because a framing this old feels less like an opinion than like a fact of nature. Contingency, because roots grow in particular soil, and the soil that fed this one has largely washed away.

CH 4How it operates today

Stripped of explicit rites, the bias operates today through quieter channels: language, defaults, humor, and the small transactions of everyday esteem. Because these channels are quiet, the framing propagates without ever being stated as a proposition anyone could examine and reject. It arrives as a turn of phrase, a raised eyebrow, a box on a form, and it does its work below the threshold at which we usually think to argue.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

What does the evidence actually show about the relationship between parenthood and maturity, and, just as important, about what people believe makes an adult? Here the findings are more interesting, and more inconvenient for the bias, than the folk framing would predict.

CH 6The hidden costs

If milestone framing merely described a common experience, it would be harmless. It becomes costly because, as a credential, it does hidden work: pricing some people as incomplete and flattering others into a growth they have not necessarily done. The costs are hidden in the ordinary sense that no one intends them and no one is billed for them openly; they are absorbed quietly by particular people and by the concept of maturity itself.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

The milestone-as-proof framing does not press equally on everyone. Its weight is distributed by gender, age, class, and circumstance, and naming that distribution is part of naming the bias. A pressure that fell evenly on all would be easier to see and easier to shrug off; what makes this one insidious is that it concentrates on particular people, in particular ways, so that each feels it as a private verdict rather than a shared structure.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

What does clear-eyed, non-coerced reasoning about this look like once the bias is named? The goal is not to invert the framing, not to declare the childless the real adults, but to dissolve the credential and return the concept of maturity to its proper use. Inversion would be the same error wearing the opposite coat, and the reader should be as suspicious of "the childless are the truly mature ones" as of the claim this book criticizes; both hang maturity on reproductive status, and both are wrong for the same reason.

CH 12Conclusion

This book has argued a narrow and, I hope, durable claim: that treating children as the proof of adulthood mistakes a status for a capacity. Maturity (responsibility, self-command, the ability to hold another's welfare as weighty as one's own) is a capacity that a person develops or fails to develop, at any age, with or without children. Milestone framing proxies that capacity by a single reproductive event, and in doing so it both flatters and slanders: it flatters reproduction as automatic character growth, crediting the event rather than the work, and it slanders the childless as perpetually unfinished, however fully they possess the capacity the credential claims to measure.

Milestone framing
the treatment of parenthood as the milestone that certifies a person as a fully grown, serious adult, and whose absence leaves a person, however accomplished otherwise, as not-quite-finished.
Maturity credential
the specific badge that milestone framing issues: adult standing conferred by the fact of reproduction rather than by any demonstrated capacity.
Capacity vs. status
the book's central distinction. Maturity is a capacity a person develops or fails to develop (self-command, responsibility, perspective); the framing treats adulthood as a status conferred at a reproductive threshold.
Adulthood markers
the transitions and criteria by which adulthood is judged. Research finds people rank capacity-based criteria (self-reliance, independent decisions, financial independence) above role transitions like parenthood.
Emerging adulthood
Jeffrey Arnett's term for a distinct, prolonged developmental stage between adolescence and settled adulthood (roughly the late teens through the twenties), in which "am I an adult yet?" becomes a chronic question.
Role transition
a change of social role (marriage, entering work, parenthood). In the research on adulthood, role transitions consistently score lower than individualistic, capacity-based criteria.
Rite of passage
Arnold van Gennep's account of the separation–liminality–incorporation structure by which societies confer a change of status. Milestone framing is its survival in a society that has abandoned explicit initiations.
Liminality
the ambiguous, in-between phase of a rite of passage; echoed in the "adulting"-era sense of being not-quite-an-adult despite doing adult things.
"Adulting"
the ironic use of "adult" as a verb ("I adulted today"). Reveals how performative and unstable adult status has become — the anxiety milestone framing exploits by offering the one act one cannot perform ironically.
"Settling down" / "growing up"
everyday phrases that function as near-synonyms for having children, transmitting the maturity credential below the level of argument, in ordinary speech.
Forced maturation (steelman)
the natalist claim that a non-negotiable dependent compels a growth that comfortable adult life does not. Conceded as often true; denied as a credentialing standard.
Self-decentering
the psychological shift from a self-focused frame to one that holds others' needs as weighty as one's own; a component of maturity the steelman argues parenthood develops especially reliably.
Selection problem
the methodological difficulty that people who become parents differ in advance from those who do not, making "parenthood matured them" hard to separate from a pre-existing trajectory.
Reliability argument
the strongest surviving objection to the book's thesis: that parenthood develops maturity more dependably than optional alternatives. Granted weight, but a reliable route is not thereby a proof.
False credential
the framing's double error: crediting every parent with maturity by the fact of reproduction, and denying it to every mature childless person; a proxy that both false-positives and false-negatives on the trait it claims to measure.
Transition to adulthood
the sociological literature (Furstenberg and colleagues) documenting the delay, disorder, and de-standardization of adulthood markers — undercutting any single milestone as the gate.
Conferral ritual
an informal incorporation rite (baby shower, birth announcement, changed forms of address) that publicly marks the new parent's changed status; no comparable ritual confers full adult standing on the childless.
Decisional distortion
the cost by which the wish to be seen as a grown-up becomes a reason to have children, a reason external to the child that a clear-eyed deliberation would identify and set aside.
