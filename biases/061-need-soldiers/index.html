BIAS 061

We Need Soldiers: Geopolitics and the Demand for Births

Part V · Economic, Demographic & Policy Pronatalism · Geopolitics and the Demand for Births

The bias: Military and great-power framing of population as strength.

The strength of a state is measured, in the first instance, by the number of its inhabitants." — a recurring maxim of cameralist and mercantilist statecraft, expressing a view widely held from the seventeenth to the twentieth century (a paraphrase of a commonplace, belonging to no single author). ## Preface to this volume There is a family of arguments for having children that does not appeal to your happiness, your lineage, or the economy. It appeals to the survival of your nation among rivals. It says: look outward. Beyond the border are other peoples, other armies, other flags, and they are growing. If we do not grow too, if we do not produce enough young bodies to fill uniforms, hold ground, and deter aggression, we will be overwhelmed. On this view a birth is not a private event alone; it is also a contribution to national strength. To have children is, in part, to do one's duty by the state's security. To decline is, faintly or explicitly, to weaken the nation before its enemies. This book is about that argument and the bias it sustains. I call it the soldier-supply bias: the tendency to treat reproduction as the manufacture of national manpower, and to convert a geopolitical anxiety about being outnumbered into pressure on individuals to bear children for the defense of the state. It is one of the oldest and most emotionally potent forms of pronatalism, because it wraps the demand for births in the language of survival, sacrifice, and patriotism, the language against which it feels almost shameful to argue. Few of us wish to be told that our reluctance to raise a large family has left the border thinly held. The argument works because it recruits our best feelings, loyalty, solidarity, the wish to protect what we love, and turns them into a lever on the most private decision we make. I want to be careful about what this book is and is not. It is not a claim that nations face no security threats, that demography never touches state capacity, or that a country's age structure and population size are irrelevant to what it can do in the world. Those would be false claims, and I will not make them. Demography is one input among many into national power, and a state emptying of young people does face real constraints. The bias I am naming is narrower and more specific. It is the slide from population is one factor in state capacity to therefore you should have children so the state has soldiers: the instrumentalization of a person's reproductive life in the service of the state's geopolitical ends, dressed up as an iron law of survival. The premise can be partly true and the conclusion still be an abuse. That gap between a real premise and an unwarranted demand is the whole subject of this book. The book's stance is critical but, I hope, fair. In Chapter 2 I put the strongest version of the argument first and in full, before I have said a word against it, because an argument this consequential deserves to be met at its best. Only then do I turn to what has changed. The heart of the critique is this: the picture of war that makes raw numbers decisive, massed conscript armies, front lines held by sheer weight of bodies, "cannon fodder" as a strategic resource, describes a form of great-power warfare that has largely passed. Nuclear deterrence, professional volunteer forces, precision weapons, drones, and the logic of industrial and technological depth have made head-count a poor proxy for military strength among advanced states. The argument that "we need soldiers, therefore have children" is running on a model of war that the world it invokes has mostly left behind. It is like insisting a nation stockpile cavalry horses because horses once decided battles: the fear is sincere and the history is real, but the instrument has changed. There is also a moral thread that runs through every chapter, and I will state it now so it is never out of view. Children are not materiel. A person is not a unit of manpower held in reserve for the state's use. To ask someone to reproduce so that the nation will have bodies to send to a future front is to treat both the parent and the unborn child as means to a collective end they never chose. Even where demographic concerns are genuine, this instrumental framing deserves the sharpest scrutiny, because it asks the most intimate decision a person can make to serve the most impersonal of purposes. And it does so, uniquely among the pronatalist arguments in this series, in the shadow of a purpose that may be lethal: the front, the trench, the casualty list. That shadow is why the soldier-supply argument, of all of them, most needs to be examined with a cold eye. The book proceeds through the series' standard twelve chapters: naming the bias, steelmanning it, tracing its origins, showing how it operates, weighing the evidence, counting the hidden costs, asking who bears the pressure, answering the objections to my own case, working through cases, mapping adjacent biases, and describing what clear-eyed reasoning looks like once the bias is named. Throughout I try to keep the sober voice the subject demands. The stakes, war, survival, the honoring of the dead, are real. That is exactly why the reasoning should be clean. A subject this grave is not served by cheap dismissal of the other side, nor by surrender to a fear dressed in patriotic colors. It is served by taking the strongest form of the argument seriously enough to see where and why it fails, and by holding, throughout, to the one thing that does not bend: that a human being is never merely material for the state's use. ## 1. Naming the bias The soldier-supply bias is the tendency to treat human reproduction as the production of national manpower, and to convert geopolitical anxiety, the fear of being outnumbered, out-mobilized, or overrun by rival states, into pressure on individuals to bear children for the strength and defense of the nation. Its one-sentence thesis, the thesis this book contests, is: a nation must reproduce in order to field armies, project power, and avoid being demographically overwhelmed by its rivals, and so individuals have a patriotic reason to have children. Consider the structure. The bias begins with a claim about states in competition, that population is an element of national power, that more people means more potential soldiers, workers-for-war, and demographic weight, and it ends with a claim about you: that your reproductive choices are a contribution to, or a withdrawal from, that national strength. The move from the first claim to the second is the whole engine of the bias. It is what turns geopolitics into a nursery. And it is a move, not an entailment: even if the first claim were entirely true, the second would not follow automatically, because a collective interest in some good does not by itself generate a personal duty to supply it with one's own body. Much of this book is an argument that the engine runs on a fuel, decisive military mass, that the modern world no longer supplies, and that even a full tank would not carry the load the bias asks it to. It helps to fix what the bias is by distinguishing it sharply from its neighbors in this series, because "population equals power" arguments come in several flavors that are easy to blur. Book 059 examines the future-workers frame: the argument that we need births to sustain the economy, pay pensions, staff the labor force, and keep the growth engine running. That is an economic claim about population, bodies as producers and taxpayers. The soldier-supply bias is its martial cousin. Here the body is valued not as a worker but as a potential combatant, a unit of military and geopolitical weight. The two overlap, since a large young population can be read as both an army and a workforce, but the frames pull on different fears and justify themselves differently. The economic frame fears insolvency; the military frame fears conquest. Insolvency is a slow, negotiable, reversible condition; conquest is figured as final, existential, and unforgiving. That difference in the imagined stakes is why the soldier-supply frame can generate a fiercer and more coercive pressure than its economic cousin: you can restructure a pension system, but you cannot restructure a nation that has ceased to exist. Book 060 examines the nation-as-family metaphor: the framing of the nation as a kin group whose "blood" must continue, whose "stock" must be replenished, whose survival is figured in the intimate language of family and heritage. That is a claim about identity and continuity. The soldier-supply bias is more coldly strategic. It is not primarily about the nation's soul or bloodline but about its capabilities: can it fill the ranks, hold the line, deter the neighbor. A committed internationalist with no romance about national blood can still fall for the soldier-supply argument if she believes, as a matter of hard-nosed realism, that her country needs bodies to survive in an anarchic world. The two often travel together, and when they fuse the result is potent and dangerous, but they are separable, and this book stays in the military-strategic lane. The distinction is worth holding because the strongest form of the soldier-supply argument does not depend on any sentiment about national identity at all; it can be made in the flat, unsentimental voice of the realist strategist, and it is in that voice that it is hardest to answer. Book 058, on the broader "strength in numbers" intuition, is the general case of which this book is a specific instance: the soldier-supply bias is the security-and-warfare application of the numbers-equal-strength idea. Where Book 058 examines the umbrella belief that a larger collective is a stronger and safer one, this book narrows to the specific arena of armies and defense, and asks whether the intuition survives contact with how modern great-power war actually works. The distinctive marks of the soldier-supply bias, then, are three. First, its unit of value is the potential combatant, the body that can be conscripted, deployed, and if necessary expended. Second, its motivating emotion is external threat: the rival across the border, the demographic race, the fear of being outbred by an adversary. Third, its demand is pointed inward at reproduction: it asks individuals to have children as a form of national service performed in advance, before the child is born, on behalf of a security future no one can see. This last mark is the strangest and the most revealing. Every other form of national service is asked of a person who exists and can consent or refuse. The soldier-supply bias asks a service of a person who does not yet exist, by asking someone else to bring them into being for that purpose. The child is enlisted before conception, drafted into a scheme decided by people they will never meet, for a war that may never come or may come in a form in which they are irrelevant. The thesis of this book is that this bias mistakes an outdated model of national power for a law of nature, and in doing so licenses one of the least defensible forms of pressure a society can place on its members: the demand that they manufacture human beings for the state to use. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 1 Quiz. 1. The strongest case for it (steelman) Before I say anything against this argument, I want to state it at full strength, in the voice of someone who believes it and believes it for serious reasons. If the argument can be answered, it must be answered in this form, not in a caricature. I ask the reader who already disagrees to resist, for the length of this chapter, the urge to rebut, and to feel the genuine weight of a case that has moved serious statesmen and shaped the policy of great nations. Begin with the world as it actually is. The international order is anarchic. There is no world government, no final authority that guarantees any nation's survival. States exist among other states, some of them larger, some hostile, all of them ultimately self-interested. History is a graveyard of peoples who could not defend themselves: nations conquered, absorbed, expelled, exterminated. Security is not a given; it is an achievement, and it can be lost. A nation that forgets this indulges a dangerous fantasy that the peaceful present will last forever. The steelman begins in realism, and realism begins with the possibility of the worst. To reason about national survival as though the long peace one happens to have been born into is permanent is not sophistication; it is a failure of imagination that history has punished again and again, and usually most severely on the peoples who assumed themselves safe. Now add demography. Among the hard elements of national power, territory, resources, industry, technology, and population, population is foundational, because every other element must be manned. An army needs soldiers. A wartime economy needs workers to build the tanks, ships, and shells. A country that loses a war of attrition is often the one that runs out of men first. This is not abstract. In the great industrial wars of the twentieth century, the ability to replace catastrophic losses and keep mobilizing fresh cohorts was decisive. Manpower was a strategic resource as real as steel or oil. A nation with a broad base of young people has strategic depth; a nation that is old and shrinking has a thin reserve and a brittle base. The proponent can point to real history here: wars in which the side that could keep finding fresh soldiers ground down the side that could not, regardless of tactical brilliance. Depth of population was, in those wars, depth of survival, the difference between a defeat one recovered from and a defeat that ended a nation. Consider what a persistently sub-replacement birth rate means over decades for a state that faces real rivals. Each generation is smaller than the last. The pool of military-age young adults contracts. Recruitment gets harder even for a volunteer force, because there are simply fewer people from whom to recruit, and those who remain are more precious to an economy that is itself short of workers. The population ages, so a larger share of national resources goes to pensions and elder care rather than to defense. Meanwhile a rival with a younger, growing population faces none of these constraints. Over a long enough horizon, the steelman argues, demography sets the outer limits of what a nation can defend. You cannot deter what you cannot man. And demography, unlike a defense budget, cannot be increased on short notice: a soldier needed in twenty years must be conceived now. The lead time is the point. By the time a threat is visible, the cohort that would have met it has already not been born. A wise state, on this view, does not wait; it provides for its manpower a generation in advance, as it provides for any other slow-maturing strategic asset. And there is the raw comparison across borders. If your nation shrinks while a neighboring or rival power grows, the balance shifts, slowly but inexorably, regardless of anyone's intentions. Relative population is relative potential. A country that is a tenth the size of a hostile giant next door lives on a different kind of sufferance than one that can match it. The pronatalist realist does not need to hate the rival or romanticize his own nation's blood. He needs only to observe that in a world where force remains the final arbiter, the side that can field, replace, and sustain more capable forces has an advantage, and that population is upstream of all of it. He can grant every point about technology and still say: technology too must be built and operated by people, and a nation with more people has, all else equal, more hands to build and operate it, more minds to invent it, and more shoulders to bear the burden of a long war. From this the steelman draws its conclusion about reproduction. If national survival depends in part on demographic strength, and if demographic strength is the aggregate of individual reproductive choices, then those choices are not purely private. A generation that declines to reproduce is, whether it means to or not, choosing to hand its children and grandchildren a weaker, more vulnerable, more easily coerced nation. There is, on this view, a genuine collective-action problem: each person, reasoning only about their own life, may rationally choose fewer children, but if everyone does so the nation as a whole is left defenseless. The state, as guardian of collective security, has a legitimate interest in the aggregate birth rate, and, the steelman says, so should every citizen who cares whether their people will be able to defend themselves a half-century from now. The freedom to have few or no children, on this account, is a freedom purchased on credit against the security of one's descendants, who will pay the bill. Put at its strongest, the argument is this: security is the precondition of everything else we value; security ultimately rests on the capacity to defend, which rests in part on population; population is the sum of reproductive choices; therefore reproduction is, in part, a security matter, and a patriot has reason to weigh it as such. This is not a foolish argument. It has moved serious statesmen and shaped real policy. It connects a hard truth about the world, that safety is not guaranteed and must be provided for, to a plausible chain of consequences ending in the nursery. The rest of the book is my attempt to show where, and how badly, it fails. But it earns that attempt by being genuinely formidable first, and I want the reader to hold its full weight in mind as the critique proceeds, so that what follows is a reckoning with the argument's best self and not a triumph over a straw one. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 2 Quiz. 1. Where it comes from The soldier-supply bias is ancient, because the link between population and military power is one of the oldest observations in statecraft. But its modern, anxious, policy-driven form is largely a product of the last three centuries, and understanding its origins helps explain why it retains such grip even as the world that gave rise to it recedes. A bias with roots this deep does not vanish simply because the conditions that nourished it have changed; it persists as a habit of mind, a reflex reached for in moments of insecurity long after the reality it once tracked has moved on. In the classical and early-modern imagination, a populous state was simply a strong state, and the equation needed no argument. Rome's manpower, its ability to raise legion after legion and absorb defeats that would have destroyed a smaller power, was understood by ancient and later writers as central to its rise. A recurring theme of classical political thought was that a city or state must maintain a sufficient population of citizens capable of bearing arms, and that a decline in that population was a decline in security. The mercantilist and cameralist thinkers of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries made the equation explicit and turned it into a maxim of government: a large population meant more soldiers, more taxpayers, more producers, and therefore a mightier crown. Rulers were counseled to encourage marriage and births and to discourage anything that thinned the population, because subjects were the raw material of power. Population policy and military policy were barely distinguishable, and the prince who wished to be strong was told, in effect, to be populous. The Napoleonic era sharpened the point. The levée en masse decreed by the National Convention on 23 August 1793, which placed all Frenchmen "in permanent requisition for the services of the armies" and put some three-quarters of a million men under arms within a year, demonstrated that a state which could mobilize its whole young male population could overwhelm rivals fielding smaller professional forces. Numbers, harnessed by the modern state and by a new ideology of the nation-in-arms, became a weapon in a new way. Before this, armies had often been limited professional bodies; now the entire manhood of a nation could be called to the colors. From that moment, the size of a nation's population of military-age men was a live strategic variable, watched and counted by every general staff in Europe. The census and the conscription roll became instruments of war planning. A generation not born was, in the cold arithmetic of the staffs, a set of divisions that could not be fielded. The most vivid and consequential origin, though, is France after the First World War, and it deserves care. Across the nineteenth century, French elites grew increasingly anxious about dénatalité, the country's comparatively low birth rate, in demographic-military terms. France watched its population grow slowly while a unifying and industrializing Germany grew faster, and the fear was explicitly about the balance of military manpower between the two rivals. Defeat in the Franco-Prussian War sharpened this anxiety into a national preoccupation: the sense that France was being demographically outpaced by a more numerous neighbor with whom another war was thought inevitable. The catastrophic French losses of the First World War, an entire cohort of young men killed or maimed, turned this long anxiety into national trauma and policy. Interwar France adopted pronatalist measures with remarkable speed. The Médaille de la Famille française, created by decree on 26 May 1920, honored mothers of large families in bronze, silver, and gold according to how many children they had raised. The law of 31 July 1920 banned the sale of contraceptives and all propaganda for contraception and abortion, reinforcing the criminal prohibition of abortion itself. Family allowances were expanded through the decade and made compulsory for employers in 1932, and the Code de la famille of 1939 consolidated the whole apparatus on the eve of the next war. These measures were framed substantially around the need to replace the war dead and to have enough men for the war with Germany that was expected to come. Here the soldier-supply bias appears in almost pure form: births demanded, by the state and by public sentiment, to fill future ranks. The grief of a bereaved nation was channeled into pressure on the wombs of the next generation, and mourning became policy. The two World Wars entrenched the bias in the twentieth-century mind because they seemed to confirm it. These were industrial wars of attrition in which manpower, the capacity to mobilize, arm, and replace enormous numbers of men, was genuinely, visibly decisive. The word cannon fodder enters common speech in this era as a bitter description of what mass war did with young men: fed them into the machine by the hundred thousand. That the phrase is bitter is the point; even as the wars seemed to prove that numbers mattered, they exposed the horror of a logic that treats human beings as expendable material. Totalitarian regimes of the period made the connection explicit and grotesque, with pronatalist campaigns aimed frankly at producing soldiers and settlers for the state's ambitions, sometimes coupled with the murderous suppression of the fertility of groups deemed undesirable. The soldier-supply logic in these hands showed its capacity to become something monstrous: not merely a demand for births but a demand for the right kind of births, enforced by the state, and its dark twin, a demand that the wrong kind not be born at all. Underneath these historical layers sits an older realist stratum of thought that treats population as a standing "element of national power," to be counted alongside geography, resources, and industrial capacity in the ledger of what makes a state strong. Twentieth-century realist writers on international politics listed population among the components of power, generally with the caveat that raw numbers matter only as they can be organized, equipped, and led (the idea is well established in the realist tradition, though it belongs to the tradition rather than to any one formulation of it). This is the intellectually respectable root of the bias, and, as we will see, even its more careful proponents rarely intended the crude inference that individuals should reproduce to supply the state with bodies. The realist who lists population as an element of power is making an analytical observation about states; the soldier-supply bias smuggles from that observation a prescription for citizens' bedrooms. The distance between the analysis and the prescription is exactly the distance this book insists upon, and much of the bias's persuasive force comes from collapsing it, from treating a sober fact about states as though it were a marching order to individuals. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 3 Quiz. 1. How it operates today If the industrial mass war that gave the soldier-supply bias its plausibility has faded, the bias itself has not. It operates today less through explicit conscription policy than through rhetoric, framing, and mood, a background hum of demographic anxiety that surfaces whenever national security is discussed. It rarely announces itself as an argument that individuals should reproduce for the state; that would sound archaic and coercive. Instead it works by making a certain description of reproduction feel natural and serious, so the demand seems to follow from the nation's security rather than from anyone's desire to direct other people's bodies. Here are the concrete mechanisms. The first is the demographic-security headline. Commentators, officials, and strategists periodically raise the alarm that a country's falling birth rate is a national-security problem: that an aging, shrinking population will not be able to man its armed forces, sustain its defense industrial base, or hold its own against a larger rival. These claims travel in op-eds, think-tank reports, and political speeches. The mechanism is framing: it takes a private demographic trend and reclassifies it as a strategic threat, which licenses public concern about, and pressure on, reproductive behavior. Once "we're not having enough children" is filed under national security, it acquires an urgency and a moral seriousness that a merely private matter would not have. Security is the category before which other considerations are expected to yield; to place reproduction inside it is to demand that individual liberty and choice give way to a collective imperative that brooks no argument. The second is the recruitment-crisis story. When a military struggles to meet recruiting targets, one available explanation is demographic: there are fewer young people, or fewer eligible ones, than there used to be. This is sometimes partly true and worth examining honestly. But it slides easily into the soldier-supply frame, the problem restated as "not enough bodies coming through," with the birth rate implicated as the deep cause. The subtle effect is to make the size of future generations feel like a defense variable that society, and by extension its prospective parents, is responsible for. In fact recruitment shortfalls usually have far more proximate causes, pay, conditions, the appeal of civilian alternatives, eligibility standards, the reputation of the service, that have nothing to do with how many children were born two decades ago. But the demographic explanation flatters a certain anxiety and points the finger at fertility, which is exactly the move the bias needs to convert a solvable institutional problem into a diffuse call for more births. The third is the rival-comparison. Nothing activates the soldier-supply bias like a chart comparing your nation's population trajectory to a rival's. The framing is almost always relative and almost always ominous: they are growing, we are shrinking, therefore the balance is tipping against us. This taps directly into the fear of being outnumbered that sits at the emotional core of the bias. It rarely pauses to ask whether raw population still translates into usable military power in the way the chart implies, or whether the "rival" is in fact a military threat at all, or whether the comparison is between comparable things. The chart does its persuasive work through sheer visual contrast, two lines diverging, and the viewer supplies the conclusion the bias wants: that diverging population lines mean diverging security. The graph is an argument disguised as a fact. The fourth is the instrumental honoring of parenthood. In this register, having children, especially several children, is praised as a contribution to the nation's future strength and resilience. The parent of a large family is figured, faintly, as a patriot doing their part; the childless are figured, faintly, as free-riders on a collective good they decline to help produce. This is rarely stated so baldly, but the structure is there in much pronatalist rhetoric: your reproduction is a service, your non-reproduction a withholding. When political leaders praise large families in the language of national vitality and future strength, they are activating exactly this frame, whether or not they intend the full soldier-supply inference. The praise is real, and so is the reproach that shadows it. The fifth mechanism is the militarized metaphor of the child. Language matters, and the bias lives in it. Children described as "the future of the nation," a generation described as needing to be "replenished," a birth rate framed as "reserves" running low, a demographic decline described as the nation "disarming itself": these figures of speech quietly encode the soldier-supply logic, treating the young as a strategic stock to be maintained at adequate levels. The metaphor does its work below the level of argument, so that one absorbs the frame without ever having been offered a claim one could examine and reject. To notice the metaphor is already to begin resisting the bias, because once you see the child being described as materiel, the description loses its innocence. A society reveals what it truly thinks of its children in the words it reaches for when it is frightened. What these mechanisms share is that none of them requires a draft, a law, or even an explicit demand. They work by framing, by making a certain description of reproduction (as national manpower) feel natural, serious, and patriotic, so that the demand for births seems to follow from the security of the nation rather than from any particular person's interest in controlling others' bodies. The bias operates today mostly as a mood and a metaphor, and it is all the more effective for being hard to argue with directly: who, after all, is against the nation being able to defend itself? The rhetorical trick is to make disagreement with a specific and questionable inference look like indifference to national survival itself. Naming the mechanisms is how one separates the reasonable concern from the unreasonable demand it is used to smuggle, and how one recovers the right to say: yes, I want my country to be safe, and no, that does not give it a claim on whether I have children. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 4 Quiz. 1. The evidence What does the evidence actually show about the relationship between population and military power in the contemporary world? This is the empirical heart of the matter, and it is where the soldier-supply argument is most vulnerable, not because demography is irrelevant, but because its relevance has been transformed by the changing character of great-power military strength. I will state the evidence carefully and mark its limits, because the honest position is not that numbers never matter but that the specific inference the bias draws is not supported by how modern military power works. Start with the most decisive fact: nuclear deterrence has severed the link between population and the ultimate security of great powers. A state that possesses a credible nuclear arsenal cannot be conquered and occupied by conventional invasion in the way that unarmed or non-nuclear states can. The whole logic of "we need enough men to avoid being overrun" assumes a form of existential conventional threat that nuclear weapons have, for nuclear-armed states and their allies, largely removed. A country of modest population with a secure second-strike capability enjoys a kind of security that no amount of extra births could have bought in the pre-nuclear age. For the great powers whose demographic anxieties are loudest, this is the single most important fact, and the soldier-supply argument almost never addresses it. The existential threat the bias trades on, being overrun, absorbed, extinguished as a nation, is precisely the threat that a credible deterrent forecloses, and it is precisely the great powers with such deterrents that produce the most soldier-supply rhetoric. The rhetoric and the reality point in opposite directions. Second, modern great-power military strength is dominated by technology, capital, and industrial-technical depth, not raw head-count. The effectiveness of a military is a function of its weapons systems, its command and intelligence networks, its logistics, its training, and its economy's ability to produce and sustain advanced materiel. A smaller, wealthier, technologically advanced force routinely outmatches a larger but poorer and less sophisticated one. Combat power per soldier has risen enormously over the last century; the number of soldiers has become a weaker predictor of what a military can do. Where population still matters, it matters largely through the economy and the technical workforce, through the engineers, the industrial base, the scientific establishment, which is closer to the future-workers frame of Book 059 than to the soldier-supply frame, not through sheer numbers of riflemen. A nation is not made militarily strong by having many young bodies; it is made strong by having an economy and a technological base capable of arming and sustaining a capable force, and that is a matter of productivity and organization far more than of population count. Third, the mass conscript army is largely obsolete for advanced states. Most leading militaries have moved toward smaller, professional, volunteer forces, because modern systems require trained specialists rather than masses of hastily drafted infantry, and because the political and human costs of mass conscription are high. The "cannon fodder" model, victory through the ability to feed enormous numbers of men into the line, describes the industrial wars of the last century, not the force structures great powers now build. When a state does need more personnel, the binding constraints are usually recruitment, retention, training capacity, and budget, not the absolute number of young people in existence. A country with tens of millions of young adults can still fail to fill its ranks if service is unattractive; a country with far fewer can field a formidable professional force if it is well resourced and well regarded. The birth rate is simply not the operative variable. Fourth, drones and precision weapons are pushing further in the same direction, substituting machines, sensors, and munitions for human bodies at the point of contact. The trend of military modernization has been, for a century, to make each person on the battlefield more lethal and to reduce the number of bodies a given effect requires. Remotely operated and increasingly autonomous systems extend this logic: they place capability where a mass of soldiers once had to stand. This is the opposite of a world in which national strength is a function of how many young people you can raise. If anything, the direction of technological change is to make raw manpower less relevant to the delivery of force over time, not more, a trajectory that runs directly against the soldier-supply premise. Now the honest limits, because the evidence does not license a triumphant "population never matters.

① In brief

Book 061 · 100 Biases of Natalism

This volume examines the soldier-supply bias: the tendency to treat human reproduction as the production of national manpower, and to convert geopolitical anxiety, the fear of being outnumbered, out-mobilized, or overrun by rival states, into pressure on individuals to bear children for the strength and defense of the nation. It is one of the oldest and most emotionally potent forms of pronatalism, because it wraps the demand for births in the language of survival, sacrifice, and patriotism.

The book stays deliberately in its lane. It is about the military and geopolitical "population equals power" argument: the claim that a nation must reproduce to field armies, project power, and avoid being demographically overwhelmed. It distinguishes this sharply from the economic future-workers frame (Book 059), which values bodies as producers and taxpayers, and from the nation-as-family metaphor (Book 060), which figures national survival in the language of blood and kinship. Here the unit of value is the potential combatant, the motivating emotion is external threat, and the demand is pointed inward at reproduction.

Read the rest of the overview

Following the series method, Chapter 2 states the strongest case for the bias first and in full, before any rebuttal. That steelman begins in hard realism: the international order is anarchic, security is a real achievement that can be lost, and population is foundational to national power because every element of that power must be manned. In the great industrial wars of the twentieth century, manpower was genuinely decisive; a persistently shrinking population thins a nation's strategic reserves while a rival's grows. If national survival depends partly on demographic strength, and demographic strength is the sum of individual choices, then those choices are not purely private, so the argument runs.

The critique that follows does not deny that nations face threats or that demography can matter. It concedes both. Its core claim is narrower and empirical: the picture of war that makes raw numbers decisive, massed conscript armies, front lines held by weight of bodies, "cannon fodder" as a strategic resource, describes a form of great-power warfare that has largely passed. Nuclear deterrence has severed the link between population and ultimate security for the great powers whose demographic anxieties are loudest. Modern military strength is dominated by technology, capital, and industrial-technical depth rather than head-count. The mass conscript army is largely obsolete for advanced states, and drones and precision weapons push further in the same direction. Where population still matters for defense, it works mainly through the economy, the future-workers channel, not through the direct supply of soldiers the bias asserts.

Beneath the empirical failure the book identifies a moral one that no revision of the facts would cure: the soldier-supply argument asks people to bring human beings into existence to serve as future material for the state, potentially to kill and die on a front chosen by others. This is instrumentalization in its sharpest form, because the end it serves can be lethal. The book traces the bias's origins in cameralist statecraft, the levée en masse, and above all interwar France's dénatalité anxiety; shows how it operates today through demographic-security headlines, recruitment-crisis stories, rival-comparisons, and militarized metaphor; counts its hidden costs; and asks who bears the pressure: women, young men, the poor, and targeted communities most of all.

The measured takeaway is a single distinction, held firmly: the difference between taking national security seriously, which one can and should do, and treating persons as manpower, which clear thinking must refuse. One can grant the hardest realism about a dangerous world and still reject the leap from national security to a demand on individual reproduction. Persons are not cannon fodder held in reserve. Naming the bias is what makes that refusal possible.

② The book in twelve chapters

CH 1Naming the bias

The soldier-supply bias is the tendency to treat human reproduction as the production of national manpower, and to convert geopolitical anxiety, the fear of being outnumbered, out-mobilized, or overrun by rival states, into pressure on individuals to bear children for the strength and defense of the nation. Its one-sentence thesis, the thesis this book contests, is: a nation must reproduce in order to field armies, project power, and avoid being demographically overwhelmed by its rivals, and so individuals have a patriotic reason to have children.

Before I say anything against this argument, I want to state it at full strength, in the voice of someone who believes it and believes it for serious reasons. If the argument can be answered, it must be answered in this form, not in a caricature. I ask the reader who already disagrees to resist, for the length of this chapter, the urge to rebut, and to feel the genuine weight of a case that has moved serious statesmen and shaped the policy of great nations.

CH 3Where it comes from

The soldier-supply bias is ancient, because the link between population and military power is one of the oldest observations in statecraft. But its modern, anxious, policy-driven form is largely a product of the last three centuries, and understanding its origins helps explain why it retains such grip even as the world that gave rise to it recedes. A bias with roots this deep does not vanish simply because the conditions that nourished it have changed; it persists as a habit of mind, a reflex reached for in moments of insecurity long after the reality it once tracked has moved on.

CH 4How it operates today

If the industrial mass war that gave the soldier-supply bias its plausibility has faded, the bias itself has not. It operates today less through explicit conscription policy than through rhetoric, framing, and mood, a background hum of demographic anxiety that surfaces whenever national security is discussed. It rarely announces itself as an argument that individuals should reproduce for the state; that would sound archaic and coercive. Instead it works by making a certain description of reproduction feel natural and serious, so the demand seems to follow from the nation's security rather than from anyone's desire to direct other people's bodies. Show all 16 terms as a list
Soldier-supply bias
The tendency to treat reproduction as the production of national manpower and to convert geopolitical anxiety into pressure on individuals to bear children for the defense of the state.
Population-as-power
The doctrine, ancient in statecraft, that a state's strength is a function of the size of its population, historically because population supplied soldiers, workers, and taxpayers.
Manpower
Human beings considered as a strategic resource — the material from which armies, war economies, and mobilizations are built.
Cannon fodder
A bitter idiom for soldiers treated as expendable material to be consumed in war; a fossil of, and a rebuke to, the instrumentalizing logic of mass warfare.
Dénatalité
The French term for the low-birth-rate anxiety that shaped French demographic-military thinking from the nineteenth century and drove interwar pronatalist policy.
Levée en masse
Mass conscription of a nation's young male population, exemplified by revolutionary and Napoleonic France, which made population size a live strategic variable.
Mass conscript army
A military built from large-scale conscription of the general population, characteristic of the industrial wars of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries and largely obsolete for advanced states today.
Professional/volunteer force
A smaller, trained, non-conscripted military, toward which most advanced states have moved, reflecting the shift from mass to technological military power.
Nuclear deterrence
The security a credible nuclear arsenal provides against conventional conquest, which largely severs the link between population size and the ultimate security of great powers.
Element of national power
A term from realist international theory listing population alongside geography, resources, industry, and technology as a component of a state's strength, with the caveat that numbers matter only as they can be organized and equipped.
Demographic-security alarm
The recurring genre of argument that a falling birth rate is a national-security threat because it will hollow out future military manpower or the base that sustains it.
Instrumentalization
Treating a person (parent or child) as a means to a collective end they never chose; in this bias, treating the unborn as future manpower for the state.
Collective-action problem (demographic)
The claim that individually reasonable choices to have fewer children may aggregate into a nation unable to defend itself, invoked to justify collective pressure on reproduction.
Strategic depth (demographic)
The idea that a broad base of young people gives a nation reserves to mobilize and losses to replace; central to the industrial-war model of manpower.
Demographic rivalry
The framing of national security as a race between the birth rates of rival peoples or states, in which being "outbred" is figured as being weakened.
War of attrition
A conflict decided by the relative capacity to absorb and replace losses over time, the model of war in which manpower is most directly decisive.
