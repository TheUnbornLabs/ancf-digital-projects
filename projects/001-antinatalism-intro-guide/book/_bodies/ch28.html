"Be fruitful, and multiply, and replenish the earth."

— Genesis 1:28

For a great many people, the question this book asks is not first answered by philosophy at all. It is answered by faith. Across the world's religious traditions, the bearing of children is woven into the deepest sense of what life is for — a blessing to be welcomed, a gift to be received with thanks, sometimes a duty laid down by God himself. To such a reader, antinatalism can look less like an argument and more like a refusal: a turning away from something sacred. That reaction deserves to be met with respect, and met carefully, because it rests on convictions that have sustained human communities for thousands of years.

This chapter takes the religious objection seriously and on its own terms. We will not pretend that faith is a simple thing to argue against, nor that the antinatalist has some knockdown reply that believers have somehow failed to notice. What we can do is lay the two views side by side honestly, note where they genuinely conflict, and — perhaps surprisingly — find that many traditions contain voices closer to the antinatalist than one might expect.

The blessing and the commandment

In the Hebrew scriptures, the very first words God speaks to humanity are a blessing tied to fruitfulness. To "be fruitful and multiply" is read by much of Judaism as a positive commandment — children are a sign of covenant, continuity, and divine favor. The Psalms call children "a heritage from the Lord" and compare a quiver full of them to the joy of a warrior. Christianity inherited this sense of creation as fundamentally good: in Genesis, God surveys what he has made and pronounces it very good. Islam, too, holds children to be a gift (hiba) from God and the continuation of the family a thing to be desired and protected.

The pattern repeats well beyond the Abrahamic faiths. In Hinduism, the householder stage of life — grihastha — carries real spiritual weight, and raising a family is understood as a way of honoring one's ancestors and fulfilling one's dharma, one's proper role in the order of things. Across these traditions the message converges: existence is a gift from a good source, and to pass that gift on is to participate in something holy.

Pronatalism — the cultural or religious stance that treats childbearing as a positive good to be encouraged. In a religious setting it is often grounded not merely in social benefit but in a belief that life itself is a blessing from God and that procreation shares in divine creativity.

Suffering given meaning

The antinatalist case leans heavily on suffering — its inevitability, and the fact that no one consents to bear it. Here is where faith offers its most powerful answer. In most religious frameworks, suffering is not the meaningless accident it can appear to be from a purely secular standpoint. It is set within a larger story that gives it a place and even a purpose.

Some Christian thinkers describe this world as a "vale of soul-making" — the idea that hardship is the very means by which a person grows into depth, courage, and love. Many traditions hold out the hope of redemption, a final setting-right in which present pain is healed or outweighed. In the religions shaped by ideas of karma and rebirth, a life of suffering is understood within a vast moral economy stretching across many lifetimes, where nothing is simply wasted. To the believer, then, the antinatalist has measured only one side of the ledger. Yes, a child will suffer — but the child is also offered meaning, communion, and a destiny that suffering cannot finally defeat.

The objectionCreation is good, and life is a gift from a good God. To say it would be better never to be born is to reject that gift and to second-guess the Creator. Within faith, suffering is not the last word: it is given meaning by a larger purpose the antinatalist simply leaves out of the picture. Procreation is not a harm imposed on the unwilling but a sacred act of love that mirrors God's own creativity.
A replyThis is a serious and coherent position, and the antinatalist need not deny that it holds together within faith. The reply is narrower than people expect. First, two old problems do not vanish inside belief. The problem of evil — why a good and all-powerful God permits the depth of suffering we actually see — has troubled the most devout theologians for centuries; it is not a secular invention, and it presses on the claim that creating life is straightforwardly good. Second, the consent point remains: even a gift can be questioned when it is given to someone who could not be asked and who must bear its full weight, including its worst possibilities, whether they would have wished it or not. To raise these is not to mock faith. It is to notice that the moral puzzle of birth survives translation into religious language rather than dissolving there.

The world-renouncing strands within faith

It would be a mistake to treat religion as uniformly pronatalist. Many of the same traditions that bless childbearing also honor those who turn away from it. Monasticism and celibacy run deep through Christian history; the consecrated life of renunciation has been held up not as a failure to live fully but as a higher calling. The apostle Paul, in his first letter to the Corinthians, frankly preferred the unmarried, childless state for those who could bear it. Buddhism centers on the recognition that existence is shot through with dukkha — unsatisfactoriness, suffering — and that the deepest aim is release from the very cycle of rebirth, not its perpetuation. Jain monks and nuns embrace radical non-attachment, including celibacy, as a path away from the bondage of worldly existence. Earlier still, certain Gnostic and Cathar currents regarded the material world with deep suspicion and looked askance at bringing new souls into it.

Many traditions bless the parent and honor the monk in the same breath.

The point is not that these strands are antinatalist in the modern philosophical sense — they are not, and it would be careless to claim them as allies. The point is gentler: the world's faiths have never spoken with a single voice about whether worldly life is the highest good or something to be transcended. The antinatalist instinct that existence carries a heavy weight is not alien to religion. It has lived inside religion all along, in the cell of the monk and the silence of the meditating ascetic.

In plain words: Most faiths treat children as a blessing, and they offer suffering a meaning that secular thinking often cannot. The antinatalist's reply is modest — the problem of evil and the question of consent do not disappear inside belief — and the conversation is friendlier than it looks, since renunciation has always had an honored place within religion too.

Holding the disagreement gently

Where does this leave a believing reader? Not, the antinatalist hopes, feeling attacked. The honest situation is that the two views begin from different starting points — one from trust in a good Creator, the other from a question about whether existence can be justified to the one who must live it. They will not always be reconciled. But they can share a concern: the wish that no child come into avoidable suffering, and the conviction that bringing a life into being is a matter of the gravest moral weight, never to be done lightly. On that much, the devout parent and the thoughtful antinatalist may find they are closer than the labels suggest.

Chapter in brief

  • Across Judaism, Christianity, Islam, and Hinduism, procreation is widely treated as a blessing, gift, or commandment, and creation itself as good.
  • Faith typically gives suffering meaning — through soul-making, redemption, or karma — which is a genuine answer to the antinatalist's emphasis on harm.
  • The antinatalist reply is narrow: the problem of evil and the consent point persist even within belief.
  • Many traditions also contain world-renouncing strands — monasticism, celibacy, Buddhism, Jainism — so religion's voice on worldly life is not single.
  • The disagreement is real but can be held with mutual respect and a shared concern for any future child.
