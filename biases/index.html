100 Biases of Natalism

Having children is treated as the thing that happens unless you stop it. This is a field guide to the machinery behind that feeling — one hundred specific mechanisms, each named, steelmanned, and examined, so a decision this large can actually be made rather than merely arrived at.

The target here is the bias, never the people. Nothing in this collection argues that having children is a mistake, or that the childfree chose more wisely. It argues something narrower: a default is not a decision, and a decision this size deserves to be one — whichever way you land.

The eight parts

The biases are grouped by the kind of machinery they use. Most people find one part describes their own experience far better than the rest — that is usually the place to start.

001–014 · PART I

Cognitive & Psychological Biases

How ordinary features of human judgement — defaults, optimism, adaptation, memory — quietly favour one answer before anyone deliberates.

015–024 · PART II

Evolutionary & Biological Framings

What happens when descriptions of biology (instinct, clocks, genes) are read as instructions about how a life must go.

025–040 · PART III

Cultural & Social Scripts

The inherited sequence — school, job, marriage, baby — and the social machinery that keeps people moving along it without re-deciding.

041–050 · PART IV

Religious, Spiritual & Moral Framings

Reproduction framed as blessing, command, or cosmic duty, and what that framing does to a person who declines it.

051–066 · PART V

Economic, Demographic & Policy Pronatalism

Birth rates as emergency: dependency ratios, pension arguments, subsidies, and the political interests behind the panic.

067–078 · PART VI

Media, Language & Rhetoric

The vocabulary and imagery — announcements, headlines, feeds, "childless" versus "childfree" — that make one path read as normal.

079–090 · PART VII

Philosophical & Ethical Fallacies

The argumentative moves themselves: burden-shifting, appeals to consequences, gift-of-life reasoning, and the confusions around people who do not yet exist.

091–100 · PART VIII

Gender, Body, Identity & Autonomy

Whose body, whose ambition, whose family counts — where reproductive expectation meets gender, identity, and autonomy.

How each page works

Every bias gets the same treatment, because consistency is what makes a hundred of them comparable:

  • In brief — what the bias is and what the volume argues, in plain language.
  • The book in twelve chapters — an expandable map of the argument, including the chapter that makes the strongest natalist case and the chapter that turns the critique on itself.
  • Key terms — flashcards for the vocabulary, because naming a mechanism is most of seeing it.
  • A ten-question check — comprehension, not a verdict on you, with the reasoning shown for each answer.
  • Reflection prompts — open questions with a private notebook that never leaves your device.
  • Sources — what the volume actually draws on, with its own uncertainty stated where the evidence is thin.

Your progress, scores, and notes are stored in this browser only. Nothing is uploaded, and there is no account, no ad, and no tracker.

This is education and reflection, not medical, legal, financial, or mental-health advice. The subject is a sensitive one; if it stirs distress, consider reaching out to someone you trust. Nothing here judges anyone's reproductive choices — the target is the pressure, not the people.
