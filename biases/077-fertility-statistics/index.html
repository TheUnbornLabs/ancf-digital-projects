Lies, Damned Lies, and Fertility Statistics

Part VI · Media, Language & Rhetoric · Book 077 · *100 Biases of Natalism*

The bias: Framing tricks in how birth and fertility numbers are reported.

① In brief

Lies, Damned Lies, and Fertility Statistics 100 Biases of Natalism · slug fertility-statistics

Numbers arrive wearing the costume of neutrality. A percentage looks like a fact; a rate looks like a measurement; a headline figure looks like something found rather than something made. That apparent innocence is what makes statistics such an effective vehicle for reproductive pressure. This book examines a narrow, practical target: the specific statistical and numerical tricks by which fertility and birth data are made to appear more alarming, more urgent, and more personally coercive than the honest numbers warrant.

The territory is deliberately tight. This is not a book about how the news frames fertility stories (Book 069), nor about the "biological clock" metaphor (Book 016), nor about the felt urgency of aging out (Book 021). It is about the numerical mechanics themselves: misleading baselines, truncated axes, the difference between a percentage and a percentage point, cherry-picked timeframes, relative risk dressed up as absolute risk, base-rate neglect, and decontextualized rates. These are the ordinary furniture of innumerate persuasion, catalogued as far back as Darrell Huff's How to Lie with Statistics (1954), and they recur in fertility discourse with striking fidelity to Huff's original examples.

Read the rest of the overview

The book's central move is a distinction: a statistic can be technically true and structurally misleading at the same time. The period total fertility rate really is what it is; a relative risk really did double; a birth rate really did fall by that percentage. The manipulation lies in what the true number is allowed to imply: the missing denominator, the chosen baseline, the suppressed distinction between a period measure and a cohort one. Because you cannot debunk a true number by calling it false, the antidote is not counter-assertion but numeracy: understanding the shape of the error rather than memorizing a corrected figure.

A book about statistics must hold itself to an unusually strict citation discipline, and this one does. It names very few specific figures and flags what it cannot verify. Its method is to expose structure, so a reader can recognize the trick regardless of which particular statistic is deployed. The famous claim that a large fraction of women over thirty-five will struggle to conceive, reportedly derived from birth records of seventeenth- and eighteenth-century French women, and criticized on those grounds by figures including Jean Twenge (flagged to verify), is treated as a case study in layered error (a pre-industrial baseline, base-rate neglect, false precision) rather than as an occasion to assert a corrected number.

The steelman in Chapter 2 is given full weight. The underlying trends are real: fertility has fallen, sub-replacement rates are widespread, populations are aging, and age-related fecundity decline is genuine. Statistics are indispensable, most demographic work is careful, and a reflexive distrust of numbers is its own failure, one that can lead a person to wave away real, decision-relevant information. The book contests none of this. It contests only the avoidable tricks that make true trends frighten and hurry people beyond what the honest numbers support.

An important, genuinely subtle thread runs throughout: the difference between the period total fertility rate and completed cohort fertility. When a generation postpones childbearing, the period rate drops sharply during the transition through a tempo effect, even if those women ultimately have as many children as their mothers. The most-cited fertility number can therefore systematically overstate the drama of a decline. This is a real subtlety, understood by every demographer and almost never explained in popular coverage.

Later chapters count the hidden costs (distorted decisions, distributed anxiety, eroded trust in numbers, policy driven by artifacts), map who bears them (women, the less numerate, the less resourced), answer the objections to the critique, and offer a portable toolkit of questions: Where does the axis begin? Doubled from what to what? What is the denominator? Why this baseline? Period or cohort? What was the source population? The aim is a stance that is neither credulous nor cynical, one that takes the information and leaves the pressure.

② The book in twelve chapters

Each chapter opens with its own summary. Note chapter 2: every volume states the strongest case for the thing it criticises before laying a finger on it — and chapter 8 turns the critique back on itself.

CH 1Naming the bias

The bias this book examines is the use of misleading statistical presentation to make fertility and birth data appear more alarming, more urgent, or more personally coercive than the honest numbers support. It is not the existence of fertility statistics, nor any particular trend they reveal, but a family of specific numerical tricks: misleading baselines, truncated axes, percentage-versus-percentage-point confusions, cherry-picked timeframes, relative-risk inflation, base-rate neglect, and decontextualized rates, deployed, whether cynically or carelessly, to push reproductive decisions in a pronatalist direction.

CH 2The strongest case for it (steelman)The strongest case against this book

Before dissecting the tricks, we owe the other side its strongest hearing, and in a book about statistics that hearing must be especially generous, because a reflexive distrust of numbers is not skepticism but a different failure. Here is the best case for taking fertility statistics largely at face value and for regarding this book's project with suspicion.

CH 3Where it comes from

Statistical persuasion is old, but the particular machinery this book examines has a traceable lineage, and understanding it clarifies why fertility discourse is such fertile ground for numerical tricks. The impulse to dress a claim in numbers predates modern statistics; rulers have counted subjects, soldiers, and taxable households for millennia, and the very word "statistics" derives from the study of the state and its populations. From the beginning, counting people was entangled with governing them, and population figures were never politically neutral. A sovereign who wanted more soldiers or more taxpayers had reasons to frame the numbers a certain way. The census was, from its origins, an instrument of power before it was an instrument of knowledge, and the fertility of a population was among the most politically charged of all quantities to count, because it measured the future strength of the state itself.

CH 4How it operates today

The bias operates through a recognizable repertoire, and cataloguing the moves is the most practical debiasing this book can offer, because each move, once named, becomes hard to un-see. Consider them in turn.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

What does the evidence actually show, both about fertility itself and about the statistical errors this book identifies? Honesty requires separating the solid from the contested, and in a book about numbers we must be especially careful not to overclaim.

CH 6The hidden costs

What does misleading statistical presentation cost, beyond the immediate error? The costs are easy to underrate because a misleading-but-true number does not feel like a harm. Yet the harms are real and, characteristically for this series, unpriced in the default framing.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

Statistical manipulation is not distributed evenly in its effects, and a distributional analysis reveals that the burden of misleading fertility numbers falls, predictably, on those already most subject to reproductive pressure.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

What does numerate reasoning about fertility statistics look like, once the bias is named? The goal is not cynicism but a set of habits, questions to ask of any figure, that let a person extract the real information while discarding the manufactured alarm. For individuals, several habits suffice to disarm most of the tricks.

CH 12Conclusion

This book argued a narrow and, I hope, useful thesis: that in fertility and birth discourse, the numbers are frequently technically true but structurally misleading, and that the reliable defense is not counter-assertion but numeracy, understanding the shape of the error rather than memorizing a corrected figure. The manipulation rarely requires falsehood. It works through baseline selection, axis truncation, the percentage-versus-percentage-point slide, relative-risk inflation, base-rate neglect, decontextualized rates, and the domain-specific presentation of a period measure as though it were a cohort outcome. Each trick exploits a predictable gap between how a statistic is displayed and how a hurried, denominator-less reader interprets it, and each happens, in fertility discourse, to push in a pronatalist direction.

③ Key terms

Show all 16 terms as a list
Period total fertility rate (TFR)
A synthetic measure: the number of children a hypothetical woman would bear if she experienced, across her life, the age-specific birth rates observed in a single year. Sensitive to the timing of births, not a count of any real woman's completed family.
Completed cohort fertility
The average number of children women in a real birth cohort actually finish their lives having. Less volatile than the period rate and not distorted by tempo effects.
Tempo effect
The temporary depression (or inflation) of a period fertility rate caused by a shift in the timing of births, such as widespread postponement, even when completed family size is unchanged.
Replacement level
The fertility rate, conventionally cited near 2.1 children per woman in low-mortality settings, required for a population to replace itself without migration.
Truncated axis (gee-whiz graph)
A chart whose vertical axis does not begin at zero, exaggerating the apparent steepness of a change. Named by Darrell Huff.
Percentage vs. percentage point
The distinction between a proportional change (a rise from 1% to 2% is a 100% increase) and an additive one (the same rise is one percentage point). Conflating them dramatizes small changes.
Relative risk
The ratio of a risk in one group to another ("doubled," "halved"). Uninformative about magnitude without the absolute baseline.
Absolute risk
The actual probability of an outcome (e.g., "two in a hundred"), which relative risk alone conceals.
Base-rate neglect
The error of assessing a statistic about a subgroup without anchoring it in the underlying population frequency.
Misleading baseline
A comparison starting point chosen to maximize the apparent size of a trend, such as anchoring to a historical spike like a post-war baby boom.
Cherry-picked timeframe
The selective choice of start and end dates that make a trend look steeper or flatter than a fuller series would.
Decontextualized rate
A figure presented without the denominator, population base, or comparison needed to interpret it.
Semi-attached figure
Huff's term for a statistic that answers a different question from the one it appears to answer.
Numeracy
The literacy of quantities: the ability to interrogate a statistic's baseline, denominator, absolute magnitude, and source population. The central debiasing tool of this book.
Advanced maternal age
A clinical label (often applied from thirty-five) that attaches a battery of relative risks to a threshold, frequently presented without absolute context.
Source population
The group in which a statistic was actually measured; a figure is only about that group and misleads when silently applied to a different one (e.g., a pre-industrial or clinical population generalized to all women today).

⑥ Sources

What this volume draws on. Where the evidence is thin or contested, the book says so rather than reaching for a number.

  • Darrell Huff, How to Lie with Statistics (New York: W. W. Norton, 1954). The canonical catalogue of statistical deception: the truncated / "gee-whiz" axis, the misleading baseline, the semi-attached figure, and the percentage-versus-percentage-point confusion. Directly underwrites Chapters 3 and 4.
  • Gerd Gigerenzer, Calculated Risks: How to Know When Numbers Deceive You (New York: Simon & Schuster, 2002; also published as Reckoning with Risk, London: Penguin). On absolute versus relative risk, natural frequencies, and how honest presentation debiases medical statistics. Underwrites the relative/absolute-risk discussion in Chapters 4 and 11.
  • United Nations, Department of Economic and Social Affairs, Population Division, World Population Prospects (successive revisions). The standard international source for national and global fertility rates, replacement-level context, and population projections. Cited as the class of authoritative source for the reality of falling and sub-replacement fertility, per citation policy, rather than as a specific unverified table.
  • Hans Rosling, with Ola Rosling and Anna Rosling Rönnlund, Factfulness: Ten Reasons We're Wrong About the World — and Why Things Are Better Than You Think (New York: Flatiron Books, 2018). On reading demographic trends without dramatization and on the human tendency toward alarmist misreading of population data.
  • Mark Twain, Chapters from My Autobiography (published serially, 1906–07), which popularized the "lies, damned lies, and statistics" line and attributed it to Benjamin Disraeli. The attribution to Disraeli is itself historically contested: no such remark has been found in Disraeli's own words, and the quip's true origin remains unsettled.
Directions the volume points to (not pinned citations)
  • Jean Twenge, "How Long Can You Wait to Have a Baby?" (The Atlantic, July/August 2013). The essay that traced the widely repeated "one in three women over 35 will not conceive" statistic to its source in French birth records from 1670 to 1830, and asked what such data can reasonably say about women living with electricity, antibiotics, and modern medicine.
  • John Bongaarts and Griffith Feeney, "On the Quantum and Tempo of Fertility" (Population and Development Review, vol. 24, 1998). The paper introducing the tempo adjustment to the total fertility rate, and the standard reference for why a period TFR overstates a decline when childbearing is being postponed rather than forgone.
  • Moira Weigel, "The foul reign of the biological clock" (The Guardian, 2016), and Labor of Love: The Invention of Dating (2016). On the metaphor's invention in the late 1970s and the uses to which it has been put since.
  • On the origin of "lies, damned lies, and statistics": the line reaches us through Twain, who credited Disraeli, but no such remark has been located in Disraeli's writings or speeches, and the attribution remains disputed.
  • On national birth-rate "percentage decline" or "collapse" figures: before repeating one, establish the baseline year chosen, whether the measure is period or cohort TFR, and whether tempo effects account for part of the change.
  • On absolute-risk figures for age-related conception probability, time-to-pregnancy, and miscarriage: the direction of these trends is well established. Any specific percentage or ratio should be traced to a primary clinical or demographic source and read in absolute as well as relative terms.
