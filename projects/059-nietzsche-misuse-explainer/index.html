Nietzsche Misuse Explainer

Philosophy · Difficulty: Advanced

Nietzsche is one of the most quoted — and most misquoted — philosophers online, including in debates about who is "strong" enough to have or not have children. This corrective explainer restores some nuance, and doubles as a lesson in not borrowing authority you haven't earned.

Common misuses

"Will to power" = domination

Often read as a licence for ruthlessness. Scholars generally read it as a drive toward growth, mastery, and self-overcoming — closer to creative striving than to bullying.

The "Übermensch" = a superior class of people

Frequently twisted into a claim that some people are inherently better. Nietzsche's Übermensch is better read as an aspiration to create one's own values after old certainties fall — not a master race.

"What doesn't kill you makes you stronger"

A real line (from Twilight of the Idols) but constantly used to dismiss others' suffering. In context it's a personal note on resilience, not a rule that hardship is always good.

"Amor fati" cropped into a slogan

"Love of fate" gets clipped to justify whatever the quoter already believes — including crude takes on having children. The idea is subtler and resists being a debate weapon.

A fairer reading

Nietzsche prized affirming life and creating values in the face of meaninglessness. His "eternal recurrence" is less a metaphysics than a test: could you will your life to repeat forever? He resists tidy use by any "side" of the birth debate — and notably, he despised the nationalism and antisemitism later projected onto him.

How the distortion happened

Part of Nietzsche's bad reputation came after his death: his sister, Elisabeth Förster-Nietzsche, edited and reframed his unpublished notes (The Will to Power) in ways that suited her own nationalist, antisemitic politics. It's a cautionary tale about reading authors in their own published words, not through interested editors — or through a meme.

The lesson

Quote carefully, in context, and from primary texts — Thus Spoke Zarathustra, Beyond Good and Evil, The Gay Science. Borrowed authority is not an argument: "a clever person said something that sounds like my view" proves nothing. If the reasoning can't stand without the famous name attached, it can't stand.
