A Timeline of the Idea

Antinatalist and pessimist thought, century by century.

"Not to be born is, beyond all estimation, best."

— Sophocles, Oedipus at Colonus

The thought that it might have been better never to exist is not a modern invention. It surfaces in ancient poetry and scripture, runs through medieval world-rejecting religion, and is given systematic form by the great pessimist philosophers of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries before reaching its sharpest contemporary statement. The timeline below traces that long line, naming real works and the people who wrote them. Dates for ancient material are necessarily approximate.

  • c. 5th c. BCE — In Greece, the dark wisdom that it is best never to be born finds voice in poetry attributed to Theognis and, most famously, in the chorus of Sophocles' Oedipus at Colonus; the legend of Silenus likewise declares non-existence the greatest good.
  • c. 5th c. BCE — The Buddha teaches the Four Noble Truths in northern India; the First Noble Truth holds that conditioned existence is pervaded by dukkha — suffering and unsatisfactoriness — and that release lies beyond the cycle of rebirth.
  • early centuries CE — Gnostic and, later, Manichaean movements regard the material world and bodily life as a kind of trap or error, casting birth into this world in a deeply negative light.
  • 1818 — Arthur Schopenhauer publishes The World as Will and Representation, grounding philosophical pessimism in the restless, never-satisfied "Will" and arguing that existence is marked by suffering.
  • 1876 — Philipp Mainländer's The Philosophy of Redemption (Die Philosophie der Erlösung) carries pessimism further, presenting a metaphysics in which the world tends toward non-being.
  • 1895 — Thomas Hardy's novel Jude the Obscure dramatizes the suffering of unwanted birth, including the chilling note "Done because we are too menny," capturing despair about bringing children into a hard world.
  • 1933 — Peter Wessel Zapffe's essay "The Last Messiah" (Den sidste Messias) argues that human consciousness overreaches its environment, and that we survive only by anchoring, isolation, distraction, and sublimation.
  • 1973 — E. M. Cioran's The Trouble with Being Born (De l'inconvénient d'être né) gives aphoristic literary form to the regret of having come into existence.
  • 1984 — Derek Parfit's Reasons and Persons founds modern population ethics, introducing the non-identity problem and the Repugnant Conclusion that frame later debate.
  • 1999 — Seana Shiffrin's article "Wrongful Life, Procreative Responsibility, and the Significance of Harm" argues that procreation imposes serious, unconsented burdens, giving the consent concern rigorous footing.
  • 2006 — David Benatar publishes Better Never to Have Been, setting out the asymmetry argument; the same year, in France, Théophile de Giraud's manifesto L'Art de guillotiner les procréateurs helps popularize the term "antinatalism."
  • 2010 — Thomas Ligotti's The Conspiracy Against the Human Race brings pessimist and antinatalist themes to a wider literary and horror-reading audience.
  • 2014 — HBO's True Detective, season one, puts pessimist ideas into mainstream culture through the character Rust Cohle, sending many viewers to Ligotti and Zapffe.
  • 2014 — Sarah Perry's Every Cradle Is a Grave offers a contemporary, accessible defense of antinatalist and pessimist conclusions.
  • 2017 — David Benatar's The Human Predicament broadens the case into a general pessimist account of meaning, death, and the quality of human life.

Read together, these moments show a question that keeps returning rather than a single school. Poets felt it, religions wrestled with it, and philosophers eventually gave it argument and structure. What changed in the modern period is not the intuition but its precision: the asymmetry, the non-identity problem, and the consent objection turned an ancient mood into a debate that can be stated, defended, and challenged.

Chapter in brief

  • The intuition that non-existence might be preferable is ancient, voiced by Greek poets, the Buddha's First Noble Truth, and world-rejecting religious movements.
  • Schopenhauer (1818) and Mainländer (1876) gave pessimism systematic philosophical form.
  • Zapffe (1933) and Cioran (1973) carried it into the twentieth century; Parfit (1984) and Shiffrin (1999) supplied modern rigor.
  • Benatar (2006, 2017), together with Ligotti, Perry, and popular culture, brought antinatalism to its current, sharply argued form.

Sources & further reading

  2. David Benatar, Better Never to Have Been (Oxford University Press, 2006) and The Human Predicament (Oxford University Press, 2017).
