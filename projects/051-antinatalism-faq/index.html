PROJECT 051

Antinatalism FAQ

Philosophy · Difficulty: Intermediate · ~8 min

Clear, respectful answers to frequent questions and misconceptions about antinatalism. Search the list, open a question, and copy any answer. The aim is understanding — you can grasp the view well while disagreeing with it.

What you will learn

  • What antinatalism does and doesn't claim.
  • How it differs from being childfree.
  • The core arguments, in brief.
  • Why it isn't hatred or nihilism.

1. Search the FAQ

What is antinatalism?

The view that creating a new person deserves serious ethical questioning, rather than being treated as an automatic good. It centres on consent, suffering-risk, and autonomy — and concerns only future births, never anyone already alive.

Is antinatalism the same as being childfree?

No. "Childfree" is a personal lifestyle choice; antinatalism is an ethical argument about the value of creating new lives. They often overlap, but you can hold either without the other.

Do antinatalists hate children or people?

No — concern for the wellbeing of any future child is often the very reason people arrive at the view. It's an ethical argument, which a content, people-loving person can hold.

Is it about existing people?

No. Antinatalism is only about future births. It says nothing against people who already exist and calls for no harm to anyone.

What's the asymmetry argument?

David Benatar's claim that the absence of pain is good even if no one enjoys it, while the absence of pleasure isn't bad unless someone is deprived — so never existing is, he argues, at least as good as existing. It's much debated.

What's the consent argument?

That birth imposes the risks of a whole life on someone who couldn't agree first — and we normally require consent before imposing serious risk. Critics raise the non-identity problem in reply.

Isn't life worth living?

Many lives clearly are. Antinatalists reply that a gamble often paying off doesn't settle whether it was permissible to take on someone else's behalf without their say.

Is antinatalism depressing or nihilistic?

Not necessarily. Many who hold it live contented, meaning-rich lives; the view is about creating new lives, not about despair. Caring deeply about reducing suffering can be its emotional root.

Can you respect parents and be antinatalist?

Yes. Respecting people who choose parenthood and questioning birth as an ethical decision are fully compatible. Disagreement is not disrespect.

Key takeaway

Antinatalism is an ethical question about creating life — not hatred, not nihilism, and never about anyone already here. Understanding it well is the best basis for agreeing or disagreeing with care.
