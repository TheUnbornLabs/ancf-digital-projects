"The absence of pain is good even if there is no one to enjoy that good."

— the disputed fourth corner of Benatar's asymmetry

The engine of David Benatar's antinatalism is his famous asymmetry between harm and benefit. In Better Never to Have Been (2006) he argues, roughly, for four claims. First, the presence of pain is bad. Second, the presence of pleasure is good. So far, unremarkable. The asymmetry comes in the third and fourth claims, which concern absence. Third: the absence of pain is good, even if there is no one for whom that absence is a benefit. Fourth: the absence of pleasure is not bad unless there is someone for whom that absence is a deprivation. From this, Benatar concludes that coming into existence always carries a real cost (the pain it brings) with no matching real benefit relative to never existing — and so never existing is always at least no worse, and usually better.

Everything turns on the third claim — usually labeled claim (c). It is the most original move in modern antinatalism, and it is also, by wide agreement, its most exposed point. This chapter lays the objection out at full strength, because an idea is only as strong as the hardest thing said against it, and because here the critic genuinely has the better innings.

Benatar's asymmetry, claim (c) — the proposition that the absence of pain is good, and good even when there exists no person to enjoy or benefit from that absence. It is this claim — that an empty world's absent pains count as a positive in the ledger — that critics regard as the weak link in the argument.

The "good for no one" objection

Start with a simple-sounding principle that many people find almost self-evident: goodness has to be good for someone. A sunset is good for the person who sees it; relief is good for the one relieved; a benefit is a benefit to a beneficiary. The notion of a good that is good for no one at all — that hangs in the air with no subject to receive it — strikes many philosophers as not just false but barely intelligible. Call this the person-affecting intuition about value.

Now apply it to claim (c). Benatar says that in a scenario where a person never exists, the absence of the pains that person would have suffered is good. But good for whom? There is no one there. The person never came to be. So who is the beneficiary of all this avoided suffering? The critic answers: no one — and a good with no possible beneficiary is no good at all. At most we can say the absence of pain is not bad, which is a very different thing. And if the absence of pain in the empty case is merely "not bad" rather than positively "good," the asymmetry collapses into a symmetry: absent pain is "not bad," absent pleasure is "not bad," and the whole argument that non-existence beats existence loses its motor.

The objection

Claim (c) smuggles in a free benefit. To make non-existence come out ahead, Benatar needs the avoided pains of the never-existent person to count as a positive good. But goodness is good for someone, and in the never-existent case there is no one for the absence of pain to be good for. So the honest verdict on the empty world's absent pain is "not bad" — neutral — not "good." Make that correction, and the ledger balances: existence has present goods and present bads; non-existence has absent goods and absent bads, both of them merely neutral. The asymmetry that was supposed to prove "better never to have been" evaporates. Benatar's conclusion does not follow from premises a reasonable person must accept; it follows only from a contested value claim that, on its most natural reading, looks false.

This objection has been pressed, in various forms, by careful readers of Benatar — among them the philosopher Elizabeth Harman in her critical review of the book, and many others in the journals that followed. It is not a fringe complaint or a misunderstanding. It targets exactly the hinge on which the whole machine swings, and it does so using a principle — that value is value for someone — that is itself one of the most widely shared in ethics. That is what makes it so formidable.

Good for whom? In the empty world there is no one for the absence of suffering to be good for.

Benatar's reply

Benatar does not concede. His response is that the objection mistakes the kind of judgment claim (c) makes. He grants there is no actual subject in the never-existent case standing to receive a benefit. But, he argues, the comparison is still legitimate, because it is made from the standpoint of the person who would exist. When we say the absence of pain is good, we are saying it is good by reference to the interests of the person who would otherwise have been — judging the alternative outcome better for the would-be person's sake, even though, if that outcome obtains, there is no one there. The "good" is comparative and impersonal: we are comparing two states of affairs and judging one less bad, not awarding a prize to a phantom.

Put differently, Benatar thinks the critic is too quick to assume that all evaluative comparisons require an actual subject present in both compared scenarios. We routinely say it is good that a possible catastrophe did not happen — good that the suffering it would have caused was avoided — without needing a population of grateful survivors to point to. If we can sensibly call avoided suffering a good thing in those cases, why not in the case of a life never started? The absence of the pains a future child would have endured can, he argues, be counted as a genuine good in just that sense.

The reply (and the standoff)

Benatar answers that claim (c) makes a comparative, impersonal judgment, not a claim about a benefit delivered to a phantom. We can judge one outcome better than another for a would-be person's sake — call avoided suffering "good" — without needing a subject present to enjoy it, just as we call a prevented disaster a good outcome. The critic answers back that this either equivocates on "good" or quietly abandons the person-affecting principle the rest of the argument seems to rely on, and that "the disaster was prevented" cases always involve actual people who were spared, which the never-existent case lacks. And so the two sides face each other across a gap that no further argument has so far closed.

Where this leaves us

It is tempting, when a beloved argument meets a hard objection, to declare a quiet winner. This book will not. The honest description is a standoff. If you find it obvious that goodness must always be good for someone, then claim (c) will look like a sleight of hand, and Benatar's asymmetry will not move you. If you are comfortable with impersonal judgments — with saying that a future containing less suffering is simply better, no beneficiary required — then claim (c) will seem like common sense, and the objection will look like an overstrict prejudice about the word "good." Reasonable, well-informed philosophers fall on both sides, and the literature has not resolved it.

What the antinatalist can fairly say is that the broader case does not stand or fall on Benatar's exact formulation. The consent worry, the risk worry, and the sheer asymmetry between how badly a life can go and how good we can realistically expect it to be — these survive even if claim (c) is rejected. What the critic can fairly say is that the most celebrated single argument for antinatalism has, at its core, a premise that many thoughtful people simply do not accept, and cannot be argued into accepting. Both of those things are true at once.

In plain words: Benatar needs "absent pain in an empty world" to count as a positive good. Critics say a good has to be good for someone, so absent pain is at most neutral — which would sink the argument. Benatar replies that the judgment is impersonal and comparative. Nobody has landed the knockout blow.

Chapter in brief

  • Benatar's asymmetry rests on claim (c): the absence of pain is good even with no one to benefit from it.
  • The central objection — "good for no one" — says value must be value for someone, so absent pain is merely neutral, which would collapse the asymmetry into symmetry.
  • This is widely regarded as the weakest point in Benatar's case, pressed by serious critics including Elizabeth Harman.
  • Benatar replies that claim (c) is an impersonal, comparative judgment, like calling a prevented disaster good — not a benefit handed to a phantom.
  • The debate is a genuine standoff and remains unresolved; the wider antinatalist case does not depend on this one premise.
