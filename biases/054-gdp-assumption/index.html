BIAS 054

Growth Needs Babies: The GDP Assumption

Part V · Economic, Demographic & Policy Pronatalism · The GDP Assumption

The bias: The premise that economic growth requires an ever-expanding population.

The gross national product measures everything, in short, except that which makes life worthwhile." — attributed to Robert F. Kennedy, University of Kansas, 18 March 1968 ## Preface to this volume There is a sentence that appears, in slightly different clothing, in editorials, central-bank speeches, and dinner-table arguments across the wealthy world: if birth rates keep falling, the economy will shrink, and we will all be poorer. It is offered not as a controversial claim but as a piece of settled arithmetic, the kind of thing only a naïf would question. Fewer babies mean fewer workers, fewer workers mean less output, less output means decline. From this chain a moral follows with suspicious ease: having children is not a private choice but an economic duty, and a society that fails to reproduce is failing, full stop. This book examines the assumption sitting under that sentence: the belief that economic growth requires an ever-expanding population, so that a declining birth rate is a threat to prosperity itself. Call it the GDP assumption. It is one of the most powerful pronatalist arguments because it wears the costume of neutrality. It does not preach. It does not invoke God or nation or the sanctity of motherhood. It points at a number that is supposed to go up, observes that a smaller population might make it go up less, and lets you draw the coercive conclusion yourself. Its power lies precisely in this indirection. A frank command to reproduce would meet resistance; a chart with a downward-sloping line, captioned with the vocabulary of crisis, produces the same pressure while appearing only to report the facts. The reader is left to supply the missing premise, and because the premise feels obvious, it is never examined. The trouble is buried in the number. "The economy" in these arguments almost always means total gross domestic product, the aggregate size of national output. But the thing that determines whether ordinary people live well is not total GDP; it is GDP per person, and more broadly the distribution and quality of what that per-person figure buys. A larger population can raise total GDP while leaving average living standards flat or lower. A country can have a growing economy and a shrinking one at the same time, depending on which number you watch. Confusing the two, treating the aggregate as if it were the measure of welfare, is the central error this book is named for. It is not an exotic error. It is the sort of mistake that hides in plain sight because the two numbers share a name, appear in the same reports, and rise together often enough that the difference between them can be ignored for years at a stretch, until a falling birth rate forces them apart and the ambiguity that was always there suddenly does real work. So the stance here is plain. It is not that population decline is costless. It plainly is not: labour markets adjust painfully, some sectors built on expansion contract, particular regions hollow out, and the transition can be hard (adjacent volumes on pensions, 053, and future labour supply, 059, treat those strains directly). The claim is narrower and, I think, sturdier. The bias lies in equating human wellbeing with aggregate growth, and then treating babies as the necessary fuel for that growth, so that the case for having children gets smuggled in as a corollary of a national accounting identity. That move deserves scrutiny, because the accounting identity does not say what it is taken to say. An identity is a definitional truth: it tells you how quantities are related by the way they are defined, not what causes what, and certainly not what anyone owes anyone. To read a duty to reproduce out of the fact that output equals workers times productivity is to mistake a bookkeeping convention for a moral discovery. The book proceeds in the series' fixed order. Chapter 1 defines the bias and marks it off from its neighbours. Chapter 2 gives the strongest pronatalist version its full due, and it is strong, resting on real economics of scale and on the serious idea that more people means more innovators. Chapters 3 and 4 trace where the assumption comes from and how it operates in ordinary speech and policy. Chapter 5 lays out what the evidence does and does not show, including the awkward-for-both-sides fact that several rich economies have raised per-capita output while their populations flattened or fell. Chapters 6 through 8 examine hidden costs, who bears the pressure, and the best objections to my own argument. Chapter 9 offers concrete cases; Chapter 10 maps the adjacent biases; Chapter 11 asks what clear reasoning looks like once the bias is named; Chapter 12 sums up without triumph. Throughout, the aim is not to argue that a smaller population is good, a separate question, but to dissolve the false necessity that says prosperity needs an ever-growing one. The distinction between dissolving a necessity and asserting its opposite matters, and I will hold to it: to show that an obligation does not follow is not to recommend the contrary course. It is only to hand a real choice back to the people whose choice it is. ## 1. Naming the bias The bias this volume examines is the tendency to treat aggregate economic growth as dependent on continuous population growth, and from there to treat childbearing as an economic obligation whose neglect impoverishes everyone. Stated as a slogan: growth needs babies. Stated as the error it contains: the size of an economy is confused with the welfare of the people inside it. Let me sharpen this in stages, because the imprecision is where the persuasive force hides. A bias of this kind survives not by being asserted clearly and defended, but by remaining slightly out of focus, so that the listener assembles a valid-seeming argument out of parts that would not withstand inspection if laid side by side. Naming it precisely is therefore already half of answering it. First, the object. Gross domestic product is the total market value of goods and services a country produces in a year. It is a measure of scale. If you add ten million more people to a country and they work and consume, total GDP almost certainly rises: more hands, more output, more transactions to tally. This tells you the economy got bigger. It does not, by itself, tell you that anyone got better off. The intuitive measure of "better off" is GDP per capita: total output divided by the number of people. And per-capita GDP can move in the opposite direction from total GDP. Add people who produce less than the existing average, and total GDP climbs while the average falls. Population growth is therefore neither a sufficient nor a necessary condition for rising living standards. It changes the numerator and the denominator at once, and the bias consists in watching only the numerator. The mathematics here is not subtle; it is the mathematics of a fraction. Anyone who has divided a bill among a table of diners understands that a larger total shared among more people can leave each person with less. The bias asks us to forget, in the case of a nation, what we grasp instantly at dinner. Second, the inference. Even granting that the pace of total GDP growth is what commentators track, the leap from "total output would grow more slowly with fewer people" to "we would be poorer" is unearned. Slower growth of a bigger pie is not the same as smaller slices. What individuals experience is roughly their slice: their income, their access to goods and services, their real standard of living. A country whose population stops growing but whose productivity keeps rising delivers larger slices from a pie that grows more slowly. The GDP assumption obscures this by keeping the conversation at the level of the whole pie. It trades on a genuine ambiguity in the word "poorer": poorer can mean "commanding fewer real resources per person," which is what the ordinary listener fears, or it can mean "belonging to an economy whose aggregate is smaller than it would otherwise have been," which is a statement about a nation's rank in a table and need not touch any individual's life at all. The argument borrows the alarm of the first meaning to lend weight to a claim that, at most, establishes the second. Third, the moral payload. The bias does not stay descriptive. Because total growth is framed as a collective good, a rising national fortune, the failure to reproduce becomes a failure to contribute to that fortune. Non-parents, and especially the deliberately childfree, are recast as free-riders on a growth engine they decline to feed. This is the pronatalist conscription at the heart of the assumption: your reproductive choices are reinterpreted as inputs to a macroeconomic aggregate, and the aggregate's imperative to grow is passed down to you as a duty. The descriptive claim and the moral claim are always running together in the same sentence, which is part of what makes the bias hard to catch: a listener who accepts the arithmetic feels they have thereby accepted the obligation, when in fact the obligation was never in the arithmetic. It was added, silently, at the point where "output would be larger" was allowed to become "you ought to help make it larger.

① In brief

There is a sentence treated as settled arithmetic across the wealthy world: if birth rates keep falling, the economy will shrink, and we will all be poorer. The stance is deliberately narrow and honest. Population decline is not costless: labour markets adjust painfully, expansion-built sectors contract, particular regions hollow out. Those strains are real, and the pension argument (Book 053) and the future-labour-supply frame (Book 059) treat them directly. The bias lies elsewhere: in equating human wellbeing with aggregate growth and then treating babies as its necessary fuel, so that the case for childbearing arrives smuggled in as a corollary of a national accounting identity.

The book gives the strongest pronatalist version its full due first. That case is strong: aggregate scale is a real good (only large economies fund frontier research, defense, deep markets); and the "more people, more ideas" argument, that minds are non-rival idea-generators, that each person is potentially "the ultimate resource," and that ideas may be getting harder to find, is a serious long-run concern about the whole species. The critique concedes these, then shows what they fail to license: the conscription of individual reproductive choice. Ideas cross borders, so no nation's own birth rate is needed for its citizens to enjoy global innovation; and "the transition is painful" is an argument for managing decline well, not for raising the birth rate.

The evidence dissolves the necessity without denying the frictions. Population growth is neither necessary nor sufficient for rising living standards. Several rich economies, Japan the standard example, raised per-capita and per-worker output while population flattened or fell; the alarming aggregate headlines largely reflected a shrinking denominator, not falling living standards. Conversely, many fast-growing populations saw per-capita income stagnate. And GDP is a flawed welfare measure even on its own terms, as Kuznets warned at its creation and as the Stiglitz–Sen–Fitoussi report argued at length.

The book traces the assumption's origins (a wartime mobilization metric that escaped its purpose; the postwar coincidence of baby boom and long boom; an ancient equation of numbers with strength), maps how it operates today (alarm-framing headlines, the default of the aggregate, growth-assuming projection models, and the dinner-table script that converts a macro aggregate into a personal debt), and names its hidden costs (the suppressed per-capita truth, the instrumentalization of children, the unpriced ecological ledger, the autonomy tax, the foreclosure of alternatives). It asks who bears the pressure (disproportionately women, the economically precarious, and members of favoured national groups) and offers the immigration test as a diagnostic for whether a "growth" argument is really economic at all.

The takeaway is measured, not triumphant. This is not a claim that a smaller population is good, nor that decline is easy. It is that once the aggregate is disentangled from the average, and the average from the wider question of what makes a life go well, the economic necessity drains out of the case for having children. What is left is not an obligation but a choice, handed back, unconscripted, to the person whose choice it actually is.

CH 2The strongest case for it (steelman)The strongest case against this book

The honest way to argue against a position is to first make it as strong as its ablest defenders would. The pronatalist growth argument is not a straw man of confused columnists; in its best form it draws on serious economics, and I want to state that form fully and sympathetically before I contest it.

CH 3Where it comes from

Beliefs this reflexive rarely spring from a single source. The GDP assumption is a confluence: of a young measurement, an economic epoch, and much older instincts about numbers and strength. To see it as a confluence rather than a discovery is already to loosen its grip, because a belief assembled from historical accidents is a belief that could have been assembled differently.

CH 4How it operates today

The assumption rarely announces itself as a thesis to be defended. It works through the plumbing of ordinary discourse: through headlines, defaults, models, and metaphors that make its conclusion feel like the natural shape of the world. This is what makes it a bias rather than merely a bad argument. A bad argument can be met and defeated in the open; a bias operates below the level at which arguments are examined, shaping which conclusions feel like common sense before any argument is offered. To answer it one must first drag it up into the light, and that means noticing the machinery.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

What do the data actually show about the link between population growth, total GDP, and the thing we care about, living standards? The honest answer is layered, and each layer weakens the necessity the bias asserts while conceding the frictions it points at. An evidence-first stance is uncomfortable for partisans of every kind, because the record refuses to deliver a clean verdict for either the alarmists or their critics. What it delivers instead is a dissolution of the alleged necessity, alongside a confirmation of some genuine costs, and the discipline is to report both without letting either swallow the other.

CH 6The hidden costs

Every framing has an economy of attention: what it foregrounds and what it lets slip into the dark. The GDP assumption foregrounds the aggregate line and, in doing so, hides a set of costs that never make it into the argument. These are not costs the assumption weighs and dismisses; they are costs its very vocabulary is unequipped to register, which is a more insidious kind of omission. A framework that could see a cost and reject it can at least be argued with; a framework that cannot see a cost at all forecloses the argument by controlling what counts as relevant.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

An assumption that converts reproduction into economic duty does not press equally on everyone. Its weight is distributed, and tracing the distribution reveals whose freedom is most at stake. A framing that spoke only of "the population" or "the birth rate" would suggest the burden falls on some abstract collective, evenly, weightlessly. It does not. It falls on particular bodies, particular purses, and particular reputations, and the abstraction is part of how the unevenness is concealed.

I have argued that "growth needs babies" mistakes aggregate size for welfare. Fairness requires that I now turn the same scrutiny on my own thesis and present the strongest objections to it. CH 10Adjacent biases

This bias does not stand alone. It interlocks with several others in the series, and seeing the connections keeps each argument in its lane while showing how they reinforce one another into a seemingly unanswerable whole. The interlocking is not incidental. It is the mechanism by which the whole family of pronatalist economic arguments achieves its apparent invincibility: each individual claim is modest and defeasible, but they are rarely presented individually, and the bundle borrows strength from the very fact that no single rebuttal can dispatch all of it at once.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

What does clear-eyed reasoning about population and prosperity look like once this bias is named? Not the opposite dogma, not "decline is good" or "growth is bad," but a set of disciplines, for individuals and for institutions, that keep the argument honest. The disciplines are unglamorous and mostly consist of asking, at the right moment, a question the framing was built to suppress. That is the nature of a bias: it is undone less by a rival slogan than by a habit of noticing.

GDP (gross domestic product)
The total market value of goods and services produced within a country in a given period; a measure of the aggregate size of an economy, not of welfare.
Total GDP
The aggregate output figure; can rise simply by adding people or transactions, independent of whether individuals are better off.
GDP per capita
Total GDP divided by population; the standard rough proxy for average living standards, and the figure that can move opposite to total GDP when population changes.
The GDP assumption
The belief examined in this book: that aggregate economic growth requires continuous population growth, so that falling birth rates threaten prosperity and childbearing becomes an economic duty.
Total-versus-per-capita confusion
The central error of the bias: treating the aggregate size of an economy as if it were the measure of the wellbeing of the people inside it.
Growth imperative
The ideological treatment of aggregate economic growth as the master goal of policy, naturalized until it seems a law rather than a choice about what to measure and pursue.
Non-rival good
A good, paradigmatically an idea, that can be used by many people at once without being used up; the basis of the innovation argument for population.
Increasing returns to population (ideas argument)
The view, associated with Romer and Simon, that more people means more potential innovators and wider use of non-rival ideas, so population drives per-capita progress, not just aggregate output.
"Ideas are getting harder to find"
The finding that sustaining a constant rate of technological progress has required steadily more researchers; a sharpening of the long-run innovation concern.
Steady-state / ecological economics
The tradition, associated with Herman Daly, treating the economy as a subsystem of a finite biosphere and questioning the possibility and desirability of indefinite quantitative growth.
Adjustment costs
The real, unevenly distributed frictions of population decline: strained services with high fixed costs, falling regional asset values, contracting expansion-dependent sectors.
Demographic-alarm framing
The default vocabulary ("time bomb," "collapse," "birth dearth") that pre-loads falling birth rates with economic threat before any argument is made.
Immigration test
The diagnostic that a polity genuinely worried about its economic aggregate has a fast remedy in working-age migration; refusing it while demanding domestic births suggests a non-economic core.
Instrumentalization of children
The moral cost of justifying childbearing as an input to growth: conceiving of children as means to a macroeconomic end set before their birth.
Dependency ratio
The ratio of non-working (young and old) to working-age people; central to the pension argument (Book 053) and often blurred into the GDP assumption.
Productivity
Output per worker or per hour; the lever that can raise both total and per-capita GDP without population growth, and thus the main substitute for headcount in the growth accounting identity.
Stiglitz–Sen–Fitoussi report
The 2009 report on measuring economic performance and social progress arguing that aggregate market output systematically misses what constitutes wellbeing.
