The Consent Argument

No one can agree to be born. What follows from that, and what doesn't.

"Procreators do not, indeed cannot, secure the consent of the person whose existence is at stake — because that person does not yet exist to be asked."

— a standard framing of the consent problem

Set the asymmetry aside for a moment. There is a second antinatalist argument that needs no special theory of value at all — only a principle most of us already live by. The principle is this: we should not impose serious burdens on another person without their consent. We ask before we operate, before we enroll someone in a trial, before we sign them up for a risk. Where asking is impossible, we tread carefully and only for the person's clear benefit. Birth, the argument runs, is the one momentous thing we do to a person while it remains flatly impossible to ask them — and that should give us pause.

The structure of consent

Consent does real moral work. It can turn an act that would otherwise be a violation into something permissible, even welcome: a surgeon's incision, a tackle in a rugby match, a loan that puts you in debt. What makes these acceptable is not that they are painless but that the person agreed to bear them. Take the agreement away and the same acts become assault, battery, and extortion. Consent is, in this sense, the hinge on which much of our ethics swings.

Now consider how procreation sits against this picture. Creating a person imposes on them the entire weight of a human life — its labors, its illnesses, its losses, its eventual death — and it does so before the person exists to be consulted. This is not a case where consent was merely overlooked or hard to obtain. It is a case where consent is structurally impossible, because there is no prior subject whose agreement could even be sought. Birth is, in this precise way, the paradigm of an act done to someone with no possibility of their agreement.

Consent — a competent person's informed agreement to have something done to them or their interests. It is what often distinguishes a permissible imposition (surgery) from a wrongful one (assault). Antinatalists note that procreation can never satisfy it, because the affected party does not yet exist.

Shiffrin's gold coins

The philosopher Seana Shiffrin sharpened this worry in a careful 1999 essay, "Wrongful Life, Procreative Responsibility, and the Significance of Harm." She offers an analogy worth telling plainly.

Imagine a wealthy man who wants to share his fortune. He flies over an isolated island and drops cubes of gold bullion — say five million dollars' worth — onto the properties of the people below, so that each can simply collect the windfall. There is no way to ask them first; the island is unreachable and the residents cannot be contacted. One man, call him Wealth, finds a gold cube has landed on him. He is now five million dollars richer. But the falling cube has also broken his arm.

Has the rich man wronged Wealth? Most of us feel a tug toward "yes," or at least "it's complicated" — even though Wealth ends up, on balance, far better off. The benefit is enormous and the injury is minor, yet something about imposing a serious, uninvited harm on someone for a benefit they never asked for sits uneasily, even when the math clearly favors them. Shiffrin's point is that being made better off, against the backdrop of never having been consulted, does not automatically clear the imposer of responsibility for the harm they caused along the way.

Birth, the argument suggests, is structurally like this. Existence may bring great goods — the gold — but it always comes bundled with real harms — the broken arm — imposed on someone who could not be asked and who, before the imposition, lacked nothing. It is worth stressing that Shiffrin herself is cautious. She does not conclude that procreation is always wrong; she argues that creating a life involves imposing significant unconsented burdens, which calls for a kind of justification we too rarely notice we owe.

Being made better off, by someone you could not refuse, does not by itself answer for the harm done in the making.

"Could not be asked" is not "would have agreed"

A crucial distinction now comes into view. There is a difference between saying that a future person could not be asked and saying that they would have agreed if they could. The first is plainly true. The second is the move most defenders of procreation want to make — and it is where the argument gets interesting.

In plain words: "I couldn't ask you" is a fact about the situation. "You'd have said yes anyway" is a guess I make on your behalf — after I've already acted. Antinatalists think the second is doing more work than it can honestly bear.

The hypothetical-consent reply — fairly stated

Objection

Real consent was impossible, true — but we can rely on hypothetical or dispositional consent. Most people, once they exist, are glad to be alive and would, if we could rewind time, endorse the decision to create them. We make consent-substituting judgments like this all the time: doctors treat the unconscious on the reasonable assumption that the patient would agree. Why should birth be different? If the person you create will almost certainly ratify their own existence, imposing it on them is not a violation but a reasonable bet on their behalf.

Reply

The antinatalist grants the appeal but finds the analogy strained. With the unconscious patient there is a real, prior person with settled values we can consult — the consent we infer is grounded in their existing preferences. With procreation there is no prior person at all; the "consent" is not inferred from anyone's values but constructed by us, after the fact, to license what we have already done. It is suspiciously convenient: we create the person, and then the very person we created supplies the agreement that excuses the creating. Worse, it is selectively applied — we count the glad survivors but cannot poll those whose lives went badly enough that they would not ratify being born, and some of them are unmistakably real. Hypothetical consent, the antinatalist concludes, is at best a courteous fiction and at worst a way of asking the victim's permission only after the deed is safely done.

A deeper objection: the wrong framework

Objection

Consent is simply the wrong tool here. Consent protects an existing person from impositions on their already-standing interests. Before conception there is no such person and so no one to wrong, no interests to override, no will to bypass. We rightly make countless unconsented decisions that shape future people — passing laws, building cities, having children who will inherit the world we leave. To demand consent from those who do not yet exist is to misapply a principle outside its home.

Reply

The antinatalist accepts that there is no prior person to wrong, but denies this rescues procreation. The harm of birth is not felt before existence; it is felt by the very person we bring about, once they exist, in a life we chose for them without any possibility of their input. That a wrong has no victim until the act completes does not make it costless — it makes it a peculiar wrong that creates its own sufferer. And the comparison to lawmaking cuts the other way: precisely because we cannot consult future people, we are expected to be cautious about the burdens we hand them, not cavalier. The impossibility of consent is, on this reading, not a loophole that excuses imposition but a reason for restraint.

None of this proves the antinatalist conclusion by itself. The consent argument is best understood as raising the bar: if you cannot ask, and you are imposing real and unrefundable burdens, the case for proceeding had better be strong, and "they'll probably thank me" may not be strong enough.

Chapter in brief

  • Consent often distinguishes a permissible imposition (surgery) from a wrongful one (assault); birth is the paradigm act done to someone who cannot possibly be asked.
  • Shiffrin's gold-coins analogy shows that conferring a large benefit while imposing an uninvited harm does not automatically clear the imposer — though Shiffrin herself stops short of blanket antinatalism.
  • "Could not be asked" is not the same as "would have agreed"; the latter is a judgment we construct after acting.
  • The hypothetical-consent reply works for prior persons (the unconscious patient) but is weaker where the agreeing party is created by the very act it is meant to excuse.
  • Even granting that there is no prior person to wrong, the harm lands on the person we create — so the impossibility of consent argues for restraint, not permission.
