PROJECT 025

Benatar Argument Explainer

Philosophy · Difficulty: Advanced · ~20 min · interactive

David Benatar's asymmetry argument (2006) is the most discussed case for antinatalism. This page lays it out fairly — the famous four-cell matrix, the premises you can test for yourself, and the strongest objections — without telling you what to conclude. The aim is to understand the argument well enough to defend it or refute it.

① The matrix② Test the premises③ The objections④ Quick check⑤ Reflection

① The asymmetry matrix

The argument compares two scenarios: a person who exists (Scenario A) and the same person never existing (Scenario B). Benatar claims the four cells are not symmetrical. Tap each cell to see how it's meant to read.

The whole argument lives in the bottom two cells: a missing harm counts as good, but a missing pleasure counts as not bad (because no one is deprived). If that holds, never existing is better than existing — for everyone.

② Test the premises

Mark each premise accept or reject. The conclusion updates to show where you land. No toggle is "correct".

③ The strongest objections

Each objection is stated at full strength, then met with a reply. The debate is real and unresolved.

Key takeaway

The asymmetry is elegant and genuinely hard to dismiss — and genuinely hard to fully accept. Most of the debate turns on a single claim: that the absence of a pleasure costs nothing when there is no one to miss it. Decide what you think of that, and you'll know where you stand on the whole argument.
