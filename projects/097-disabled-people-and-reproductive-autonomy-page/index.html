PROJECT 097

Disabled People and Reproductive Autonomy

Ethics · Difficulty: Advanced · ~18 min · ethics page

Reproductive autonomy belongs to everyone — including disabled people, fully and without exception. That means freedom from coercion in any direction: the right to have children, with the support to do so, and the equal right to remain childfree — each by genuine choice, never imposed.

Disability-led: "Nothing about us without us." This page is written to affirm autonomy, not to speak over disabled people. It exists partly because, historically and still today, disabled people’s reproductive choices have too often been overridden by others. The whole point is that those choices are theirs.

② Coercion has two faces

Autonomy means freedom from pressure in both directions — not just one.

The principle is constant: the disabled person decides, with whatever information and support they choose. Others inform and support; they do not override.

Key takeaway

The history here is painful: disabled people’s reproductive lives have too often been decided for them. The corrective is simple and absolute — autonomy belongs to the person, full stop. That means the real support to parent if they choose, the freedom to remain childfree if they choose, and no coercion in either direction. Nothing about us without us.
