Chapter Seven · Part Three · 1,980 words

The Whole-Life Evaluation Challenge

T he non-identity problem asked whether a created person can be harmed at all. This last objection grants, for the sake of argument, that they can be, and attacks from the opposite flank. It accepts that creation imposes harm and denies that this ends the accounting. It is where the accounting begins. A life is not a heap of harms to be tallied in isolation. A life is a whole, and a whole can be good, good as a unified thing, good in a way the part-by-part bookkeeping of the asymmetry cannot capture and is not entitled to dismiss. If a created life is good as a whole, then the harms it contains are not free-floating debits. They are elements of a totality the person who lives it can affirm, and the bare fact that the totality holds some suffering condemns the creation no more than the bare fact that a symphony holds dissonance condemns the symphony. This is the whole-life evaluation challenge, and of all the objections in this volume it is the one that most directly contests the antinatalist's conclusion rather than the machinery behind it. It is also the one a thoughtful natalist is most likely to press from sincere conviction rather than philosophical maneuver.

The Challenge at Full Strength

The asymmetry, and indeed all three pillars, share a structural feature. They evaluate the goods and harms of existence componentially, weighing the presence and absence of pain and pleasure as separable items. Premise One assesses the pain. Premise Two assesses the pleasure. The comparison sets the columns side by side. The whole-life challenge contends that this componential method is the wrong method for the thing being evaluated, because the value of a life is not the sum of the values of its moments. A life has narrative structure. Projects pursued and completed. Relationships that deepen over decades. Suffering that is redeemed by what it makes possible. Meaning that arises precisely out of the overcoming of difficulty. The good of a hard-won achievement cannot be separated from the hardship. The good of a love cannot be separated from the risk and loss it courts. Evaluate such a life moment by moment, debiting each pain and crediting each pleasure, and you destroy the very structure its value consists in. You will have measured everything and weighed nothing.

And when a life is evaluated as a whole, the challenge continues, the verdict is often unmistakably positive. People do not merely report, defensively, that their lives come out net-positive. They affirm their lives as good wholes, narratively meaningful, worth the suffering they contained, not in spite of the structure but through it. If that affirmation is veridical, if some whole lives really are good as wholes, then the act that created such a life conferred something of real value, and the antinatalist's componential ledger, which can see the suffering only as an unredeemed debit, has missed the thing that matters most. The harm is real. The whole is good. And a real harm inside a good whole is not obviously a wrong to have brought about. This, the natalist says, is why almost everyone's considered judgment runs against the antinatalist conclusion, even among those who concede every premise. The premises evaluate parts. Lives are wholes.

The Source of the Challenge: Velleman on the Shape of a Life

The challenge has a precise philosophical source, and naming it shows how serious it is, because it does not come from the friends of procreation. It comes from the philosophy of well-being itself. J. David Velleman, in "Well- Being and Time," argues that the value of a life is not the sum of the momentary well-being enjoyed within it. Two lives can contain exactly the same quantity of momentary good and bad and yet differ in value, because they differ in shape. A life that begins in hardship and climbs to fulfilment tells a story of improvement; a life with the same moments in reverse order tells a story of decline; and the first is the better life, though the arithmetic of its moments is identical to the second. Velleman's explanation is that a life carries narrative value, an irreducible good belonging to the whole and not reducible to any sum of its parts, and that later events can change the value of earlier ones by redeeming them, giving a meaning to past suffering that it did not have while it was endured. The struggle becomes the first chapter of a triumph; the wasted years become, in retrospect, preparation. The meaning runs backward as well as forward.

If Velleman is right, the asymmetry has a real problem, and so do all three pillars, because all three evaluate componentially. They read a life as a column of pains and pleasures to be summed, and Velleman's argument is precisely that lifetime value is not such a sum. The suffering that the asymmetry enters as a fixed debit may, in the whole, be a redeemed thing, an element without which the life's narrative good would not exist, the way the dissonance is not a flaw in the symphony but a condition of its resolution. This is the strongest possible form of the whole-life challenge, and it is owed to a philosopher of well-being writing with no stake in procreation at all, which is exactly why it cannot be dismissed as motivated.

David DeGrazia turns this consideration directly against the antinatalist inference. Granting that creation imposes harm, he denies that the harm settles the moral question, because a created life that is a good narrative whole is benefit-conferring in a way the bare ledger of harms cannot capture, and the step from "creation harms" to "creation is impermissible" therefore fails. His is the most careful natalist deployment of the whole-life view, and it is the refusal that the final chapter concedes a reasonable person may make. The replies that follow do not pretend to dislodge Velleman's account of well- being. They grant it and ask what, even granting it, the challenge actually establishes.

The Three Replies

The antinatalist has three replies, and their quality is uneven. Grading them honestly is the work of the section.

The first reply returns to the comparison Chapter One made foundational, and it is the strongest. Grant entirely that a created life can be good as a whole. Grant the narrative structure, the redeemed suffering, the veridical affirmation. The antinatalist's claim was never that lives are bad wholes. It was that coming into existence is a harm relative to never having existed, and the goodness of the whole life does not touch that comparison, because the alternative was not a less good whole but no whole at all, and no whole at all holds no deprivation, by Premise Four. The good-whole life is good for the person who exists. But the person who exists did not need to be brought into existence in order to be spared the lack of that good, because before existence there was no one lacking it. On this reply the whole-life challenge changes the subject. It establishes that existence, once conferred, can be a good whole, which the antinatalist never denied, and leaves untouched the claim that conferring it imposed a harm answering no prior need. The reply is clean if the comparative framework of Part One holds. Its weakness is that it leans entirely on that framework, so a reader who found the symmetry objection persuasive finds this reply leaning on a cracked column.

The second reply is weaker and should be handled with care, because it shades toward the contempt the series forbids. It questions whether whole- life affirmations are veridical, whether the narrative goodness people report is real or a consoling story the mind tells to make an imposed existence bearable, the same adaptive optimism that surfaced in the retrospective-endorsement objection of Chapter Three. There is genuine psychology behind the worry, and it is not nothing. But pressed hard it convicts the contented of self- deception about the one thing they might be expected to know best, the worth of their own lives, and it does so selectively, doubting the affirmations that inconvenience the thesis while trusting the suffering that supports it. A defender may note the worry. A defender may not lean on it, because to lean on it is to tell people their meaning is a delusion, which is both cruel and, given that the antinatalist's own judgments issue from the same psychology, self-undermining.

The third reply concedes the most, and for that reason it is the most honest. It grants that for the person who exists, a good whole life may indeed be a good whole life, narrative structure and all, and relocates the antinatalist claim entirely to the standpoint of the decision to create. From the prospective standpoint, before any person exists, the creator is choosing whether to impose a condition whose value is uncertain. It might be a good whole. It might be a life of unredeemed suffering. The creator cannot know which, on a being who has no need of the gamble's upside and a real exposure to its downside. On this reply the whole-life challenge is true retrospectively and beside the point prospectively. That some created lives turn out to be good wholes does not justify imposing the gamble on every created being, any more than the fact that some imposed risks turn out well justifies imposing risks without consent. This reply gives up the strong claim that existence is always and unconditionally a harm and keeps the more defensible claim that creating is the unjustified imposition of an unconsented gamble. It quietly hands the weight back to the consent argument of Chapter Four. It is the most honest reply because it concedes what the whole-life challenge gets right and preserves only what survives the concession.

What This Chapter Leaves Open

The whole-life evaluation challenge is, in this volume's assessment, the most resilient objection to the antinatalist conclusion, and the chapter will not dress its replies as stronger than they are. The first reply is clean but borrows the comparative framework of Part One and falls if that framework falls. The second reply is psychologically real but morally hazardous and must not be the one bearing the load. The third reply is the most honest and the most costly. It concedes that existence may be a good whole for the one who lives it and retreats to the claim that creating is an unjustified imposition of an uncertain gamble, a retreat that strengthens the position's honesty while narrowing its ambition, and that leans the weight onto the consent pillar rather than the asymmetry.

What the challenge finally reveals is the lesson the non-identity problem taught from the other side. The strong, unconditional claim, that every existence is, full stop, a harm, is the version most exposed, because the whole- life challenge has real force against it and the most honest reply gives ground to it. The modest claim, that creating a person imposes, without their consent and against no prior need, a serious and uncertain condition, and that this imposition is what stands unjustified, is the version that survives, because it does not need to deny that the imposed condition sometimes turns out to be a good whole. It needs only that the imposition was not the creator's to make. A reader who has come this far should notice that the volume has been trading altitude for solidity, chapter by chapter. The grandest formulations belong to Part One and draw the heaviest fire. The claim left standing at the end is quieter, narrower, and far harder to dislodge. That trade is not a retreat from the position. It is the position learning where it actually stands, which is the only knowledge worth building nine more books on, and the explicit subject of the chapter that closes this volume.
