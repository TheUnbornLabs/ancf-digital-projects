PROJECT 024

Philosophy Timeline

Learning Tools · Difficulty: Intermediate

A study timeline of real thinkers and works often discussed around birth ethics and childfree thought. Filter by era, and expand any entry for a little more. Dates are approximate and educational — always read the originals to judge the ideas yourself.

Filter by era

  • 5th c. BCE — Greek tragedy
    Sophocles' Oedipus at Colonus contains the famous line that it is best "never to have been born at all."
    More

    The sentiment recurs across ancient Greek poetry and reflects an early, pre-philosophical version of the question antinatalism formalises.

  • ~3rd c. BCE — Hegesias of Cyrene
    A Cyrenaic philosopher who argued happiness is largely unattainable, earning the nickname "the death-persuader".
    More

    Also relevant: Buddhist analysis of dukkha (suffering/unsatisfactoriness) as intrinsic to existence — though Buddhism's response differs sharply from antinatalism.

  • 1819 — Arthur Schopenhauer
    The World as Will and Representation develops a systematic philosophical pessimism about the balance of suffering in life.
    More

    Schopenhauer is the most influential 19th-century source for later pessimist and antinatalist thinking.

  • 1933 — Peter Wessel Zapffe
    "The Last Messiah" frames human consciousness as over-developed for the world it faces, an early existential antinatalism.
    More

    Zapffe's essay later influenced eco-philosophy and writers like Thomas Ligotti.

  • 1973 — Emil Cioran
    The Trouble with Being Born — aphoristic reflections circling the value of having come into existence.
    More

    Cioran is literary rather than systematic, but widely read within these conversations.

  • 1979 — Bioethics & autonomy
    Beauchamp & Childress's Principles of Biomedical Ethics helps make consent and autonomy central formal concepts.
    More

    This formalised the language of autonomy that reproductive-rights debates still use today.

  • 2006 — David Benatar
    Better Never to Have Been sets out the modern asymmetry argument and names the contemporary debate.
    More

    This is the single most cited academic work in current antinatalist discussion.

  • 2010s — Online communities
    Childfree and antinatalist discussion grows rapidly on the internet, alongside books like Blackstone's Childfree by Choice (2019).
    More

    The conversation broadened from academic philosophy into lived experience and community.

  • Today — Reproductive autonomy
    Debates continue across ethics, law, environment, and personal life — in many directions at once.
    More

    The throughline remains the same: who decides, on what grounds, and for whom.
