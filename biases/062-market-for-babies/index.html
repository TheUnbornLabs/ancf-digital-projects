BIAS 062

A Market for Babies: Corporate Interest in More Consumers

Part V · Economic, Demographic & Policy Pronatalism · Corporate Interest in More Consumers

The bias: Commercial stakes in a growing population of buyers.

"The consumer is the most important person in the economy." — attributed widely in twentieth-century marketing literature; the sentiment, if not the exact phrasing, is a fair summary of an entire discipline.

① In brief

Book 062 · 100 Biases of Natalism

Every person is, among many other things, a potential customer. Businesses that sell things therefore have a plain structural interest in there being more lives to sell to: more people means more households, more purchasing occasions, more markets. This book examines what happens when that entirely ordinary interest, trivial at the level of any single firm, aggregates into a diffuse cultural pressure to reproduce. It calls the result commercial pronatalism, or the market-for-babies bias: the systemic tendency of consumer capitalism to treat a growing population as a growing market, and thereby to reinforce the assumption that having children is the normal, expected, and rewarding path.

The book is careful about what it does and does not claim. It is not a conspiracy theory. No boardroom votes to raise the birth rate, and the mechanism is structural and incentive-based, not directed. It does not claim that marketing makes people have children; on the contrary, the book treats as decisive the fact that fertility has fallen, often below replacement, across exactly the societies most saturated with family marketing. And it does not condemn serving families, which is honorable work that has helped clothe, feed, and protect most of the world's children. The claim is narrower: that the commercial interest in more consumers is one real, under-examined input into the ambient pronatalism of modern life, and that naming it lets us reason more clearly.

Read the rest of the overview

Following the series template, the book opens by naming the bias precisely and distinguishing it from its neighbors: the macroeconomic growth assumption (Book 054, people as drivers of GDP), the future-workers frame (Book 059, people as producers), advertising (Book 067, the delivery machinery), and the fertility industry (Book 098, which sells the means of conception). This book's lane is the demand side: babies and children as future and present consumers, and the "baby economy" built directly around them.

Chapter 2 steelmans the other side first and fully: families are genuinely served, profit is legitimate, advertising informs as well as persuades, and, most powerfully, commercial pronatalism has conspicuously failed to raise birth rates, making it at most a very weak force. The following chapters trace the bias's roots (the invention of the consumer, Zelizer's "priceless child," and the growth imperative), its living mechanisms (ambient normalization, life-stage marketing, the manufacture of parental anxiety, the baby-product ecology, and cultural seepage), and the honest state of the evidence (solid on the structural interest and the baby economy; plausible but unmeasurable on the cultural effect; firmly negative on any effect on fertility).

The hidden costs, the book argues, are not extra babies but a distorted cultural background: an over-affirmed, over-provisioned, under-costed image of the family path; a commercially thin image of every alternative; a commodification of care that recasts love as provision; a ratchet of expensive, anxious parenting; and an unpriced ecological externality. These press hardest on women, on financially squeezed families, and on inexperienced new parents. The book then turns its critique on itself, engaging the strongest objections, including that it has named a force that demonstrably does nothing, and grants that the thesis must remain modest.

Four case studies ground the argument: the retailer pregnancy-prediction machine, the harmful history of infant-formula marketing and the 1981 WHO code, the market in monetized parental anxiety, and the commercial creation of family milestones. The closing chapters ask what clear-eyed, non-coercive reasoning looks like once the bias is named: separating affirmation from argument, love from provision, and honestly costing a choice the market prefers to celebrate. The takeaway is measured and non-coercive: not fewer children, but a choice handed back to the chooser, a little cleaner than the market left it.

② The book in twelve chapters

Each chapter opens with its own summary. Note chapter 2: every volume states the strongest case for the thing it criticises before laying a finger on it — and chapter 8 turns the critique back on itself.

CH 1Naming the bias

The bias this book examines can be stated in a sentence: consumer capitalism has a structural interest in a growing population, because more people means a larger market, and this interest quietly reinforces the cultural assumption that people should have children. Call it commercial pronatalism, or the market-for-babies bias.

CH 2The strongest case for it (steelman)The strongest case against this book

Before criticizing commercial pronatalism, I owe the other side its best case, stated sympathetically, in full, and without the sneer that too often stands in for argument. Here it is.

CH 3Where it comes from

Commercial pronatalism is a young bias with old roots. Its raw material, the human tendency to invest heavily in children, is ancient, but its specific modern form required three developments: the invention of the consumer, the invention of childhood as a market, and the marriage of growth-dependent capitalism to a rising population. Each deserves a moment.

CH 4How it operates today

If the previous chapter explained the bias's roots, this one traces its living mechanisms, the concrete channels through which commercial pronatalism reinforces the cultural default of childbearing. These are not sinister plots; they are ordinary business practices whose aggregate effect is a pronatalist atmosphere. Five mechanisms stand out.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

Here the book must be scrupulously honest, because the evidence is genuinely mixed and the temptation to overclaim is strong. What can actually be established about commercial pronatalism, and what cannot?

CH 6The hidden costs

If commercial pronatalism barely moves birth rates, where is the harm? The costs are real but subtle, and they fall less on the fertility statistics than on the quality of the choice and the honesty of the culture around it. Several deserve naming.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

Biases are never distributed evenly, and commercial pronatalism presses hardest on some people. Mapping who bears its weight sharpens the critique and guards against the false universalism that treats "the consumer" as a person without a gender, a class, or a culture.

CH 8The counter-arguments to the critiqueObjections to the critique

Intellectual honesty requires turning the critique on itself. The strongest objections to this book's thesis deserve a fair hearing, not a straw-man's dismissal. Several are serious.

CH 9Case studies / illustrations

Abstract claims need concrete instances. Four illustrations show commercial pronatalism operating, each real, each honestly qualified, none proving more than it should.

CH 10Adjacent biases

Commercial pronatalism does not act alone; it interlocks with other biases in this series. Mapping the joints prevents this book from claiming territory that belongs to its neighbors, and shows how the biases reinforce one another into the thick rope of ambient pronatalism.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

What does clear-eyed, non-coerced reasoning about reproduction look like once commercial pronatalism is named? The aim is not to reject markets, resent products, or refuse the genuine goods that family commerce provides. It is to restore the reproductive choice to the chooser by making one more of the ambient forces visible. Several disciplines help, for individuals and for institutions.

CH 12Conclusion

This book has argued a narrow, carefully bounded thesis: that consumer capitalism has a structural interest in a growing population, because more people means a larger market, and that this interest quietly reinforces the cultural default of childbearing, chiefly by affirming, provisioning, and celebrating the family path while leaving its alternatives commercially thin. The bias is not a plot. No one is pulling a lever. It is a slope produced by a thousand independent, mostly legitimate business decisions, aggregated into an ambient tilt.

Show all 16 terms as a list
Commercial pronatalism
The structural tendency of consumer capitalism to treat a growing population as a growing market, thereby quietly reinforcing the cultural default of childbearing. The central bias of this book.
Market-for-babies bias
Informal name for commercial pronatalism, emphasizing the treatment of new people as new consumers.
Growth imperative
The structural condition under which competitive firms must grow to survive; one route to growth is an expanding customer base, which population growth supplies.
Demand-side pronatalism
The framing of children as future and present consumers (this book), as distinct from supply-side framings of children as producers, workers, or taxpayers (Book 059).
Baby economy
The industries built directly around infants and children: formula, diapers, toys, furniture, apparel, food, media, and services.
The priceless child
Historian Viviana Zelizer's term for the modern child, reconceived as economically "useless" but emotionally invaluable, and thereby an object of intense spending.
Life-stage marketing
Marketing organized around major life events (marriage, home-buying, childbirth) that trigger wholesale reorganizations of spending and unusual openness to new brands.
Manufacture of parental anxiety
The commercial incentive to sustain and monetize parents' fears through reassurance products, "expert" content, and safety and optimization goods.
Commodification of care
The recasting of caring-for a child as buying-for a child, whereby love is scripted as provision.
Ambient normalization
The cumulative effect of pervasive family-oriented imagery in making the family path the unmarked, expected, default life.
Under-furnished alternative
The relative commercial thinness of scripts, milestones, and goods around the childfree life, which can register as lack rather than as a valid life.
Intensive-parenting ratchet
The commercially-assisted upward drift in the standard of "adequate" parental provision, which raises the felt cost and stakes of raising a child.
Structural interest
An interest arising from a firm's position in the economy rather than from anyone's deliberate wish or plan; the non-conspiratorial basis of the bias.
Pregnancy-prediction machine
Shorthand for the analytics apparatus that detects new or expectant parents from purchasing patterns in order to target them early; emblem of the market's eager reception of a new consumer.
International Code of Marketing of Breast-milk Substitutes
The 1981 World Health Organization code adopted in response to harmful infant-formula marketing practices.
Salience versus outcome
The distinction, central to the book, between shifting the cultural salience and normality of parenthood (which the bias may do) and shifting the aggregate birth rate (which it does not).

⑥ Sources

What this volume draws on. Where the evidence is thin or contested, the book says so rather than reaching for a number.

  • Viviana A. Zelizer, Pricing the Priceless Child: The Changing Social Value of Children (New York: Basic Books, 1985). The foundational social history of the modern child's shift from economically useful to emotionally priceless, and the resulting reorganization of childhood around spending.
  • World Health Organization, International Code of Marketing of Breast-milk Substitutes (Geneva: WHO, 1981). The official international code adopted in response to infant-formula marketing practices that discouraged breastfeeding.
  • David Benatar, Better Never to Have Been: The Harm of Coming into Existence (Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2006). The canonical modern antinatalist argument; background to the series rather than to this specific bias.
  • United Nations, Department of Economic and Social Affairs, Population Division, World Population Prospects (recurring revisions). Standard source for headline global and national fertility data, including sub-replacement fertility across most advanced economies. The revisions are updated regularly, so consult the current one.
Directions the volume points to (not pinned citations)
  • Juliet B. Schor, Born to Buy: The Commercialized Child and the New Consumer Culture (Scribner, 2004). On the marketing of childhood and children as consumers.
  • Susan Linn, Consuming Kids: The Hostile Takeover of Childhood (2004). On child-directed marketing and its effects.
  • Charles Duhigg, The Power of Habit: Why We Do What We Do in Life and Business (Random House, 2012). The source of the widely retold account of a large retailer's pregnancy-prediction modeling.
  • Gary Cross, Kids' Stuff: Toys and the Changing World of American Childhood (Harvard University Press, 1997). A history of the American children's toy market.
  • Ann Hulbert, Raising America: Experts, Parents, and a Century of Advice About Children (Knopf, 2003). On the market in parenting "expertise" and the cultivation of parental anxiety.
  • Sharon Hays, The Cultural Contradictions of Motherhood (Yale University Press, 1996). On the ideology of "intensive mothering" and its escalating standards.
  • The trade and market-research literature on the size of the global infant-formula, diaper, toy, and juvenile-products markets. Be warned that the headline dollar figures vary wildly between sources, depending almost entirely on how each one draws the boundary of the category.
