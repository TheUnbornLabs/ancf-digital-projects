PROJECT 008

Suffering-Risk Thought Experiment

Philosophy · Difficulty: Intermediate · ~22 min · reflection exercise

This is a reflection exercise, not a test or a forecast. It explores one of the hardest questions in birth ethics: when you decide for a future person who cannot choose, how much risk of suffering is acceptable? The simulator makes the trade-off visible instead of abstract — including a "veil of ignorance" test where the life on the table could be yours. No numbers here predict any real person's life; they are tools for thinking.

① The setup ② Risk simulator ③ Decision frames ④ Thought-experiment cards ⑤ Where's your line? ⑥ Quick check ⑦ Reflection

① The setup

The decision problem — to create a person is to make a one-time, irreversible choice on behalf of someone who cannot be consulted, staking their entire life on outcomes no one can guarantee. It is a decision under deep uncertainty, made for another party, with no chance to ask or to undo.

Every life carries some probability of going wonderfully, some of going terribly, and a wide spread in between. Optimists and antinatalists often agree on this; they disagree about how to decide under that uncertainty, and especially about how much weight the worst outcomes deserve when you're gambling with a life that isn't yours. The tools below don't resolve that — they make the structure of the gamble visible.

③ The frames philosophers use

There's no neutral way to "decide under risk" — the frame you pick shapes the answer. Tap each to see how it reads the same gamble differently.

④ Thought-experiment cards

Five short scenarios that isolate one feature of the problem. Read each, jot your gut response (saved on this device), then reveal the angle it's probing.

⑤ Where's your line?

Setting aside the simulator: in general, what chance of a seriously hard life feels acceptable to you, to justify creating a person who can't be asked? There is no correct number — just notice where your own line sits, and what it implies.

Key takeaway

There is no neutral arithmetic that settles this. The honest disagreement is about how to act under deep uncertainty for someone who can't consent — and especially how much the worst outcomes should weigh. Seeing the gamble laid out, and noticing which frame you reach for, is worth more than any single number. Whatever you conclude, you've looked at the structure of the question squarely.

If reflecting on suffering stirs up distress that's hard to carry, please consider reaching out to someone you trust or a qualified professional. You deserve support.
