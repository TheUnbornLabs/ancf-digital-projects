The Non-Identity Problem

T hree times now the argument has come to the same locked door. The asymmetry's comparative reading needed to compare existence with non-existence "for" a person and could not say for whom. The consent argument's reply to "proves too much" leaned on a line between benefiting a prior person and generating one. The priority-of-suffering pillar granted that its conclusion turns on how the goods and harms of a created life are weighed for the being created. Behind all three sits one problem, the deepest in the philosophy of procreation, and it does not belong to antinatalism. It is a problem for everyone who reasons about bringing people into being, natalist and antinatalist alike. Derek Parfit named it the non-identity problem, in Reasons and Persons in 1984, and a reader who grasps it will see why this whole field is genuinely hard rather than a matter of one side simply being confused.

This chapter does not solve the non-identity problem. No one has solved it to general satisfaction in the four decades since Parfit posed it, and a volume that claimed to dispatch it in twenty pages would be the exact overreach this series exists to refuse. What the chapter does is show what the problem is, why it threatens the antinatalist argument in particular, and what each available response costs. The honest conclusion comes early so it can frame the rest. The antinatalist position survives the non-identity problem about as well as any position in the field does, which is to say imperfectly, by taking a stance on contested ground that reasonable people contest in the other direction.

The Problem Itself

Most of our moral thinking is person-affecting. An act is wrong, on this natural view, because it makes some particular person worse off than they would otherwise have been. There is a victim, identifiable at least in principle, with a legitimate complaint: you made things worse for me. Harm is comparative and personal. It is being-worse-off-than-you-would-have-been, and it needs a "you."

Procreation breaks this framework, and it breaks it in a way that is easy to state and hard to escape. Suppose a person is brought into existence with a life that is difficult but, on the whole, worth living. More good than bad, a life they themselves endorse. Did the act of creating them harm them? On the person-affecting view, harm means making someone worse off than they would otherwise have been. So what is the alternative against which we measure? For this person, the only alternative is never having existed at all. And never having existed is not a state they would have been in, better or worse, because there would have been no them to be in any state. We cannot say creation made them worse off than non-existence, because non-existence is not a condition that they could have occupied and found worse. There is no one there to compare. So on a strict person-affecting view, creating a person with a life worth living does not harm them, because there is no one for whom the alternative was better. The created person, glad to exist, has no complaint of the form "you made things worse for me," since the only alternative for them was to be no one at all.

Feel the force of this, because it cuts straight at the antinatalist. The whole position holds that coming into existence is a harm. But harm, on the person- affecting view that most of our morality runs on, requires a victim made worse off. And the non-identity problem says the created person, the one with a decent life above all, was not made worse off, because the comparison the antinatalist needs, existence worse than non-existence for them, has no subject on its non-existence side. The antinatalist seems forced to say either that there is a harm with no one harmed, or that the comparison can be drawn for a being who does not exist. Both are exactly the moves Parts One and Two found contested. The non-identity problem is not a fresh objection. It is the name for the difficulty all three pillars kept running into.

Why It Belongs to Everyone: Parfit's Cases

It would distort the position to present the non-identity problem as a uniquely antinatalist embarrassment, because its original and most famous applications cut just as hard against ordinary natalist morality. Parfit did not pose the problem to attack procreation. He posed it in the course of a search for what he called Theory X: a principle for assessing outcomes that change who will exist, one that would deliver the right verdicts without paradox. He never found Theory X, and neither has anyone since. The cases that drove the search are worth setting out in full, because they fix the shape of the difficulty more precisely than any abstract statement, and because they show the problem belongs to everyone.

Start with the case that gave the problem its name in popular discussion, the fourteen-year-old girl. A girl of fourteen chooses to have a child. We judge that she should have waited several years, until she could give a child a far better start, and that, exceptional circumstances aside, her choice was wrong. Yet the child she has at fourteen is not the child she would have had at twenty-four. A different conception, a different egg, a different person entirely. The child who actually exists owes its existence to her not having waited. Had she waited, this child would never have been; some other child would have lived in its place. So this child, even with a harder start than a later child would have had, cannot say it was made worse off by her choice, because its only alternative was not a better life but no life at all. The person-affecting framework, which finds a wrong only where some person is made worse off, cannot locate the wrong here. Almost everyone is certain a wrong occurred. Saying in virtue of what is the whole problem.

Parfit's second case scales the difficulty up to whole societies. Imagine a choice between two long-range policies, Depletion and Conservation. Depletion raises quality of life slightly for the next two centuries, then lowers it sharply for everyone who lives after. Conservation gives a somewhat lower quality of life now, still well worth living, and a markedly higher one after two hundred years. Conservation looks like the better choice, and most people judge Depletion not merely worse but wrong. Now the twist that makes it a non-identity case. A policy carried out across centuries reshapes who meets whom, who is conceived when, which lives begin at all. The people alive three hundred years after Depletion are not the people who would have lived after Conservation. They are different people, who owe their very existence to Depletion having been chosen, and whose lives, though degraded, remain worth living. They cannot complain that Depletion made them worse off, because without it they would not have existed. Person-affecting morality, which condemns a choice only when it makes some existing or future person worse off, seems unable to condemn Depletion. And yet we are sure Depletion should be condemned.

Parfit's third case sharpens the knife to a clinical point. Imagine a health service can fund one of two programmes, but not both. The first screens prospective parents and advises some to postpone conceiving for a couple of months, because a temporary condition would otherwise leave their child with a serious but non-fatal disability. Postponing means a different, healthy child is conceived; the disabled child who would have existed simply never does. The second programme treats a condition in fetuses already conceived, curing the disability in the very child who will be born. Suppose each programme would prevent the same number of disabilities at the same cost. The second benefits an identifiable individual, this child, cured. The first benefits no one in particular, because the healthy child it yields is a different child, not the disabled one made well. If only person-affecting reasons count, the second programme looks more worth funding, since only it has identifiable beneficiaries. Yet almost everyone thinks the two are equally worth funding. Preventing a disability by determining which child is born seems no less valuable than preventing one by curing the child who comes. Once more the person-affecting framework returns a verdict we cannot accept.

Parfit drew the radical lesson, and it is worth stating in his own register. If person-affecting morality cannot condemn the fourteen-year-old, cannot fault Depletion, cannot rank the two medical programmes as equal, then person-affecting morality is incomplete. He concluded that wrongs need not have victims, that an act can be wrong without being worse for any particular person, and he spent the rest of his great book hunting for the principle that would deliver the right verdicts in these cases without absurdity. The hunt kept failing in a particular way. Every purely impersonal candidate, every principle that counted outcomes by their total or average welfare rather than by what they did to specific people, seemed to lead to the Repugnant Conclusion, on which a vast population of lives barely worth living comes out better than a small flourishing one. So the impersonal escape from the non-identity problem ran straight into a second paradox. Theory X had to thread between them, and Parfit could not find the thread. David Boonin, in the one book-length study devoted to the problem, frames it as a set of individually plausible claims that cannot all be true together, and surveys the ways out only to find each carrying a cost most of us would rather not pay. Four decades on, Theory X remains missing.

Now see what the three cases share, and what they share with the antinatalist's claim, because this is the reframing the whole chapter turns on. In each case an act that plainly calls for moral assessment falls on people whose existence and identity depend on the act itself, so that they cannot be said to be made worse off by it. The antinatalist's claim has exactly this form. Creating a person falls on someone whose existence depends on the act, who therefore cannot be said to be made worse off by it, since the only alternative was the non-existence in which they were no one at all. The antinatalist is not making a strange move that drops them into a paradox no one else faces. They are making the very move everyone makes who condemns the fourteen- year-old or faults Depletion, and they inherit the very same problem, no more and no less.

This matters enormously for a fair framing of the antinatalist position. The antinatalist is not uniquely stuck in a paradox while everyone else reasons cleanly. Everyone is in the paradox. The only question is which response to it one adopts, and whether the antinatalist's response is more or less costly than the natalist's. On that question the antinatalist has a respectable case. Not a victory, a respectable case, and the rest of the chapter lays it out.

Parfit's Cases, in Detail

The non-identity problem is one of those results that looks like a verbal trick until you see the cases worked in full, at which point it stops looking like a trick and starts looking like a wall. The cases deserve that full sight, because their detail is exactly what makes the problem impossible to wave away.

Start with the engine that drives all of them, what Parfit called the time- dependence of identity. If a particular person had not been conceived at the moment they were in fact conceived, that person would never have existed at all. A different sperm and a different egg, or the same pair a month later, produce a different person, not the same person under altered circumstances. You owe your existence to a particular conception at a particular instant, and almost any change to the conditions leading up to it, a delay, a different choice, a different policy, would have meant some other person in your place and none of you. This is not exotic metaphysics. It is the ordinary fact that you are this person and not the sibling your parents might have had instead. Grant it, and the non-identity problem follows on its own, because it means that most choices which seem to affect "future people" do not improve or worsen the lot of particular future people. They change which future people there are.

Parfit's first case fixes the point at the scale of a single family. A girl of fourteen chooses to have a child now. Because she is so young, the child's early years are marked by her inexperience and the instability of her circumstances; the child has a life clearly worth living, but a worse start than a child born to her later would have had. We want to say she should have waited, that she has done something she ought not to have done. But the child she has now would not have existed had she waited. Waiting does not give the same child a better start. It produces a different child entirely, from a different conception years later. So the child she actually has cannot complain of having been made worse off, because the only alternative for that child was never to exist. The person-affecting framework, which locates wrong in someone's being worse off than they would otherwise have been, can find no one here who is worse off, and therefore can find no wrong, even though almost everyone is sure a wrong has occurred.

The second case lifts the same structure to the scale of a civilization, and this is the version that should trouble anyone who cares about the future at all. Imagine a society choosing between two policies, Conservation and Depletion. Under Conservation, resources are husbanded and the standard of living is sustained across many generations. Under Depletion, the standard of living over the next century or two is slightly higher, after which it falls sharply and stays low for the far more numerous generations that follow. Almost everyone judges Conservation the better choice. But the two policies, pursued over centuries, would so reshape the conditions of life, who meets whom, who marries, who is conceived and when, that the people eventually living under Depletion are not the same people who would have lived under Conservation. They are different people, who owe their very existence to the Depletion policy having been chosen. However degraded their world, they are not worse off than they would otherwise have been, because the alternative for them was not a better life but no life. Strict person-affecting morality cannot condemn Depletion on their behalf, and may even have to favor it for the nearer generations it does benefit. Yet our conviction that Conservation is better does not waver. The problem is now a problem about whether we can have any coherent ethics of the far future at all.

David Boonin, in the major book-length treatment of the problem, makes its bite unmistakable with a case of his own and then does something most philosophers flinch from. Wilma wants a child. Her doctor tells her that if she conceives now, the child will be born blind, with a life diminished but clearly worth living; if she takes a pill daily for two months and conceives after, she will instead have a different, sighted child. Wilma declines to wait and conceives now. Her child, Pebbles, is born blind. Had she waited, a different child, Rocks, would have existed instead. Boonin lays out the argument as five plausible premises leading to one implausible conclusion: that Wilma's act of conceiving Pebbles rather than Rocks is not morally wrong. He then examines, with great care, every available way of rejecting one of the five premises, the move almost everyone wants to make, and argues that each rejection fails. His own conclusion is to accept the implausible conclusion. Wilma did nothing wrong. He bites the bullet the rest of the field is desperate to avoid.

Boonin's position matters here for a reason that cuts in two directions at once, and it is the cleanest illustration of why this problem belongs to everyone. His bullet-biting is, on its face, the natalist's most direct escape from the antinatalist. If acts in non-identity cases are simply not wrong, because they wrong no one who is made worse off, then procreation, the non-identity act par excellence, is not wrong either, and the antinatalist conclusion is blocked at the root. But look at what the escape costs the one who takes it. To say procreation is not wrong on these grounds is to say, in the same breath, that Wilma did nothing wrong in choosing her blind child over a sighted one, that the girl of fourteen did nothing wrong, that a society does nothing wrong in choosing Depletion. The natalist who reaches for Boonin's solution to block antinatalism must swallow every one of those verdicts, which offend ordinary moral conviction at least as deeply as antinatalism does. The antinatalist and the commonsense natalist are in the same boat after all. Each must either locate a wrong that has no victim, by going impersonal or going wide, or accept Boonin's bullet and give up the intuitions that made them want to convict Wilma in the first place. There is no third place to stand that everyone finds comfortable.

The depth of the problem is best measured by its own author. Parfit spent the last part of Reasons and Persons searching for what he called Theory X: a satisfactory account of beneficence that would explain our judgments in these cases, locate the wrong without requiring a victim, and avoid the Repugnant Conclusion that impersonal aggregation seems to invite. He did not find it, and said so. He came to think that personal identity matters far less to morality than we instinctively suppose, and leaned toward an impersonal accounting precisely because the person-affecting one kept failing here. The philosopher who posed the problem could not solve it on person-affecting terms, and judged the difficulty grave enough to threaten our hope of a complete moral theory. That is the order of difficulty the antinatalist is dealing with, and it is the same order of difficulty everyone else in the conversation is dealing with too.

The Three Routes, and What Each Costs

There are, broadly, three ways out of the non-identity problem, and the antinatalist can take any of them, each at a characteristic price.

The first is the impersonal route. Abandon the strict person-affecting requirement and allow that some acts can be wrong, or some outcomes worse, without being worse for a particular identifiable person. On this route, creating a person whose life holds avoidable suffering can be impersonally bad, bad because it adds suffering to the world, or instantiates a worse state of affairs, even if no person is made comparatively worse off. This is just the impersonal value invoked by Premise Three on its first reading, and it dissolves the non-identity problem cleanly. The harm of creation need not attach to a worse-off subject, because the badness is impersonal. The cost is exactly what it was in Chapter Two. It commits the antinatalist to impersonal value, which the person-affecting theorist rejects, and it must answer the charge that impersonal principles spawn their own paradoxes, the Repugnant Conclusion most famously, where a vast population of barely-worth-living lives is ranked above a small flourishing one. The antinatalist who takes this route trades the non-identity problem for the standard burdens of impersonalism. A real trade, not a free escape, but one many find worth making, since the rival natalist views have to shoulder impersonal commitments of their own to avoid endorsing the fourteen-year-old's choice.

The second is the wide person-affecting route. Keep a person-affecting framework but widen it. Count the welfare of whoever exists in either of the outcomes being compared, rather than insisting on a single person who exists in both. On this route, comparing "create this suffering-prone person" with "do not create," we may count the suffering of the person who exists in the first outcome as a reason against it, without needing them to exist in the second outcome to be "made worse off." This is the very principle Chapter Three named as the asymmetry's most serious rival, and here is the irony the honest defender has to face. The wide person-affecting principle, which threatened to replace the asymmetry as the explanation of the four sub- asymmetries, also offers the antinatalist a way through the non-identity problem. The antinatalist can simply adopt it. The cost is that the wide principle is delicate to state without either collapsing back into the impersonal view or generating the wrong verdicts in mirror cases, and that adopting it concedes the asymmetry was never the load-bearing structure; the wide principle was. For a defender who has accepted Chapter Three's verdict, that cost is already paid.

The third is the comparative-without-a-subject route. Insist, against the no-subject objection, that we can coherently judge non-existence better or worse "for" a merely possible person, that "it would have been better for her never to have existed" expresses a genuine comparison between two complete world-states, one holding her suffering and one not, even though she is absent from the second. This route keeps both the person-affecting flavor and the antinatalist conclusion, but it has to defend the intelligibility of better-for-a- non-existent-being, which is the no-subject objection of Chapter Three all over again. The cost is metaphysical. It asks us to accept a kind of comparison that strict person-affecting intuitions resist, and of the three it is the route on which reasonable philosophers most often dig in and refuse to follow.

The pattern across all three routes is the chapter's central finding. The antinatalist is not refuted by the non-identity problem, because each route offers a coherent stance that preserves the conclusion. But every route requires adopting a contested position on the metaphysics of harm and merely possible people, and on each the natalist can plant their feet and decline to walk. The non-identity problem does not decide the question. It raises the price of entry for every position, antinatalist and natalist alike, and it reveals that no one in this debate reasons from neutral ground.

The Proposed Solutions, and Why Some Cut the Wrong Way

The three routes above are the antinatalist's ways of living with the non- identity problem. The wider literature has tried to solve it, and the antinatalist has a stake in how those attempts fare, because some of the leading solutions, if they work, work against the antinatalist as much as for the natalist. This is a fact the position has to face squarely, and facing it is more instructive than any of the routes.

The most discussed solution is Caspar Hare's. He proposes that the talk of "her child" in a non-identity case is ambiguous between a de re and a de dicto reading. Read de re, "her child" names a particular person, and that person, who owes their existence to the act, is not made worse off. Read de dicto, "her child" names a role, whoever comes to fill it, and the role's occupant is made worse off when the worse-off child is created rather than the better-off one. On Hare's view we can locate a genuine victim in the de dicto sense, and so preserve the ordinary judgment that the fourteen-year-old does wrong, without abandoning person-affecting morality. If Hare is right, the antinatalist gains something, because the created person can be wronged de dicto, as the occupant of the role "the person created," even though no particular individual is made worse off than they would otherwise have been. The gain is real but contested; the de dicto victim is a strange kind of victim, and critics, Boonin among them, deny that de dicto worseness carries the moral weight Hare needs.

The second solution is the bluntest, and it is David Boonin's. Having surveyed every attempt to dissolve the non-identity problem and found each wanting, Boonin concludes that there is no problem to solve. There is only a valid argument, and we should accept its conclusion. On his view it is simply true that, in the central cases, the act wrongs no one, that creating the worse- off child rather than a different better-off one is not wrong in the person- affecting sense, and that our contrary intuition is the thing that should give way. This is biting the bullet, and it is a respectable thing to do when the alternatives all cost more. But notice where the bullet lodges for the antinatalist. If we accept, with Boonin, that bringing into existence a person whose life is worth living wrongs no one, because they are not made worse off than they would otherwise have been, then the antinatalist's central claim, that being created is a harm to the created person, is exactly what Boonin's bullet denies. The most thoroughgoing person-affecting response to the non- identity problem does not rescue the antinatalist. It cuts against them, because it concludes that non-identity creation harms no one, and creation is a non-identity case. The same is true of the dissolving move made by Velleman, on which the merely possible alternative person merits no concern, and which is offered precisely to make the non-identity worry disappear. A worry that disappears takes the antinatalist's comparative harm with it.

This is the honest and slightly uncomfortable finding the chapter has been building toward. The non-identity problem is not a difficulty the antinatalist can hope to see "solved" in their favor by the field, because the leading solutions are person-affecting, and person-affecting solutions tend to conclude that creating a worth-living life wrongs no one. The antinatalist's natural home is therefore not among the solutions but among the routes: the impersonal route, which denies that wrongs require victims, or the de dicto reading, which manufactures a victim of a contested kind. David Heyd, surveying the same ground, concludes that the problem is simply intractable, that no solution will command assent, and that we should learn to work around it. For the antinatalist that verdict is, oddly, the safest available, because an intractable problem refutes no one. What it does instead is exactly what this chapter has said throughout: it raises the price of every position and lets no one reason from neutral ground.

What This Chapter Leaves Open

The non-identity problem is the deepest difficulty in the field, and the volume will not pretend to have dispatched it. Four things can be said, and said with confidence.

It is not a uniquely antinatalist problem. It scandalizes ordinary natalist morality just as badly, in the fourteen-year-old case and a hundred others, so no one who has not also solved it for their own view can wield it as a clean refutation of antinatalism. The antinatalist has not one but three coherent routes through it, which is more escape than a position in real trouble usually enjoys. Every route has a price, impersonal value, or a delicate wide principle, or contested better-for-the-nonexistent, and intellectual honesty consists in naming which route one is taking and paying its price in the open rather than pretending the door was never locked. And the non-identity problem is the ground on which the asymmetry, the consent argument, and the priority of suffering all three finally rest, which is why this volume placed it after all three pillars rather than before. A reader who has followed the argument this far should now see the true shape of the foundation. Three pillars above ground, and beneath all of them one contested bedrock: the metaphysics of how good, bad, harm, and benefit attach to people whose existence is the very thing in question. The position stands on that bedrock. So does every alternative to it. No one in this conversation stands anywhere else.
