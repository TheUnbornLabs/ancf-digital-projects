"An idea you cannot see the weaknesses of is an idea you do not really understand."

— a principle this book has tried to keep

A teaching book written from inside a view owes its readers the strongest case against that view, set out fairly and without straw men. Antinatalism has serious critics — careful philosophers who have read Benatar closely and concluded that the argument fails. This chapter gives six of them a fair hearing. Each objection here is real, published, and respected; some antinatalists find them answerable, but no honest reader should pretend they are trivial. We close with an acknowledgment of what remains genuinely contested.

David Wasserman: the goods of life are real and weighty

Wasserman, who has written extensively on disability and the ethics of reproduction, presses hard on the claim that existence is a net harm. He argues that the antinatalist systematically understates the goods of a life and overstates its harms, and that most people's own considered judgment — that their existence is a benefit to them — should carry real evidential weight rather than being explained away. If the lives actually being lived are, by their own lights, worth living, then the premise that coming into existence is a harm looks not proven but assumed.

Elizabeth Harman: the non-identity problem cuts the other way

Harman is known for sharp work on the metaphysics of harm and the "non-identity problem." She challenges the idea that a person can be harmed by being brought into existence at all. Harm, on a standard view, makes someone worse off than they would otherwise have been — but the alternative to a person's existence is not a better life for that person; it is their never existing, which is no state of theirs to be compared. If birth cannot straightforwardly harm the one born, the antinatalist's central claim loses its footing. Harman also argues that creating a life that will contain real goods can be a positive benefit, not merely the avoidance of one.

Christopher Belshaw: a more limited conclusion

Belshaw takes pessimism seriously and yet resists the universal conclusion. Drawing on his work on death and the value of lives, he argues that whatever force the considerations have, they do not establish that all procreation is wrong — at most they bear on particular cases, particular lives, particular likely outcomes. His position is a useful reminder that one can grant much of the antinatalist's mood while denying the sweeping "always wrong" verdict, landing instead on something far more qualified.

The serious objections are not insults to the view — they are the tests it has to pass.

Thaddeus Metz: meaning can outweigh suffering

Metz, a leading writer on the meaning of life, brings a dimension the asymmetry argument tends to flatten. Even granting that life contains suffering, he argues that lives can contain meaning — connection, achievement, love, contribution — and that meaning is a good substantial enough to make existence worthwhile even when it is not pleasant on balance. An accounting that weighs only pleasure against pain, he suggests, misses much of what makes a life valuable to the one living it, and so reaches a verdict the fuller picture would not support.

Saul Smilansky: paradox and the limits of the conclusion

Smilansky, a philosopher drawn to moral paradox, has examined antinatalism with characteristic unease about tidy answers. He probes the strange practical and moral implications of taking the view seriously — what it would mean if widely adopted, and whether our deep, near-universal commitment to the goodness of new life might itself be evidence the argument has gone wrong somewhere. His contribution is less a single knock-down objection than a sustained pressure: a sense that a conclusion this radical should make us look again at the premises that produced it.

Rivka Weinberg: procreation can be permissible under conditions

Weinberg has built one of the most careful philosophical treatments of procreative ethics, taking the risks and the non-consent of the future child entirely seriously — and still concluding that procreation can be permissible when certain demanding conditions are met. Rather than denying the antinatalist's worries, she tries to answer them: to specify when the gamble of creating a life is one a responsible person may take. Her work shows that one can accept the weight of the antinatalist's concerns without accepting the antinatalist's conclusion.

The shape of the disagreement

Step back and the objections fall into a few families. Several (Wasserman, Metz) insist on the reality and weight of life's goods — that the antinatalist's ledger is miscounted. Several (Harman, and the broader literature) attack the asymmetry and the concept of harm at the argument's core, denying that birth can wrong the one born. Others (Belshaw, Weinberg) accept much of the worry but reject the universal "always wrong" verdict, carving out cases where procreation is permissible. And Smilansky adds a person-affecting and paradox-based unease about where the whole line of thought leads. Together they form a genuine and respectable opposition.

Objection

"If this many capable philosophers reject antinatalism, doesn't that settle it? The weight of expert disagreement suggests the view is simply mistaken."

Reply

Numbers do not settle philosophy — on almost every deep question the experts are divided, and majorities have been wrong before. What the critics establish is not that antinatalism is false but that it is contestable: its key moves, especially the asymmetry and the claim that birth can harm, are exactly the points where serious people get off the bus, and an honest antinatalist has to engage those points rather than wave them aside. The right response to a strong objection is to take it as a test. Some antinatalists think the asymmetry survives Harman's challenge and that Metz's "meaning" is itself shadowed by suffering; the critics think otherwise; the argument continues. That is what a living philosophical question looks like. Holding a view while acknowledging it might be wrong is not weakness — it is the only intellectually honest way to hold any contested view at all.

What remains contested — an honest close

It would be a poor guide that ended by declaring victory. So here is the honest accounting. The asymmetry argument — the engine of Benatar's case — is not universally accepted, and capable critics regard it as flawed; whether it holds is genuinely open. Whether coming into existence can count as a harm at all remains disputed at the level of basic metaphysics. Whether the goods and meaning of life outweigh its suffering is a question on which reasonable, informed people land on opposite sides. And whether the conclusion should be universal or merely conditional is contested even among those sympathetic to the worry. What is not seriously contested is that the question deserves to be asked — that creating a person is a weighty act, and that "we have always done it" is no answer. On that much, antinatalists and many of their critics agree. The rest is yours to weigh.
