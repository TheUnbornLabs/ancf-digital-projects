Pronatalism: The Water We Swim In

The unspoken assumption that everyone should want children — and where it comes from.

"We do not see the water we swim in until something asks us to name it."

— a paraphrase of an old observation about culture

Imagine asking a roomful of people why they want children. For most of them the question itself feels odd, almost rude — like asking why they want to breathe, or why they prefer being alive. The answer is some version of "you just do," or "it's what people do," or "that's the whole point, isn't it?" Now reverse it. Ask a childfree person why they don't want children, and watch how readily a justification is demanded — a reason, a story, a defense. That asymmetry is not an accident, and it is not really about you. It is the shape of the water everyone is swimming in. It has a name: pronatalism.

Pronatalism — the set of cultural attitudes, social pressures, and institutional structures that promote childbearing and quietly treat parenthood as the default, expected, and most complete adult path. It is not a single belief or a stated policy so much as a background hum: the assumption, rarely spoken aloud, that of course you will have children, and that wanting otherwise calls for explanation.

Naming pronatalism is the single most useful thing this chapter can offer, because a pressure you cannot see operates on you without consent. Once you can point to it — "ah, that is pronatalism talking" — a great deal of the personal sting drains out of the awkward dinners and the well-meant interrogations. The question stops being what is wrong with me? and becomes look at the assumption being made. That shift, from self-doubt to clear sight, is the quiet gift of having a word.

How it shows up

Pronatalism is rarely loud. It lives in defaults and figures of speech, which is exactly what makes it hard to notice. A few of its everyday homes:

Language. We speak of couples "starting a family," as though two people who love each other are not yet a family until a child arrives. We call adults without children "childless," a word built around an absence, and we ask the newly married "so, when are you two going to have kids?" — when, never whether. The grammar itself assumes the outcome.

Media and story. Films and advertisements treat the baby as the natural happy ending, the proof that a character has finally grown up or healed. Childfree characters, when they appear, are often coded as cold, selfish, damaged, or destined for a late-life change of heart. The narrative arc bends, almost reflexively, toward the cradle.

Family and ritual. Holidays organize themselves around children. Relatives track a young couple's reproductive timeline with open interest. A grandparent's longing, sincerely felt, can land as a standing expectation. None of this is malicious; it is simply the channel everyone assumes the river will flow down.

Religion and tradition. Many faith traditions hold childbearing as a blessing, a duty, or a form of stewardship — "be fruitful and multiply" echoes through centuries of teaching. For many believers this is a source of real meaning. It also forms one of pronatalism's deepest tributaries, shaping expectations far beyond the explicitly religious.

Policy and institutions. Tax codes, benefit systems, parental leave, family-oriented housing and scheduling — many of these are built around the household-with-children as the standard unit. Whether and how they should be is a genuine and worthwhile debate, taken up in Chapter 29. The point here is narrower: the default assumption that a normal adult life includes raising children is woven into structures most people never think to question.

A pressure you cannot see operates on you without your consent.

Where it comes from

Pronatalism is old, and for most of history it was simply survival. When infant mortality was high, when children were hands for the farm and the only pension a person could expect, when nations measured strength in population, encouraging childbirth was not an ideology — it was arithmetic. Societies that did not renew themselves did not continue. Some of pronatalism's force is the long echo of that real and once-urgent need.

Layered on top are economic and political interests that have, at various times, made the encouragement more deliberate. States have promoted childbearing to grow armies, workforces, and tax bases; today, aging populations and shrinking birth rates have revived official anxiety about who will fund pensions and staff the future. These are not imaginary concerns. A society genuinely does need a next generation, and people who raise that generation take on real labor and cost on everyone's behalf. Honoring that is not the same as demanding it of everyone.

A short history of naming it

The word and the critique are surprisingly recent. As early as 1916, the psychologist Leta Hollingworth wrote about the "social devices" — laws, customs, beliefs, and forms of praise and shame — that societies use to ensure women keep reproducing, even framing this as something that has to be actively engineered precisely because it is not purely instinctive. Her essay is a remarkably clear-eyed early naming of the machinery.

The term pronatalism entered wider use in the 1970s, carried by a small wave of childfree activists and scholars. Ellen Peck, author of The Baby Trap, co-founded the National Organization for Non-Parents (later N.O.N. / the National Alliance for Optional Parenthood), which argued that parenthood should be a genuine choice rather than an automatic expectation — and that the culture had quietly stacked the deck against choosing otherwise. They did not argue against having children; they argued for the option, and for naming the pressure that obscured it. That reframing — from "what's wrong with you?" to "look at the default" — is their lasting contribution.

The assumption

"Calling it 'pronatalism' makes it sound like a conspiracy — as if society is plotting to force everyone to breed."

A fairer view

It is the opposite of a conspiracy. A conspiracy is secret and coordinated; pronatalism is open and uncoordinated — an unexamined default that almost no one chose and most people pass along without noticing. Naming it is not an accusation against parents or grandparents, who are simply living inside the same assumption. It is a request to make the assumption visible, so that having children becomes a real decision and not having them stops requiring a defense.

Worth knowing

  • Pronatalism is structural and cultural, not personal. When a stranger asks "when are you having kids?", they are usually voicing a default, not judging you specifically.
  • It serves a real function — societies do need to renew themselves — which is exactly why it is so deeply embedded and so rarely questioned.
  • Recognizing it is not the same as resenting parents. The two ideas are fully separable, and most childfree people hold both: respect for parenthood, and freedom from the assumption.
  • The vocabulary is roughly a century old in scholarship and about fifty years old in public life — which is part of why the childfree choice can still feel novel.

Once you have the word, you start seeing it everywhere — in a greeting card, a tax form, a casual question, a film's final scene. This can feel disorienting at first, even a little lonely. But seeing clearly is its own kind of footing. You are no longer being acted on by something nameless. You can engage with the assumption rather than absorb it, agree with the parts that are fair, and gently decline the rest. The chapters that follow put that footing to use — in handling the questions people ask, and in navigating the workplaces and policies built on the old default.

Chapter in brief

  • Pronatalism is the cultural and institutional assumption that parenthood is the default, expected adult path — a background hum, not a stated rule.
  • It shows up in language ("starting a family," "when, not whether"), media, family ritual, religion, and policy.
  • Its roots are partly practical — historically, childbearing meant survival — and partly economic and political, which is why it remains embedded.
  • It was named and critiqued by Leta Hollingworth (1916) and, in the 1970s, by Ellen Peck and the National Organization for Non-Parents.
  • Naming it reframes the question from "what's wrong with you?" to "look at the assumption" — and it is not an attack on parents.
