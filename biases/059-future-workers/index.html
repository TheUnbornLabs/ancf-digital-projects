BIAS 059

Future Workers: Children Framed as Labor Supply

Part V · Economic, Demographic & Policy Pronatalism · Children Framed as Labor Supply

The bias: Reproduction justified by the economy's need for hands.

Man is not merely a means to be used by another as if he were a thing, but must in all his actions be regarded as an end in himself." — Immanuel Kant, Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals (1785), paraphrasing the Formula of Humanity ## Preface to this volume There is a way of arguing for children that sounds hardheaded, forward-looking, and civically responsible all at once. It goes like this: someone has to do the work. Someone has to staff the hospitals, farm the fields, code the software, and empty the bedpans in the nursing homes where we will one day lie. If this generation does not produce a next one large enough to fill those roles, the work will go undone, the shelves will empty, the machines will idle, and the whole apparatus of a functioning society will seize up. Therefore we must have children, enough of them, to supply the labor that tomorrow requires. Call this the future-workers frame: the justification of reproduction by appeal to the economy's future need for hands, minds, and skills. It is one of the most respectable-sounding of all pronatalist arguments, because it does not moralize about God, nature, or the sanctity of motherhood. It talks about staffing ratios and skills shortages. It comes dressed in the language of human resources and workforce planning, and it lands with a weight that feels less like preaching than like planning. That is precisely what makes it worth a book. A frame that announces itself as ideology invites scrutiny; a frame that presents itself as arithmetic slips past the guard. The future-workers argument wears the costume of the spreadsheet, and a spreadsheet, we assume, does not have a moral commitment hiding inside it. It does. This book examines that frame and the bias inside it. The bias is not the observation that a society needs productive members. That observation is true, and I will grant it fully and early. The bias is the move from there to you should have children as labor inputs for tomorrow's economy: the reduction of a future, unborn, particular person to an economic function decided in advance, and the enlistment of that reduction as a reason to reproduce. When a baby is valued, and urged, chiefly as a "future worker," something has gone quietly wrong. A person who does not yet exist is being conceived, in both senses, as a means to an end fixed before her first breath. The stance of this volume is therefore twofold and, I hope, honest. On one side: labor-force sustainability is a genuine concern. Care work in particular does not automate easily, and a society with too few working-age people relative to those needing care faces real strain. I will not pretend otherwise, and Chapter 2 gives that concern its strongest voice before I say a word against it. On the other side: the frame that answers this concern by treating children as a labor supply to be topped up commits a distinct and serious error. It instrumentalizes persons. It treats the arrival of a human being as an input decision, how many units of future labor should we commission, and it thereby violates something most of us believe about why people matter, which is that they matter as ends, not as staffing solutions. Two clarifications about the lane this book stays in. It is not the pension argument (Book 053), which worries about who will fund retirees through pay-as-you-go transfers, a claim about fiscal dependency ratios. Nor is it the GDP-growth assumption (Book 054), which confuses the aggregate size of an economy with the welfare of the people in it. This book concerns the more specific and more revealing frame: children as labor supply, the future person valued as a set of working hands. It is revealing because, more nakedly than its cousins, it shows what happens when reproduction is justified instrumentally: a human life is priced as an economic input. The pension frame reduces the future person to a taxpayer; the GDP frame reduces her to a statistic. The future-workers frame reduces her to a worker, and of the three that reduction is the most intimate, because it reaches past what she will pay or contribute to what she will do with her days, her hands, her attention, the very texture of a working life. I want to be candid, too, about why this frame deserves its own volume rather than a footnote in the others. Its persuasive power is unusual. Most pronatalist arguments can be met by a counter-value: to "God wants children" one answers with a different conviction, to "it's natural" one answers with the naturalistic fallacy. But the future-workers frame does not ask you to share a value. It asks you to accept a fact about staffing, and then quietly attaches a reproductive imperative to that fact as though the imperative followed by logic. It does not. The whole labor of this book is to pry the true fact apart from the false imperative welded onto it, so that one can hold the fact, as I do, without swallowing the demand. The book proceeds in the series' fixed order. Chapter 1 defines the bias and separates it from its neighbours. Chapter 2 makes the strongest case for the frame, in full and in good faith. Chapters 3 and 4 trace where it comes from and how it operates today. Chapter 5 weighs the evidence, including the alternatives to reproduction that the frame conveniently forgets. Chapters 6 through 8 examine hidden costs, who bears the pressure, and the best objections to my own critique. Chapter 9 offers concrete cases. Chapter 10 maps the adjacent biases. Chapter 11 asks what clear reasoning looks like once the bias is named. Chapter 12 concludes without triumph. Throughout, the aim is not to deny that societies need workers, but to dissolve the false step that turns "we need workers" into "you should make them.

① In brief

Children Framed as Labor Supply

There is a way of arguing for children that sounds hardheaded and civic-minded: someone has to do the work, staff the hospitals, farm the fields, and care for us when we are old, so we must have enough children to supply tomorrow's labor. This volume names that argument the future-workers frame and examines the bias inside it.

The bias is not the observation that a society needs productive members. That observation is true, and the book grants it fully. The bias is the move from there to you should have children as labor inputs for tomorrow's economy: the reduction of a future, unborn person to an economic function fixed before she exists, and the enlistment of that reduction as a reason to reproduce. When a baby is valued and urged chiefly as a "future worker," a person who does not yet exist is being conceived as a means to an end decided in advance.

Read the rest of the overview

The steelman (Chapter 2), presented first and in full. Work must be done, and doing it requires people. Care work above all, bathing the frail, reassuring the frightened, is labor-intensive, relational, and resistant to automation, and its demand rises as populations age. Skilled workers take decades to train, so decisions about the workforce of 2060 must be made now. Immigration relocates workers rather than creating them; automation stalls exactly at care; participation and longer working lives have limits. The frame fastens onto a real problem, and the book concedes its premises.

Where the frame errs. From "society needs its work done" the frame slides to "we need future workers" and then to "therefore people should reproduce," smuggling in three unearned assumptions: that native births are the primary way to meet labor needs (versus immigration, automation, participation, productivity); that a collective need should fall as a private reproductive duty, especially on women; and, deepest, that it is acceptable to value a prospective person as a labor input at all. The Kantian objection is decisive: persons must be treated never merely as means but always also as ends. A child commissioned as a worker is instrumentalized from before birth.

Where it comes from. Children truly were labor in agrarian and early-industrial economies; the abolition of child labor was a rejection of the child-as-input in favor of the child-as-person. Human capital theory (Gary Becker) made "people as capital" analytically respectable, a descriptive framework prone to prescriptive misuse. A managerial epoch turned populations into forecastable "labor supply." Today the frame operates through a vocabulary of shortage ("worker deficit," "skills gap"), a pronatalist pivot from collective concern to private duty, and forecasting instruments that sort people into "working-age" and "dependent."

The evidence. Fertility has fallen and populations are aging (UN World Population Prospects). But the frame holds productivity, participation, working lifespans, immigration, and automation artificially fixed while varying only births. It also ignores a decisive fact: a child supplies no labor for two decades and costs enormously in between, making reproduction the slowest and costliest labor-supply tool, and pronatalist policies have moved birth rates little.

Hidden costs. The frame welcomes the child on condition of her usefulness; writes off those who cannot work as failed inputs; displaces faster, fairer solutions; and trains a society to see all persons as functions. Who bears the pressure: women (whose bodies are enlisted), the working poor, migrants (wanted as workers, unwanted as members), and disabled people (rendered as "dependents").

The book stays in its lane, distinct from the pension argument (053, funding retirees) and the GDP assumption (054, aggregate output), and cousin to the fetishization of "the economy" (062). Its takeaway: take the labor concern seriously and refuse the instrumental answer. Meet a labor need with labor policy, pay, train, and welcome workers, and hold firm that persons are ends, not staffing solutions. The economy's need for hands is real; but no one should be urged into being merely to supply them.

② The book in twelve chapters

Each chapter opens with its own summary. Note chapter 2: every volume states the strongest case for the thing it criticises before laying a finger on it — and chapter 8 turns the critique back on itself.

CH 1Naming the bias

The bias this volume examines is the tendency to justify and urge childbearing on the ground that the future economy will need workers, thereby valuing a prospective human being primarily as a unit of labor supply. Stated as a slogan: have children, the economy will need the hands. Stated as the error it contains: a future person is reduced to an economic input, and reproduction is recommended as the way to manufacture that input.

CH 2The strongest case for it (steelman)The strongest case against this book

Before I contest the future-workers frame, I owe it the strongest and most sympathetic statement I can manage, not the version a tired columnist tosses off, but the version its ablest defenders would insist on. And it is genuinely strong. Anyone who waves it away has not understood the problem it is responding to.

CH 3Where it comes from

The future-workers frame feels modern, spoken as it is in the vocabulary of labor markets and human resources. But its roots run deep, through an economic epoch, a scholarly theory, and much older arrangements in which children really were, quite literally, labor.

CH 4How it operates today

The future-workers frame does its work less through explicit argument than through a settled vocabulary and a set of institutional reflexes that make the instrumental view of the child feel like ordinary common sense. To see the bias operating, watch the language and the machinery.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

What does the evidence actually show about the labor concern the future-workers frame invokes, and about the alternatives to reproduction that the frame tends to ignore? The honest answer is that the concern is partly real, its severity is uncertain and contested, and the range of responses is far wider than "have more children."

CH 6The hidden costs

If the future-workers frame merely overstated a real problem, it would be a mistake but not a bias worth a book. What makes it worth examining is what it hides: the costs that go unpriced when children are valued as labor supply. Several are worth drawing out.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

Biases are not felt equally. The future-workers frame, because it converts a collective labor need into a reproductive demand, presses hardest on those whose bodies and lives are enlisted to meet it, and its instrumentalizing logic falls with particular weight on the very people it renders invisible.

CH 8The counter-arguments to the critiqueObjections to the critique

A critique that only heard itself would not be worth trusting. So let me state, as fairly as I can, the strongest objections to this book's own thesis, and answer them honestly, conceding where they land.

CH 9Case studies / illustrations

Abstractions about instrumentalization become clearer against real cases. Here are four, historical and contemporary, that show the future-workers frame, and its costs, in action. I state them at the level of established pattern rather than pinning them to figures that shift with each revision.

CH 10Adjacent biases

The future-workers frame does not stand alone. It interlocks with a family of pronatalist biases treated elsewhere in this series, borrowing force from them and lending force back. Mapping the connections keeps the lane clear and shows how the biases reinforce one another.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

What does clear-eyed, non-coerced reasoning about the labor concern look like once the future-workers frame is named? The goal here is not to deny that societies need work done, but to separate the true concern from the instrumental error, and to hold onto the person the frame reduces. Some markers of thinking well.

CH 12Conclusion

The future-workers frame begins with something true and ends somewhere false, and the whole of this book has been an attempt to mark exactly where the truth stops and the error begins.

Future-workers frame
The justification of childbearing by appeal to the future economy's need for workers; the bias examined in this volume.
Instrumentalization
Treating a person (or prospective person) primarily as a means to an end external to herself, rather than as an end in herself.
Formula of Humanity
Kant's principle that one must treat persons never merely as means but always also as ends in themselves; the core objection to the frame.
Human capital
The skills, knowledge, and health embodied in people, analyzed as a form of capital; a descriptive framework (associated with Gary Becker) prone to prescriptive misuse.
Labor supply
The quantity of labor available to an economy; the input-vocabulary into which the frame casts prospective persons.
Working-age population
The demographic bracket (often ages 15–64) treated as the labor-supplying share of a population; a category that sorts persons by productive function.
Dependency ratio
The ratio of non-working-age people to working-age people; an instrument that renders the young, old, and disabled as "dependents."
Skills shortage / skills gap
A shortfall of workers with particular training; a shortage-framing that presents falling births as an input deficit.
Labor-force participation
The share of working-age people actually in the labor force; a margin that can expand effective labor supply without additional births.
Lead time
The two-plus decades between a birth and a child's entry into the workforce; the delay that makes reproduction the slowest labor-supply tool.
Pronatalist pivot
The move from a collective observation ("we need workers") to a private reproductive directive ("you should have children").
Care work
Labor-intensive, relational care of dependent persons; the sector where the labor concern is strongest and automation weakest.
Ends and means
The distinction, central to the Kantian objection, between valuing something for its own sake (an end) and for what it yields (a means).
Priceless child
Viviana Zelizer's term for the modern, economically "worthless" but emotionally invaluable child; the historical departure from the child-as-labor-input.
Demographic dividend
The growth boost a society can enjoy when its working-age share is large relative to dependents; a term that frames people as an economic windfall.
The ultimate resource
Julian Simon's phrase for population as a source of ideas and productivity; a key plank of the steelman.

⑥ Sources

What this volume draws on. Where the evidence is thin or contested, the book says so rather than reaching for a number.

  • Immanuel Kant, Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals (1785). Source of the Formula of Humanity ("treat humanity... never merely as a means but always at the same time as an end") and the ends/means distinction central to this book's objection.
  • Gary S. Becker, Human Capital: A Theoretical and Empirical Analysis, with Special Reference to Education (New York: National Bureau of Economic Research / Columbia University Press, 1964; expanded editions 1975, 1993). The foundational statement of human capital theory — treated in this book as descriptive analysis, important to read as such rather than as prescription.
  • Viviana A. Zelizer, Pricing the Priceless Child: The Changing Social Value of Children (New York: Basic Books, 1985). The history of the child's shift from economic asset (valued substantially for labor) to sentimentally "priceless," economically "worthless" treasure.
  • Julian L. Simon, The Ultimate Resource (Princeton: Princeton University Press, 1981); The Ultimate Resource 2 (1996). The optimistic case for population as the source of ideas and productivity — cited for the steelman's strongest form.
  • United Nations, Department of Economic and Social Affairs, Population Division, World Population Prospects (successive revisions). Standard source for global and national fertility, aging, working-age population shares, and dependency ratios. The revisions are updated regularly, so consult the current one.
Directions the volume points to (not pinned citations)
  • Charles I. Jones and coauthors on innovation's dependence on the number of researchers, and the thesis that "ideas are getting harder to find" — work associated with Bloom, Jones, Van Reenen, and Webb.
  • Recent reviews of labor-force projections and skills shortages in aging high-income societies, from the OECD and from national statistical and labor-ministry sources. These date quickly; go to a current one.
  • The empirical literature on the effectiveness of pronatalist policies (baby bonuses, tax incentives) on fertility, which generally finds small and often temporary effects. See also Book 055.
  • Studies on automation's uneven impact across sectors, and specifically on the resistance of relational care work to automation.
  • Scholarship documenting care-worker shortages in aging societies such as Japan, Germany, Italy, and South Korea. Staffing figures move year by year; national data is the place to find them.
  • Contemporary work in procreative ethics applying the Kantian ends/means distinction to reproduction and to arguments from societal need.
  • Historical scholarship on child labor in industrialization, and on the reform movements — factory acts, compulsory schooling, minimum working ages — that reframed the child as a person to be raised rather than a labor input.
