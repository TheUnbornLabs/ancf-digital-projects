"Coming into existence is always a harm."

— David Benatar, Better Never to Have Been

If the earlier figures in this book are the ancestors of antinatalism, David Benatar is its leading living defender — the philosopher who took a mood and a tradition and turned them into a tight, contemporary argument that analytic philosophers could not simply wave away. More than any single thinker, he is the reason the word antinatalism is now discussed in seminar rooms, journals, and podcasts rather than dismissed as a curiosity. His case is calm, carefully built, and deliberately unsentimental — which is part of why it has provoked such an unusually wide debate.

Benatar was born in South Africa in 1966 and is a professor of philosophy at the University of Cape Town, where he has long chaired the department. He is famously, almost completely, private: he gives few personal interviews, no photographs circulate widely, and he prefers that his arguments be judged on their merits rather than through the lens of his biography. In 2006 he published Better Never to Have Been: The Harm of Coming into Existence, the book that defined modern philosophical antinatalism, and in 2017 he followed it with The Human Predicament, a broader and somewhat gentler meditation on meaning, death, and whether optimism or pessimism better fits the facts of human life.

Three lines of argument

Benatar does not rest his case on a single move. He offers several independent arguments, each meant to stand even if the others fail. We introduce them briefly here; Part II of this book examines them in full, with their machinery and their critics.

The first and most famous is the asymmetry argument. Benatar contends that there is a basic imbalance between pleasure and pain when we compare existing with never existing. The absence of pain is good even if no one exists to enjoy that good, whereas the absence of pleasure is not bad unless there is someone who is deprived of it. Run this asymmetry through, he argues, and never coming into existence is never worse than coming into existence — and is usually better.

The second is the quality-of-life argument. Human beings, Benatar says, systematically overrate how good their lives are, thanks to well-documented psychological tendencies toward optimism. Look honestly at the daily and lifelong tally of discomfort, frustration, illness, anxiety, loss, and decline, and even lives we call "good" contain far more bad than we admit.

The third is a set of misanthropic arguments — the claim that human beings cause so much harm to other people, to animals, and to the world that bringing more humans into being is, on those grounds too, hard to justify.

The asymmetry — Benatar's signature claim that the absence of pain is good (even with no one to enjoy it) while the absence of pleasure is not bad (unless someone is deprived). If correct, this tilts the comparison so that never existing cannot be worse than existing — and is generally better.

The crucial nuance: not pro-suicide

Here we must be careful, because this is the point most often mangled in summaries of his work. Benatar argues that coming into existence is always a harm. He does not argue that people already alive should end their lives. These are different questions, and he keeps them firmly apart.

The reason is that an existing person is not in the same situation as a merely possible one. Someone who already exists has interests, projects, relationships, and a powerful stake in their own continuation. To never have started a life harms no one — there is no one there to be harmed. To end an existing life cuts across a real person's deep interest in going on, and very often makes them worse off. So Benatar can hold, without contradiction, that it would have been better never to be born and that, given that we are here, continuing to live is usually the better course. The argument is about the door into existence, not the door out.

This distinction is not a footnote; it is essential to reading Benatar responsibly, and it is essential to this book's whole stance. Antinatalism concerns future births. It is never a counsel of despair or self-harm for anyone already living. Benatar is explicit on the point, and we will return to it.

Reception

Few recent works of moral philosophy have been argued over as much. Better Never to Have Been drew detailed responses from philosophers who accepted the seriousness of the question while contesting nearly every step of the asymmetry. Benatar's calm, analytic style raised the level of the debate: critics could no longer dismiss antinatalism as mere gloom, because here was a careful argument that had to be answered on its own terms. Whether or not one is persuaded, his work made the position something a serious thinker has to engage rather than ignore.

Objection

The asymmetry argument has been the target of sustained, sophisticated criticism, and many philosophers think it does not hold. Why grant that the absence of pleasure is "not bad" when no one is deprived, but that the absence of pain is straightforwardly "good" when, equally, no one is relieved? Critics argue the two cases should be treated alike — either both matter when no one exists, or neither does — and that Benatar's asymmetry smuggles in the conclusion it is supposed to prove. If that objection succeeds, the most distinctive pillar of his case may fall.

Reply

This is the central battleground, and it deserves a fuller hearing than a single paragraph can give — which is exactly why Part V of this book takes up Benatar's critics in detail and lets the strongest objections speak at length before weighing the replies. For now, two things can be said fairly. First, Benatar anticipated this very challenge and offered reasons for treating the two absences differently, grounded in widely shared intuitions — for instance, that we feel no duty to create happy people merely so that happiness exists, yet we do feel a duty not to create people who will suffer. That difference in our ordinary moral reactions is what the asymmetry tries to capture. Second, even readers who reject the asymmetry often find the other arguments — about systematically overrated quality of life, and about the harm humans cause — independently troubling. The honest verdict is that the debate is live and unsettled. We are not asking you to take Benatar's word; we are asking you to follow the argument into Part V and judge for yourself.

In plain words: Benatar gives the modern, careful case that being born is always a harm — through an asymmetry between pleasure and pain, our tendency to overrate our lives, and the harm humans cause. Crucially, he is not pro-suicide: starting a life and continuing an existing one are different questions, and his argument is only about the first.
