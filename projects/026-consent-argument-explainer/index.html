PROJECT 026

Consent Argument Explainer

Philosophy · Difficulty: Intermediate · ~10 min

The consent argument asks a simple, sharp question: how can it be permissible to impose the risks of a whole life on someone who could not possibly agree first? This explainer builds the case with everyday analogies, lets you test each premise, and gives the leading replies a fair hearing.

What you will learn

  • Why consent matters everywhere else we act on a person.
  • The three senses of consent: actual, hypothetical, and proxy.
  • The premises of the argument, which you can accept or reject.
  • The strongest replies, including the non-identity problem.

1. An everyday analogy

We normally ask before acting on someone's body or life: surgery, a loan in their name, a tattoo. We treat un-consented impositions as needing strong justification. Birth is the one act done to a person before they can be asked at all. The argument simply applies our ordinary standard to that unique case.

Actual consentA real person agrees in advance — impossible before birth. Hypothetical consentWhat a reasonable person would agree to — but a non-existent being has no preferences to consult. Proxy consentDeciding for another, as parents do for a child — contested for someone who does not yet exist.

3. The leading replies

Consent only applies to existing people

You cannot wrong someone who does not yet exist; there is no one being denied a choice. Whether "possible persons" have moral standing is the crux.

The non-identity problem

A person exists only because they were born; compared to never existing, it's hard to say they were made worse off — so "harm" may not apply in the usual way.

We act for future people all the time

Schooling, vaccination, climate policy — many un-consented decisions for future people are clearly good. Why is birth different?

After-the-fact endorsement

Perhaps what matters is whether a person, once existing, endorses having been born — and most do, supplying a kind of retrospective consent.

Key takeaway

The consent argument is powerful precisely because it uses a standard we already accept everywhere else. Whether birth is a genuine exception — and why — is exactly where reasonable people divide. Knowing your reason is worth more than knowing your verdict.
