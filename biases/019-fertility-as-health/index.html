BIAS 019

Fertility as Fitness: When Reproduction Becomes a Health Metaphor

Part II · Evolutionary & Biological Framings · When Reproduction Becomes a Health Metaphor

The bias: Framing childlessness as a bodily malfunction to be fixed.

The medicalization of early life and childbirth may be doing as much to disable autonomous coping as to enable survival." — paraphrase of a theme in Ivan Illich, Medical Nemesis (1976) --- ## Preface to this volume There is a quiet grammar in the way many of us talk about having children. We speak of "reproductive health," of a "biological clock," of "declining fertility," of bodies that are "still able" or "no longer able." The words sound clinical, neutral, almost hygienic. And often they are doing honest work: they describe the real machinery of bodies, real hardships, real medicine that helps real people. But the same vocabulary carries a passenger that is not neutral at all. Wrapped inside the language of reproductive health is a soft assumption that a healthy body is a reproducing body, and, by quiet implication, that a body which does not reproduce has failed at something it was built to do. This book is about that implication. It examines the bias by which non-reproduction gets framed as a bodily malfunction: a defect to be diagnosed, a failure of health to be treated, a deviation from what a properly functioning organism "should" do. Call it the medicalization of reproduction. The claim is not that medicine has no business near fertility, which it plainly does, but that a legitimate medical concern (helping people who want children and cannot have them) has spread into an illegitimate one (treating the childless body as such as pathological, and treating the choice not to reproduce as a symptom rather than a decision). I want to be careful and I want to be honest, because this is territory where carelessness wounds people. Involuntary infertility is a genuine hardship. For many who long for a child and cannot conceive, it is a source of real grief, and the medicine that helps them is a genuine good. Nothing in this book disputes that. To treat a person's suffering, to name a condition so it can be addressed, to fund research into it: these are humane acts. The bias this book criticizes is not the treatment of infertility. It is the extension of disease-framing beyond that legitimate case: the slide from "your body is not doing something you want it to do, and we can help" to "a body that does not reproduce is, by that fact, unwell.

① In brief

Book 019 · 100 Biases of Natalism

There is a quiet grammar in how we talk about having children: "reproductive health," "biological clock," "declining fertility," "diminished reserve." The words sound clinical and neutral, and often they do honest work. But wrapped inside the language of reproductive health is a passenger that is not neutral: a soft assumption that a healthy body is a reproducing body, and, by implication, that a body which does not reproduce has failed at something it was built to do.

This book examines that implication: the fertility-as-health bias, the tendency to frame non-reproduction as a bodily defect, a malfunction to be diagnosed and corrected, rather than a fact about a life to be understood. In one sentence: it is the mistaking of a reproductive capacity for a reproductive obligation, dressed in the neutral-sounding language of medicine.

Read the rest of the overview

The book separates three moves. Describing the reproductive system in terms of function and dysfunction is ordinary physiology, not biased. Treating a person who wants a child and cannot conceive is legitimate, humane medicine, not biased. The bias is the third move: extending that disease-frame to non-reproduction as such, chosen or unchosen, so that the childless body becomes a defective body regardless of what its owner wants. The tell is a should smuggled into a claim about what a body can do.

Because this is tender territory, the book is emphatic and repeated on one point: involuntary infertility is a real hardship, and treating it is a genuine good. The critique is not aimed at that care, at patients, or at parents. It is aimed at a framing, and it argues that the framing actually harms infertility patients, by loading their grief with the added burden of feeling "broken."

Chapter 2 gives the strongest case for the health framing before any criticism: infertility hurts and calling it a health matter secures care and replaces blame with biology; the reproductive system is genuinely a functional system; fertility can be a real epidemiological indicator (e.g. falling sperm counts); and medicalization gives people options and agency. The book grants every one of these, and argues that none licenses the recoding of non-reproduction as a defect.

Later chapters trace the framing's roots (teleology, statecraft, the medicalization theory of Illich and Conrad, and Crawford's healthism); its mechanisms today (the "clock" metaphor, the language of "decline" and "reserve," the intake form, the WHO's classification of infertility as "a disease of the reproductive system," exact wording to verify, and healthist self-management); and its evidence. On evidence, the book is candid: fertility does decline with age and specific conditions are real, but fertility is a loose index of general health, and whether infertility is a "disease" is genuinely contested, partly because its status as a harm depends on whether the person wants to conceive.

The hidden costs fall on autonomy of self-understanding, on infertility sufferers themselves, and on the epistemic move that dresses a value as a fact and thereby closes an open question. The pressure falls unevenly, hardest on women, on the aging, on the poor, and on the marginalized. A full chapter answers objections to the book's own thesis, sharpening rather than overturning it.

The constructive close asks readers to keep one distinction bright: between what a body can do and what it should do, between treating a desire and judging a life. Medicine can rightly help those who want children; it need not, and should not, imply that a non-reproducing body is unwell. The measured takeaway: the body is not failing when it does not reproduce. It is only a body, doing or not doing one of the many things bodies can do, in a life whose meaning was never medicine's to decide.

② The book in twelve chapters

Each chapter opens with its own summary. Note chapter 2: every volume states the strongest case for the thing it criticises before laying a finger on it — and chapter 8 turns the critique back on itself.

CH 1Naming the bias

The bias examined in this book is the tendency to frame non-reproduction as a health defect: to treat a body that does not produce children as, by that fact, malfunctioning, and to treat the state of not reproducing as a condition to be diagnosed and corrected rather than a fact about a life to be understood. In one sentence: the fertility-as-health bias is the mistaking of a reproductive capacity for a reproductive obligation, dressed in the neutral-sounding language of medicine.

CH 2The strongest case for it (steelman)The strongest case against this book

Before criticizing the health framing, I want to build the best version of it, the version a thoughtful person could hold in good conscience, and hold it up in full. The framing is not foolish. It survives because it captures several things that are genuinely true.

CH 3Where it comes from

The fertility-as-health framing did not fall from the sky. It is the confluence of several older currents, each of which deposited part of the assumption we now inherit. To see the framing as assembled rather than given is already to loosen its grip, because what was built can be examined, and what was inherited can be declined.

CH 4How it operates today

The bias rarely announces itself. It works through the ordinary furniture of language and institutions, in ways that feel like plain description rather than argument. Consider the mechanisms, each of which is individually defensible and which together enact the framing without anyone having to assert it.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

What can be established with evidence, and what cannot, about the claim that non-reproduction is a health defect? The question is worth posing sharply, because the framing draws much of its authority from an aura of scientific settledness that the actual evidence does not underwrite.

CH 6The hidden costs

If the fertility-as-health framing were merely imprecise, it would hardly be worth a book. The reason it matters is that it carries costs, most of them borne quietly by people who never chose the frame. The costs are hidden in a specific sense: the framing presents itself as care, and care is the last place we look for harm.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

Biases distribute their weight unevenly, and the fertility-as-health framing is no exception. Its pressure falls hardest along lines of gender, age, class, culture, and ability. A framing that appears universal, offered to all as neutral health advice, turns out on inspection to press its thumb in particular places, and the pattern of the pressure tells us something about whose autonomy the framing most quietly overrides.

CH 8The counter-arguments to the critiqueObjections to the critique

A fair book must turn its argument on itself. Here are the strongest objections to this book's thesis, stated as forcefully as I can, and answered honestly. The point of the exercise is not to score debating points but to find the real limits of the critique, so that what survives is only what deserves to.

CH 9Case studies / illustrations

Four illustrations show the framing at work, each chosen to display a different mechanism. They are drawn from ordinary life rather than from any single person's story, and each is meant to make visible a move that usually passes unnoticed.

CH 10Adjacent biases

The fertility-as-health bias does not stand alone. It interlocks with others in this series, and seeing the connections keeps us from mistaking one bias for another. The mapping matters because pronatalist pressure is not a single argument one can refute once; it is a mesh of overlapping framings that reinforce each other, and untangling them is the only way to answer each on its own terms.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

What does clear-eyed reasoning about reproduction look like once the fertility-as-health framing is named? Not the rejection of medicine, and not a counter-dogma that reproduction is bad for the body. Just a set of distinctions carefully kept. The aim is not to replace one verdict with another but to withdraw the verdict entirely, leaving the facts intact and the judgment where it belongs, with the person whose body and life are in question.

CH 12Conclusion

This book has argued for a single, careful distinction. There is a legitimate medicine of reproduction, the study and treatment of conditions that frustrate people who want children, and there is a bias that grows out of it like a shadow: the framing of non-reproduction as such as a bodily defect, of the fertile body as the body that "should" be used, and of the non-reproducing body as, quietly, unwell. The first is a good. The second is the fertility-as-health bias, and it is the thing this volume set out to name. The distinction is fine but not fragile; it holds under pressure, and it is the load-bearing wall of everything the book has claimed.

Fertility-as-health bias
the tendency to frame non-reproduction as a bodily defect, mistaking a reproductive capacity for a reproductive obligation in the neutral-sounding language of medicine.
Medicalization
the process (Peter Conrad) by which non-medical problems or ordinary human conditions come to be defined and treated as medical — as illnesses or disorders.
Medical Nemesis
Ivan Illich's thesis (1976) that the expansion of medical jurisdiction can itself become harmful, converting ordinary human conditions into problems requiring professional management and eroding people's capacity to cope on their own terms.
Healthism
an ideology (Robert Crawford) that elevates the pursuit of health to a super-value and moral duty, locating responsibility for health within the individual.
Teleology
reasoning about a thing in terms of its purpose or end (telos); here, the premise that reproductive organs exist "for" reproduction and fail when unused.
Reproductive health
a domain of medicine concerning the reproductive system; a term that can quietly carry the assumption that a healthy body is a reproducing one.
Disease classification
the formal designation of a state as a disease; performative rather than merely descriptive, because it licenses diagnosis, treatment, funding, and moral weight.
Involuntary infertility
the condition of wanting to conceive and being unable to; a real hardship whose treatment is legitimate and which this book explicitly defends.
Voluntary childlessness
the state of not reproducing by choice; a life-decision, not a symptom or defect, though the bias tends to recode it as one.
Biological clock
a loaded metaphor framing age-related fertility change as a countdown toward a deadline, importing urgency and dread beyond the neutral facts about odds.
Ovarian reserve / "decline"
clinical terms for age-related changes in fertility; medically meaningful but carrying a valence of deficit and loss.
Advanced maternal age
a clinical euphemism for older pregnancy; polite in form, but framing later reproduction as a deviation from a younger norm.
Capacity vs. obligation
the distinction the bias fuses: what a body can do versus what it should do; keeping the two apart dissolves much of the bias.
Desire-relative framing
the sound frame in which medicine treats frustrated reproductive desires rather than judging the non-reproducing body itself.
Naturalistic premise
the underlying assumption (see Book 022) that reproduction is what bodies are "for," on which the health-defect framing often silently relies.
Iatrogenesis
harm caused by medical intervention itself; in Illich's broader sense, including the social and cultural harm of medicalizing ordinary life.
Unexplained infertility
a diagnosis of infertility with no identifiable pathology; a case that sits especially awkwardly in the disease category.

⑥ Sources

What this volume draws on. Where the evidence is thin or contested, the book says so rather than reaching for a number.

  • Illich, Ivan. Medical Nemesis: The Expropriation of Health. London: Calder & Boyars, 1976. (Later expanded as Limits to Medicine.) The foundational critique of medicalization and iatrogenesis.
  • Conrad, Peter. The Medicalization of Society: On the Transformation of Human Conditions into Treatable Disorders. Baltimore: Johns Hopkins University Press, 2007. The standard modern account of medicalization; source of the working definition used in this book.
  • Sontag, Susan. Illness as Metaphor. New York: Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 1978. On the moral and social freight carried by disease-language; directly relevant to the metaphor argument in Chapters 4 and 6.
  • Benatar, David. Better Never to Have Been: The Harm of Coming into Existence. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2006. Cited as adjacent to the series' broader debate, not to the specific health-framing thesis.
  • Demographic background — for the long-run global decline in fertility and the spread of sub-replacement total fertility rates, see the UN World Population Prospects and the national statistical offices.
Directions the volume points to (not pinned citations)
  • Crawford, Robert. "Healthism and the Medicalization of Everyday Life." International Journal of Health Services, 1980. The essay that named healthism.
  • World Health Organization. Fact sheet on infertility, which defines the condition as "a disease of the male or female reproductive system defined by the failure to achieve a pregnancy after 12 months or more of regular unprotected sexual intercourse." See also the WHO/ICMART glossary of ART terminology.
  • Martin, Emily. The Woman in the Body: A Cultural Analysis of Reproduction. Boston: Beacon Press, 1987. A feminist analysis of the medical framing of women's reproductive bodies.
  • Zola, Irving Kenneth. "Medicine as an Institution of Social Control." Sociological Review, 1972. The canonical early statement of the medicalization thesis.
  • Conrad, Peter, and Joseph W. Schneider. Deviance and Medicalization: From Badness to Sickness. 1980; expanded edition 1992.
  • The mid-twentieth-century framing of menopause as a "deficiency disease" requiring hormone replacement. Robert Wilson's popular writing of the 1960s is the usual starting point; the wider historical literature on it is substantial.
  • ESHRE and ASRM (the European Society of Human Reproduction and Embryology and the American Society for Reproductive Medicine) on the clinical definition of infertility, conventionally the failure to conceive after twelve months of regular unprotected intercourse.
  • A note on precision: this volume deliberately argues at the level of the finding rather than the figure. Where the underlying numbers are contested — as much of the fertility literature is — the text says so rather than borrowing a false exactness from a single study.
