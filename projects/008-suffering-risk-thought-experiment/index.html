PROJECT 008

Suffering Risk Thought Experiment

Philosophy · Difficulty: Intermediate

This is a reflection exercise, not a test. It explores one of the hardest questions in birth ethics: when you decide for a future person who cannot choose, how much risk of suffering is acceptable? Use the slider to make the abstraction concrete, then sit with the prompts.

1. The set-up

Imagine you are deciding, on behalf of a person who does not yet exist, whether they will be born. You cannot ask them. You cannot guarantee a good outcome. You only know that their life will contain some joy and some suffering, in proportions you cannot control.

The exercise pulls on three tensions worth naming as you think:

  • Certainty vs. probability — does a guaranteed small harm feel different from a possible large one?
  • Deciding for another — does choosing on someone else's behalf raise the bar compared with taking a risk yourself?
  • The veil — if you did not know which life you'd be born into, what risk would you accept?

2. The risk slider

There is no correct number. The point is to notice where your own line sits — and why moving it feels uncomfortable.

3. Guided reflection

Four prompts. Sit with each and notice your reasoning, not just your conclusion. Saved on this device only.

4. Frames philosophers use

Different lenses pull the answer in different directions. Tap to expand.

Expected value

Weigh each outcome by its probability and add them up. On this view, a high chance of a good life can outweigh a small chance of a bad one — the logic behind most everyday risk-taking.

The precautionary view

Some harms are so severe that you should avoid imposing any real risk of them, regardless of the upside. Here, "it'll probably be fine" is not enough.

Non-aggregation

Perhaps one person's severe suffering cannot simply be "cancelled out" by another's joy. If so, averages and expected values miss something morally important.

Optimism & agency

A future person is not a passive recipient of risk — they can find meaning, reduce suffering, and shape their own life. This is a serious reply to the precautionary view.

In closing

Notice that the difficulty does not come from bad faith on any side — it comes from genuinely competing intuitions about risk, certainty, and deciding for someone else. Whatever number your slider landed on, you have just done the thing the exercise asks for: you made the trade-off visible instead of automatic.
