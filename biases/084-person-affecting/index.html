BIAS 084

Nobody There to Benefit: Person-Affecting Confusions

Part VII · Philosophical & Ethical Fallacies · Person-Affecting Confusions

The bias: Muddles about benefiting or harming people who don't yet exist.

We are in favour of making people happy, but neutral about making happy people.
① In brief ② The book in twelve chapters ③ Key terms ④ Ten-question check ⑤ Reflection ⑥ Sources ⑦ Related biases

① In brief

This volume examines a quiet but load-bearing assumption in pronatalist reasoning: that bringing a happy person into existence benefits that person, that having a child "gives them the gift of a good life," as though a favour were being done to a waiting recipient. Call this the beneficiary confusion. Its trouble is structural. Ordinary benefit-talk is comparative: to benefit someone is to make them better off than they otherwise would have been, which requires two states of one persisting subject to compare. But before a child is conceived there is no subject. The alternative to their existing is not their existing worse; it is no one at all. A comparison between someone and no one is not obviously a comparison, and so "you'd be doing them a favour" quietly presupposes a beneficiary who does not yet exist.

The book's stance is critical of pronatalist bias and evidence-first, but it is not a brief for any particular population ethics. Its target is narrow and, for that reason, defensible: not the strong claim that creating a good life certainly benefits no one, but the unearned move from "a good life is good" to "creating it benefits its liver." Whether or not the person-affecting principle is finally true, the pronatalist may not simply assume a pre-existing beneficiary and treat the matter as closed.

Following the series method, Chapter 2 steelmans the opposing view first and in full: the strongest cases that existence is a benefit (existence-comparativism), that a world with more flourishing is impersonally better, that gratitude for existing is real evidence, and that the procreative asymmetry the critic relies on is itself unstable. These are given a genuine hearing, not a straw one. The book's tools are the person-affecting principle (an outcome can be worse only if worse for someone), Jan Narveson's neutrality ("in favour of making people happy, but neutral about making happy people"), and above all Derek Parfit's non-identity problem from Reasons and Persons (1984): because different procreative choices produce numerically different people, there is no antecedent child to be benefited or deprived by the decision.

Read the rest of the overview

Later chapters trace the confusion's sources (a grammar built for transactions among the living; the projection of one's own imagined non-existence as loss; religious life-as-gift residue; pronatalist institutional incentive), and show it operating today through the deprivation script ("you'd be denying a child life"), the gratitude appeal ("aren't you glad you were born?"), and the civic rhetoric of "the generation we chose not to have." The evidence chapter is scrupulous about limits: the non-identity problem is sturdy, but person-affecting views themselves strain under non-identity cases and struggle to ground the asymmetry, and defenders of "existence is a benefit" have real arguments. The honest verdict is a stalemate of intuitions that leaves the pronatalist's confident benefit-claim contested at best.

The hidden costs are a deliberation with a phantom stakeholder pressing toward procreation; manufactured grief in the childfree and childless for a "someone" who is no one; corrupted population policy that treats the never-born as victims; and a debased concept of "gift." These press hardest on women, on the childfree by choice, on the involuntarily childless, and on populations subject to demographic engineering.

Seeing past the bias does not settle whether to have children; it relocates the decision to the people who actually have stakes in it, trades guilt for gravity, lets gratitude keep its warmth while shedding its rhetorical freight, and keeps real duties to actual future people apart from pseudo-duties to the never-born. The measured takeaway is deflationary, not triumphant: the beneficiary must be shown, not smuggled, and until shown, the right response to "you'd be benefiting them" is a single deflating question the confusion cannot answer: whom?

② The book in twelve chapters

Each chapter opens with its own summary. Note chapter 2: every volume states the strongest case for the thing it criticises before laying a finger on it — and chapter 8 turns the critique back on itself.

CH 1Naming the bias

The bias examined in this book is the beneficiary confusion: the disposition to treat the creation of a person as an act that benefits that person, on the model of how we benefit an already-existing person by improving their situation. Stated as a thesis, the book's claim is this: the common pronatalist premise that having a child confers a benefit on the child, that it "gives them the gift of a good life," smuggles in a pre-existing beneficiary who does not exist, and once that smuggling is exposed, a central plank of everyday pronatalist reasoning loses its footing.

CH 2The strongest case for it (steelman)The strongest case against this book

Honesty and this series' method require that the opposing view be given its best day in court, and first. So set aside, for this chapter, every suspicion raised above, and let us build the strongest case that creating a good life does benefit the one created, or is at least good in a way that grounds real reasons to procreate. The reader should treat this chapter not as a foil but as a live challenge that the rest of the book must actually meet.

CH 3Where it comes from

If the beneficiary confusion is a genuine error, it is a remarkably robust one, and robust errors usually have deep sources. A slip that could be dislodged by a single argument would not survive as this one does, embedded in speech, felt in the body, reinforced by institutions. Several sources converge here, and their convergence is the point: no one of them explains the tenacity, but together they make it intelligible.

CH 4How it operates today

The beneficiary confusion rarely announces itself. It operates through everyday scripts, defaults, and turns of phrase that pass without challenge because their grammar feels innocent. What makes it powerful is precisely that it is never asserted as a thesis, where it could be examined and contested, but only presupposed inside sentences whose surface content is something else. Here is where to catch it.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

Because the target here is a conceptual confusion rather than an empirical claim, "the evidence" is largely argumentative and philosophical. There is no experiment that settles whether creating a person benefits them; the question is not the kind that a dataset resolves. But there are things that can be shown by argument with something close to rigour, and the honest accounting includes what cannot be settled from the armchair, and where the reasoning runs into genuine, unresolved difficulty.

CH 6The hidden costs

If the beneficiary confusion is doing rhetorical work it has not earned, what does that cost, and whom? Even granting that the underlying metaphysics is contested, the unexamined assumption carries prices that a clearer frame would reveal. The costs are real whether or not the person-affecting verdict is finally correct, because they flow not from the metaphysics being settled one way but from the assumption being made without being settled, from a contested claim doing the work of a settled one.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

Biases are never distributed evenly. The beneficiary confusion, because it operates through the "gift of life" and deprivation scripts, presses hardest on those most exposed to pronatalist expectation, and its costs fall unequally along lines that track existing patterns of social pressure. A confusion that were merely intellectual might be a matter for seminar rooms; this one reaches into biographies, and it reaches into some biographies far more than others.

CH 8The counter-arguments to the critiqueObjections to the critique

The steelman of Chapter 2 gave the pronatalist their best positive case. Fairness now requires the strongest objections to this book's own thesis, the claim that "you'd benefit your child by creating them" rests on a confusion. Several are serious, and a couple of them constrain the thesis in ways the honest reader should carry forward.

CH 9Case studies / illustrations

Abstract argument obscures how the confusion actually moves. Four illustrations, drawn to be recognisable rather than to name real individuals, show it operating at different scales and with different degrees of resistance.

CH 10Adjacent biases

The beneficiary confusion does not stand alone; it interlocks with several biases catalogued elsewhere in this series. Mapping the joints prevents both duplication and the mistake of treating a family of errors as one, and it shows exactly where the present book's territory begins and ends.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

Suppose the confusion is named and its grip loosened. What does clear-eyed, non-coerced reasoning about procreation look like on the other side? The aim here is not to reach a verdict for or against having children, which is each person's to make, but to describe reasoning freed of one specific illusion, and to show that freedom from the illusion does not amount to a verdict against children, as pronatalist rhetoric sometimes fears.

CH 12Conclusion

This book took aim at a small, load-bearing phrase, the gift of life, and the assumption folded inside it: that bringing a happy person into existence benefits that person, as we benefit someone already here by improving their lot. Call it the beneficiary confusion. Its structure is a presupposition, not an assertion: it does not argue that a beneficiary exists, it quietly supposes one, inside the grammar of "give," "deny," "deprive," and "owe." Because it works by presupposition, it is unusually hard to see and unusually easy to deploy, and it can shape a decision without ever surfacing as a claim its holder would defend, or even recognise as held.

Show all 16 terms as a list
Beneficiary confusion
The bias central to this book: assuming that creating a person benefits that person, on the model of benefiting an already-existing subject, thereby presupposing a beneficiary who does not yet exist.
Person-affecting principle (restriction)
The claim that an outcome can be better or worse only if it is better or worse for someone — value must cash out in effects on particular subjects.
Narveson's neutrality
Jan Narveson's view that we have reason to make (existing) people happy but no comparable reason to make happy people (create new lives): "in favour of making people happy, but neutral about making happy people."
Non-identity problem
Derek Parfit's problem (Reasons and Persons, 1984): because different procreative choices produce numerically different people, "harming" or "benefiting" a future child cannot compare that child's outcome to a better one for the same child, since the alternative is a different person's existence (or none).
Comparative benefit
Benefit as making someone better off than they otherwise would have been; requires two states of the same subject to compare.
Existence-comparativism
The view that coming into a good existence can be genuinely better for the person who comes to be, even absent a prior subject; the main defence of "existence is a benefit."
Impersonal value
Value a state of affairs has independently of being good for any particular person; basis of the reply that "a world with more flourishing is simply better."
Procreative asymmetry
The claimed asymmetry between strong reasons not to create miserable lives and absent or weak reasons to create happy ones (see Book 079).
Deprivation script
The everyday rhetorical pattern framing non-procreation as withholding a life from, or depriving, a determinate future child.
Gratitude appeal
The argument from a person's gladness at existing to the conclusion that existence was a benefit conferred, and thence that declining to procreate wrongs a future child.
Gift-of-life framing
The locution treating procreation as a gift given to the child, presupposing a giver, a recipient, a transferred thing, and gratitude owed.
Threshold (the)
The point before a person exists, at which pronatalist rhetoric locates a beneficiary; the confusion lives entirely here and does not touch duties to actual children.
Manufactured deprivation
Distress generated in the childfree or childless by the false presupposition that they are denying a specific someone a life.
Adaptive preference / survivorship (on gratitude)
The complication that those glad to exist are, by definition, the ones who exist to report, and their preferences are shaped by the very life in question.
Repugnant conclusion
(Book 083) The total-view result that a vast population of barely-worthwhile lives can outweigh a smaller flourishing one; a puzzle of aggregation, distinct from the beneficiary question.
Waiting-soul intuition
(Book 046) The quasi-metaphysical picture of a pre-existing child longing to be born; the vivid form of which the beneficiary confusion is the deflated residue.

⑥ Sources

What this volume draws on. Where the evidence is thin or contested, the book says so rather than reaching for a number.

  • Parfit, Derek. Reasons and Persons. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 1984. — Canonical source of the non-identity problem, the discussion of person-affecting principles, and the procreative asymmetry. Foundational to Chapters 1, 5, 8, 10.
  • Benatar, David. Better Never to Have Been: The Harm of Coming into Existence. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2006. — Employs a version of the asymmetry between the badness of creating suffering and the (non-)value of creating happiness; adjacent to, though distinct from, the person-affecting arguments of this book.
  • Narveson, Jan. "Utilitarianism and New Generations." Mind, 1967. — Origin of the neutrality view: reason to make people happy, not to make happy people. The source of the "make happy people / make people happy" contrast that has organized the debate ever since.
  • The falling-fertility demographic backdrop — attributed, per policy, to the class of authoritative sources (United Nations World Population Prospects; national statistical offices) rather than to any invented precise table. Used only as context in the Preface and Chapters 4–5.
  • Narveson, Jan. "Moral Problems of Population." The Monist, 1973. — Further development of the neutrality position first stated in the 1967 paper.
  • Holtug, Nils. Persons, Interests, and Justice. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2010. — The most sustained defence of existence-comparativism: that coming into a good existence can itself benefit the person who comes into it. The principal opponent of Chapter 6.
  • Harman, Elizabeth. "Can We Harm and Benefit in Creating?" Philosophical Perspectives 18 (2004). — Argues that we can do both, on a non-comparative account of harm that does not require a better alternative life for the same person. The leading existence-benefit position.
  • Broome, John. Weighing Lives. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2004. — On betterness and the value of adding people; relevant to the impersonal-value steelman in Chapter 2 and to Book 083's territory.
  • Arrhenius, Gustaf. "An Impossibility Theorem for Welfarist Axiologies." Economics and Philosophy 16 (2000), and his subsequent theorems. — On the conditions no axiology, person-affecting or impersonal, can jointly satisfy.
Directions the volume points to (not pinned citations)
  • Roberts, Melinda A. "The Nonidentity Problem," Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, along with the SEP's entry on "The Repugnant Conclusion" for the impersonal-value neighbour (Book 083). Both are revised periodically and are the best starting points for a reader new to the literature.
  • Jeff McMahan on the asymmetry and on the ethics of creating lives, which is where a reader who finds Chapter 8's replies too quick should go next.
