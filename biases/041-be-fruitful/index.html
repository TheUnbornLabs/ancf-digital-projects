Be Fruitful and Multiply: Scripture as Reproductive Command

① In brief

Be Fruitful and Multiply: Scripture as Reproductive Command Part IV — Religious, Spiritual & Moral Framings

This volume opens Part IV of 100 Biases of Natalism and examines one specific religious framing: the reading of ancient scriptural injunctions, above all Genesis 1:28, "be fruitful and multiply," as a standing, universal, personal command to procreate. It is deliberately narrow. It does not treat children-as-divine-gift (Book 042) or procreation-as-sacred-duty in general (Book 043); it stays in the lane of Scripture-as-command: how a particular sentence is read, and how that reading is deployed as a mandate.

The book's stance is critical of a bias, not of faith. It insists on treating Scripture and believers with genuine respect, and it gives the command reading its strongest, most sympathetic hearing before questioning it. The target is not the interpretation but the bias: the near-automatic slide from "a blessing given to a nascent humanity in an empty, high-mortality world" to "a personal obligation on you, now, to have children." That slide happens so smoothly that the interpretive steps in between vanish from view.

Read the rest of the overview

The core distinction the book draws is between four questions that the bias collapses into one: What kind of speech-act is Genesis 1:28: blessing, command, promise, description? To whom is it addressed: the primal figures, humanity as a whole, the covenant community, or each individual? What is its scope, and what exceptions does the tradition itself recognize? And what, in actual circumstances, would faithfulness require? The bias answers all four at once, always in the direction of maximal personal obligation, without noticing that a choice was made.

The steelman (Chapter 2) is presented in full and sympathetically: the Hebrew is imperative; the charge is repeated to Noah after the flood; Jewish tradition counts p'ru u'rvu as the first of the 613 commandments and works out its law with great care; strong Christian strands read it as God's will for fruitfulness in marriage; and theologically, to procreate is to co-operate with the Creator and to say yes to life and hope. This reading is coherent, textually grounded, traditionally authoritative, and serious.

The critique then shows that it is nonetheless a reading. The identical phrase is spoken to fish and birds in Genesis 1:22 as evident blessing; the traditions that read it as command hedge it with exceptions, debate its scope, traditionally address its formal obligation to men rather than all, and honor as holy the very celibacy a universal command would forbid. The evidence, textual and interpretive, not demographic, points consistently to nuance the popular bias erases.

The costs of the foreclosure fall unevenly: on the involuntarily infertile, who bear a second wound beyond their grief; on the celibate, whose vocation the mandate reading cannot fully honor; on the poor, for whom unweighed family size can mean deprivation; on women, who carry the lived pressure whatever the formal doctrine assigns; and on communities that lose access to their own richer interpretive inheritance.

The book fairly engages the strongest counter-arguments (that the sophisticated tradition avoids the bias, that uncertainty might reasonably be resolved toward obedience, that the tradition's exceptions prove the command, and that the reading protects genuine goods) and concedes much to each, concluding that the reading survives scrutiny but the bias does not.

The measured takeaway is a restoration, not a verdict: return the interpretive step. A believer who, having seen the options, embraces the command is doing something admirable and freely chosen. What the book resists is anyone arriving there, or being judged for failing to, without ever knowing there was a choice. The asking, it argues, is not a failure of faith but a form of its seriousness.

② The book in twelve chapters

Each chapter opens with its own summary. Note chapter 2: every volume states the strongest case for the thing it criticises before laying a finger on it — and chapter 8 turns the critique back on itself.

CH 1Naming the bias

The bias this book names can be stated in a single sentence: the tendency to hear the words "be fruitful and multiply" as a standing, universal, personal command to procreate, rather than as one possible reading among several of an ancient blessing given in a particular setting. The thesis of the book follows from it: even granting the full authority of Scripture, the move from Genesis 1:28 to a personal obligation on any given person today is an interpretive act, not a self-evident deduction, and the bias consists in mistaking the interpretation for the plain sense.

CH 2The strongest case for it (steelman)The strongest case against this book

Now I want to make the command reading as strong as I honestly can, because it is stronger than its critics often allow, and because a critique that has not first understood its opponent is worthless. I will not hedge this chapter; I will argue it as its ablest defenders would.

CH 3Where it comes from

Having heard the command reading at its strongest, we can ask a genealogical question: where does the bias, the reflex that hears the verse only as command, come from? Understanding the origins is not a refutation. A belief can have a perfectly understandable origin and still be true. But tracing the genealogy helps us see why one reading became so dominant that its alternatives can feel like novelties or even like evasions, and that is worth seeing.

CH 4How it operates today

An ancient command survives into the present not as an abstraction but through concrete mechanisms: the language people use, the defaults they inherit, the scripts they follow, the pressures they feel. This chapter traces how the command reading of Genesis 1:28 actually operates in ordinary religious life today, long after the empty, high-mortality world that first gave it urgency has passed. The mechanisms are worth examining one by one, because the bias does its work not through argument but through a thousand small, unexamined moves.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

What can be established with reasonable confidence about the text, its history, and its interpretation, and what remains genuinely open? This chapter tries to separate the two honestly, because the strength of the critique depends on being fair about the evidence. I would rather concede everything the tradition can prove than win by overstating what it cannot.

CH 6The hidden costs

If the command reading were simply one defensible interpretation held with awareness of its alternatives, there would be little to critique. The costs this chapter traces arise from the bias, from treating the reading as plain, only, and universally binding, not from the reading itself held reflectively. The distinction is not a technicality; it is the whole moral difference between a conviction and a weapon.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

Biases are rarely evenly distributed, and the command reading of Genesis 1:28 presses hardest on some people and lightly on others. Mapping that distribution is part of taking the bias seriously, because a framing that is comfortable for the many can be quietly crushing for the few, and the comfort of the many is often what keeps the crushing invisible.

CH 8The counter-arguments to the critiqueObjections to the critique

Intellectual honesty requires putting the strongest objections to this book's own thesis, and engaging them rather than waving them away. If the critique cannot survive its ablest opponents, it does not deserve to survive. Several objections are serious, and I will not pretend otherwise.

CH 9Case studies / illustrations

Abstract argument is sharpened by concrete instances. Here are several, drawn to illustrate how the command reading works and where the bias shows itself. They are illustrative composites and historical patterns rather than claims about named individuals; the aim is to make visible a dynamic, not to report on anyone in particular.

CH 10Adjacent biases

This book is one panel in a larger triptych on religious framings, and its argument is sharper when its borders with the neighboring volumes are clearly drawn. The bias examined here, Scripture-as-reproductive-command, interlocks with several others in the series without duplicating them, and mapping the seams helps keep each volume honest about its own scope.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

What does clear-eyed, non-coerced reasoning about Genesis 1:28 look like, once the bias is named? Not the abandonment of Scripture or tradition, that would be its own distortion and a poor trade, but a recovery of the interpretive freedom the traditions themselves contain and have always contained. This chapter sketches what that recovery looks like, for individuals and for institutions, and insists throughout that seeing past the bias is a deepening of faith's seriousness, not a departure from it.

CH 12Conclusion

This book has tried to hold two commitments together that can seem in tension, and that some readers will have wished I would let fall apart: deep respect for Scripture and for those who read it as command, and a critique of the bias that hears the command reading as the plain, only, and universally binding sense of Genesis 1:28. If I have kept them together, it is because I do not think they are actually in tension. One can revere a text and still notice how it is being read.

Genesis 1:28
The verse at the close of the first creation account containing "be fruitful, and multiply, and replenish the earth, and subdue it"; the central text of this book.
Genesis 9:1
The reissuing of "be fruitful and multiply" to Noah and his sons after the flood; cited as evidence the charge is enduring rather than tied to Eden.
P'ru u'rvu
The Hebrew imperative phrase "be fruitful and multiply"; in Jewish tradition standardly counted as the first of the commandments (mitzvot).
Mitzvah (pl. mitzvot)
A divine commandment in Jewish law. The classification of p'ru u'rvu as a mitzvah fixes its command status within that tradition.
Speech-act
What an utterance does: commands, blesses, promises, or describes. Which speech-act Genesis 1:28 performs is the pivot of the interpretive dispute.
Blessing reading
The interpretation of Genesis 1:28 as a bestowal of favor and fertility rather than a legal command; supported by the identical phrase spoken to animals in Genesis 1:22.
Command reading
The interpretation of Genesis 1:28 as a binding instruction to procreate; the subject of the book's critique when treated as plain, only, and universal.
Scope
The question of to whom an injunction is addressed: the primal figures, humanity as a whole, the covenant community, or each individual.
Foreclosure
The core mechanism of the bias: the erasure of a genuine interpretive question so that one reading appears self-evident.
Ben Azzai
A Talmudic sage remembered for teaching the gravity of the procreation commandment while himself not marrying (exact source and wording to verify, tractate Yevamot).
Covenantal reading
The interpretation tying "multiply" to God's promise of descendants to Abraham and the continuation of the covenant people.
Openness to life
A phrase in some Christian traditions holding that marriage should remain disposed toward procreation; often grounded partly in Genesis 1:28.
Dominion / "subdue it"
The clause following "be fruitful and multiply" in 1:28; read by some as strengthening the command and by others as marking the whole as a grant of blessing and stewardship.
Steelman
Stating an opposing view in its strongest, most sympathetic form before critiquing it; applied to the command reading in Chapter 2.
Anachronism
Reading a later concept (e.g., modern individual obligation) back into an ancient text; a risk on both sides of the dispute.
Discernment
The prayerful, particular weighing of a decision; the faculty a rigid command reading can crowd out.
Communal accounting
The social practice by which family size becomes a public marker of faithfulness, moving the bias from private conscience into communal enforcement.

⑥ Sources

What this volume draws on. Where the evidence is thin or contested, the book says so rather than reaching for a number.

  • The Book of Genesis, chapters 1, 2, and 9 — including Genesis 1:22 (the blessing on sea creatures and birds), 1:28 ("be fruitful, and multiply, and replenish the earth, and subdue it"), and 9:1 (the charge reissued to Noah). Consult a scholarly translation with notes (e.g., the JPS Tanakh or an annotated study Bible) for the Hebrew and the translation apparatus.
  • King James Version, Genesis 1:28 — the source of the epigraph, quoted verbatim: "And God blessed them, and God said unto them, Be fruitful, and multiply, and replenish the earth, and subdue it."
  • David Benatar, Better Never to Have Been: The Harm of Coming into Existence (Oxford University Press, 2006) — cited as series-adjacent background on the antinatalist side of the broader debate, not on the exegesis.
  • New Testament passages on singleness and celibacy — the Gospels' report of Jesus's teaching on those who remain unmarried "for the kingdom" (Matthew 19) and Paul's discussion in 1 Corinthians 7. Cited generally as evidence that Scripture honors deliberate childlessness; consult a scholarly edition for the wording.
  • Demographic facts — global fertility decline and sub-replacement fertility in many countries, attributed to the class of source (UN World Population Prospects; national statistical agencies; WHO for infertility prevalence) rather than to any precise figure. This book makes no numerical claim it cannot stand behind.
Directions the volume points to (not pinned citations)
  • Babylonian Talmud, tractate Yevamot — on the commandment of procreation, its minimum, the debate over women's obligation, and the Ben Azzai passage.
  • Mishnah, order Nashim — on what fulfills p'ru u'rvu: the "son and a daughter" position and the House of Hillel / House of Shammai dispute.
  • Maimonides, Sefer HaMitzvot (Book of Commandments), and the standard enumerations of the 613 commandments. The two reckonings are worth holding side by side. The Torah-order enumerations — the Sefer HaChinuch is the best known — begin with p'ru u'rvu, which is the source of the familiar claim that it is the "first" commandment. Maimonides, ordering the commandments by topic rather than by where they fall in the text, lists it as positive commandment 212 and traces it to Genesis 9:7 rather than 1:28. Neither reckoning is a mistake, and the popular version that fuses them into "Maimonides put it first" is.
  • Scholarly commentaries on Genesis 1 — on 1:28 as blessing and grant of dominion, and on the parallel between 1:22 (the animals) and 1:28 (the humans), which is where much of this book's argument begins.
  • Scholarship on the rabbinic debate over the scope of the procreation commandment, including the exemption of women, its rationale, and modern responses to it.
  • Works on the hermeneutics of applying ancient scriptural injunctions to modern individuals.
  • Christian theological treatments of Genesis 1:28, marriage, and "openness to life."
  • Companion volumes in this series: Book 042 (A Gift You Cannot Refuse: Children as Divine Blessing), Book 043 (The Sacred Duty to Procreate), Book 045 (The Sin of Not Bearing: Contraception and Childlessness as Failing).
