The Things We Don't Say: Silence Around Parental Regret

Part VI · Media, Language & Rhetoric · Silence Around Parental Regret

The bias: The taboo that keeps ambivalent parents quiet.

The most powerful controls are the ones we never notice, because we have already agreed, in advance and in silence, not to say the thing that would trigger them.
① In brief ② The book in twelve chapters ③ Key terms ④ Ten-question check ⑤ Reflection ⑥ Sources ⑦ Related biases

① In brief

Silence Around Parental Regret · 100 Biases of Natalism

Some sentences can be said at a dinner table and some cannot. "I love my children more than my own life" is sayable anywhere. "There are days I wish I had never become a parent" is sayable almost nowhere, even though a single person can, and often does, feel both at once. This book is about the second sentence and the machinery that keeps it unsaid.

Its subject is not the frequency of parental regret. It is the taboo: the socially enforced prohibition on admitting regret or serious ambivalence about the parental role, and the way that prohibition distorts the information available to everyone deciding whether to become a parent. Whatever the true amount of regret, the amount audible in public is lower, and lower in a structured, non-random way that consistently favors reproduction. That directional distortion is why the silence functions as a pronatalist bias rather than mere noise.

Read the rest of the overview

The book stays deliberately in its lane, distinct from three neighbors. It is not Book 039, which dismantles the claim that no one regrets having children; this book explains why that claim is so easy to believe, because the taboo removes the counterexamples before anyone forms the belief. It is not Book 008, which treats the sampling filter by which only glad voices survive; this book concerns the active social prohibition that thickens that filter. And it borrows, without re-arguing, the sacralization of family from Book 028 as the source of the taboo's moral charge.

Three well-developed ideas do the analytical work. Timur Kuran's preference falsification (Private Truths, Public Lies, 1995) explains how people publicly voice a contentment they do not fully feel when honesty is punished, making a private view look nonexistent. Elisabeth Noelle-Neumann's spiral of silence explains how those who sense their feeling is a disreputable minority fall quiet, making it look rarer and silencing the next person. Orna Donath's research on regretting motherhood (2015/2017) demonstrated both that the feeling exists in a coherent, non-pathological form and, through the ferocity of the reaction to it, how strong the taboo is. A fourth, domain-specific mechanism gives the taboo its unusual grip: the impossibility-of-saying-it structure, the linguistic trap by which "I regret the role" cannot be voiced without risking being heard as "I reject the child," a thing most regretful parents do not feel.

That distinction, regretting the role versus not loving the child, runs through every chapter. The taboo survives partly by refusing to let anyone say the first without being heard as saying the second, and much of the book's constructive work is to hold the two apart.

Following the series template, the book steelmans the taboo first and at full strength (Chapter 2): it may protect children too young to hold the distinction, families that run on unspoken forbearance, parents facing a punishing climate, and the fragile capacity to commit to an unforeseeable future. That steelman is weighed, not dismissed. Later chapters trace the taboo's origins across four historical layers (Chapter 3), its everyday mechanisms, the script, the punishment of deviation, the linguistic trap, the reward asymmetry, the internalized audience (Chapter 4), the evidence and its honest limits (Chapter 5), the hidden costs (Chapter 6), who bears the pressure, especially mothers (Chapter 7), the strongest objections to the book's own thesis (Chapter 8), four case studies (Chapter 9), the adjacent biases (Chapter 10), and what honest speech would look like (Chapter 11).

The book takes no position on whether anyone should have children, and its argument is compatible with most parents being genuinely glad. Its target is not reproduction but the corruption of the information on which reproduction is chosen. Breaking the silence is not anti-child and not anti-parent; it is pro-honesty. A prospective parent who hears the whole of what earlier parents could not say, and chooses parenthood anyway, has been served by the lifting of the silence, not thwarted by it. The things we don't say do not thereby cease to be true; they only cease to be available to the people who need them.

② The book in twelve chapters

Each chapter opens with its own summary. Note chapter 2: every volume states the strongest case for the thing it criticises before laying a finger on it — and chapter 8 turns the critique back on itself.

CH 1Naming the bias

The bias examined in this book is not a feeling and not a statistic. It is a prohibition on speech, the socially enforced rule that a parent may not publicly admit regret or serious ambivalence about having become a parent, together with the downstream distortion that rule produces in the shared pool of knowledge about what parenthood is like. We can name it the regret taboo, or, in the vocabulary of the study of silence, a case of enforced preference falsification around reproduction. The one-sentence thesis of this book: because admitting parental regret is socially prohibited, the regret that exists is systematically driven out of public speech, and prospective parents are therefore deciding on the basis of a record from which an entire class of honest testimony has been removed.

CH 2The strongest case for it (steelman)The strongest case against this book

Before criticizing the taboo, we must state, at full strength and first, the case that the taboo is good, that the prohibition on voicing parental regret serves real and important functions, and that a world without it would be worse. This is not a courtesy owed to symmetry. If the steelman holds, the book fails, and it deserves to be built as if by its most capable advocate. A critique that has not felt the full weight of the thing it criticizes has not earned the right to proceed.

CH 3Where it comes from

A prohibition this powerful is not an accident of etiquette. It has origins, and tracing them shows both why the taboo is so deep and why it is not the timeless law of nature it presents itself as. The silence around parental regret is built from at least four historical layers, each laid down for its own reasons and each still exerting force.

CH 4How it operates today

The taboo does not announce itself. There is no rule written down, no authority that enforces it. It operates through the ordinary texture of everyday life, through what gets said and what gets swallowed, what is rewarded and what is punished, in a thousand small interactions. Understanding the bias means understanding these concrete mechanisms, because it is in them that an abstract prohibition becomes a lived constraint.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

What can actually be known about a silence? The methodological problem is acute: the thing we most want to measure is, by definition, the thing people will not say. Any direct survey asking "do you regret having children?" runs straight into the taboo it is trying to measure: respondents will falsify their answer for the same reasons they falsify it everywhere else. This does not make evidence impossible, but it demands that we read it with the distortion in mind, and that we treat the methods that reduce the distortion as more trustworthy than the ones that don't.

CH 6The hidden costs

Grant, for the sake of argument, the entire steelman of Chapter 2: the taboo protects children, families, and the fragile capacity for commitment. Even granting all of it, the silence carries costs, costs that are hidden because a silence, by its nature, hides what it costs. Naming them is the analytical core of this book.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

A taboo does not press equally on everyone. Its weight falls hardest along the existing fault lines of gender, culture, class, and circumstance, and mapping that distribution reveals both who is most silenced and why the silence has proven so durable.

CH 8The counter-arguments to the critiqueObjections to the critique

This book's thesis, that the taboo on parental regret harms prospective parents and the regretful alike by corrupting the shared record, invites serious objections. Intellectual honesty requires stating them at their strongest and answering them without evasion. Some land partial blows. The reader should weigh them and decide.

CH 9Case studies / illustrations

Abstractions about silence become clearer through instances in which the silence, and its breaking, can actually be observed. Four illustrations, drawn from the public record and from recurring patterns, show the taboo in operation.

CH 10Adjacent biases

This bias does not stand alone. It interlocks with others in the series, and seeing the connections both clarifies its boundaries and shows how the pronatalist system reinforces itself across mechanisms. The point of this chapter is to map the joints without duplicating what the neighboring volumes cover in full.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

What would it look like to reason and speak honestly about parenthood once the taboo is named? The goal here is not to prescribe feelings, still less to encourage anyone to voice regret to a child or to abandon the genuine protections the steelman identified. It is to describe what clear-eyed, non-coerced handling of this bias looks like, for individuals, for those around them, and for institutions.

CH 12Conclusion

This book has argued a single, deliberately narrow claim: that a powerful social taboo forbids the public admission of parental regret, that this prohibition operates through well-understood mechanisms of preference falsification and the spiral of silence, and that its effect is to remove an entire class of honest testimony from the record on which prospective parents reason. The claim is narrow by design. It does not assert that regret is common; it is compatible with regret being rare. It asserts only that whatever regret exists is systematically hidden, in a direction that consistently favors reproduction, and that the hiding is a genuine epistemic harm regardless of the underlying frequency.

Show all 15 terms as a list
Regret taboo
The socially enforced prohibition on publicly admitting regret or serious ambivalence about having become a parent. The central object of this book.
Preference falsification
(Timur Kuran, Private Truths, Public Lies, 1995) The routine act of publicly expressing a preference one does not privately hold, because the social cost of honesty is high. Aggregated across many people, it can make a widely held private view appear nonexistent.
Spiral of silence
(Elisabeth Noelle-Neumann) The tendency of people who perceive their view as a losing or socially unsafe minority to withhold it; their silence makes the view look rarer, which pressures the next person into silence, tightening the spiral.
Impossibility-of-saying-it structure
The linguistic trap by which "I regret the parental role" cannot be voiced without risking being heard as "I reject my child," forcing the regretful parent into silence.
Regretting the role vs. loving the child
The central distinction the book insists on throughout: regret about the transformation of one's life is compatible with, and usually coexists with, genuine love for the specific child who exists.
Enforced silence
Absence of testimony produced not by there being nothing to say but by the active prohibition and punishment of its expression. Distinct from mere absence.
Directional distortion
A bias that skews the shared record consistently in one direction (here, toward reproduction), as opposed to random noise that merely adds error without a systematic tilt.
Internalized audience
The stage at which a person enforces a taboo on themselves without needing any external audience, suppressing the forbidden thought before it fully forms. The taboo's most complete victory.
Script
The rehearsed, socially correct answer to how parenthood is going ("exhausted but so happy," "hard but the best thing") that performs and re-enforces the sanctioned feeling in everyday exchanges.
Sanctioned struggle-talk
Permitted complaint about the difficulty of parenting that always resolves into affirmation. It functions as a release valve, letting pressure escape while keeping the one truly forbidden sentence — "I would not do it again" — unsaid.
Cascade
The rapid emergence of previously suppressed testimony once a channel offers anonymity and the visible company of others, reversing the spiral of silence. Evidence that the prior silence was enforced rather than empty.
Sacralization
The framing of parenthood, and especially motherhood, as sacred and identity-constituting. It supplies the taboo's moral temperature and makes regret register as sacrilege rather than mere complaint. (Adjacent theme: Book 028.)
Linguistic trap
See impossibility-of-saying-it structure: the collapse, in a listener's ear, of a statement about the role into a statement about the child.
Epistemic harm
Damage to the quality of shared knowledge, distinct from harm to anyone's feelings; here, the corruption of the record from which prospective parents reason.
Regretting motherhood
The specific, articulate, non-pathological form of regret about the maternal role documented in Orna Donath's research (2015/2017), distinct from depression and from not loving one's children; the study that broke the taboo into public view.

⑥ Sources

What this volume draws on. Where the evidence is thin or contested, the book says so rather than reaching for a number.

  • Kuran, Timur. Private Truths, Public Lies: The Social Consequences of Preference Falsification. Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press, 1995. — The foundational treatment of how people misrepresent private preferences under social pressure, and how this produces public unanimity that does not exist privately.
  • Donath, Orna. Regretting Motherhood: A Study. Berkeley, CA: North Atlantic Books, 2017. — The book-length study of mothers who, asked whether they would become mothers again, said no, while frequently affirming love for their specific children; the work whose reception demonstrated the taboo's strength better than any argument could. First developed in Hebrew and German, and preceded by the 2015 journal article listed below.
  • Noelle-Neumann, Elisabeth. The Spiral of Silence: Public Opinion — Our Social Skin. Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 1984. — The account of how people who perceive their view as an unsafe minority withhold it, reinforcing the appearance of consensus and silencing the next person.
Directions the volume points to (not pinned citations)
  • Donath, Orna. "Regretting Motherhood: A Sociopolitical Analysis." Signs: Journal of Women in Culture and Society, 2015. — The article that started the conversation, and the one worth reading before any of the coverage of it.
  • Hays, Sharon. The Cultural Contradictions of Motherhood. Yale University Press, 1996. — On the ideology of intensive mothering, and the background to Chapter 3's account of how parenting intensified.
  • The scholarship on the reception, virality, and cross-national spread of the #regrettingmotherhood conversation. This book cites the pattern rather than any single study, because the pattern is what the argument needs.
  • The survey research reporting the proportion of parents who say they would, or would not, have children again. Read the question wordings before the numbers. The estimates run from single digits to low double digits depending on how the question is asked and where it is asked, which is a finding about the taboo as much as about the regret.
  • The empirical literature applying preference-falsification and spiral-of-silence models to reproductive, family, and parenting attitudes. Both mechanisms are well established in political and social-attitude research; their application to parental regret in particular is an extension this book makes on argument rather than one it found already made, and I have tried to say so wherever it matters.
  • Donath's interviews, in her own presentation of them, for the words of the women themselves. This book quotes no one directly, which is a deliberate choice: people who broke a silence at real cost should be read where they chose to speak, and not paraphrased into someone else's argument.
  • A note on sourcing: This book's argument is built so as not to depend on any frequency figure, and that is the point rather than a convenience. How many parents regret parenthood is not known, cannot presently be known, and would not settle the question if it were. A taboo strong enough to keep people from saying the thing is also strong enough to keep the surveys from measuring it. Where the reader wants a number here and does not get one, the missing number is the subject.
