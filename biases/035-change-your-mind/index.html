BIAS 035

You'll Change Your Mind: The Great Dismissal

Part III · Cultural & Social Scripts · The Great Dismissal

The bias: The refusal to treat a stated choice not to reproduce as final or real.

The powerful can, and often do, exercise a distorting influence on the credibility we grant to the powerless." — a paraphrase of the argument in Miranda Fricker, Epistemic Injustice: Power and the Ethics of Knowing (2007) ## Preface to this volume There is a sentence that almost every person who says they do not want children will hear, often many times, from many mouths: You'll change your mind. It arrives in various dress: You're too young to know what you want. Everyone says that at first. Wait until you meet the right person. It's just a phase. You'll regret it. Once your clock starts ticking, you'll feel differently. The dressing changes; the structure does not. The structure is this: a person states a decision about their own life, and the listener declines to accept that the statement means what it says. The decision is heard not as a decision but as a way station, a temporary error the speaker will grow out of on the way to the true destination, which is parenthood. This book is about that refusal. Its name is the great dismissal: the treatment of a stated intention not to have children as provisional by default, and of the pronatalist outcome as the inevitable expression of the person's real self. The bias is not the asking; the interrogation of the childfree about their reasons is a separate norm, examined in Book 027. It is not the thought-terminating claim that parenthood is unimaginable from the outside, "it's different when they're yours," examined in Book 036. The great dismissal is narrower and, in a way, sharper: it is what happens after the person has answered. They have stated their position clearly. And the position is set aside, unheard, as if the stating of it carried no authority at all. I want to be exact about the stance of this volume, because the subject invites caricature. It is a plain fact that some people who say in their twenties that they do not want children go on to have them and are glad. Intentions about the far future are not perfectly stable, and elders who say "I've seen people change" are frequently telling the truth about what they have seen. Chapter 2 gives this observation its full and fair weight before any critique begins; a book that could not survive stating the other side honestly would not be worth writing. The critique that follows does not deny that minds change. It denies that the possibility of change licenses treating a present, competent, stated decision as void. That move, from "you might" to "you will," and from "you will" to "so what you're telling me now doesn't count," is where the bias lives. The tools this book uses are drawn mostly from epistemology and from ordinary observation. The central concept is testimonial injustice, from the philosopher Miranda Fricker: the wrong done to someone when a prejudice causes a hearer to give their word less credibility than it deserves. When a thirty-year-old woman says she does not want children and is told she will change her mind, she is being handed exactly this wrong. Her testimony about her own life is discounted, not on the evidence, but on a prior conviction about what women, or the young, or "everyone," really want underneath. A second tool is the double standard, which does the diagnostic work of a control group: no one says "you'll change your mind" to a newly pregnant twenty-two-year-old, though her intention is at least as consequential and, on the raw statistics, at least as likely to be attended by difficulty. The asymmetry reveals that the dismissal is not really about the instability of intentions in general. It is about one direction of intention in particular. A word on tone is owed before we begin. This is not a book against parents, and it is emphatically not a book against the people who say "you'll change your mind." Most of them mean well; many of them are the people we love most; and a good number of them will turn out, in individual cases, to have guessed correctly. The aim is not to convict them of malice, which is almost always absent, but to make visible a habit of speech so ordinary that neither speaker nor hearer usually registers what it does. Bias, in the sense this series uses the word, is rarely a matter of ill will. It is a matter of defaults so deeply grooved that they operate beneath the level of intention, shaping who gets believed before anyone has decided to believe or disbelieve. The great dismissal is one of those grooves. Naming it is the first step toward being able to choose, rather than merely to reach for, the words we use when someone tells us how they intend to live. The book proceeds along the series template. It names the bias and separates it from its neighbors; it steelmans the natalist intuition in full; it traces the origins of the dismissal and the mechanisms by which it operates; it weighs the actual evidence on the stability of childfree intentions and on regret, which turns out to be more favorable to the childfree than the folk wisdom assumes; it counts the hidden costs, distributes the pressure, answers the objections to its own thesis, illustrates with cases, maps the adjacent biases, and asks what it would look like to take a person's stated reproductive decision at face value. Throughout, the target is the bias, never the parent and never the child. To notice that "you'll change your mind" is a way of not listening is not to say that changing one's mind is shameful, or that having children is a mistake. It is only to say that a person's account of their own future deserves, at minimum, to be heard as an account and not dismissed as a symptom. ## 1. Naming the bias The great dismissal is the disposition to treat a person's stated intention not to have children as non-final by default, provisional, immature, or simply mistaken, and to treat the eventual arrival at parenthood as the true and inevitable outcome that the person will, in time, recognize as their own. Two elements deserve emphasis, because together they distinguish this bias from a merely optimistic guess about the future. The first is invalidation of the stated choice. The dismissal does more than predict that the person may come to want children. It declines to credit the decision they have actually announced. The announcement is reclassified, in the moment, from a report of a settled position to a report of a passing state: a phase, a fear, a not-yet-knowing. The second element is the presumption of conversion: the assumption that there exists a real self, latent in the person, whose desires run toward children, and that the childfree stance is a temporary veil over that self. The two elements reinforce each other. Because the true self is assumed to be pronatalist, the present stance can be discounted; because the present stance is discounted, the assumed true self is never tested against it. It is worth pausing on how unusual this structure is when applied to other decisions. If a friend told you they had decided to become a doctor, or to move abroad, or to leave a religion they were raised in, you might question them, worry for them, even predict they would come to regret it. But you would not ordinarily treat their statement as evidence of a passing confusion soon to be corrected by their "real self." You would treat the statement as what it is: a report, from the person best positioned to make it, about the shape they intend their life to take. The great dismissal withholds from the childfree declaration precisely the presumption of authorship that we extend, almost automatically, to nearly every other consequential self-report. That withholding is the phenomenon this book is about. It helps to fix the boundaries by contrast with adjacent biases in this series. Book 027 concerns the interrogation norm, the felt entitlement to demand that the childfree justify themselves, to ask "but why?" and "what if?" The interrogation is about extracting an answer. The great dismissal is about what is done with the answer once given: it is the refusal to let the answer settle the matter. One can be dismissed without ever being interrogated (a stranger simply says "you'll change your mind" and moves on), and one can be interrogated by someone who, in the end, does accept the answer. Book 036 concerns "it's different when they're yours," a distinct thought-terminator that grants the childfree person's present feelings but insists those feelings cannot survive contact with one's own child. That claim is about the alleged transformative power of the experience; the great dismissal is about the alleged unreality of the decision. And Book 099, on regret and the fear of it, supplies one of the great dismissal's favorite scripts, you'll regret it, but treats regret as its main subject, where here it is one instrument among several. A further clarification guards against a natural misreading. The great dismissal is not the mere holding of a private probabilistic belief. A listener may privately estimate that a given young person is, statistically, somewhat likely to have children after all, and hold that estimate quietly, without it touching how they treat the person's word. That private estimate is not the bias. The bias is the conversational and practical act of setting the stated decision aside, of treating it as not yet counting, of planning and speaking as though the announced future were a placeholder for the real one. The distinction matters because it locates the wrong precisely: not in what one guesses about another's future, which one is free to do, but in the refusal to grant the other's present testimony the standing of testimony. A person can believe you might change and still believe you, right now, about what you have decided. The dismissal is the collapse of that second belief into the first. The one-sentence thesis of this book: "You'll change your mind" is not primarily a prediction; it is a refusal to accept a competent person's stated decision as authoritative over their own life, and it is applied in one direction only, toward children, never away from them. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 1 Quiz. 1. The strongest case for it (steelman) The honest way to write this book is to concede, at the outset and without flinching, that the people who say "you'll change your mind" are not simply wrong about the world. They are often drawing on something real, and the strongest version of their view deserves to be stated in full before it is answered. Begin with the plain fact of the matter: minds do change. Intentions about whether to have children are not fixed points; they are forecasts about a distant, transformed version of oneself, made under conditions of great uncertainty. A person of twenty-two is predicting the preferences of a person of thirty-five whom they have never met and cannot fully imagine: someone with a different partner, a different body, a different set of friends who have themselves become parents, a different relationship to time and mortality. Forecasting one's own future desires is a notoriously error-prone business; we systematically mispredict how we will feel, and the childfree intention is not exempt from this. Some meaningful fraction of people who state, sincerely and competently, that they will never have children go on to have them, and a good number of those report that they are glad they did. That is not folklore. It happens. The point about mispredicting our future selves deserves its full weight, because it is not a folk superstition but a well-documented feature of human psychology. We are poor forecasters of our own future preferences; we underestimate how much we will change; we imagine the future self as essentially the present self in different circumstances, when in fact the future self may want quite different things. If this is true generally, it is true of reproductive intentions too. The twenty-five-year-old who cannot imagine wanting children is, in part, failing to imagine the person they will become, and the elder who insists that person is coming is pointing, however clumsily, at a real limit in the young person's self-knowledge. To deny this outright would be to pretend that the childfree are uniquely exempt from a fallibility that touches everyone. Now add the epistemic position of the elder. A parent or grandparent who says "you'll change your mind" is, very often, generalizing from real observation. They have watched cohorts of young people over decades. They have seen the friend who swore off children at twenty-five holding a baby at thirty-three and radiant. They have, perhaps, been that person themselves, remembering their disdain for the whole enterprise, and remembering the day the disdain quietly dissolved. From the inside, this does not feel like prejudice. It feels like the transmission of hard-won pattern recognition from someone who has seen the movie before to someone who is still in the opening scene. There is a real asymmetry of experience, and it would be strange to pretend it counts for nothing. The elder is not inventing the pattern; they are reporting something they have genuinely observed, and the fact that it is inconvenient to the critique does not make it false. There is, further, a genuine kindness that can motivate the sentence. Reproductive windows narrow with age, especially for women, and some decisions foreclose others irreversibly. An older person who has watched a friend grieve a door that closed while they were sure they did not want to walk through it may say "you'll change your mind" as a clumsy attempt to keep that door open a little longer, to protect the young person from a regret the elder fears on their behalf. The motive is not always the imposition of a script; sometimes it is love wearing the only words it has. It is worth dwelling on this, because the critique to come will not deny the love; it will only ask whether the love is well served by the words. A parent who has themselves felt the joy of a child they nearly did not have, and who cannot bear the thought that someone they love might miss it, is not acting from a desire to control. They are acting from a desire to give. That the gift is misdelivered does not make the impulse behind it ignoble. Finally, the steelman can point to a structural fact: society must reproduce itself, and a culture that took every youthful declaration of childlessness as final would be, at the level of intuition, forecasting its own dissolution. There is a diffuse, half-conscious cultural interest in not letting the young close the reproductive question too early or too casually. Read charitably, "you'll change your mind" is one of the soft social pressures by which a community keeps the option of the next generation alive. A society entirely without such pressures is not obviously a society that would persist; and a person who feels the pull of that structural concern is not simply prejudiced but responsive, however dimly, to a real collective stake. All of this is real, and none of it is dismissed in what follows. The task of the rest of the book is not to deny that minds change or that elders have seen things. It is to ask whether these truths license what "you'll change your mind" actually does, which is to set aside, in the present, a competent person's stated account of their own life. The steelman establishes that the dismisser has reasons; it does not establish that the reasons reach as far as the dismissal goes. That gap, between what the reasons support and what the sentence performs, is the whole subject of the chapters that follow. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 2 Quiz. 1. Where it comes from The great dismissal did not arise from nowhere. Several deep currents feed it, and naming them helps to see why a sentence that purports to be a neutral prediction so reliably runs in a single direction. The oldest current is demographic and religious. For most of human history, and in most religious traditions that shaped the cultures we inherit, childbearing was not one life-path among several but the path: the expected, blessed, and often commanded outcome of adult life. "Be fruitful and multiply" is not a suggestion in the texts that carried it. Where marriage-and-children was the near-universal template, a young person's declaration against children could only be read as an aberration to be corrected, because there was no recognized life within which it made sense. The template long outlived the theology. Even in thoroughly secular settings, the assumption that parenthood is the destination and childlessness a detour is a fossil of the era in which that was simply true for almost everyone. When a wholly non-religious relative says "you'll change your mind," they are, without knowing it, speaking a sentence shaped by centuries in which the alternative was scarcely conceivable. The second current is the intuition, older than any doctrine, that reproduction is what people are for. Whatever one thinks of evolutionary framings, it is uncontroversial that we are descended from an unbroken line of successful reproducers, and that cultures pervasively encode the expectation of childbearing in their scripts, rites, and honors. This lends the pronatalist outcome the feel of a natural terminus, the thing a life "arrives at," and makes any stated deviation feel like a temporary fault in an otherwise self-correcting mechanism. The presumption of conversion is, in part, this intuition wearing modern clothes: the belief that nature will, in time, reassert the "real" desire beneath the aberrant declaration. It is worth being careful here, because the move from "we are descended from reproducers" to "therefore each person must really want to reproduce" is a leap the intuition makes silently and illegitimately. That our ancestors reproduced tells us why the desire is common; it does not tell us that the desire is universal, or that its absence in a given person is a malfunction awaiting repair. The intuition treats a statistical tendency as an individual destiny, and it is precisely that slide that the dismissal enacts. The third current is specifically about whose testimony gets discounted, and here the history is gendered. For a long time, and in many places still, women were not treated as fully authoritative narrators of their own wants, especially about reproduction, where their desires were held to be shaped by an inner biology (the "maternal instinct," the "clock") that they might not consciously recognize but that would eventually speak for them. This is the crucial inheritance for our purposes. It supplies a ready-made theory under which a woman's stated preference can be overridden by an assumed deeper preference she does not yet feel. "You'll change your mind" is, in its most common form, this theory in miniature: your word about yourself is provisional because a truer word is coming from within. The theory is powerful precisely because it cannot be refuted by anything the woman says. Whatever she states, the "real" preference is defined as the one that has not yet spoken, and so her present testimony is structurally disqualified in advance. This is not a neutral epistemic posture; it is an inheritance from a long history of treating women's self-reports about their own bodies and futures as data to be interpreted by others rather than as testimony to be believed. A fourth, more recent current is institutional and economic. Modern states and economies with aging populations and pension systems built on continuous reproduction have a material stake in births, and this stake filters down into a cultural mood that treats declared childlessness as a problem to be managed rather than a choice to be respected. Public anxiety about falling fertility, whatever its merits as policy, tends to frame the childfree not as citizens making a legitimate choice but as a shortfall to be reversed, and that framing licenses, at the level of everyday speech, the treatment of any individual's childfree declaration as something that ought to change. None of these currents is a conspiracy; each is simply a channel worn deep by long use, down which the sentence "you'll change your mind" runs so easily that it feels less like a judgment than like water finding its level. The point of naming them is not to assign blame but to explain the strange confidence of the sentence, the way it arrives as if it were obvious, and to show that its obviousness is inherited rather than earned. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 3 Quiz. 1. How it operates today Stripped of its history, the great dismissal survives in ordinary life through a small set of concrete mechanisms: scripts, reclassifications, and conversational moves that do their work quickly and often without any conscious malice. It is worth laying these out precisely, because the bias hides in its own smallness; each individual instance seems too trivial to object to, and it is only when the mechanism is named that the pattern beneath the instances becomes visible. The first mechanism is the reclassification of the statement. When a person says "I don't want children," the dismissing listener performs an instant recategorization: what was offered as a decision is received as a symptom. The linguistic markers are consistent. "It's just a phase" reclassifies the decision as developmental. "You're too young to know" reclassifies it as premature. "Everyone says that at first" reclassifies it as a cliché, a thing people say rather than a thing this person means. In each case the content of the statement is not engaged; its status is downgraded, from testimony to noise. This is the crucial and easily missed feature: the listener does not argue with the decision, does not offer reasons against it, does not treat it as a claim that could be true or false. They reach past it, to a category, and file it there. An argument would at least honor the statement as a statement; the reclassification denies it even that. The second mechanism is the appeal to the hidden true self. Here the listener grants that the person feels as they say, but relocates the real preference somewhere the person cannot currently see it: in their future body ("wait until your clock starts ticking"), in an as-yet-unmet partner ("you'll feel differently with the right person"), or in an experience they have not had ("hold a baby and you'll know"). The stated self is conceded and immediately outranked by an imagined self who wants children. Because that imagined self is by construction inaccessible to the speaker, no present statement can ever refute it. This is what makes the dismissal so frustrating to be on the receiving end of: it is structured to be unfalsifiable from the inside. Whatever the person says, the reply is that the deciding self has not yet arrived. The childfree person is placed in the position of trying to prove a negative about a future they have not lived, which is impossible, and the impossibility is then read as further evidence that they simply do not yet understand themselves. The third mechanism is asymmetric prediction. The sentence is offered as a neutral forecast about the instability of intentions, but it is deployed only against intentions of one kind. The expectant parent is not told "you'll change your mind." The person who wants children is not asked to prove the stability of their desire. Only the childfree declaration triggers the prediction, which reveals that the prediction is not really about instability but about direction. The default outcome is protected; the deviation is treated as reversible. This asymmetry is the single most useful diagnostic in the whole book, because it separates the genuine observation (intentions of all kinds can change) from the biased application (only childfree intentions are treated as not yet counting). If the concern were truly about the volatility of youthful plans, it would fall on the young expectant parent as heavily as on the young childfree one. It does not. It falls in one direction, and the direction is the tell. A fourth mechanism is conversational finality. "You'll change your mind" functions as a thought-terminator: it does not invite discussion, it closes it. There is no good reply. To argue is to seem to protest too much, which is read as confirmation ("she's very defensive about it; she'll come around"). To fall silent is read as assent. The sentence is a small trap, and part of its persistence is that it is socially costly to escape without appearing to prove the dismisser's point. This trap-like quality is worth naming because it explains why the childfree so often simply stop talking about the subject with certain people; the conversation has been rigged so that engagement confirms the dismissal and silence permits it, and the only winning move is not to play. The cost of that, examined later, is a quiet withdrawal that the dismisser rarely notices. These mechanisms operate at every scale, from the family dinner to the clinician's office, where a young person seeking permanent contraception may be told, in effect institutionally, that their stated decision is not to be trusted and must be preserved against for their own future good. They appear online, where a stranger will tell a childfree writer they will change their mind with the same confidence a relative brings to the dinner table. They appear in the offhand remark of a colleague, the raised eyebrow of an acquaintance, the gentle certainty of a friend who has just become a parent. The medium changes; the move is the same. And because the move is so small and so common, it accumulates without anyone deciding that it should, which is exactly the way defaults do their work. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 4 Quiz. 1. The evidence If "you'll change your mind" is offered as a prediction, then it is answerable to evidence, and the evidence is worth stating carefully, including the parts that complicate the critique. A prediction that hides from data is not a prediction at all but a wish; so the fair thing is to ask what actually happens to childfree intentions over time, and to report it whether or not it flatters the argument. Begin with the honest complication. Reproductive intentions are not perfectly stable, and this is well established. Studies that follow people over time find that stated intentions about childbearing revise in both directions: some who intended to have children do not, and some who intended to remain childless later have them. Anyone who claims that a stated childfree intention is a guarantee of a childfree life is overstating the data. Minds move. The steelman of Chapter 2 was not a rhetorical concession; it reflects a real feature of how intentions behave over time. It would be dishonest, and self-defeating, to pretend that the childfree declaration is a contract with the future. It is a statement of present intention, and present intentions, like all of them, are revisable. But the folk wisdom claims much more than "intentions sometimes revise." It claims that the childfree declaration is characteristically unstable, a phase most people grow out of, and that the pronatalist outcome is the reliable destination. On this stronger claim, the weight of the evidence runs the other way. Research on people who are firmly and voluntarily childfree (those who have decided, as opposed to those who are merely postponing or are involuntarily without children) finds that this decision, once genuinely settled, tends to be durable, and that the rate at which such people later reverse and have children is low, not high. The "everyone changes their mind" premise is, for the committed childfree, not supported. There is a large and important difference between "a young person who is unsure and leaning against children may come to want them," which is true and unremarkable, and "a person who has firmly decided against children will predictably reverse," which is what the sentence asserts and what the data do not bear out. The distinction that does most of the work here is between the postponers and the deciders. Much of the impression that the childfree "always change their minds" comes from lumping together two very different groups: those who are delaying children while remaining open to them, and those who have genuinely closed the question. The postponers do frequently go on to have children; that is what postponement means. But their revisions are then read back onto the deciders, as if the two were one population. Once the groups are separated, the picture changes. The genuinely decided are not a way station on the road to parenthood; they are, for the most part, people who have arrived at a stable position and stay there. The folk wisdom's confidence borrows its plausibility from the postponers and then spends it on the deciders, who did not earn it. The evidence on regret is more pointed still, because "you'll regret it" is the dismissal's trump card. Here the pattern is striking and fairly consistent across studies: people who choose to remain childfree report low levels of regret about that decision. Meanwhile, and this is the part the folk wisdom never mentions, a non-trivial share of parents report having experienced regret about becoming parents, a finding difficult to study because it is so heavily stigmatized that many who feel it will not say so. The asymmetry the dismissal assumes, that childfree people will be flooded with regret while parents rarely are, is not what the data show. If anything, the regret risk runs in a direction the sentence never acknowledges. The stigma point is worth pressing: because admitting regret about parenthood is close to unspeakable, the measured rate almost certainly understates the true one, which means the comparison is if anything more favorable to the childfree than the raw numbers suggest. Two honesty flags belong here. First, definitions matter enormously: findings about the "childless" as a whole mix together the voluntarily childfree, the temporarily postponing, and the involuntarily infertile, and these groups differ sharply; the durability result is about the genuinely voluntary, committed group. A study that reports high rates of "childless people later having children" may be measuring the postponers and the once-infertile-now-treated, and tells us little about the committed childfree. Second, decades of social change mean older longitudinal data may understate the stability of childfree intentions today, when the choice is more visible, more supported, and more available as a recognized life-path than it was for the cohorts many older studies followed. A person deciding against children in a culture that offers them models of a childfree life is in a different epistemic position from one deciding against children when no such models existed, and their intention may well be more stable in consequence. The safe summary is this: the evidence does not license the strong prediction. Most committed childfree people do not change their minds; regret does not fall as the folk wisdom expects; and "you'll change your mind" states as near-certainty something the data render, at best, a minority outcome. The sentence claims the authority of experience; the experience, gathered systematically rather than anecdotally, does not support it. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 5 Quiz. 1. The hidden costs Because "you'll change your mind" feels mild, a bit of unsolicited optimism, easily brushed off, its costs are easy to overlook. They are real, and they accumulate. The mildness is in fact part of the problem: a cost that arrives in small, deniable increments is a cost no single instance of which seems worth objecting to, and so the whole is borne without ever being tallied. The first and most fundamental cost is epistemic. To be told, repeatedly, that one's own account of one's own life is not to be believed is to suffer what Fricker calls testimonial injustice: a wrong done to a person specifically in their capacity as a knower. The childfree person is not disagreed with so much as treated as incompetent to report their own settled preference. Over time, this has a corrosive secondary effect Fricker also names: the person may come to doubt their own standing as a reliable narrator of their wants, second-guessing a decision not because new reasons have appeared but because their word has been so consistently discounted. The cost here is to a person's confidence in their own mind, which is not a small thing to be charged for a decision one has already made. Fricker's insight is that this is genuinely damaging and not merely unpleasant: the capacity to be believed about oneself is bound up with one's standing as a rational agent, and to have it chronically withheld is to be pushed, quietly, toward a diminished sense of one's own authority. It is possible to talk a person out of trusting their own settled judgment simply by refusing, often enough, to trust it for them. A second cost is the foreclosure of real conversation. Because the dismissal is a thought-terminator, it forecloses exactly the discussion that might, on the natalist's own terms, be valuable. If an elder genuinely believes a young person is deciding too hastily, the way to help is to open a conversation about it, and "you'll change your mind" slams that conversation shut. The sentence that claims to care about the young person's future self makes it impossible to actually explore that future together. What could have been counsel becomes a verdict. The irony is sharp: the person most convinced that the childfree decision deserves further thought is, by using the dismissal, the person who most reliably prevents that further thought from happening. Real exploration requires that the young person's current view be taken seriously enough to be examined; the dismissal declines to take it seriously at all, and so nothing is examined. A third cost is distortion of the person's actual planning. A decision that is never treated as real is a decision no one helps you build a life around. The childfree person whose declaration is perpetually provisional in the eyes of family, employers, and clinicians finds that the practical scaffolding a life-plan needs (recognition, contingency, support) is quietly withheld, held in reserve against the day they "come to their senses." Their stated future is treated as a placeholder, and so it is under-supported relative to the pronatalist future everyone assumes is really coming. This is not a matter of feelings alone. It has concrete consequences: financial planning oriented around a life that no one around you will help you plan, medical decisions deferred or refused, career trajectories shaped by an assumption you have explicitly disavowed. The person is asked to live a life whose reality the people around them have declined to grant, which means living it with less help than the assumed-default life would receive. A fourth cost falls on the relationship itself. To dismiss someone's stated decision is, however warmly meant, to communicate that you do not accept a basic fact about who they are. Repeated across years, this quietly erodes trust and intimacy; the childfree person learns which people cannot be told the truth without receiving a correction, and adjusts what they share accordingly. A daughter stops mentioning her plans to a mother who always answers with the dismissal; a friend edits herself around the friend who cannot hear it; whole regions of a life go unspoken, not because they are shameful but because speaking of them invites the same small refusal every time. The dismisser rarely notices the distance opening, because from their side nothing unkind was ever said. That is the signature of this bias: its costs are borne almost entirely by the person dismissed, and remain nearly invisible to the person doing the dismissing. The relationship cools by a degree the dismisser never registers, and the person who cooled it believes, sincerely, that they were only being loving. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 6 Quiz. 1. Who bears the pressure The great dismissal is not distributed evenly. It falls hardest on those whose testimony a culture is already primed to discount, which means the bias tends to compound existing inequalities in who is believed. This is worth stating plainly, because it locates the harm not as a free-floating unfairness but as one that lands, predictably, on the people least equipped to absorb it. Gender is the largest axis. Women hear "you'll change your mind" far more often than men, and hear it in a more specific and more overriding form, because it can be attached to a supposed biological authority (the maternal instinct, the ticking clock) that is imagined to know a woman's real desires better than she does. A man who says he does not want children is more likely to be taken at his word, or at most teased; a woman who says the same is more likely to be told that her body will, in time, correct her. The dismissal thus reactivates an old pattern in which women's reproductive testimony is treated as provisional pending confirmation from an inner biology they are not credited with understanding. The asymmetry between how the identical sentence lands on a man and on a woman is itself revealing: if the concern were genuinely about the instability of intentions, it would fall equally on both, since men's intentions are no more stable than women's. That it falls harder on women shows that the operative theory is not about intentions in general but about women in particular, and about the specifically female "real self" that is imagined to want children whatever the woman says. Age is the second axis, and it interacts with the first. The young are told they are "too young to know," which is a general-purpose discount on the testimony of anyone below a shifting threshold, a threshold that has a way of moving upward just ahead of the person, so that they are somehow never quite old enough for the decision to count until, conveniently, the reproductive window has narrowed. The maddening feature of the age discount is precisely its mobility: the twenty-two-year-old is too young, the twenty-eight-year-old still has time to change, the thirty-four-year-old is warned the window is closing, and at no point in this progression is there a moment when the decision is simply accepted as made. The threshold is not a real epistemic boundary but a moving disqualification, and the young childfree woman sits at the intersection of both axes and receives the dismissal in its most concentrated form: too young to know, and possessed of a body that will overrule her, both at once. Relationship status modulates the pressure. The partnered childfree person is often told they will change their mind together, or that the right partner has not yet done their transformative work; the single childfree person's decision is treated as merely the absence of an opportunity rather than a choice, and so as especially unreal. In both cases the stated decision is subordinated to an imagined future configuration of the person's life. The single person hears that they simply have not met the person who will change everything; the partnered person hears that the relationship will, in time, produce the desire it has not yet produced. Either way the decision is not credited as a decision but read as a stage in a story whose ending the listener has already written. Culture and community matter too. In families and communities where childbearing carries heavy religious or collective significance, the dismissal is intensified and moralized: the stated decision is doubted and then treated as a temporary lapse from duty. Here the person may face something beyond skepticism: a kind of loving refusal to hear, sustained by an entire community, which is far harder to withstand than a single relative's remark. When every voice a person trusts speaks the dismissal in unison, and speaks it as care, the internalizing pressure is enormous; there is no outside vantage from which the person's own testimony is affirmed, and the effort required to hold one's stated decision as real against a whole community's gentle disbelief is correspondingly greater. The general shape is clear. The great dismissal presses most on those already least likely to be believed: women more than men, the young more than the old, those embedded in high-expectation communities more than those outside them. It is, in this sense, an amplifier: it takes a person's existing credibility deficit and applies it precisely at the moment they are trying to state one of the most consequential decisions of their life. The people who most need to be believed about this decision are, by the bias's own distribution, the people least likely to be. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 7 Quiz. 1. The counter-arguments to the critique A book that steelmans its opponent in Chapter 2 owes its opponent a second hearing here, against its own thesis. Several objections have force, and the honest course is to state each at its strongest before replying. The first objection: isn't this just honest realism? Intentions do change; the evidence in Chapter 5 conceded as much. If a fair fraction of young declared-childless people go on to have children, then "you'll change your mind" is sometimes simply true, and warning someone of a real possibility is not an injustice but a service. The reply is exact. The critique does not target the observation that some people change their minds; it targets the treatment of a present stated decision as thereby void. "Some people in your position later revise" is a true and respectful thing to say. "You'll change your mind" is a different speech act: it converts a minority possibility into a confident prediction and uses that prediction to decline to credit the decision the person has actually stated. One can be a realist about the instability of intentions and still take a person's present decision at face value. The bias is not realism; it is the extra, unearned step from "you might" to "so this doesn't count." A realist reports a probability and leaves the authority where it lies; the dismisser converts the probability into a warrant to overrule. The difference is not a quibble about tone. It is the difference between informing a person and refusing to believe them. The second objection: aren't the elders' experience and motive worth something? An older person really has seen minds change and may really be acting from love. Granted, and Chapter 2 granted it fully. But experience of a pattern does not confer the right to override an individual's testimony about themselves, and love does not license disbelief. The kind thing to do with hard-won pattern recognition is to offer it as counsel ("here is something I've seen; it may or may not be you"), not to wield it as a verdict that closes the conversation. The motive can be good and the speech act still wrong. We accept this readily in other domains: a doctor's experience does not entitle them to override a competent patient's informed refusal, and a parent's experience does not entitle them to choose their adult child's spouse. Experience earns a hearing, not a veto. The elder's decades of observation are a genuine asset when offered as a gift the young person can weigh, and a genuine wrong when imposed as a ruling the young person cannot appeal. The third objection: isn't the double-standard argument overdrawn? Perhaps the reason no one says "you'll change your mind" to expectant parents is simply that having a child is irreversible while remaining childless is reversible, so warnings only make sense in the reversible direction. This is the strongest objection, and it deserves credit for identifying a genuine asymmetry. There really is a difference between the two directions: the person who has a child cannot undo it, while the person who remains childless can, for a time, reverse course, and it is not unreasonable to think warnings belong where the door can still be closed. But it proves less than it claims. First, the childfree decision is not always reversible; for the person seeking permanent contraception, or the woman near the end of her reproductive window, it is precisely as irreversible as the parent's, yet the dismissal intensifies rather than relents. If reversibility were the true concern, the dismissal should ease as the decision becomes irreversible; instead it grows more insistent, which suggests the concern is not really reversibility at all. Second, if the concern were truly reversibility, the natural speech act would be "keep your options open," not "your stated preference is not real." "Keep your options open" respects the person's current decision while flagging its revisability; "you'll change your mind" denies the current decision outright. The double standard is not fully explained by reversibility, and the residue it leaves, the presumption that only childlessness needs correcting, is the bias itself. The fourth objection: doesn't the critique risk the opposite error, treating every youthful declaration as sacrosanct and discouraging people from staying open? No. To take a stated decision at face value is not to forbid its revision; it is to let the person, not the listener, be the author of any revision. Openness that the person chooses is autonomy; openness imposed by a listener who will not believe them is the dismissal wearing a friendlier mask. The book does not ask anyone to treat their twenty-two-year-old self's declaration as binding on their thirty-five-year-old self. It asks only that the authority to revise belong to the person who holds the decision, and that others believe them in the meantime. A person who stays open because they have chosen to is exercising exactly the self-authorship the book defends; a person kept "open" by the refusal of others to credit their decision is being denied it. The two look similar from outside and are opposite in what they do to the person. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 8 Quiz. 1. Case studies / illustrations Four brief illustrations show the great dismissal at work across different settings, without standing in for data. Each is composite and hypothetical, chosen to display the structure of the bias rather than to report a particular life; the point of each is not the drama but the shape of the move being made. The dinner table. A woman of thirty tells her extended family she and her partner have decided not to have children. An aunt smiles and says, "Oh, you'll change your mind, everyone says that." The sentence is offered warmly and closes the topic; there is no reply that does not sound defensive. Note the structure: the woman has stated a decision reached with her partner over years, and it is received as a thing "everyone says," reclassified from a considered position to a reflexive cliché. The aunt is not cruel and does not think she has done anything but express affection. Notice, too, the trap in miniature. If the woman lets it pass, her silence is read as the beginning of coming around. If she pushes back, her insistence is read as protesting too much, the very defensiveness that supposedly precedes conversion. There is no move available to her that the frame cannot absorb as confirmation. This is the bias in its purest, mildest, and most common form: no malice, no argument, no engagement with what was actually said, and no exit that does not seem to prove the aunt's point. The clinic. A woman in her mid-twenties requests a permanent contraceptive procedure. The clinician, sincerely concerned for her future self, is reluctant: she is young; she might change her mind; she might one day regret foreclosing the option. Her stated, competent, repeated decision is treated as something to be protected against rather than acted upon. Here the dismissal has become institutional, encoded not in a relative's remark but in a professional's caution, and its effect is that an adult's clear decision about her own body is subordinated to a hypothetical future self the clinician imagines on her behalf. What makes this case sharp is that the same clinician would not hesitate to perform an irreversible procedure the young woman requested in the other direction, nor would they refuse a young man the equivalent operation with equal readiness. The asymmetry that runs through the whole book reappears here in its most consequential form, because here the dismissal goes past wounding and withholds a medical service a competent adult has requested, on the grounds that her decision might not survive a future the clinician has imagined for her. The workplace. A young employee mentions to a manager that she is not planning to have children, in the context of long-term projects. The manager files this as provisional ("she'll settle down"), and the informal calculus of who gets stretch assignments and long-horizon roles quietly proceeds on the assumption that she will after all take parental leave in a few years. Her stated plan is not believed enough to be planned around, and so she is under-invested in relative to a future everyone assumes is really coming. The dismissal here costs her nothing said aloud and something real in opportunity. And it is nearly impossible to contest, because the manager will never announce the reasoning; the stretch assignment simply goes to someone else, for reasons that can always be given another name. The disbelief is invisible even to the manager, who would be genuinely puzzled to be told he had discounted her word, and who believes he is merely being prudent about a future he takes himself to understand better than she does. The self. A man in his early thirties has said for a decade that he does not want children and has meant it. But he has been told he will change his mind so many times, by so many people he loves, that he has begun to wonder whether he is missing something in himself, not because any new reason has appeared, but because his own testimony has been so consistently overruled that he has half-adopted the overruling. This is testimonial injustice completing its circuit: the discount applied from outside has been internalized, and the person now doubts his own settled mind on the strength of others' refusal to believe it. He is not reconsidering because he has found a reason to; he is reconsidering because he has been disbelieved so often that disbelief has begun to feel like information. No one intended this outcome, which is exactly why it is worth naming. Each person who told him he would change his mind meant only to share a kindly certainty; the aggregate of their certainties is a man estranged from his own clear preference, unable to tell whether the doubt he now feels is his own or merely the residue of everyone else's. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 9 Quiz. 1. Adjacent biases The great dismissal does not act alone; it interlocks with several other biases in this series, and seeing the joints keeps this book in its lane. Mapping the neighbors is not mere bookkeeping; it prevents the bias from being inflated into a theory of everything, and it clarifies exactly which small move this book is about. Its nearest neighbor is Book 027, on the interrogation norm, the felt entitlement to demand that the childfree justify their decision. Interrogation and dismissal are sequential moves in the same social encounter. First the childfree person is asked to account for themselves (027); then, once they have, the account is set aside (035). The two can appear together so smoothly that they feel like one gesture, but they are distinct: interrogation is about compelling an answer, dismissal about refusing to credit it. One can occur without the other. A relative may demand your reasons and then, hearing them, actually change their view of you, which is interrogation without dismissal; a stranger may say "you'll change your mind" and walk on, never having asked a thing, which is dismissal without interrogation. Keeping them apart matters because the remedies differ: the cure for interrogation is to stop demanding accounts, while the cure for dismissal is to believe the accounts once given. A second neighbor is Book 036, "it's different when they're yours." This is the great dismissal's more specific cousin. Where the great dismissal says the decision is not real, "it's different when they're yours" concedes the decision but insists the feeling behind it cannot survive contact with one's own child. Both are thought-terminators; both relocate the person's true preference to an inaccessible future; but 036 makes a particular claim about the transformative power of the experience, while 035 makes a general claim about the unreality of the stated choice. They are often used in tandem ("you'll change your mind, and anyway it's different when they're yours"), which is worth noticing precisely so that the two are not merged. The tandem is powerful because it covers both flanks: if you insist your decision is real, you are met with the claim that the feeling will change anyway; if you insist your feeling is real, you are met with the claim that you cannot know until it is your own child. Together they leave the childfree person no ground of self-knowledge on which to stand, which is why disentangling them is worth the effort. A third connection is Book 099, on regret and the fear of it. "You'll regret it" is one of the great dismissal's standard scripts, but regret is 099's central subject; here it appears only as one instrument by which the present decision is discounted. The division of labor: 099 examines whether the fear of regret is well founded; 035 examines how the threat of regret is used to overrule a present decision. The two books need each other. Without 099's evidence that regret does not fall as predicted, 035's claim that the threat is misused would be weaker; without 035's account of the speech act, 099's evidence would lack a picture of how the fear of regret actually operates in conversation. More distantly, the great dismissal draws on the general pronatalist default that runs through the whole series, the background assumption that parenthood is the destination and childlessness a deviation. The dismissal is one of the default's enforcement mechanisms: the specific conversational move by which the default reasserts itself when someone declares against it. Where other biases in the series describe the default's content, this one describes one of its immune responses, the reflex by which the culture metabolizes a declared exception and returns it, in the listener's mind, to the assumed norm. Naming these joints matters for tidiness, and more importantly because it prevents this book from over-claiming. The great dismissal is a narrow bias, the invalidation of a stated choice, and it does its damage precisely by borrowing force from the larger structures around it. On its own it is a single sentence; what makes the sentence heavy is everything behind it. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 10 Quiz. 1. Seeing past it What would it look like to reason clearly about someone else's stated reproductive decision, once the great dismissal has been named? The correction is smaller than it might seem, and it costs the listener almost nothing. It does not require adopting any view about whether children are good, or whether the childfree life is preferable, or whether the person is deciding wisely. It requires only a change in what one does with the person's word. For individuals, the core move is to take the statement as a statement. When a competent adult says they do not want children, the default response, as with any other consequential life decision they announce, is to believe that they mean it, and to treat it as their decision to hold and, if they ever choose, to revise. This does not require pretending that intentions never change. It requires only that any acknowledgment of possible change be offered as information the person can use ("some people in your situation do come to feel differently; only you'll know") rather than as a verdict that overrides what they have said. The test is simple: does the response leave the person the author of their own future, or does it appoint the listener as the author instead? A response that leaves authorship with the person can contain doubt, experience, even warning; what it cannot contain is the refusal to believe. One may say "I worry you'll regret it" as an honest confession of one's own fear, which leaves the decision where it belongs, and this is wholly different from "you'll change your mind," which takes the decision away. A useful discipline is the reversal check. Before saying "you'll change your mind," ask whether you would say the equivalent to someone announcing the opposite decision. Would you tell an expectant parent "you'll change your mind about wanting this child"? If the sentence only runs in one direction, that is a sign it is not the neutral realism it presents itself as, but the pronatalist default speaking. Noticing the asymmetry is often enough to disarm the impulse, because most people, once they see that they would never dream of saying the reverse, recognize that their prediction was never really about the fragility of intentions. The check works precisely because it exposes the direction, and the direction is the whole of the bias. It is a small habit of mind, but a reliable one: run the sentence backward, and if it becomes absurd or cruel in reverse, it was never neutral going forward. For those who genuinely believe a young person may be deciding too fast, the constructive path is counsel, not verdict: open a real conversation, share what you have seen without claiming it settles anything, and above all keep the discussion open rather than closing it with a thought-terminator. The elder's experience is worth offering; it is not worth imposing. Offered as a gift, pattern recognition respects the person; wielded as a prediction, it overrides them. The practical difference is audible in the grammar. "You'll change your mind" is a sentence about the listener, delivered as fact. "When I was your age I felt as you do, and I want you to know it shifted for me, though it may not for you" is a sentence about the speaker, offered as experience. The first closes; the second opens. And the second, being honest about its own uncertainty, is far likelier to be heard, because it does not put the young person in the position of having to defend their reality before they can consider anything else. For institutions, the correction is to design for the stated decision. Clinicians can take a competent adult's clear, informed, repeated request seriously rather than protecting against a hypothetical future self; the appropriate safeguard is genuine informed consent, not disbelief. A patient who has been fully informed of the permanence and the possibility of future regret, and who still requests the procedure, has met the standard we apply to every other irreversible medical choice, and the disbelief that singles out this one is not caution but bias. Employers can plan around what a person actually says about their life rather than around a demographic assumption of what they will "really" do. Families can extend to the childfree future the same recognition and support they would extend to a pronatalist one, rather than holding it in reserve. In each case the reform is the same in spirit: stop treating the stated decision as a placeholder for a decision the person has not made, and start treating it as the decision they have. None of this forbids anyone from changing their mind, and none of it claims that changing one's mind is a failure. Seeing past the great dismissal simply relocates the authority over a person's reproductive decision back to the person, where, on any account of what it means to respect an adult, it belonged all along. --- Check Yourself — Chapter 11 Quiz. 1. Conclusion This book has argued that "you'll change your mind," in its many forms, is not primarily the prediction it presents itself as. It is a refusal, a refusal to accept a competent person's stated decision not to have children as final, real, or authoritative, coupled with the presumption that the pronatalist outcome is the person's inevitable true self surfacing in time. The bias operates by reclassifying a decision as a symptom, by relocating the person's "real" preference to an inaccessible future self, and by running its supposedly neutral prediction in one direction only. Its deepest harm is epistemic, in Fricker's sense: it discounts a person's testimony about their own life on the basis of prejudice rather than evidence, and it does so to those (women, the young, the embedded) whose word a culture is already primed to doubt. The critique has been held to honest limits. Minds do change; intentions are not perfectly stable; elders have really seen conversions and often speak from love. Chapter 2 conceded all of this in full, and Chapter 5 declined to overstate the data. But the evidence, taken as it is, does not license the strong claim the folk wisdom makes. The committed voluntarily childfree mostly do not reverse their decision; regret does not fall as the dismissal assumes, and may run in the unmentioned opposite direction; and "you'll change your mind" states as near-certainty an outcome that is, at most, a minority path. What remains genuinely contested is the reversibility argument, the thought that warnings are appropriate only in the reversible direction, and this book has tried to grant it its real force while showing that it does not, in the end, explain the whole of the asymmetry it points to. The residue that reversibility cannot account for, the insistence that only childlessness needs correcting, and the way the dismissal intensifies rather than relents as the childfree decision becomes irreversible, is the bias standing exposed once its best justification has done all the work it can. It is worth restating, at the close, what the book does not say, because the subject invites the reader to hear more than is meant. It does not say that children are a mistake, that parents are dupes, or that anyone who ever uttered "you'll change your mind" was acting in bad faith. Most who say it love the person they say it to. The argument is compatible with thinking parenthood among the best things a life can hold; it asks nothing about the value of children and everything about the standing of a person's word. One can believe children are a great good and still believe a childfree adult when they say children are not for them. These are not in tension; the folk wisdom only makes them seem so by treating the childfree declaration as the one self-report a loving listener is entitled to overrule. The measured takeaway is narrow and, for that reason, hard to dismiss. It is not that having children is a mistake, or that changing one's mind is shameful, or that no one should ever mention to a young person that intentions can shift. It is only this: a person's account of their own reproductive future deserves to be heard as an account and not set aside as a symptom, and the authority to revise that account belongs to the person who holds it, not to the listener who doubts it. ① In brief

Almost everyone who says they do not want children hears the same sentence, often for years: you'll change your mind. It comes dressed a dozen ways (you're too young to know, everyone says that at first, wait until your clock ticks, you'll regret it), but the structure is constant. A competent adult states a decision about their own life, and the listener declines to accept that the statement means what it says. This book calls that refusal the great dismissal: the treatment of a stated intention not to have children as provisional by default, coupled with the presumption that parenthood is the person's inevitable true self, waiting to surface.

The book is careful to stay in its lane. It is not about the asking; the entitlement to interrogate the childfree about their reasons belongs to Book 027. It is not about "it's different when they're yours," a separate thought-terminator examined in Book 036. The great dismissal is narrower: it is what happens after the person has answered clearly, and the answer is set aside as if it carried no authority at all. Two elements define it, the invalidation of the stated choice and the presumption of eventual conversion, and they reinforce each other, so that the assumed pronatalist "true self" is never tested against the person's real words.

The stance is critical of the bias but scrupulously honest about the other side. Chapter 2 steelmans the natalist intuition in full and first: minds genuinely do change, intentions about a distant future self are unstable, and elders who say "I've seen people change" are often telling the truth about what they have observed, sometimes from love and a wish to keep a narrowing door open. The book concedes all of this before it criticizes anything.

What it denies is that the possibility of change licenses treating a present, competent decision as void. The central tool is Miranda Fricker's concept of testimonial injustice (Epistemic Injustice, 2007): the wrong done when prejudice causes a hearer to discount a speaker's word. When a woman says she does not want children and is told her body will correct her, her testimony about her own life is discounted not on evidence but on a prior conviction about what she "really" wants. A second tool, the double standard, works like a control group: no one tells an expectant parent "you'll change your mind," which shows the prediction is not about the instability of intentions in general but about one direction of intention in particular.

On the evidence, the book is exact. Intentions do revise in both directions; that much is real. But the stronger folk claim, that the childfree declaration is characteristically unstable and parenthood the reliable destination, is not supported for the genuinely, voluntarily childfree, whose decision tends to be durable and rarely reversed. The regret evidence cuts sharper still: the childfree report low regret, while a stigmatized and under-studied share of parents report regretting parenthood, the opposite of what "you'll regret it" assumes. The safe summary is that the data render "you'll change your mind," stated as near-certainty, a minority outcome at best.

The costs the book counts are mostly quiet: the epistemic harm of being disbelieved about one's own life (and eventually doubting oneself), the foreclosure of the very conversation an elder might want, the under-support of a life-plan no one treats as real, and the slow erosion of trust, all borne by the dismissed, nearly invisible to the dismisser. The pressure falls hardest on women, the young, and those in high-expectation communities.

The correction is small and costs the listener almost nothing: take the statement as a statement, run the reversal check, offer experience as counsel rather than verdict, and let the person remain the author of any revision. That is not a surrender of realism. It is extending to one direction of decision the credibility we already grant, without a second thought, to the other.

② The book in twelve chapters

Each chapter opens with its own summary. Note chapter 2: every volume states the strongest case for the thing it criticises before laying a finger on it — and chapter 8 turns the critique back on itself.

CH 1Naming the bias

The great dismissal is the disposition to treat a person's stated intention not to have children as non-final by default, provisional, immature, or simply mistaken, and to treat the eventual arrival at parenthood as the true and inevitable outcome that the person will, in time, recognize as their own.

The honest way to write this book is to concede, at the outset and without flinching, that the people who say "you'll change your mind" are not simply wrong about the world. They are often drawing on something real, and the strongest version of their view deserves to be stated in full before it is answered.

CH 3Where it comes from

The great dismissal did not arise from nowhere. Several deep currents feed it, and naming them helps to see why a sentence that purports to be a neutral prediction so reliably runs in a single direction.

CH 4How it operates today

Stripped of its history, the great dismissal survives in ordinary life through a small set of concrete mechanisms: scripts, reclassifications, and conversational moves that do their work quickly and often without any conscious malice. It is worth laying these out precisely, because the bias hides in its own smallness; each individual instance seems too trivial to object to, and it is only when the mechanism is named that the pattern beneath the instances becomes visible.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

If "you'll change your mind" is offered as a prediction, then it is answerable to evidence, and the evidence is worth stating carefully, including the parts that complicate the critique. A prediction that hides from data is not a prediction at all but a wish; so the fair thing is to ask what actually happens to childfree intentions over time, and to report it whether or not it flatters the argument.

CH 6The hidden costs

Because "you'll change your mind" feels mild, a bit of unsolicited optimism, easily brushed off, its costs are easy to overlook. They are real, and they accumulate. The mildness is in fact part of the problem: a cost that arrives in small, deniable increments is a cost no single instance of which seems worth objecting to, and so the whole is borne without ever being tallied.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

The great dismissal is not distributed evenly. It falls hardest on those whose testimony a culture is already primed to discount, which means the bias tends to compound existing inequalities in who is believed. This is worth stating plainly, because it locates the harm not as a free-floating unfairness but as one that lands, predictably, on the people least equipped to absorb it.

Four brief illustrations show the great dismissal at work across different settings, without standing in for data. Each is composite and hypothetical, chosen to display the structure of the bias rather than to report a particular life; the point of each is not the drama but the shape of the move being made.

CH 10Adjacent biases

The great dismissal does not act alone; it interlocks with several other biases in this series, and seeing the joints keeps this book in its lane. Mapping the neighbors is not mere bookkeeping; it prevents the bias from being inflated into a theory of everything, and it clarifies exactly which small move this book is about.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

What would it look like to reason clearly about someone else's stated reproductive decision, once the great dismissal has been named? The correction is smaller than it might seem, and it costs the listener almost nothing. It does not require adopting any view about whether children are good, or whether the childfree life is preferable, or whether the person is deciding wisely. It requires only a change in what one does with the person's word.

CH 12Conclusion

This book has argued that "you'll change your mind," in its many forms, is not primarily the prediction it presents itself as. It is a refusal, a refusal to accept a competent person's stated decision not to have children as final, real, or authoritative, coupled with the presumption that the pronatalist outcome is the person's inevitable true self surfacing in time. The bias operates by reclassifying a decision as a symptom, by relocating the person's "real" preference to an inaccessible future self, and by running its supposedly neutral prediction in one direction only. Its deepest harm is epistemic, in Fricker's sense: it discounts a person's testimony about their own life on the basis of prejudice rather than evidence, and it does so to those (women, the young, the embedded) whose word a culture is already primed to doubt.
