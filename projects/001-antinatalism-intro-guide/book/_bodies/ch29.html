"The fact that something is natural says nothing about whether it is good."

— a common gloss on the naturalistic fallacy

Of all the replies to antinatalism, the most immediate is also the most instinctive: it just feels unnatural. Every living thing strives to reproduce. The drive to have children runs deeper in us than language or reason; it is written into our bodies, our longings, the ache people feel when they hold an infant. To set oneself against that current can seem not merely mistaken but perverse — a small rebellion against the whole order of life. "It goes against nature" is rarely offered as a careful argument. It is offered as something more like a gut verdict, and a powerful one.

This chapter takes that gut verdict seriously, because it deserves to be. But it also asks the hard question hiding inside it: when we say something is natural, have we said anything at all about whether it is good? That question turns out to open a door the objection did not mean to open.

From "is" to "ought"

Philosophers have a name for the move at the heart of the naturalness objection. To argue that procreation is right because it is natural is to slide from a fact about how the world is to a claim about how we ought to act. This slide is so common, and so often unjustified, that it has a name: the naturalistic fallacy, or more precisely the appeal to nature. The trouble is easy to see once it is pointed out. Many things are perfectly natural and plainly bad — disease, predation, the death of the young, the casual cruelty that runs through the animal world. And many things we rightly prize are profoundly unnatural — medicine, written law, the idea that strangers have rights, the restraint that stops us from striking back when we are wronged.

The naturalistic fallacy (appeal to nature) — the mistaken inference that because something is natural it is therefore good or right, or that because something is unnatural it is therefore bad or wrong. "Natural" describes how things are; "good" is a moral judgment, and the one does not follow automatically from the other.

Notice what this does not claim. It does not claim that natural things are bad, or that we should override our nature at every turn. It claims only something modest: that "it is natural" cannot by itself settle whether an act is good. The fact that we evolved to want something is a fact about us, not a verdict in its favor. If it were, then every evolved impulse — including the ugliest ones — would carry its own moral license, which no one actually believes.

Why our instincts might mislead us here

There is a sharper edge to this for the question of birth specifically. The reproductive drive is not a neutral observer. It is the single most heavily selected impulse in all of biology, because every one of our ancestors, without exception, succeeded in reproducing. A creature built by that process is unlikely to be a fair judge of whether reproducing is a good idea. Its enthusiasm is the whole point.

This connects to a theme that runs through the antinatalist literature: optimism bias, the well-documented human tendency to expect better outcomes than the evidence warrants. We systematically underestimate how likely we are to face illness, divorce, accident, or grief, and we extend that rosy forecast to the lives we imagine for our children. From the antinatalist's angle, this is no coincidence. A species that saw the risks of life clearly might hesitate to pass life on; natural selection had every reason to fit us with rose-colored lenses precisely where reproduction is concerned. So when we feel, deep in the gut, that having children is obviously good, the antinatalist asks us to pause — not because the feeling is weak, but because it is exactly the feeling we would expect to have whether or not it were true.

The drive to reproduce is the one witness in this trial who cannot be impartial.
The objectionCalling this a "fallacy" is too quick. Our deepest instincts are not noise to be reasoned away — they are the accumulated wisdom of millions of years of survival, and a moral theory that contradicts the most universal human drive should suspect itself, not its critics. Moreover, antinatalism wants to overrule one of our strongest intuitions while leaning on others — that suffering is bad, that consent matters. You cannot distrust human intuition wholesale and still build an argument out of it. A view that saws off the branch it sits on is in trouble.
A replyThis is the strongest form of the objection, and the antinatalist should concede a real point inside it: blanket distrust of human nature is self-defeating, because every argument, antinatalism included, must rest on some intuitions we are willing to trust. The reply is not wholesale suspicion but targeted suspicion. We routinely override particular evolved impulses — for vengeance, for tribal favoritism, for aggression — without abandoning reason itself; ethical progress largely consists of such overrides. The antinatalist claim is only that the reproductive drive is one impulse we have specific, principled reason to scrutinize, because its very job was to make us want to reproduce regardless of the costs to the one created. That is not sawing off the branch. It is checking one particular branch that we have good cause to think was built to hold our weight whether or not it is sound. Whether the scrutiny ultimately vindicates or undermines the impulse is left open — the demand is only that we look.

In plain words: "It's natural" doesn't prove "it's good" — plenty of natural things are awful and plenty of good things are unnatural. The reproductive urge is especially suspect as a moral guide, since evolution designed it to feel compelling no matter what. But the critic is right that we can't distrust all our intuitions; the honest move is to single out this one for a closer look, not to throw reason overboard.

What the objection gets right

It would be unfair to leave the naturalness objection in tatters, because it carries a true insight. Our instincts are not always wrong; far more often than not, they encode something that kept our ancestors alive and even point toward genuine goods — love, attachment, the fierce protectiveness a parent feels. A philosophy that sneered at all of that would be both arrogant and foolish. The antinatalist position, at its most careful, does not sneer. It says that on this one question — whether to create a new center of suffering and joy who cannot be consulted — we have unusually good reason to think our instincts are not neutral judges, and so we should reason the matter through rather than let the gut decide alone. That is a demand for reflection, not for the abolition of human feeling.

Chapter in brief

  • The naturalness objection says antinatalism defies instinct and "goes against nature."
  • The naturalistic fallacy shows that "natural" does not entail "good": natural things can be terrible, and many goods are unnatural.
  • The reproductive drive is an especially poor moral judge, since evolution built it to feel compelling regardless of cost — a bias linked to optimism bias.
  • The critic fairly notes that total distrust of intuition is self-defeating; the antinatalist reply is targeted, not wholesale, suspicion.
  • The objection's true insight: instincts often encode real goods, so the call is for reflection on this one question, not contempt for human feeling.
