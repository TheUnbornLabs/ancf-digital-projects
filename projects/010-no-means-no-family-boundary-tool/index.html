3. How to hold a boundary

A boundary is not a single sentence; it's a stance you can repeat. A few techniques:

The broken record

You don't need a new argument each time. Calmly repeat the same short line: "I've made my decision, and I'd like to move on." Repetition is the technique.

Don't J.A.D.E.

You are not required to Justify, Argue, Defend, or Explain. Over-explaining invites debate. A boundary is a statement, not a request for permission.

Name it, then redirect

"I notice this keeps coming up. I'm not going to discuss it — tell me about your week instead." Acknowledging the pattern can defuse it.

The escalation ladder

Start gentle. If it continues: state the boundary plainly. If it still continues: name a consequence you can keep — "If it keeps coming up, I'll change the subject or head out." Then follow through, calmly.

It's okay to step back

If someone repeatedly ignores your boundary, taking space is a valid, loving response — to yourself. Distance is not the same as cutting off.

In closing

A good boundary is calm, short, and repeatable — and it protects the relationship as much as it protects you. You are allowed to decide what you will and won't discuss. Saying "no" kindly, and meaning it, is a complete sentence.
