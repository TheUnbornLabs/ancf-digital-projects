"Live the questions now. Perhaps you will then gradually, without noticing it, live along some distant day into the answer."

— Rainer Maria Rilke, Letters to a Young Poet

We began this book with a promise. The opening chapter said that antinatalism asks us to take a question seriously without forcing us to accept any particular answer — and that the distance between those two things would run through everything that followed. Now, at the close, that promise is also the destination. This final chapter is not another argument. It is an invitation to live thoughtfully with a question you may never fully resolve, and to do so with kindness toward yourself and toward everyone who has answered it differently.

The relief of an unfinished question

Somewhere along the way, many readers feel a pressure to settle — to declare themselves an antinatalist or a critic, to file the matter away and move on. That pressure is worth resisting. Some questions are not meant to be closed, and the question of whether it is right to bring a new person into the world may be one of them. It has occupied serious minds across cultures and centuries without yielding a single, agreed-upon answer. There is no shame, and considerable honesty, in holding it open.

To live with an open question is not to be confused or weak-minded. It is to take the question seriously enough not to force it shut before its time. You can carry a hard question the way you carry any deep one — about meaning, about how to live, about what we owe one another — letting it inform you without demanding that it conclude.

Some questions are not problems to be solved but companions to be kept.

Intellectual humility, honestly

One thing the journey through this book should have made plain is how strong the arguments are on more than one side. The antinatalist case rests on real and uncomfortable truths — about consent, about risk, about suffering that no life entirely escapes. And the case against it rests on real truths too — about meaning, joy, resilience, and the goods that only a life can hold. A thoughtful person can feel the pull of both and decline to pretend that either has been decisively defeated.

Intellectual humility — the disposition to hold one's own conclusions with an awareness that they might be mistaken; to take seriously the possibility that thoughtful people who disagree may see something one has missed. It is not indecision, and not relativism. It is honesty about the limits of what any of us has worked out.

Humility of this kind is not a failure to reach a conclusion. You may, in the end, hold a clear view — for or against — and hold it firmly. The humility lies in how you hold it: with the door left ajar, with respect for the reasons that move others, without the certainty that turns a position into a wall.

Taking the question seriously without taking a side

It is entirely coherent to find this question genuinely serious and still not accept any single answer to it. In fact, that may be the most defensible place to stand. You can agree that creating a person is a weighty decision deserving real reflection — the modest claim from chapter one — without committing to the strong conclusion that procreation is always wrong, and without dismissing the strong conclusion either.

This is the posture the book has tried, throughout, to model: every major argument given in its strongest form, then handed straight to its sharpest critic. If you have finished feeling the force of both, the book has done its work, whatever you have decided.

Objection

Isn't "holding the question open" just a comfortable way of avoiding it? At some point you have to live — and living means deciding. Endless reflection sounds like an excuse to never commit to anything.

Reply

There is truth here: at the level of action, life does require decisions, and most people will, in fact, either have children or not. Holding the question open is not a way of dodging that. It is a way of making whatever decision you make with your eyes fully open — and, just as importantly, of refusing to harden into contempt for those who decide otherwise. The alternative to open-handed reflection is not decisiveness; it is false certainty, the kind that mistakes a difficult question for an easy one and looks down on everyone who finds it hard. You can act decisively in your own life while remaining genuinely humble about the underlying question. Those are not in tension. The person who has thought hardest about this is often the slowest to be sure — and the kindest to those who landed elsewhere.

Equal respect, all the way down

If there is one thing to carry out of this book, let it be this. The antinatalist and the parent are not enemies, and neither is a fool. Both are responding, in their own way, to something real — the antinatalist to the weight of suffering and consent, the parent to the goods of love, hope, and a life worth living. To treat both with equal respect is not a refusal to think clearly. It is the very thing clear thinking, fully followed, leads to.

This matters most for the people closest to you. The parents who raised you, the friends who chose children, the partner who sees this differently than you do — none of them is diminished by a philosophical disagreement, and the disagreement is not worth a single fractured bond. Hold the idea as seriously as you like. Hold the people more gently still.

In plain words: Take the question seriously. Take a side if you must. Take care of the people on every side of it, always.

A closing word

One last reminder, the same one this book has returned to whenever it mattered: the antinatalist question concerns future births and nothing else. It is never a reason for despair about your own life or anyone else's, and never a reason for self-harm. If these pages have stirred something heavy, let it be the seriousness of a great ethical question — not a verdict on the worth of a life already being lived. Your life, and every life around you, has value that no argument in this book touches.

We opened by promising a question taken seriously, not an answer imposed. That is the gift, if it is one. You arrive at the last page not with a conclusion handed to you, but with a question made clearer — and with the freedom, which was always yours, to live thoughtfully alongside it. Hold it well, and hold one another well. That is enough.

Chapter in brief

  • Some questions are meant to be held, not closed; living thoughtfully with an unresolved question is honest, not weak.
  • Intellectual humility means holding your view — even a firm one — with the door left ajar and respect for those who disagree.
  • You can find the question serious without accepting any single answer, which fulfills the book's opening promise.
  • Equal respect for antinatalists and parents alike is where clear thinking leads, not a retreat from it; hold ideas seriously and people gently.
  • The question is about future births only — never a reason for despair or self-harm. Every existing life has value no argument here touches.
