Frequently Asked Questions

Short, direct answers to the questions newcomers ask most.

This chapter answers the questions newcomers ask most often, in short and direct form. Throughout, keep one thing in mind: antinatalism is a view about future births — about whether to create new people — and the serious versions of it reject coercion and harm of every kind. Fuller treatments of each point appear in the chapters this book has already covered.

Q: Is antinatalism just hating children?

No — and usually the reverse. Concern for the well-being of any future child is often what draws people to the view in the first place. Many antinatalists are devoted to the children already in their lives as relatives, teachers, and carers.

Q: Is it the same as being childfree?

No. "Childfree" describes a personal lifestyle choice not to have children, for any reason at all. Antinatalism is an ethical argument about the value of coming into existence; it may inform a childfree choice, but the two are distinct.

Q: Does it want existing people to die?

No. This is the most important confusion to clear up. Antinatalism concerns whether to create new people; it says nothing against the lives of those who already exist, who have interests, attachments, and a powerful stake in continuing. It is never a reason for despair or self-harm.

Q: Isn't it just depression?

No. It is an ethical argument that stands or falls on reasons, not moods. A cheerful, people-loving person can hold it for reasoned motives, and a miserable person can reject it. The philosophy and a person's mental state are easy to confuse and important to keep apart.

Q: Is it eugenics?

Clearly not. Eugenics is about which kinds of people should exist and often involves coercion and group ranking. Antinatalism makes no distinction between groups and applies its concern equally to all potential births; it opposes coercion entirely.

Q: Do antinatalists think you're a bad person for having kids?

Generally no. The view questions a specific decision; it does not condemn the billions who have made the opposite choice, usually with love and hope. Disagreeing with a choice is not the same as despising those who made it.

Q: What's the asymmetry in one line?

The absence of pain is good even when no one exists to enjoy it, while the absence of pleasure is bad only if there is someone who is thereby deprived — so never existing has an advantage that existing cannot match.

Q: Can a religious person be antinatalist?

Yes. Strands of Buddhist, Gnostic, and other traditions take a sober or negative view of birth and embodied existence. While many religions are strongly pronatalist, the antinatalist intuition has deep religious as well as secular roots.

Q: Is it anti-human or misanthropic?

Not necessarily. There is a "misanthropic" argument that points to the harm humans do, but the central antinatalist case is the opposite in spirit: it springs from compassion for the would-be child and a wish to spare them avoidable suffering.

Q: What about the survival of the species?

Antinatalists generally reply that the survival of the species is not, by itself, a good that outweighs the interests of the individuals who would have to be created and to suffer. In any case, the view is a moral argument, not a program imposed on anyone — actual human reproduction is not about to halt.

Q: Is adoption the answer?

For many antinatalists, caring for a child who already exists fits the view well, since it helps a person already here rather than creating a new one. It is not a duty the philosophy imposes, but it is one way people put their concern for children into practice.

Q: Can you hold the view and still be happy?

Yes. Believing that it would have been better not to have been born is consistent with valuing the life you have, your relationships, and your projects. Many antinatalists describe their outlook as calm and even relieving rather than bleak.

Q: Does it call for any coercion or law?

No. The classic antinatalist conclusion is simply to refrain — to not do one thing. Nothing in the argument licenses force, pressure, or legal compulsion, and reputable antinatalist writers reject all of these.

Q: What's the difference between antinatalism and efilism or VHEMT?

Efilism extends the concern to all sentient life and welcomes the end of sentience; it goes far beyond mainstream antinatalism. VHEMT (the Voluntary Human Extinction Movement) urges humans to stop reproducing on chiefly environmental grounds, strictly voluntarily. Antinatalism proper is narrower and is an ethical argument rather than a movement or program.

Q: Where should I start reading?

Begin with Zapffe's short essay "The Last Messiah" (1933) and David Benatar's Better Never to Have Been (2006), then read a fair-minded critic such as David Wasserman or Elizabeth Harman to test the argument. The Further Reading chapter lays out a fuller path.

Chapter in brief

  • Antinatalism concerns future births only; it is not about existing people and is never a reason for despair or self-harm.
  • It is not child-hatred, depression, eugenics, or a condemnation of parents, and it calls for no coercion or law.
  • It differs from efilism and VHEMT, can be held by religious and secular people alike, and is compatible with a happy, engaged life.
  • A good starting path: Zapffe and Benatar, read alongside their strongest critics.
