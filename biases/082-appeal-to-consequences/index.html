BIAS 082

But If Everyone Stopped: The Appeal to Consequences

Part VII · Philosophical & Ethical Fallacies · The Appeal to Consequences

The bias: Rejecting a choice because universalizing it would end humanity.

The consequences of a proposition are not evidence of its truth.
① In brief ② The book in twelve chapters ③ Key terms ④ Ten-question check ⑤ Reflection ⑥ Sources ⑦ Related biases

① In brief

Almost anyone who has voiced a doubt about whether to have children has met the same reply: but if everyone stopped, humanity would go extinct. The sentence is meant to end the conversation, and it usually does. This book examines that reply as a bias, the reflex it calls the extinction-stopper, and asks whether it does the argumentative work it is asked to do. Its answer, argued carefully and non-coercively, is that it does not.

At the logical core lies the appeal to consequences (argumentum ad consequentiam): inferring that a claim is false because its being true would have bad results. This is invalid. Whether creating a person can harm that person, or whether most people's reasons for reproducing are examined, is settled by the reasons for and against those claims, not by whether we would enjoy living in a world where people believed them. "Extinction would be bad, therefore antinatalism is false" simply does not follow, however real the badness of extinction may be.

Around that core the book identifies a cluster of supporting confusions. The universalization jump rewrites one person's private doubt as a universal recommendation ("if everyone did it") that no actual doubter has proposed. The slippery-slope compression telescopes the enormous distance from a single hesitation to the total, voluntary cessation of all human reproduction into one asserted step. The Kantian universalizability test and the reductio ad absurdum are both borrowed for their authority and then misapplied; the reductio requires a conclusion that is contradictory, not merely sad. And the whole move rests on a factual premise, that questioning tends toward universal childlessness, which the demographic record does not support: fertility is falling in many countries, but sub-replacement is a world still full of children, nowhere near zero.

Read the rest of the overview

The book follows the series template. Crucially, Chapter 2 builds the strongest natalist case first, in earnest: that human continuation may be a great good (drawing on Scheffler and Schell), that reasoning catastrophic-if-universalized deserves suspicion, that intolerable conclusions are legitimate defeasible evidence of hidden error, and that a foundational practice enjoys a presumption. Chapter 8 then presses the strongest objections to the book's own thesis and concedes real ground: appeals to consequences are legitimate in practical reasoning, a genuine reductio is valid, and a deeply counterintuitive conclusion can be a reason to re-examine premises. The pivotal distinction survives all of this: every legitimate cousin of the move is a reason to keep reasoning, whereas the extinction-stopper is used to stop it.

Later chapters trace the reflex's origins (evolutionary, religious, demographic-political, philosophical, and social), its everyday mechanisms, the questions it hides (individual deliberation, honest cost-accounting, the child's own interests, epistemic character, and even the seriousness of the pro-continuation case), and who bears its pressure most heavily: disproportionately women, and often, under the universal language of "humanity," a particular national or ethnic group whose birth-rate is the real concern.

The stance is deliberately modest and non-coercive in both directions. The book does not argue that anyone should stop having children, does not predict or welcome extinction, and does not claim antinatalism is true. It leaves the genuinely contested value question, whether extinction would be bad and for whom, to the adjacent volumes on the asymmetry (079), person-affecting views (084), aggregation (083), and future generations (089). Its narrow, durable claim is that the badness of extinction is the wrong kind of consideration to settle an argument about an individual reproductive choice no one is universalizing. Naming the reflex tells no one whether to have children; it only restores the freedom to reason about it.

② The book in twelve chapters

Each chapter opens with its own summary. Note chapter 2: every volume states the strongest case for the thing it criticises before laying a finger on it — and chapter 8 turns the critique back on itself.

CH 1Naming the bias

The bias examined in this volume is the tendency to reject any critical examination of reproduction by invoking the consequence that if everyone stopped having children, humanity would go extinct, and to treat that consequence as though it settled the matter. Call it, for short, the extinction-stopper: a specific appeal to consequences aimed at foreclosing inquiry rather than answering it. The name is chosen with care. It is no ordinary appeal to consequences. It is the one that reaches for the largest possible consequence, the end of everything human, and uses that maximal stake to license a minimal amount of thought.

CH 2The strongest case for it (steelman)The strongest case against this book

Before any criticism, the strongest version of the natalist position deserves a full and sympathetic hearing, stated as its most thoughtful defenders would state it, not as a straw figure. What follows is that case, presented in earnest. We think, by the chapter's end, that a real and important concern is buried inside the reflex we are criticizing, and that separating the concern from the reflex is the whole point. A steelman is not a courtesy; it is a condition of honest criticism. If we cannot state the other side in a form its ablest holder would sign, we have not understood what we mean to answer.

CH 3Where it comes from

Why does the extinction-stopper feel so much stronger than it is? Its grip is not an accident of any one culture; it draws on several deep sources at once, and understanding them helps explain both its persistence and its overreach. A move that were merely a logical mistake would be easy to drop once pointed out. This one is hard to drop precisely because its roots run below the level of argument, into biology, faith, statecraft, and the ordinary management of social discomfort. To see the roots is not to be freed of them, but it is the beginning of being able to hold them at arm's length.

CH 4How it operates today

The extinction-stopper does its work not in treatises but in ordinary exchanges, and it does so through a small repertoire of recognizable maneuvers. Naming them is useful precisely because, once named, they are hard to un-see. A move that operated only in the dark would be safe from criticism; these operate in the open, at conversational speed, protected only by the fact that no one has stopped to label them. Labeling is most of the cure.

CH 5The evidenceWhat the evidence shows

What does the world actually show about the scenario the extinction-stopper conjures? Here we must be careful to separate what is well established, what is contested, and what is sheer projection, and to keep firmly in view that empirical facts about fertility cannot, by themselves, settle the normative question. They can, however, deflate the specific factual premise that "everyone is about to stop." That premise is not a value judgment; it is a prediction about human behavior, and predictions about human behavior are exactly the sort of thing that evidence can check. When we check it, the premise fails.

CH 6The hidden costs

Every conversation-stopper has a price, paid in the questions it prevents from being asked. The extinction-stopper is unusually costly because the questions it forecloses are among the most consequential a person can face. Consider what gets silenced when "but if everyone stopped" is allowed to end the discussion. The costs are easy to miss precisely because they are costs of omission: nothing bad visibly happens when a question goes unasked, and so the ledger of what the reflex destroys must be reconstructed rather than simply observed.

CH 7Who bears the pressure

Biases are not distributed evenly. The extinction-stopper falls hardest on those already most subject to reproductive expectation, and mapping its incidence reveals that its function is often less to preserve the species than to preserve a particular arrangement of who owes children to whom. A logical error, examined only as logic, seems to float free of any person; examined as a social practice, it lands, and it lands on some shoulders far more than others. Tracing where it lands tells us something the pure logic conceals: what the reflex is actually for.

CH 8The counter-arguments to the critiqueObjections to the critique

A book that only steelmanned its opponents once, in Chapter 2, and then coasted, would not deserve trust. So here we turn our own thesis around and press the strongest objections to it. Some, we will concede, land partial hits. A thesis that could survive no serious objection would be too weak to be worth defending; the point of this chapter is to find exactly how much weight ours can bear, and to say plainly where it cannot.

CH 9Case studies / illustrations

Abstractions become clearer against concrete instances. Four brief illustrations show the extinction-stopper operating, and one shows what its absence looks like. The point of the illustrations is not to prove that the reflex is always deployed in bad faith, it usually is not, but to make its recurring shape visible across settings so different that the shape itself, once seen, becomes unmistakable.

CH 10Adjacent biases

The extinction-stopper does not stand alone. It interlocks with several other biases catalogued in this series, and locating it in that web both sharpens its boundaries and shows why it is worth a volume of its own. We map the connections without reproducing the neighboring analyses. The value of the map is diagnostic: many real conversations deploy two or three of these moves at once, braided so tightly that answering one leaves the others intact, and only someone who can name each strand separately can untangle them.

CH 11Seeing past itSeeing past it

Suppose the reflex has been named and its mechanisms exposed. What, concretely, does clear-eyed reasoning about reproduction look like once the extinction-stopper no longer functions as an off-switch? The answer is undramatic, which is itself instructive: seeing past the bias does not deliver a verdict on antinatalism; it restores the conditions under which the real questions can be asked. No new doctrine arrives to replace the old reflex. What arrives is only the ordinary possibility of thinking, which the reflex had quietly removed.

CH 12Conclusion

We set out to examine a single, ubiquitous move: the rejection of any questioning of reproduction on the ground that if everyone stopped, humanity would end. The book's thesis has been that this move, as typically deployed, does not do the work it is asked to do. It is an appeal to consequences, an inference from the badness of an outcome to the falsity of the reasoning that seemed to point toward it, and such inferences are invalid, because the truth of a claim is independent of whether we like its consequences. Around that logical core we found a cluster of supporting confusions: a universalization applied to a choice no one is universalizing; a slippery slope from one person's doubt to the species' end, asserted rather than shown; a misuse of the Kantian test and of the reductio; and a factual premise, that questioning tends toward universal cessation, that the demographic record simply does not support.

Show all 16 terms as a list
Appeal to consequences (argumentum ad consequentiam)
The fallacy of inferring that a claim is false (or true) because its being true would have bad (or good) consequences. The logical core of the bias examined in this book.
Extinction-stopper
This book's term for the specific appeal to consequences that invokes "if everyone stopped having children, humanity would end" to foreclose inquiry into reproduction, rather than to answer it.
Universalization jump
The unwarranted rewriting of an individual's doubt or choice as a universal recommendation ("if everyone did it"). It manufactures the alarming scenario the extinction-stopper then evaluates.
Consequence substitution
Replacing engagement with an argument's premises by an evaluation of the argument's (imagined) consequences; redirecting attention from the joints of the reasoning to the horror of an outcome.
Reductio ad absurdum
A valid argument form that refutes a premise by showing it entails a contradiction or falsehood. Misused when the "absurd" conclusion is merely unwelcome rather than genuinely incoherent.
Universalizability (Kantian)
The categorical-imperative test of whether one can will the maxim of one's action as a universal law without contradiction. Misapplied when an unconditional maxim no actual doubter holds is substituted for the conditional one they do hold.
Tu quoque
The "you too" reply (e.g. "you exist, so you can't question existence") that mistakes a fact about the arguer for a refutation of the argument.
Slippery-slope compression
Telescoping an enormous causal distance — one person's doubt to universal, permanent cessation of all human reproduction — into a single asserted step, with no mechanism supplied.
Collective-action confusion
Treating a vast number of independent individual reproductive choices as though they were one coordinated, species-wide decision that no one is actually making or proposing.
Descriptive vs. normative
The distinction between predicting what people will do (keep reproducing) and refuting a claim about what is true or right. The extinction-stopper blurs the two.
Person-affecting view
The position that an outcome is bad only if it is bad for some person. On strict versions, a world in which no future people ever exist wrongs no one, since there is no one it is worse for (see Book 084).
Asymmetry (Benatarian)
The claim that the absence of pain is good even when no one enjoys that good, while the absence of pleasure is not bad unless there is someone deprived of it. Bears on whether extinction is a genuine loss (see Book 079).
Defeasible evidence
Evidence that gives a reason to believe or re-examine but can be overridden by stronger considerations. The legitimate status of "intolerable conclusions" — a reason to look again, not a refutation.
Reflective equilibrium
The method of adjusting general principles and particular judgments against one another until they cohere; the honest setting in which an unwelcome conclusion is weighed against an intuition.
Conversation-stopper
Any move that ends inquiry by making it feel dangerous or unnecessary rather than by answering it. The genus of which the extinction-stopper is one species.
Sub-replacement fertility
A total fertility rate below roughly 2.1 children per woman. A world of sub-replacement fertility is still full of children; it is nowhere near zero reproduction — the factual gap the extinction-stopper ignores.

⑥ Sources

What this volume draws on. Where the evidence is thin or contested, the book says so rather than reaching for a number.

  • Benatar, David. Better Never to Have Been: The Harm of Coming into Existence. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2006. — The central contemporary statement of antinatalism, including the pleasure/pain asymmetry relevant to whether extinction is a loss.
  • Kant, Immanuel. Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals. 1785. Many standard translations and editions. — Source of the universalizability test and the categorical imperative frequently invoked (and misapplied) in the "what if everyone did it?" move.
  • Parfit, Derek. Reasons and Persons. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 1984. — Foundational on person-affecting views, obligations to future generations, and the repugnant conclusion; anchors the cross-references to Books 083, 084, and 089.
  • Scheffler, Samuel. Death and the Afterlife. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2013. — A serious philosophical articulation of the value of humanity's continuation and its role in underwriting present meaning; used in the Chapter 2 steelman.
  • Schell, Jonathan. The Fate of the Earth. New York: Knopf, 1982. — A classic meditation on the distinctive badness of human extinction; used in the Chapter 2 steelman.
  • Walton, Douglas. Fundamentals of Critical Argumentation. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2006. — A practical treatment of argumentation schemes and the traditional fallacies, including the appeal to consequences. The standard place to send a reader who wants the general form of the error this book studies in one instance.
  • Finneron-Burns, Elizabeth. "What's wrong with human extinction?" Canadian Journal of Philosophy 47, nos. 2–3 (2017). — Separates the several distinct reasons extinction might be thought bad and argues that, on a contractualist view, only some of them survive. A far more disciplined version of the intuition the extinction-stopper leans on.
  • Hume, David. A Treatise of Human Nature. 1739–40. Many standard editions. — The source of the is/ought observation that anchors the descriptive/normative distinction drawn in Chapter 5.
