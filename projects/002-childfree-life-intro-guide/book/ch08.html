The Regret Question, Honestly

What the evidence and lived experience actually say about regret — both ways.

"Regret is not a verdict on a life. It is one feeling among many, and it visits every kind of choice."

— a reflection drawn from research on decision-making

"But what if you regret it?" is the question that follows the childfree decision more closely than any other. It is asked with genuine concern, and it deserves a genuine answer — not a cheerful dismissal, and not the alarmist warning it is sometimes meant to be. This chapter takes the regret question seriously and honestly, which means resisting two temptations: the temptation to promise you will never regret a childfree life, and the temptation, common in pronatalist culture, to imply that you certainly will. The truth is quieter and more useful than either. Regret is real, it is human, and it runs in both directions.

The evidence is limited and mixed

The first honest thing to say is that the research here is thinner than the confidence of either side suggests. Studying regret well is genuinely hard: it is a private, shifting feeling; it is shaped by how the question is asked; people are reluctant to admit it, especially about children; and following the same individuals across decades is difficult and rare. As a result, anyone who tells you that "most childfree people end up regretting it" or that "no one ever regrets being childfree" is reaching past what the evidence can support.

What can be said carefully is this. A number of studies and surveys suggest that the large majority of people who chose to be childfree report being content with that choice and do not regret it. That is reassuring, but it is a general pattern, not a personal guarantee, and it does not erase the minority for whom regret is real. Honesty means holding both facts at once: most childfree people seem to do well by their own account, and some do feel loss. Neither half cancels the other.

In plain words: The evidence is limited and mixed. Most childfree people report no regret, but some do — and regret also exists among parents. The honest answer to "will you regret it?" is "probably not, but no choice this large comes with a guarantee."

Regret runs both ways

The "what if you regret it?" question almost always assumes regret flows in only one direction — from childlessness toward longing. But the conversation has, in recent years, been forced open at the other end too. The Israeli sociologist Orna Donath gave voice to a long-silenced experience in her research and in the resulting book Regretting Motherhood (2017), in which mothers spoke, often for the first time, about loving their children yet wishing they had not become parents. Her work did not claim that regret is common among parents, and it should not be read that way. What it did was break a taboo: it established that parental regret exists and can be spoken about, which makes the one-directional version of the question intellectually dishonest.

Every path carries the possibility of regret. The question is not which choice is safe, but which choice is yours.

Hold the comparison fairly. Some childfree people grieve a road not taken; some parents privately wish they had chosen otherwise. Both are real, both are human, and neither is more shameful than the other. What this symmetry dissolves is the idea that parenthood is the "safe" choice and childfree the "risky" one. There is no risk-free option. There is only the choice you can stand behind, made with open eyes.

Common worry

"If even a few childfree people regret it, isn't that proof the choice is dangerous — and shouldn't that fear settle the decision toward having children?"

In reality

By that logic, the existence of parental regret would equally prove parenthood is dangerous — and no one accepts that conclusion. The presence of some regret on a path tells you the path is consequential, not that it is wrong. Fear of regret is a poor sole basis for either choice, because both choices carry the possibility. A decision driven mainly by "what if I regret the other thing?" tends to produce exactly the resentment it was trying to avoid.

What seems to reduce regret

If regret cannot be ruled out, it can at least be made less likely — and here the patterns are encouraging and within your influence. Regret, in both directions, appears less about which choice was made and more about how it was made and how life unfolded around it. A few threads recur:

  • Ownership. Choices made deliberately, for one's own reasons, sit easier over time than choices made to please others or by drift and default.
  • Support and connection. Loneliness inflates regret. A childfree life rich in relationships, purpose, and community tends to feel chosen rather than lacking. (Later chapters address building exactly this.)
  • Framing. A life understood as full of its own goods ages better than one understood mainly as the absence of children.
  • Honesty over time. Revisiting the choice as life changes — and being truthful with yourself — beats clinging to a decision out of pride or pretending a settled question is still open.
  • Circumstance. Some of what shapes regret is health, finances, and luck, which no one fully controls — a reason for self-compassion whichever way things go.

Notice that none of these is "have children, just in case." Regret is not reduced by hedging against a feeling; it is reduced by making a genuine choice and then building a good life on top of it. The same is true for parents. The protective factor is integrity with oneself, not the particular fork taken.

Regret is not destiny

Finally, even where regret appears, it is worth keeping it in proportion. Regret is a feeling, not a sentence; it can coexist with a life that is, on balance, deeply good, and it can soften or pass. People who occasionally wonder about a road not taken are not living in ruin — they are living the ordinary human condition, in which every large choice closes some doors and we sometimes glance back at them. To choose childfree is not to gamble your future happiness; it is to take one of several real and respectable paths through a life, each with its own joys and its own occasional backward glances. Made honestly, on your own terms, it is no more a recipe for regret than any other.

Chapter in brief

  • Research on regret is limited and mixed; sweeping claims in either direction outrun the evidence.
  • Most childfree people report contentment with their choice, but a minority feel loss — and regret also exists among parents.
  • Orna Donath's Regretting Motherhood (2017) opened the conversation about parental regret, showing the "what if you regret it?" worry is not one-directional.
  • There is no risk-free path; regret reflects how a choice was made and how life unfolds more than which choice it was.
  • What reduces regret — ownership, connection, framing, honesty over time — is largely within reach, and none of it is "have children just in case." Regret is a feeling, not a destiny.
