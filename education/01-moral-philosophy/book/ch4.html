The Consent Argument

E verything so far has leaned, one way or another, on the asymmetry, and the last chapter left the asymmetry standing as a strong position rather than a settled one. If the volume stopped there, a reader won over by the symmetry objection could fairly walk away. This chapter is the first of two reasons not to. It builds a pillar that owes the asymmetry nothing.

The consent argument needs no Premise Three, no Premise Four, no four sub-asymmetries, no impersonal value. Grant that the whole of Part One fails and this pillar still stands, because it starts somewhere else. It starts from a plainer thought, one most people already hold. Imposing a serious, unavoidable, life-shaping condition on someone who cannot agree to it calls for a justification, and procreation cannot supply one of the right kind.

The argument's most careful modern form belongs to Seana Shiffrin, and a word about her is owed before we borrow it, because honesty in this series starts with not drafting allies who never enlisted. Shiffrin does not conclude that procreation is wrong. Her 1999 paper argues something more cautious. Procreation is morally weighty in a way ordinary moral thought underrates; it imposes the risk of serious harm without consent; the usual justifications for imposing harm on others do not carry over to it cleanly; and all of this should make us far more morally serious about having children than we tend to be. The antinatalist takes her framework, her analysis of when unconsented harm is permissible, and presses it toward a conclusion she herself declines to draw. That is a legitimate move. It has to be made in the open. What follows is the antinatalist's argument built on Shiffrin's foundation, not Shiffrin's own view, and keeping that line visible is exactly the kind of care the series refuses to drop.

When Imposing Harm Without Consent Is Allowed

Start where the argument starts, with a fact that looks at first to cut against it. We impose unconsented conditions on each other all the time, and most of these impositions are perfectly fine. A surgeon operating on an unconscious accident victim imposes a serious bodily intervention with no consent. A parent vaccinating an infant imposes a harm, a needle, a moment of pain, a small risk, with no agreement from the child. Society lays the burdens of law and taxation on people who never signed up for them. If unconsented imposition were wrong as such, ordinary life would be one long atrocity. So the consent argument cannot rest on the bare fact that procreation imposes without consent. It has to show that procreation lacks the particular features that make those other impositions all right.

What are those features? Shiffrin's framework picks them out with care. The impositions we accept tend to share a structure. They are undertaken to prevent a greater harm to the very person they fall on, or to rescue that person from a peril they are already in, in circumstances where consent cannot be had and where a reasonable person would be presumed to want the help. The surgeon cuts to save a life already in danger. The vaccine harms a little to head off grievous illness in the same child. Each imposition is defensive. It answers a threat that already hangs over the person. It leaves them better off by their own baseline. And the missing consent is excused because the person, through no fault of the agent, cannot give it while the clock runs.

Now hold procreation against that template and watch it fail to fit at every joint. The being brought into existence faces no peril awaiting rescue. Before creation there is no being in peril at all, no prior threat the imposition answers, no baseline of the person's own that creation improves. The imposition is not defensive. It is generative. It does not save someone from a danger; it manufactures the someone, and with the someone the entire set of dangers that existence will hold. And the missing consent is not missing because the person cannot give it at the moment. It is missing because there is no person to give it, and the very act of imposition is what calls the non- consenting party into being. Procreation imposes a profound, mixed, unconsented condition, life with all its goods and all its grave risks, on a party who is brought into existence by the imposition itself, for whom no prior peril made the imposition a rescue, and from whom consent was not merely unobtained but unobtainable in principle. None of the features that excuse the surgeon and the vaccinator is present. That is the core of the argument, and it borrows not one brick from the asymmetry.

Pure Benefits, and the Shape of Shiffrin's Claim

Shiffrin's framework has a piece worth drawing out on its own, because it is the hinge of the whole consent argument and it connects this pillar to the asymmetry of Part One more tightly than first appears. The cases where we accept unconsented imposition, she observes, are overwhelmingly cases where the imposition wards off a harm. The surgeon prevents death; the vaccinator prevents disease. What we are far more reluctant to accept is the unconsented imposition of harm in order to deliver a benefit, especially what Shiffrin calls a pure benefit: a good whose absence would not have been a harm or a deprivation. A windfall is a pure benefit. Being spared a danger is not; it is the removal of a harm. The distinction is sharp. To press a harm on someone without their consent to save them from a worse harm is the surgeon's case, and we permit it. To press a harm on someone without their consent so that they may enjoy a pure benefit, a good they were in no way lacking, is a different matter, and we are rightly wary of it, because the person was, by hypothesis, under no deficit that needed curing.

Now apply this to creation, and watch the consent pillar and the asymmetry turn out to be the same insight in two languages. The goods of a life are, with respect to the never-created person, pure benefits in exactly Shiffrin's sense. Their absence before creation was no harm and no deprivation, because there was no one being deprived. This is Premise Four, arrived at from the consent side rather than the value side. So procreation is the imposition, without consent, of the serious harms of existence, in order to confer goods that are pure benefits, goods the recipient was in no way lacking. It is the disfavored category, not the surgeon's favored one. The harms of life are real and fall on someone; the goods of life, however real, answer no prior need, because before creation there was no one with a need. Shiffrin's framework and Benatar's asymmetry converge here on one structural fact, that creation trades guaranteed harm for ungrounded benefit, and a reader who has held the asymmetry will recognize the consent argument as its deontic twin rather than a wholly separate claim. The independence advertised at the start of the chapter is real at the level of premises, since the consent argument needs no axiology of absent pain. It is looser than it looks at the level of the underlying picture, since both turn on the thought that the goods of existence relieve no prior lack.

The Stakes, the Missing Signature, and the Locked Exit

Three features of the imposed condition deepen the case. They are worth drawing out, because together they explain why procreation is not just one more unconsented imposition but an unusually grave one.

The first is the size of the stakes. The condition imposed is neither minor nor reversible. It is the whole human predicament: exposure to illness, bereavement, humiliation, terror, and the certainty of death, set alongside whatever goods the life will hold. We are careful about consent in proportion to stakes. We demand elaborate informed consent for medical procedures far less consequential than existence itself. Procreation imposes the highest-stakes condition there is and obtains no consent at all. The gap between the gravity of what is imposed and the complete absence of agreement is the first thing that should give us pause.

The second feature is that the missing signature is structural, not incidental. In ordinary high-stakes impositions we treat the inability to consent as a regrettable gap, to be filled as well as we can, by proxies, by advance directives, by the standard of what a reasonable person would want. Procreation can reach for none of these. There is no person whose wishes a proxy could represent, no prior self whose values an advance directive could record, no reasonable-person standard that applies to a party who does not yet exist and has, as yet, no nature to be reasonable about. The gap is not a gap in our knowledge of the person's will. It is the absence of any will to know. This is what sets the procreative case apart from every other imposition. Not that consent was hard to get, but that there was, in principle, no one to get it from.

The third feature is the one Shiffrin draws out most memorably. The imposed condition comes with an exit, and the exit costs more than anyone could reasonably be asked to pay. The standard reply to "you imposed this on me without my consent" is "and you are free to undo it." But the only exit from existence is death, and for the existing being who now has every interest in living, death is a catastrophe. It is not a clean return to the pre-imposition baseline. It is a fresh and terrible harm laid on top of the first. The person burdened with existence cannot decline it without paying a price that makes the exit no exit at all. A contract you cannot leave without destroying yourself is not one anyone would call fair, and existence is imposed on just those terms. Put the three together, maximal stakes, no possible signature, an exit priced past reason, and the force of the argument comes clear.

Hypothetical Consent, and Why It Does Not Rescue Procreation

The most natural defense of procreation against the consent argument appeals to hypothetical consent. Granted that actual consent is impossible, perhaps we may presume the consent the person would give, or will give, once they exist and find their life worth living. Most people do endorse their existence after the fact. Was their consent not, in a sense, safely presumed?

The appeal is tempting, and it fails, for a reason clean enough to state exactly. Hypothetical consent does real work in the cases the consent argument already grants, the unconscious surgical patient above all, where we ask what this existing person, with their known or reasonably inferred values, would want. It works there because there is a someone whose hypothetical will we are reconstructing: a person with a history, a character, preferences that predate the emergency. Procreation has no such someone. The "consent" would have to be assigned to a being who does not exist and who, before creation, has no values to consult. To presume their consent is not to reconstruct a will. It is to invent one and pin it to a void, which is not consent in any sense that does moral work. It is a fiction wearing consent's clothes.

The appeal to future endorsement fares no better, because it runs the order of operations backward. The imposition happens first. The endorsement, if it comes at all, comes only afterward, and you cannot retroactively consent on someone's behalf to the act that created them by pointing to an attitude they could form only once they already exist. The endorsement is real, and it matters for how we should treat the existing person. It is not nothing. But it cannot license the original imposition, because it did not and could not exist when the imposition was made.

The Objection That Matters: Proving Too Much

Here is the objection the consent argument has to survive, and it is a strong one, sharp enough that an honest treatment cannot file it down. If imposing serious, unavoidable, life-shaping conditions on a non-consenting future person is impermissible, then the argument seems to condemn far more than procreation. It seems to condemn a vast range of acts we take to be not just permissible but admirable. Every choice that shapes the conditions of future people imposes unconsented conditions on people who cannot agree: environmental policy, the building of institutions, the transmission of a culture, the bare decision to leave the world in any particular state. If imposing life's risks without consent is wrong, why is imposing the specific risks of the world they will inherit not equally wrong? Pressed consistently, the consent principle appears to indict the whole project of acting in ways that affect the unborn, which is to say almost everything we do. And an argument that condemns too much condemns nothing, since we know that shaping the future for those who cannot consent is sometimes exactly right.

The reply turns on a distinction the objection slides past, and the quality of the reply is the measure of whether the consent argument is a serious pillar or an overreach. The distinction is between imposing conditions on a person who will exist anyway and imposing existence itself. Environmental policy, institution-building, cultural transmission, all shape the world that future people, people who are coming regardless, will inhabit. These acts affect which conditions existing-to-be people will meet, against the settled background of their existence. Procreation comes before all of that. It does not adjust the conditions of a life that is on its way; it initiates the life, and with it every condition at once, including the bare exposure to harm that the other acts take for granted. So the consent argument, stated properly, does not say "never impose any unconsented condition on the future." It says the imposition of existence itself, the generative act that creates both the person and their entire exposure to harm, with no prior peril to justify it and no possible consent to license it, is the one imposition that fits none of our templates for permissible unconsented harm. That is a narrower claim, and it slips the "proves too much" objection by locating procreation's distinctiveness in its being the generative imposition rather than one of the countless modifying ones.

The residue is real, and the objection is not fully dissolved. The line between generating a condition and modifying one is clean in the central cases and blurry at the edges. A decision that brings a person about and sharply fixes the quality of their life, knowingly conceiving into severe deprivation, say, sits awkwardly across the boundary, and the wide-person- affecting theorist will press that the generative-versus-modifying distinction is doing suspiciously convenient work just where it is needed. The consent argument answers the objection in its central form and leaves a contested margin. That is the report, and it is why this pillar too is offered as strong rather than unanswerable.

The Strongest Reply: Procreation as Permissible Risk

There is a more sophisticated natalist response than any considered so far, and the chapter would be evading its duty to omit it. It comes from Rivka Weinberg, whose The Risk of a Lifetime is the most careful book-length treatment of procreative permissibility, and its power is that it concedes almost everything the consent argument asserts and still reaches the opposite conclusion. Weinberg grants that procreation imposes life's risks on someone who cannot consent. She grants that existence is no intrinsic benefit, that life is a risk rather than a gift. She does not reach for the comforting fictions of glad endorsement or presumed agreement. And from those concessions she argues, not that procreation is therefore wrong, but that it is a risk imposition that can be permissible, under fair conditions, the way many serious unconsented risk impositions are permissible.

The argument has two moves the consent pillar must answer. The first is that unconsented risk imposition is pervasive and often legitimate; we impose risks on future and present people constantly, and we judge such impositions by whether they meet fair terms, terms Weinberg spells out in contractualist principles balancing the would-be parents' real interest in procreating against the future person's interest in a flourishing life, rather than by the impossible test of actual consent. The second is more pointed. Children, Weinberg argues, do not have consent rights of the kind the argument demands, so the absence of consent cannot by itself condemn the act; here, as in much of how we treat children, justified paternalism takes the place of consent.

The reply to Weinberg is not that she is careless, because she is not, but that her two moves meet the distinctions the chapter has already drawn. The pervasiveness point is the "proves too much" objection in a more rigorous dress, and it meets the same answer: the risks we permissibly impose on future people are almost all modifying risks, imposed against the settled background of lives that are coming anyway, while procreation is the generative imposition that creates the risk-bearer along with every risk. Fair terms can govern how we shape an existence that will occur; the question the consent argument presses is whether the existence may be initiated at all, and a fairness principle that already presupposes a future person to be fair to does not reach it. The consent-rights point cuts the other way from how it is offered. To say children have no consent rights, so paternalism must stand in, is to concede that consent is unavailable and to rest the permission on paternalism instead. But paternalism is justified, in the surgeon's case and elsewhere, precisely when it serves the prior interests of the person it is exercised upon. Procreation has no such prior person and no such prior interest to serve, which is the whole difficulty; calling the imposition paternalistic names the gap rather than filling it. What the exchange with Weinberg yields is not a defeat for either side but a sharpening. She is surely right that procreation is sometimes permissible if it is governed like other risk impositions, and the antinatalist's claim must be that it is not relevantly like them, because it is generative rather than modifying and answers no prior interest. That Weinberg herself, having argued for permissibility, still classes procreation as not merely a welfare risk but a moral risk, a thing one might get wrong and bear responsibility for, is the measure of how far even the careful optimist has moved from the comfortable default. The disagreement that remains is real and turns on the generative-versus-modifying line, which is the same contested margin the previous section marked, now confirmed from a second direction.

What This Chapter Leaves Open

The consent argument's great virtue is its independence. It does not rest on the asymmetry, and a reader who threw out the whole of Part One can take this argument up unharmed. Its great vulnerability is the "proves too much" objection, which it answers with a distinction, generative versus modifying imposition, that holds firmly in the central cases and frays at the margins. Two further limits should be set down without softening.

The first: the argument's premises about permissible unconsented harm are themselves substantive moral claims, not neutral data. A critic who holds a more permissive view of when we may impose on the non-consenting, who thinks, for instance, that conferring a life full of goods is a benefit that licenses the imposition the way a clear benefit licenses the surgeon, will not be moved. The argument can press back that existence is not a benefit conferred on a prior person, since there was no prior person to benefit, the same point the asymmetry made and the same point the non-identity problem of Chapter Six will scrutinize. But at that juncture the consent argument leans back against the very ground Part One left contested. The pillars are independent in their starting points. They share a horizon, and the horizon is the metaphysics of benefiting and harming people who do not yet exist.

The second, and the most candid: Shiffrin herself, whose framework this is, did not reach the antinatalist conclusion, and the gap between her caution and the argument's conclusion is not one she failed to notice but one she chose not to cross. A reader is entitled to ask why. The answer is that the step from "procreation is morally weighty and under-justified" to "procreation is impermissible" needs an extra claim: that the weight can never be discharged, that no goods of the imposed life can justify the unconsented imposition of it. And that extra claim is exactly what the whole-life evaluation challenge of Chapter Seven contests. The consent argument is a real second pillar. It is not a pillar that stands wholly clear of the disputes the other chapters take up. No pillar in this field does, and a defender who claimed one did would be selling the reader the false security this series was built to refuse.
